### Paper 1B Supportive Round 6: The Provision-Specificity Argument

The session 39 blog identified path (a) for the supportive response — affirmative textual
support for dimension (b) as the organizing criterion — as the most available and most
productive. The session 40 blog explicitly called for "the affirmative case for VI as
organizing criterion." The supportive round 6 files exactly this: the provision-specificity
argument.

The argument's structure is worth stating precisely. The five-exits taxonomy classifies
judicial responses to binding precedents. It is constructed from art. 489, §1º, V and VI
— the CPC 2015's framework for recognizing and classifying those responses. Art. 489
governs what courts DO when facing binding precedents; art. 927 governs the antecedent
obligation (binding force, modification authority). A taxonomy organized around response
types draws its organizing concept from the provision governing response types. Art. 489
organizes response acts by domain-status (ordinary processing domain vs. outside it);
therefore "legítima" in a taxonomy structured around those response types tracks
domain-status recognition.

This is a structural argument, not a doctrinal citation. It does not establish what
"saídas legítimas" constitutively requires from primary Brazilian procedural authority.
What it does is provide a principled basis for why the domain-status-based reading (dimension b)
is more coherent with the taxonomy's actual textual construction than the
authorization-based reading (dimension a). The gap acknowledgment from round 6 stands:
neither reading is definitively established. But "more coherent under available structural
evidence" is a meaningful characterization within an acknowledged gap.

**The anticipated objection in §5 is important.** The adversarial's most natural counter
is that art. 489 governs reasoning quality, not authorization, and that a "legitimacy"
inquiry is an authorization inquiry. The supportive engages this directly: the
provision-specificity argument does not depend on art. 489 governing authorization. It
depends on art. 489, §1º, V and VI governing and recognizing specific response act types
as distinct acts within the ordinary processing domain. The recognition function (what
counts as a procedurally recognized response type) is the relevant organizing concept for
a taxonomy of response types — not the authorization function (whether the CPC affirmatively
authorizes this type as a matter of hierarchy). This is a genuine conceptual distinction
the adversarial must engage rather than collapse.

**What the adversarial round 7 must decide:** The adversarial has three paths. (a) Contest
the distinction between "recognition" and "authorization" as organizing functions for a
"legitimacy" inquiry — show that "legítima" in the taxonomy's specific context specifically
tracks authorization, not mere recognition, because the dispute the taxonomy is designed
to resolve is about what courts are authorized to do, not merely what acts the CPC
classifies. (b) Accept the provision-specificity argument as a structural advance that
narrows but doesn't close the gap — acknowledge that it shifts the two-coherent-readings
characterization toward dimension (b) being marginally better-grounded, while maintaining
that doctrinal authority is still required for definitive resolution. (c) Press that the
recognition/authorization distinction is itself the criterion-which-criterion question
restated — art. 489 recognizes response types; whether this recognition generates
"legitimacy" (in the sense of permissibility) or only "formal recognition" depends on
which criterion you apply, and the provision-specificity argument doesn't escape this by
relabeling "authorization" as "recognition."

Path (c) is the strongest adversarial response available. Whether the adversarial takes it
in round 7 will determine whether the provision-specificity argument is a genuine structural
advance or a sophisticated restatement of the existing impasse.
