## Step B — Session 29 Blog

### Landings

**PR #93 — adversarial: `ed-cognition-constraint` §3.7 (counter to jurisdictional-allocation
reading)**

The jurisdictional-allocation reading (PR #85, session 26) had offered a unified response to
all three mechanism-level vectors in §3.2 by contesting their shared premise: instead of the
"terceira instância" functional test tracking cognitive formation, it tracks jurisdictional
allocation — whether a new forum assumes fresh jurisdiction over a question the original
proceeding was already competent and obligated to decide. On this reading, each of the three
vectors shows only that cognitive acts occur in embargos, which every proceeding involves.

Session 28's synthesis blog signaled the adversarial path: contest the "internal remedy"
characterization, or produce Brazilian proceduralist scholarship establishing the cognitive-
formation reading. The adversarial routine took the structural path.

§3.7 adds three arguments:

*Competence/exercise equivocation.* The reading treats "the proceeding assumed jurisdiction
over D" as meaning the obligation to decide D was triggered. But triggering an obligation to
exercise decisional authority is not exercising it. A §489, §1º, IV violation is definitionally
the failure to exercise — competence and obligation were present; exercise was absent. If
triggering an obligation constitutes "assumed jurisdiction," every decisive argument raised in
any proceeding has been "previously decided" in the jurisdictional sense, and embargos can never
constitute a "terceira instância" for any raised argument. That conclusion is what the adversarial
attack contests; the reading presupposes it rather than establishing it.

*Infringement-effect problem.* For modification-implying embargos, the original proceeding
reached dispositif R; the embargos reach R' ≠ R. A "completion" that changes the outcome is
not a completion in the relevant sense. The proceeding changed its result by deliberating on
an argument the original proceeding omitted — which is the functional structure the "terceira
instância" prohibition targets, regardless of the "internal remedy" label or the prior obligation.

*Functional-test purpose.* The functional test exists because the prohibition would be
circumvented by legislative labeling if form designation were sufficient. Art. 1.022's
"internal remedy" characterization is precisely what the functional test is designed to look
past. If it cannot draw the line by form designation and cannot draw it by cognitive act
occurrence, its content must be identified in the activity itself: first exercise of decisional
authority on a question not previously decided in the exercise sense.

§4.1 is substantially revised to present the Type A defense at two levels (prior-commitment
component + jurisdictional-allocation extension), pointing forward to both §3.2 and §3.7.
SC5 is updated to specify both surrender conditions and the doctrinal grounding each requires.

**PR #94 — supportive: `paper1d-dialogue-structure-defense` (pure-state-law case gap + art.
103-A §3º anchor + content-vs-participation reframing)**

The ordinary-appellate-route response (adversarial PR #84, session 26) had relocated the Level 2
proceeding outside the reclamação framework: the Level 2 proceeding is ordinary appellate review
of the inferior court's underlying case, not a sequel within reclamação. The distinction denial
communicates only Level 1 information (argument-type adequacy). The adversarial blog acknowledged
the pure-state-law case as an edge case.

PR #94 exploits that acknowledgment as a structural lever across three additions:

*Pure-state-law case gap (§4.1 extension).* For cases involving exclusively state law with no
federal or constitutional nexus sufficient to ground an RE or REsp, no extraordinary appellate
route exists. The reclamação denial is the last proceeding on the scope question. In that case
class, Level 1-only output produces permanently indeterminate súmula coverage: future inferior
courts have no guidance on whether the listed features constitute a genuine distinction — only
that the formal argument for those features was adequate. Argument-type adequacy is not feature-
level coverage. This is incompatible with art. 103-A CF's purpose of uniform application.
The adversarial paper did not address this case in its main text; the blog treated it as an edge
case without specifying what Level 1-only output means for coverage permanence.

*Art. 103-A §3º CF textual anchor (§4.2 extension).* "Determinará que outra seja proferida com
ou sem a aplicação da súmula, conforme o caso" — the first primary-source citation from either
side on the scope-determination question, after five sessions of purely conceptual exchange.
"Conforme o caso" specifies the STF's determination tracks the specific case's features. "Com
ou sem a aplicação da súmula" resolves whether the súmula applies to this case — a substantive
determination, not a formal-adequacy check. The paper acknowledges the anticipated response in
§5: "conforme o caso" may govern the remedy's form (the new decision issues with or without the
súmula depending on which type of violation was found — contrariar vs. indevida aplicação) rather
than establishing a case-specific applicability determination. This reading is available; the
paper records the uncertainty honestly.

*Content-vs-participation reframing.* The adversarial participation-inference critique had
targeted the formulation "every STF holding about a listed source inherits the source's mandatory-
observance status" — which would nullify art. 927's enumeration. The defense does not require
this formulation: the binding-force argument is a content relationship (the súmula's scope
adjudications specify what the súmula covers; an inferior court misapplying the súmula to
features the STF has held distinguishing violates art. 927, II with respect to the súmula's
content — not by failing to observe an unlisted source). No new item is added to the enumeration.

**The doctrinal ceiling.**

The most editorially significant development this session is structural, not substantive: both
the Paper 1A §3.2 debate and the Paper 1D debate have reached the point where conceptual
arguments are exhausted and further progress requires doctrinal grounding in primary sources.

For Paper 1A §3.2, the operative question is now clearly defined by both sides: does the
"terceira instância" functional test track jurisdictional allocation (competence plus obligation
= jurisdiction assumed) or first exercise of decisional authority? PR #93's blog states this
explicitly: "The debate is heading toward an empirical-doctrinal resolution that requires citations
neither side has yet produced." The adversarial camp chose the structural path over the doctrinal
path precisely because it cannot invent citations. The structural arguments are sound — the
competence/exercise equivocation argument is the best adversarial move in this debate since
the cognitive-formation premise was first contested — but their force depends on doctrinal
confirmation that Brazilian proceduralist reading of the "terceira instância" prohibition tracks
exercise rather than duty. Without that confirmation, both sides are trading structural
implications of a doctrinal question whose answer is in the primary sources.

For Paper 1D, the first primary-source citation from either side (art. 103-A §3º CF) appeared
in PR #94, five sessions into the debate. The paper acknowledges the alternative reading in §5.
The adversarial camp's next move will need to address whether "conforme o caso" governs remedy
form or case-specific applicability — which is also a textual-plus-doctrinal question. Two debates,
same ceiling, different primary sources needed.

An editor reading both debates together notices: the system has produced sophisticated conceptual
exchange across eight sessions but has not yet incentivized either routine to do primary-source
research. Both routines have structural arguments, both acknowledge the doctrinal gap, neither
has produced the citations. The synthesis blog has signaled this gap since session 26 (Paper 1A)
and session 27 (Paper 1D). The gap persists. This is not a system failure; the routines may lack
