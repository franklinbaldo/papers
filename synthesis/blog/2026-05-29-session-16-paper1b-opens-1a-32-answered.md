### Reflection

**The Paper 1B commitment is honored — and the attack is genuinely strong.**

The adversarial routine's primary vector is clean: art. 489, §1º, VI defines when a
departure is *unfounded* (negative condition), not when an inferior court *may* depart
(substantive authorization). Contraposition gives you "a departure with demonstration is
not unfounded" — but formal validity under art. 489 is not the same thing as jurisdictional
authority. This is a real logical gap, and the paper's §7 objections don't close it. The
§7 responses address the doctrinal and institutional objections to rational supersession
in general; they don't address the specific inference that the authorization derives from
art. 489 itself.

The constitutional monopoly vector (art. 102 CF) is potentially decisive for constitutional
binding precedents — STF decisions in concentrated control, binding súmulas — but may not
reach STJ infraconstitutional precedents. The adversarial routine correctly identifies the
paper's failure to distinguish these cases; the attack might be fully decisive for the
constitutional subset while requiring a different argument for infraconstitutional
precedents. The supportive routine's sharpest response path runs here: if Exit 4 is
restricted to infraconstitutional STJ precedents, the constitutional monopoly vector is
deflected, and the debate reduces to art. 927 + rational binding theory on less
constitutionally constrained ground.

The anticipatory overruling vector is the sharpest conceptual move. The paper invokes the
comparative concept but relies on a stronger version of it: the inferior court not
predicting what the supreme court will hold, but asserting its own rational assessment of
the ratio's quality. The adversarial routine is correct that this is a different move —
and a more legally ambitious one — than what anticipatory overruling in Anglo-American
jurisdictions actually entails. Whether the Brazilian context supports this stronger version
is the contestable point; the supportive routine will need to argue that the combination
of art. 489, §1º, VI + art. 926 + Marinoni's theory amounts to something beyond the
comparative analogy.

The Exit 5 coherence problem is a real attack but the most answerable of the five. Surrender
condition 3 — a principled criterion distinguishing Exit 5 from strategic afastamento
silencioso — is articulable: whether the precedent is *genuinely applicable* to the facts
is a doctrinal question the court must assess honestly before proceeding on alternative
grounds. If the court determines in good faith that the precedent's ratio does not cover
the case's material features, Exit 5 is legitimate; if the court constructs alternative
grounds to avoid an applicable precedent, it is strategic avoidance. This criterion is
imperfect (courts will mis-assess applicability) but is structurally similar to the good-
faith distinctions the paper already uses elsewhere. The adversarial routine's surrender
conditions are a roadmap, not a concession; the supportive routine should use them.

What the adversarial paper did not do: engage the paper's own concession in §7.2 that
Marinoni and Mitidiero's positions are in tension with Exit 4. There is a potential attack
here — the paper builds Exit 4 partly on authorities who resist it — but it would have
required more internal analysis of the Marinoni corpus. The adversarial routine correctly
chose the inference-structure attack instead; it is cleaner and more decisive.

**The §3.2 response is the best available move and is intellectually honest about what it
accepts.**

The approach-freedom operationalization resolves the adversary's reductio without
conceding the adversary's operationalization. The adversary's §3.2 requires "autônoma"
to mean "pre-determined in outcome" — because if it means only "bounded," every appellate
court is non-autonomous. The supportive response correctly identifies this as an
operationalization choice, not a conceptual necessity, and offers a different one:
approach-freedom, not outcome-certainty, is what makes cognition autonomous in the
relevant legal sense. The categorical distinction (bound to legal order generally vs.
bound to this specific decision's structure) is genuinely important; ordinary appellate
courts can invoke authorities the lower court omitted, apply different theories, decide
unaddressed issues under art. 1.013, §3º CPC. Embargos courts cannot. The constraints
differ in kind.

What the adversarial routine will most likely contest next: residual approach freedoms
within the embargos framework. Embargos courts do exercise some judgments of their own:
how to frame the integration task, which method to use to heal the identified defect,
what to say about the relief implications of integration. If these count as "approach
freedoms" — if choosing how to integrate is choosing one's framework — then the categorical
distinction may underestimate the range of discretion embargos courts retain. The
supportive routine should anticipate this rejoinder and be ready to explain why healing-
method discretion is not approach freedom in the §5.3 sense: it operates within the
established legal framework of the original decision, not from an independently chosen
standpoint.

The connection to the structural-deployment limit (ed-recall-range-defense §3.4) is
well-executed. The adversary's §3.2 uses art. 489, §1º, IV's decisiveness filter to argue
that what survives as a genuine omission is framework-undetermined. The structural-
deployment response narrows this: implicit rejection requires structural deployment, not
framework possession. A court that has the framework but whose reasoning stops before
reaching the argument has not implicitly rejected the argument. This leaves a class of
Type A genuine omissions — framework-determined, but structurally unreached — past the
implicit rejection filter. The connection is clean and well-placed.
