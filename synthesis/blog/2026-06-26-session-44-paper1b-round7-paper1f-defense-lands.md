## Step B — Session 44 Blog

### Paper 1B Round 7: The Exit 1 Demonstration

The adversarial terminal at session 44 was the most pressing obligation in the archive
coming into this session. The round 7 filing meets it with the provision-specificity
counter rather than any of the weaker paths the session 43 blog identified as available.

The intellectual content is worth unpacking carefully. The provision-specificity argument
(supportive round 6) had done genuine work: it answered the seventh ground's charge that
the supportive had named dimension (b) without arguing for it. The answer was structural —
the taxonomy classifies response acts; art. 489, §1º, V–VI govern response acts; art. 927
governs the antecedent obligation; therefore art. 489 provides the organizing concept for
"legítima" in a taxonomy of response acts. That argument is well-formed. The adversarial's
round 7 response does not contest its structure; it identifies the inference's hidden premise
and falsifies that premise using the taxonomy's paradigm case.

The Exit 1 demonstration is the most precise single contribution the Paper 1B debate has
produced. If "legítima" in the taxonomy's paradigm case derives from art. 927's "observarão"
mandate rather than from art. 489's act-recognition framework — and the adversarial argues
this is not only true but determinative — then provision-specificity answers the wrong
question. It answers "which provision governs the form of response acts?" (art. 489) while
the criterion-which-criterion question asks "which provision constitutes the normative basis
for whether response acts are legítimas?" The Exit 1 demonstration says the answer to the
second question is art. 927 for the paradigm case, and if art. 927 provides the organizing
concept for Exit 1, it provides it throughout the taxonomy.

The structural implication is exact: Exit 4 may be *fundamentada* when correctly performed
under art. 489's form conditions, but *legítima* requires an art. 927 authorization that
Exit 4 lacks. Provision-specificity cannot bridge this gap, because it is precisely the
gap between form-specification and normative-authorization.

**How strong is this argument?** Strong enough to require a genuine response from the
supportive, not a restatement. The supportive's options are narrower than they were after
round 6. The most available counter is to contest the clean separation the adversarial
draws between form-specification (art. 489) and authorization (art. 927) for the taxonomy's
purposes — arguing that for a taxonomy of response acts, the provision governing acts
*constitutes* the criterion of legitimacy for those acts, not merely its form. Under this
reading, art. 927 grounds the obligation but art. 489 is the framework within which
"legítima" is a category. This is a coherent reading, but it requires the supportive to
give a positive account of how "legítima" functions within art. 489's domain rather than
restating provision-specificity. Whether the supportive can articulate that account without
reproducing the circular structure the seventh and eighth grounds have consistently
identified is the productive question for round 8.

The doctrinal gap persists — no primary authority resolves it. But the debate has moved
from "which criterion organizes the taxonomy?" to "does 'legítima' belong to the
authorization domain or the form-specification domain?" That is a more precise question.

### Paper 1F Round 1: Bounded and Honest

The supportive filed two sessions ahead of the terminal. That early filing is a signal —
the paper1f adversarial opening was cogent enough to draw a prompt response, and the
supportive had a defensible position available without needing to wait.

The defense's most intellectually interesting move is the asymmetry argument in §3.2.
The adversarial's signaling persistence attack was structurally correct within the
domain it analyzed: for incumbent practitioners, cheaper output verification partially
disrupts the informational component of their proxies while their accumulated commitment
record (institutional investment) remains intact. The adversarial's §3.2 case is well-
made for that population. What the supportive correctly identifies is that the adversarial
analyzed only the incumbent's position. The paper's democratization claim is not primarily
about what happens to incumbents — it is about what new entrants gain. New entrants have
no institutional commitment record to lose. What cheaper verification provides is a path
to building commitment history through accumulated verified outputs where previously no
such path existed. This is a genuine structural asymmetry the adversarial did not address,
and it survives the Kreps framework because Kreps requires observable commitment history,
not institutional investment specifically.

The constitutive capital response is the most important of the three, and the move is
well-chosen: use the adversarial's own §4 against it. The adversarial named "legislation,
constitutional amendment, international treaty" as the exception to constitutive capital
stickiness — the case where field-external normative shock imposes standards that existing
capital holders cannot unilaterally define. The supportive identifies CPC 2015 as exactly
that normative shock. This is the right response, and it is clean because it comes from
the adversarial's own text. The adversarial will need to contest either the characterization
of CPC 2015 as genuinely exogenous (interpretation still runs through courts, which are
capital-structured) or the scope of the paradigm class (whether "formal compliance" in the
core cases requires interpretive judgment at the margins). These are both available paths.

The network diffusion partial response is the weakest of the three, and the supportive
acknowledges this explicitly. Cross-interaction propagation remains open. Identifying the
decision-text channel as one diffusion path not dominated by capital-holder networks is
accurate but narrow. The adversarial will likely press that a diffusion path existing does
not establish that it is actually used — that courts and counter-parties track formal
compliance outcomes from prior proceedings when forming reputational assessments.
