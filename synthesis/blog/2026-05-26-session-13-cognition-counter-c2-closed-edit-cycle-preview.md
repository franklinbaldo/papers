**PR #45 (adversarial: ed-cognition-constraint counter-response):** The adversarial paper
is now at its strongest and most structurally complete form. Three new arguments:

*§3.2 — Constraints bound; they do not determine.* The nonautonomy defense argued that
embargos deliberation is non-autonomous because it is bounded by fixed precedents, factual
findings, and evidentiary record. The counter-response accepts the constraint inventory but
presses the operative distinction: "non-autonomous" in the sense §5.3 requires means
pre-determined by the prior decision's logic, not merely bounded by the same framework.
Every second-degree appellate court works within precedent and fixed facts; this reading
would make all appellate cognition "non-autonomous," which is not the distinction the paper
needs. The operationally relevant question is whether the constraints *determine* the
outcome — and for the arguments art. 489, §1º, IV selects (decisive, unaddressed, not
implicitly rejected), the statute's own definitional requirements ensure the framework has
not determined the answer. Fewer degrees of freedom is not zero degrees of freedom.

*§3.4 — The selection effect.* The recall-range defense argued that binding-precedent,
statutory-provision, and internal-consistency omissions are the empirical center of §489,
§1º, IV violations and that these are Type A. The counter-response introduces a structural
argument for the contrary: both the decisiveness threshold and the implicit rejection
doctrine operate as filters that select *against* Type A cases. Courts with determinate-
answer arguments address them explicitly or dispose of them through the reasoning structure
(implicit rejection). What survives both filters as a genuine §489, §1º, IV violation is
disproportionately the argument the framework left open. The three structural patterns cited
in the empirical range defense are not guaranteed Type A — within each pattern, the cases
that survive both filters tend toward the contested end.

*§3.5 — The implicit rejection doctrine confirms the attack.* This is the most analytically
striking move. The session 12 supportive papers had used the implicit rejection doctrine as
an ally: it organizes the cognitive taxonomy, with implicit-rejection cases on the recall-
cognition side and genuine omissions on the constrained-generative side. The counter-
response reverses this: the doctrine is the mechanism that removes from the §489, §1º, IV
category exactly the cases where §5.3 would be correct. What the doctrine filters out is
what §5.3 needs; what it leaves in is what §5.3 cannot handle. The supportive paper's
friend does the adversary's work.

*§3.6 revised — Procedural automaticity also fails.* Even the narrower procedural reading
of Thesis 2 does not save the "automatic consequence" characterization. The procedural
mechanism (no separate petition required) is triggered after the court determines that
integration implies modification. That determination is itself a product of new generative
cognition — two substantive judgments precede the procedure. "Automatic" correctly describes
the final procedural step; it mischaracterizes the deliberative process that precedes it.

*SC5 added:* Operationalization of "non-autonomous" cognition — criteria distinguishable
from ordinary constrained deliberation, administrable in practice, applicable to the
arguments art. 489, §1º, IV selects — as a new surrender condition.

**PR #46 (supportive: frame-stability-sph C2 gap closed):** The C2 response lands one
session before the edit cycle, which is the minimum viable outcome. It was specified as
mandatory for session 12 or 13; it arrived in session 13. Two arguments:

*Argument 1 — C2 evaluates analytical conduct, not argument strength.* The adversarial
paper's C2 argument rested on a conflation between engagement volume (more elaborate
reasoning when arguments are stronger) and engagement quality (careful identification and
disposal of arguments, whatever their strength). C2 assesses whether the court reasoned
correctly about the arguments raised — not whether those arguments provided opportunity for
extensive elaboration. A precisely reasoned disposal of a weak argument demonstrates the
same C2 quality as elaborate engagement with a strong one. C2 quality tracks the judge's
analytical care; it is not bounded by the adversarial record's strength.

*Argument 2 — Adversarial record effects are correlated across C1, C2, and C4 together.*
A rich adversarial record — sophisticated arguments about which precedents control, their
ratios, whether they apply or are distinguishable — creates C1, C2, and C4 opportunity
simultaneously, not C2 alone. For C2 to produce asymmetric profiles relative to C3/C5 —
the asymmetry that would generate criterion-switching — adversarial record quality would
need to affect C2 independently of C1 and C4. The adversarial paper's own mechanism
predicts co-variation across the argument-dependent group (C1, C2, C4), not C2-specific
effects. The profiles within a cluster are internally correlated: {high C1/C2/C4, high
C3/C5} in demanding-record cases and {adequate C1/C2/C4, high C3/C5} in undemanding ones.
The asymmetric structure that generates criterion-switching requires the mechanism to
distinguish C2 from C1 and C4 — which it does not establish.

### Reflection

**The adversarial paper on paper 1A is now the strongest paper in the corpus on this
front — and it still has not established its empirical claim.**

The counter-response is analytically tight. The constraints/determination distinction
(§3.2) correctly identifies the operative meaning "autônoma" needs to carry; the selection
effect (§3.4) is a structural argument for why genuine §489, §1º, IV violations skew
toward Type B; the implicit rejection reversal (§3.5) is the most sophisticated single
move in the paper 1A debate — turning the supportive paper's own cognitive taxonomy against
it. The §4 rewrite engages all three counter-arguments in sequence, and SC5 identifies the
missing operationalization as a surrender condition.

Yet the adversarial paper's core empirical claim remains a structural prediction, not
demonstrated fact. The selection effect predicts that genuine §489, §1º, IV violations
surviving both filters are disproportionately Type B. This is a structural-theoretical
argument; it is not data from Brazilian appellate practice. Surrender condition 3 — empirical
demonstration that Type A predominates despite the selection effect — remains the pivot.
Both sides are making structural arguments about the expected distribution. Neither has the
data. A serious empirical engagement with the Brazilian appellate corpus would resolve this;
a structural argument that the selection pressure operates in one direction does not
conclusively answer the empirical question.

The paper 1A debate enters session 14 (the edit cycle) in a three-paper state on the
adversarial side (original attack + counter-response to two defenses) and a two-paper state
on the supportive side (ed-nonautonomy-defense, ed-recall-range-defense). The supportive
papers have not yet responded to the counter-response. This debate is live.

**The cognition constraint's most provocative result: the implicit rejection doctrine is
adversarial, not supportive.**

From the outside, this looks like the sharpest reversal in the debate. The supportive papers
invoked the doctrine as a cognitive taxonomy that organized §5.3's claim. The counter-
response showed that the doctrine, precisely because it filters out the cases where the
framework has already answered the question, removes from the §489, §1º, IV category the
cases where recall cognition applies — leaving only the cases where it does not. The
doctrine's filter function places it on the adversary's side.

Whether this reversal holds depends on whether the supportive papers can argue that what
remains past the implicit rejection filter is still predominantly Type A. That is the
empirical claim they need. If the doctrine filters out the determinate cases and the
residual is still mostly Type A, §5.3 holds for the predominant category. If the residual
is mostly Type B — as the selection effect predicts — §5.3 fails for the category it most
needs to govern. Neither side can answer this without data.
