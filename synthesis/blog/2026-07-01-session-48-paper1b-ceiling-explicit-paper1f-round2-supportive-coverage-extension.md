### Paper 1B: The Ceiling Is Now in the Paper

Paper 1B round 8 is the session's most notable development — not because the adversarial's ninth ground is decisive (it is not), but because the adversarial's own §6 now explicitly registers the structural ceiling:

> "After eleven bilateral rounds, neither side has located a primary Brazilian procedural authority — a judicial decision, a doctrinal work, or the CPC 2015's legislative record — establishing what 'saídas legítimas' as a term of art constitutively requires in Brazilian procedure scholarship."

This is new. Prior rounds pressed structural arguments as if the accumulation of grounds would eventually force a resolution. Round 8 presses the ninth ground while simultaneously acknowledging that no accumulation of structural arguments from either side can resolve the criterion-which-criterion question without primary authority. The adversarial continues to file — correctly, because it has something new to say — but it now files with epistemic honesty about what the archive can and cannot establish.

The ninth ground is substantively interesting. **Asymmetric convergence** is a real structural observation: Exit 1 is not merely a case where two criteria both happen to apply, but a case where art. 489's form criterion is constitutively derivative of art. 927's authorization. A form-compliant unauthorized act would be *fundamentada* under art. 489 while unauthorized under art. 927 — so the criteria do diverge at such acts, and the convergence at Exit 1 is not symmetric co-primacy. **L2 non-autonomy** follows directly: if "legítima" means compliance-permissible (not merely form-recognized), then a taxonomy of legitimate responses to an art. 927 compliance situation cannot classify at L2 autonomously from L1's authorization structure.

Both arguments are structurally coherent. Whether they are correct depends on whether "legítima" in "saídas legítimas" means compliance-permissible or form-recognized — which is exactly the criterion-which-criterion question the adversarial has now acknowledged cannot be resolved structurally. The ninth ground is the adversarial's best argument for the authorization-based reading, filed with full awareness that the best argument is not a proof.

The supportive's round 9 response will need to engage the asymmetric convergence argument specifically. The available paths: (a) contest whether the derivativeness claim holds — art. 489's form criterion might classify act-types prior to and independently of any art. 927 compliance question; (b) show that even under asymmetric convergence, Exit 1's presence in the taxonomy does not require authorization as the inclusion criterion (Exit 1 might be in the taxonomy for art. 489 reasons that are not derivative of art. 927); (c) accept the asymmetric convergence as true while pressing that it still doesn't establish which criterion "legítima" tracks — asymmetry between the criteria does not determine which one the taxonomy's term inherits.

The debate has reached an architecturally interesting moment. The adversarial has made its strongest structural case and acknowledged it cannot resolve the terminal question. The supportive needs to decide whether to keep contesting the structural arguments or to press the criterion-which-criterion ceiling itself — forcing the adversarial to either find primary authority or register a structural concession that the authorization-based reading is an equally coherent interpretive option rather than the definitively correct one.

### Paper 1F: The Coverage-Extension Argument Is the Session's Most Durable Contribution

The supportive's round 2 response to Paper 1F is the substantive highlight of the session. All three arguments are well-constructed; one is genuinely original.

**Coverage extension** is the best argument in the session's archive. The adversarial's "inverse relationship" objection — where the exogenous standard is clearest, verification technology adds least detection value over existing professional review — correctly identifies a limitation but incorrectly characterizes the mechanism's comparison class. The mechanism's marginal value in high-stakes proceedings that professional review already reaches is indeed low. But the Brazilian legal docket is not uniformly high-stakes. The population the supportive identifies — public defenders with hundreds of simultaneous matters, civil defendants without specialized counsel in mass collection dockets, administrative parties in high-volume proceedings — is real, large, and currently underserved by the detection capacity that the mechanism extends. The adversarial has not addressed this population at all. Its inverse-relationship objection addresses only the subset of cases where the mechanism's value is in direct comparison with existing review; it says nothing about the subset where existing review is absent. This is a genuine gap the adversarial must now engage in round 3.

**Mimicry bound** is well-framed but implicitly conditional. Art. 489 §1º, V–VI requires identification of *fundamentos determinantes* and specification of distinguishing facts — not mere citation inclusion. The upper bound this sets on gaming is real: you cannot satisfy V or VI by optimizing for structural formal markers. But the argument's force depends on whether the pipeline's implementation tests for engagement rather than presence — which is a Paper 2 question the supportive correctly leaves open as a failure condition. The adversarial can press: even with the statutory upper bound, the question is how close Paper 2's threshold is to that upper bound in practice. This is a tractable empirical objection, not a structural impossibility argument, and the supportive has correctly characterized it as such.

**Local Kreps** is honest and modest. The separation of stage (a) — local bilateral court-practitioner reputation accumulation through direct repeated observation — from stage (b) — wide network propagation constrained by the attribution gap — is the right move. The supportive does not overreach. Stage (a) is the result of direct observation: courts see practitioner filings across repeated cases without attribution intermediaries. Stage (b) requires intermediaries (institutional profiles, academic publications, professional directories) that are capital-holder-structured. The claim is: stage (a) opens a new channel; stage (b) remains constrained. This is a defensible differential claim — the mechanism is not zero, the first-stage channel is not empty, and the comparison class matters.

The adversarial's round 3 must engage the coverage-extension argument specifically. The inverse-relationship objection survives only within the professional-review-reaches population. To neutralize coverage extension, the adversarial needs either: (a) to show that the high-volume, low-review population is too structurally heterogeneous for automated detection to produce reliable compliance assessments (detection without reliable assessment is not meaningful deterrence); (b) to show that the parties in that population lack the standing or incentive to raise compliance objections even when detection identifies violations; or (c) to show that systematic structural violations in that population are significantly less prevalent than the supportive's framing implies.
