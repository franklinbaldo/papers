### Editorial Assessment: Paper 1B Round 5 (PR #115)

**The prospective-taxonomy counter is the session's clearest structural contribution.**

Supportive round 4's prospective-taxonomy argument was identified in the session 33
synthesis blog as "the session's most important contribution across both routines' work"
and the hardest adversarial target. Round 5 meets it directly.

The conceptual distinction between criterion A (instance-level retrospective confirmation)
and criterion B (type-level prospective framework authorization) is precise and new to the
debate's six-round history on Paper 1B. The round 4 argument correctly identified that
criterion A is retrospective. The round 5 response correctly identifies that the adversarial
camp has never relied on criterion A. This is not a sidestep — the adversarial paper's
authorization argument was always framed in terms of what the CPC 2015's enacted provisions
authorize as a matter of type, not what reviewing courts will confirm in a given instance.
Art. 1.037 authorizes distinguishing. Art. 927 authorizes recognizing supersession. No
provision authorizes inferior-court departure by error-in-ratio argument. All three answers
are available before the court acts. Criterion B is prospectively determinable.

The adversarial blog acknowledges the ceiling: "I do not have a citation. No fabrication."
The structural advance is genuine; the debate's ceiling remains the same.

**The legislative-history argument is sound but faces a tractable response.**

The fundamentação/authorization independence argument correctly identifies that art. 489,
§1º, VI and art. 927 address different legal questions — the former a threshold condition
for a decision's reasoning quality, the latter a hierarchy question about which courts can
do what. The legislature's simultaneous enactment of both provisions without providing
substantive authorization for inferior-court departure through art. 927 is the core of
the argument.

The supportive camp's most available response: press a holistic reading of the CPC 2015
as a unit. The combination of art. 489, §1º, VI (specifying form for departure) and the
absence of an explicit prohibition in art. 927 (which addresses source-court modification)
might constitute legislative authorization by design — the legislature regulated the *how*
of departure, knowing the authorization question was contested, and chose not to prohibit
it in art. 927 either. The adversarial camp's round 5 surrender conditions already anticipate
this: the legislative-history path requires showing art. 489, §1º, VI was enacted as an
authorization provision, which is a strong claim. But a softer version — the legislature
regulated departure comprehensively through multiple provisions as a system — is available
without requiring that one provision carry the whole authorization weight. The supportive
camp has not yet advanced this systemic reading.

**Nine rounds in, the doctrinal citation gap is the debate's unresolved ceiling.**

Both sides have been unable to produce a primary source establishing what "saídas
legítimas" constitutively requires in Brazilian precedent scholarship. This is session 36;
the gap has existed since session 24 when the synthesis first named it. Neither side shows
signs of locating the citation. The structural arguments are increasingly refined — the
adversarial criterion A vs B distinction is precise; the supportive prospective-taxonomy
argument was well-targeted — but they are arguments about what the CPC permits, not
about what established doctrine says "saídas legítimas" requires. At some point a debate
that cannot resolve its foundational question through the available record will need to
either (a) produce a citation or (b) acknowledge the question is genuinely open in the
literature and characterize what the structural arguments establish about the interpretive
options. Neither side has proposed path (b) explicitly. An honest ceiling acknowledgment
in both papers would serve the author better than continued structural refinement that
circles the same unanswered question.

### Editorial Assessment: Paper 1A §3.2 Round 9 (PR #116)

**The framework-determinacy asymmetry is the most structurally decisive move in several
rounds of this debate.**

After eight bilateral exchanges, the Paper 1A §3.2 debate has produced a genuinely
unexpected structural configuration: the adversarial paper's §3.2 uses the criterion
(framework-determinacy — "constraints determine the outcome... recall cognition") that
the adversarial §3.8 then characterizes as insufficient to ground the jurisdictional-
allocation reading. The asymmetry exists within the adversarial paper itself, across §3.2
and §3.8, and the round 9 defense correctly names it.

The adversarial camp's most available response: argue that the §3.2 concession does NOT
use reading (c) / framework-determinacy as its criterion. The §3.2 language — "the
constraints do determine the outcome... This is recall cognition" — could be read as an
observation about cognitive type (what cognition the court performs), not as a claim that
the constitutional characterization follows from the framework-determinacy criterion as
such. Under this reading, §3.2 concedes that the attack mechanism (generative cognition)
has no purchase in Type A cases because those cases don't involve generative cognition —
a factual claim about cognitive type, not a structural claim about framework-determinacy
as a jurisdictional criterion. If this reading holds, the asymmetry dissolves: §3.8 can
correctly say framework-determinacy is insufficient for constitutional characterization
without contradicting §3.2's concession, which would be operating on different grounds.

Whether this response succeeds depends on whether "the constraints do determine the
outcome" can be parsed as a purely cognitive observation independent of the criterion
the jurisdictional-allocation reading proposes. It cannot be parsed that way cleanly:
"determine the outcome" implies something about what the framework commits to, which is
precisely reading (c). The adversarial camp would need to show that the §3.2 language
is purely about cognitive classification — what the court *does* — rather than about
what the framework *determines* — which is what reading (c) says. This distinction is
fine but potentially arguable.

**Convergence on constitutional substance is the round's most important development.**

The statutory-coherence concession point may be the debate's most under-noticed
structural development: both sides now agree that Type A modification-implying embargos
are constitutionally permissible under the CPC 2015. The adversarial §3.8 type-sensitivity
response accepts this in the course of contesting the vocabulary. The supportive round 9
identifies this acceptance clearly.

What remains is a vocabulary dispute: whether the constitutional demarcation is correctly
described as "exercise-exclusive reading, type-sensitive" (adversarial) or "jurisdictional-
allocation reading" (supportive). This is not a trivial dispute — the vocabulary choice
has implications for adjacent cases and for the paper's theoretical contribution to
Brazilian procedure scholarship. But it is a narrower dispute than the debate's earlier
stages, when the adversarial camp was contesting whether Type A cases were problematic
at all.

The synthesis blog for session 34 asked whether Paper 1A §3.2 would settle after seven
rounds if the adversarial round 8 failed to respond. It did not settle — round 8 was filed
on time, and round 8 advanced three genuine arguments. Round 9 narrows the debate
further. The question for the next adversarial filing: is the constitutional vocabulary
dispute substantive (different legal implications for adjacent cases) or purely nominal?
If substantive, the adversarial camp needs to specify the different implications. If the
two vocabularies reach all the same conclusions for all the same cases, the debate should
acknowledge this rather than prolonging it.

**The mechanism-specificity argument: limited but well-placed.**

The formal availability of future adversarial arguments is what it is — the adversarial
camp can always articulate a new mechanism. But the round 9 defense is right to name the
absence explicitly: eight rounds of bilateral development produced one mechanism as the
adversarial paper's entire basis for the constitutional concern. The §3.2 concession, read
in that context, implies something more than a narrow scope statement. The adversarial camp
should either articulate the alternative mechanism or acknowledge that no alternative
mechanism currently exists in the paper.

requires an empirical claim but could be structured around what existing annotators do

named categories.

The adversarial routine should not underestimate Paper 1C's terminal. This is not a
peripheral debate — the taxonomy paper's contribution claim depends on establishing that
the necessary/contingent axis provides value that other annotation approaches do not
provide. The adversarial round 2 established genuine pressure on this claim. A supportive
settlement of Paper 1C would extend the pattern: three consecutive supportive settlements

sustaining engagement across concurrent live debates.
