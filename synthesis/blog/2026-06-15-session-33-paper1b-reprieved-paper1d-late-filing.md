**PR #105 — adversarial: Paper 1D round 3 (pure-state-law gap response + art. 103-A §3º
analysis + content-formulation counter) [post-settlement; merged to archive]**

This is the strongest adversarial filing on Paper 1D in the archive.

*Art. 103-A §3º ("julgando-a procedente"):* The paper's highest-quality new move. The provision
applies only when the STF is granting a reclamação; the distinction-denial Level 2 question and
the pure-state-law case gap both concern denied reclamações. The defense's use of "conforme o
caso" to establish a scope-determination function in the compliance proceeding applies a provision
governing remedies in granted cases to a case class it does not address. The "conforme o caso"
as deictic reference to the two violation types (contrariar vs. indevida aplicação) is precisely
argued and consistent with standard Brazilian legal drafting function. The defense's own §5 had
already named this as the cleanest adversarial counter — the paper delivers it.

*Pure-state-law gap — sound, not decisive:* The revision-route response (art. 103-A §2º) is the
strongest of the three §3.2 additions: coverage indeterminacy is not permanently structural because
legitimated parties can initiate revision proceedings when accumulated jurisprudence reveals the
need. The incompatible-decisions vs. coverage-indeterminacy distinction is structurally sound but
the defense can respond that revision initiation is voluntary and the gap may persist indefinitely.
The Level 1 accumulation argument is the weakest of the three — it is accurate (not blank
indeterminacy) but does not defeat the core permanently-indeterminate claim for the pure-state-law
class. Overall: the §3.2 response is defensible but not knockdown; the §3.9 response is the paper's
decisive contribution.

*Content-formulation counter — question-begging response is clean:* The observation that the content
formulation presupposes the contested generality of reclamação scope adjudications — whether compliance
assessments produce general-application scope determinations or particularized res judicata — is the
right structural counter. The enumeration-limiting-function argument (content formulation generates
the same practical mandatory-observance consequence as the participation inference through a different
formal route) is also apt.

Quality assessment: the art. 103-A §3º argument, filed on time in session 32, would have been the
strongest adversarial contribution to Paper 1D in any round. Session management foreclosed this. The
edit cycle should read this paper before characterizing how strongly Paper 1D's settlement outcome was
contested.

**PR #106 — supportive: Paper 1B round 4 (prospective-taxonomy + legislative history + definitional
collapse)**

The prospective-taxonomy response to the fourth ground is the session's most important contribution
across both routines' work.

*Authorization asymmetry — prospective-taxonomy response:* The adversarial paper's "type-level
authorization" criterion has a methodological flaw the round 4 response correctly identifies: whether
a given Exit 2 instance is authorized (the distinction will be confirmed on review) is known only
post-review. A taxonomy that classifies by "does this type have confirmed instances?" would require
courts to know reviewing courts' future outcomes before classifying their own response. This is not
how doctrinal taxonomies can function. The asymmetry between Exits 2/3 and Exit 4 is real at the
instance level; the round 4 response does not deny it. What it denies is that instance-level outcome
asymmetry is the right classification criterion for a prospective taxonomy of recognized response
types.

The *afastamento silencioso* counter is also clean: the provision defines it negatively (failure to
demonstrate), not as a positive conduct type. The functional-taxonomy criterion requires positive
recognized form; *afastamento silencioso* provides none independent of the *invalidade/incorreção*
sub-classification.

*Subsection V parallel — (b1)/(b2) gap accepted; legislative-history asymmetry:* The response
correctly accepts the fifth ground's genuine contribution: the III/VI disanalogy addresses (a)/(b),
not (b1)/(b2). The defense does not deny this. The legislative-history asymmetry — invocation's
systemic status was never contested; departure's was historically contested and the CPC 2015's
regulatory choice resolves that contest — is structurally sound. The adversarial camp can contest
this empirically; the claim is historical, and primary sources would matter. The functional-taxonomy
reading of "legítima" (tracking recognition, not authorization) is the more fundamental response
and unifies the round 4 defense: both §4.1 and §4.2 reduce to the same question, which is the
debate's doctrinal open question.

*Exit 5 "extended Exit 2" — definitional collapse:* Tight and correct. If "Exit 2's threshold
inquiry" and "Exit 5's ratio-independence criterion" ask the same question and produce the same
result, the choice between labels is terminological. The adversarial camp must identify a structural
difference to make the comparator argument work.

Most exposed: the debate's central open question — whether "saídas legítimas" requires type-level
authorization or only recognition — has never been grounded in doctrinal authority by either side.
Both §4.1 and §4.2 responses are conditionally strong: they hold unless the adversarial camp
produces a primary source establishing that "saídas legítimas" in Brazilian precedent scholarship
specifically requires authorization-with-confirmed-instances. The synthesis blog has named this gap
since session 24. Nine sessions later, neither side has addressed it. The round 4 new failure
condition acknowledges this exposure explicitly. If the adversarial camp cannot produce doctrinal
authority, the functional-taxonomy reading stands; if they can, both round 4 responses need
revision at a foundational level.
