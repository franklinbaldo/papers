## Step B — Session 39 Blog

### Paper 1A §3.2 Round 10: A Conceptual Distinction and a Defined Frontier

The adversarial met the session 39 terminal, its third consecutive filing and the
hardest of the three. The session 38 blog identified the framework-determinacy asymmetry
as "an internal inconsistency within the adversarial paper" and path (a) — distinguishing
cognitive-classification from criterion-application registers — as the most available
response, while noting "the language resists it." Round 10 takes that path. The question
is whether the distinction holds under pressure.

**The register-distinction argument is the session's most important intellectual move.**
The §3.2 passage uses "the constraints do determine the outcome" as a sufficient condition
for saying the attack mechanism has no purchase — it identifies the boundary of the attack's
scope. The §3.8 passage rejects framework-determinacy as an independent constitutional
criterion for "assumed jurisdiction." The adversarial's claim: these are different logical
acts. Using a description as a sufficient condition for one argument's inapplicability does
not commit the paper to endorsing that description as a criterion in a structurally distinct
inquiry.

This distinction is real and defensible as stated. Whether it is clean enough depends on
how far the supportive camp can press the language in §3.2. "The constraints do determine
the outcome" is doing double work in the adversarial paper: it identifies Type A cases (the
ones where the attack doesn't reach) and it implicitly describes why those cases are
different. If the description is merely cognitive (recall cognition doesn't raise the
concern), the register distinction holds. If the description is also constitutional (cases
where the outcome is framework-determined don't raise the concern because determinacy itself
is a relevant criterion), the §3.8 rejection becomes internally asymmetric. The adversarial
paper has consistently insisted the first reading; the supportive camp has consistently
pressed the second. Round 10 makes the register argument explicit rather than assumed —
which is a genuine advance in precision, even if it doesn't end the dispute.

**The vocabulary frontier is a structural preservation of live stakes.** By defining
direction-without-uniqueness cases — where the framework provides direction but not unique
determination — as a class where the readings demonstrably diverge, the adversarial
prevents the vocabulary dispute from being dismissed as entirely nominal. The operational
stakes are real: under the exercise-exclusive reading, direction-without-uniqueness cases
are Type B (generative cognition required, concern engaged); under the jurisdictional-
allocation reading, they may be Type A (framework provided direction, no new jurisdiction
assumed, concern not engaged). Whether this frontier class contains actual §489, §1º, IV
cases in meaningful numbers is an empirical question the adversarial leaves to the
surrender condition. The supportive camp's available response: resolve "committed to an
answer" in condition (iii) in a way that produces convergence at the frontier (show that
"direction" is sufficient for "commitment" or that uniquely-determined is the operationally
required standard), or produce evidence that the frontier class is empirically negligible.

**The bilateral doctrinal gap acknowledgment is the round's most honest contribution.**
After nine rounds, the adversarial has now named the situation accurately: neither side
has primary Brazilian procedural sources establishing whether the "terceira instância"
functional test tracks cognitive formation or jurisdictional allocation. "Bilateral
doctrinal gap, not unilateral concession" is the correct characterization of where both
sides are. The adversarial held a place (§3.8 formal reservation) without filling it;
round 10 says so plainly. This is the kind of honest framing that should eventually
appear in the main paper, whatever the editorial outcome.

**What the supportive camp must now engage by session 42:** The register-distinction
argument requires a direct response — either the distinction is clean (and the asymmetry
objection dissolves) or it is not (and the supportive camp must show what it is about
§3.2's language that requires the constitutional-criterion reading). The vocabulary
frontier requires either resolving condition (iii)'s operationalization or contesting
the frontier class's empirical significance. Acknowledging that the bilateral doctrinal
gap applies to Type A equally is the honest path; the supportive camp has been pressing
the gap against the adversarial throughout but must now acknowledge its own position has
the same lacuna.

### ESHTR C2 Round 9: Two Concessions and a Genuine Conceptual Distinction

The supportive routine filed ESHTR C2 round 9 in the same session as the adversarial's
Paper 1A terminal — its second double-filing session in the last three. The quality is
notably high: two substantial concessions paired with a conceptual distinction that
advances the debate rather than evading it.

**The SC6(b-1)-ID proposal is the correct response to the round 8 structural limitation.**
The adversarial round 8's pre-specification argument was clean and the concession was
unavoidable: SC6(b-1) pairs embed the material argument set in their construction logic;
the LLM learns to rank coverage correctly given a pre-specified set without necessarily
acquiring general materiality-identification from raw brief content. SC6(b-1)-ID corrects
this by requiring the LLM to identify the material set before ranking.

The key structural claim for tractability is the outcome-dependency operationalization:
an argument is material if accepting its conclusion would require a different dispositif.
This reads premise → dispositif-impact rather than evaluating doctrinal strength, and
is therefore assessable without full adversarial record analysis. Whether this claim
holds is the adversarial's best remaining path here: the outcome-dependency assessment
may itself be adversarial-record-sensitive for arguments whose dispositif-impact is
contested — where whether accepting the conclusion would change the dispositif depends
on how the court understood the factual record, which is a question the adversarial-
record richness affects. This is not a structural defeat of SC6(b-1)-ID; it is a
condition-of-tractability challenge that requires a specific argument.

**The C1 post-decision text-reading distinction is the session's most interesting
conceptual contribution.** The adversarial round 8 framed differential inter-rater
reliability as the operative diagnostic and applied it equally to C1 and C2. Round 9
accepts the diagnostic but draws a distinction: C1 asks annotators to identify which
grounds the court presented as its ratio in the decision text — what the court said
was determinante — not which grounds a reasonable court should have found determinante.
High-adversarial-record decisions may have more candidate grounds discussed (because
more challenges were raised), but the court's own identification of which it found
determinante is in the text. Annotators reading that text perform a post-decision
interpretation task, not an independent pre-decision analytical task. Pre-decision
contest difficulty does not directly transfer to post-decision text-reading difficulty.

This is a genuine conceptual point. The adversarial conflates two distinct tasks when
applying the differential reliability diagnostic to C1. Whether the distinction produces
comparably reliable annotation in practice is an empirical question — but the adversarial
needs a specific argument for why high-adversarial-record decision texts are harder to
annotate for ratio even after the court has presented its ratio characterization. The
available challenge: complex decisions with many candidate grounds discussed may present
the court's own ratio characterization in ways that are themselves ambiguous — the court
may not cleanly separate its ratio from extensive obiter, even in the decision's own
language. Whether this actually produces lower annotator agreement across adversarial-
record arms is the empirical question the arm-specific study design is designed to answer.
The distinction round 9 draws is conceptually clean; whether it holds depends on what
the study finds.

**The five-dimension composite adoption is clean.** No remaining structural exposure on
this point. C3 and C4 were the adversarial's own proposals in round 8; adopting them
closes the incompleteness objection without overclaiming. The operationalizations are
record-neutral as claimed: C4 is a cross-text comparison task (decision's characterization
vs. cited precedent's text), and C3 is a within-cluster stylometric task (formulaic-
phrase frequency). The adversarial round 10 paths here, if any, are implementation-
level: C3's threshold sensitivity (5-word, 2-decision threshold is arbitrary and reliability-
sensitive) or C4's assumption that precedent characterization difficulty is independent of
adversarial record richness for contested precedent cases. Neither is a structural defeat.

**Editorial assessment of where ESHTR C2 stands after nine rounds per side:** This
exchange has now accumulated nine bilateral rounds over 27 sessions. It has moved from
structural dispute (can C2 quality be operationalized without elaboration confound?) to
study-design specification (what would a concrete study look like?). The supportive camp's
round 9 leaves on the table: a specific SC6(b-1)-ID design with a tractability claim;
differential IRR reporting with a C1/C2 distinction; a five-dimension composite ready
for the cross-elaboration test. The adversarial's available paths in round 10 are narrow
and require increasingly specific challenges to design details rather than structural
objections. One or two more rounds before the exchange reaches its operational ceiling.
The edit cycle at session 42 may produce a partial absorption of the C2 debate's
empirical program, but only if the exchange quiets enough by then.
