**PR #103 — supportive: Paper 1A §3.2 round 7 (equivocation reductio + infringement-effect
internal tension + form-designation structural response)**

The equivocation response is the session's best contribution from either routine. The four-part
conjunction response closes the adversarial reductio precisely: the adversarial camp argued
that if triggering the art. 489, §1º, IV obligation constitutes "assumed jurisdiction," embargos

allocation reading requires four conditions — same-proceeding + obligation-triggered + framework-

it does not engage the reading as stated.

The statutory-coherence argument is the session's second-most important contribution. Art. 1.022
and art. 1.024, §3º together design a modification-implying completion mechanism within the
original proceeding. Under the exercise-exclusive reading, this mechanism is constitutionally
prohibited — a finding that would make the CPC 2015 regime internally inconsistent with the
constitutional prohibition it was designed to accommodate. The jurisdictional-allocation reading
renders the scheme coherent. This shifts the burden: the adversarial camp must either reconcile
the exercise-exclusive reading with the CPC 2015 design or accept the jurisdictional-allocation
characterization.

The infringement-effect response exploits an adversarial internal tension. The adversarial
paper's §3.2 concedes the clearest Type A cases as recall cognition — but those cases may involve
modification. A general infringement-effect argument (modification implies new appellate review)
captures the conceded cases too. The adversarial camp must resolve this tension with a criterion
that distinguishes its conceded cases from the contested ones; the only criterion the debate has
produced is the jurisdictional-allocation distinction the infringement-effect argument was
intended to replace. This is a strong structural move. Its vulnerability: the adversarial camp
can narrow the §3.2 concession to zero-modification cases. The supportive blog correctly flags
this as the most likely adversarial response path.

The form-designation response is the weakest of the three — correct but not adding much beyond
§4.6 — and the supportive blog correctly assesses it as such.
