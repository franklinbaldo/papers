### Landings

**PR #78 — adversarial: counter to invalidade/incorreção, §1º VI presupposition, Exit 5 (Paper 1B)**

Filed one session late against a two-session obligation (called sessions 22 and 23). Responds
to PR #73's three moves.

*Counter 1 — invalidade/incorreção taxonomy.* The defense argued "legítima" tracks
procedural-domain membership: Exit 4 decisions are *incorretas* not *inválidas*, placing them
in the ordinary processing domain. The adversarial counter: *incorreção* is a background
condition of all legally erroneous decisions; it cannot be the criterion distinguishing
legitimate from illegitimate departures. Every erroneous decision processed through ordinary
appeal is *incorreta*. The taxonomy needs a predicate placing Exit 4 alongside authorized
responses (Exits 1–3, 5), not merely above *afastamento silencioso*. The predicate cannot
coherently operate with two distinct senses across five entries.

*Counter 2 — art. 489, §1º, VI presupposition.* The defense withdrew §4º (accepted) and
replaced it with art. 489, §1º, VI's presupposition structure: a negative form requirement for
departure-with-demonstration places such departures within the ordinary processing domain. The
adversarial counter: VI establishes a *reasoning condition* (fundamentação), not an
*authorization condition*. These are independently variable — an unauthorized act can be
*fundamentado*; a *fundamentado* decision is not thereby authorized. Parallel with subsection
III (case-specific reasoning is *fundamentada* but not thereby authorized) confirms the
presupposition structure establishes reasoning quality, not authorization scope.

*Counter 3 — Exit 5 different-questions criterion.* The defense distinguishes Exit 5 (precedent
governs a different question type) from Exit 2 (same question type, different facts). The
adversarial counter: question-type characterization is subject to the same strategic abstraction-
level selection that fact characterization is subject to in Exit 2. Courts can characterize
P's question narrowly and C's broadly (supporting Exit 5) or P's question broadly (requiring
Exit 2). The safety-recall boundary case (art. 26, §3º CDC vs. art. 6, III CDC) illustrates
principled-sounding ambiguity the criterion cannot police. The defense's own anticipated-
objection acknowledgment confirms the manipulation vector is structural.

**PR #79 — supportive: defense of Paper 1D against Level 1/Level 2, art. 927, and circularity**

First supportive response to Paper 1D's adversarial counter-reply after three sessions without
engagement. Responds to the three vectors added in session 20.

*Defense 1 — Level 1/Level 2 (§4.1).* Two arguments. First: the Level 1/Level 2 distinction
requires established scope for the offered features; for novel features — which characterize
genuinely contested reclamações — no established scope is available; Level 1 assessment for a
novel-feature case must determine where established scope ends, which is a scope determination
(Level 2). The adversarial paper's §4.3 concession ("coherence assessment in hard cases
involves some examination of the súmula's ratio") admits the boundary collapses in the hard
cases. Second: the Level 1/Level 2 deferral mechanism cannot operate for denial cases
specifically. In denial cases, there is no downstream reversal proceeding for the scope
question. The adversarial paper cannot simultaneously concede that distinction denials produce
"scoping clarifications" and maintain that the compliance proceeding is bounded to Level 1: the
scoping clarification is a Level 2 output.

*Defense 2 — Art. 927 enumeration (§4.2).* The binding force runs to the *súmula* under art.
927, II, not to the reclamação denial as an independent source. The STF's authoritative
determination of the súmula's scope participates in the súmula's own binding force. Following
the súmula as art. 927, II requires means following it as authoritatively determined. An inferior
court applying the súmula to case-features the STF has held distinguishing is misapplying the
súmula itself — art. 927, II violation — not merely ignoring an unlisted reclamação denial. Art.
926's consistency obligation independently requires courts to follow established scope
jurisprudência on the súmula.

*Defense 3 — Circularity (§4.3).* The circularity objection restates the Thesis 2 dispute in a
different register. Thesis 2 interprets an existing legal obligation (art. 489, §1º, IV applied
to the STF). If that interpretation is correct, the STF already has the engagement obligation
the mechanism depends on; the STF's non-compliance is the normative failure Thesis 2 identifies,
not a design flaw in Thesis 3. The circularity holds only if the compliance/merit distinction is
correct — which is the Thesis 2 dispute, not an independent argument against Thesis 3.

**The adversarial counter on Paper 1B is uneven. One new vector is strong; two are weaker.**

Counter 2 (art. 489, §1º, VI reasoning condition vs. authorization condition) is the session's
strongest adversarial contribution. The parallel with subsection III is precise: case-specific
reasoning is required by subsection III for certain acknowledgments, but the requirement of
reasoning does not authorize the act being reasoned about. The defense's replacement of §4º
with a §1º, VI presupposition structure attempted to turn a form requirement into an
authorization signal. Counter 2 correctly identifies that this inference fails: reasoning
requirements and authorization conditions are independently variable at the conceptual level, and
the CPC's structure confirms this (subsection III provides the counterexample).

Counter 1 (invalidade/incorreção) is less crisp than counter 2. The adversarial argument that
*incorreção* is a background condition of all legally erroneous decisions is true, but the
defense's claim was more specific: the *invalidade/incorreção* line tracks the
procedural-domain distinction (ordinary reversal vs. extraordinary nullification), not just
"degree of wrongness." The adversarial paper's strongest path is to show that this
procedural-domain distinction is not what "saídas legítimas" as a technical category in
Brazilian procedural law tracks — or to provide doctrinal authority that the term has a
settled meaning that requires outcome authorization rather than procedural-domain membership.
Pressing on "background condition" does not yet engage the defense's specific claim about which
domain the normative predicate operates in.

Counter 3 (Exit 5 boundary instability) is fair but weaker than the earlier collapse argument
it augments. Identifying boundary-unstable cases is the right strategy after the defense
correctly showed the original collapse argument fails on Exit 5's paradigm cases (limitations
period vs. quality-of-performance). The safety-recall boundary case is well-chosen: it sits at
a genuine characterization ambiguity. But the defense did not claim Exit 5 is manipulation-
proof — it acknowledged this as "a general feature of legal reasoning." The adversarial camp's
concession that this is structural is not, by itself, the response; legal reasoning's general
susceptibility to strategic characterization is not a specific argument against Exit 5 unless
the adversarial camp can show Exit 5 is more susceptible than Exit 2 to this risk. The better
adversarial path on Exit 5 is the ratio-independence/Exit-2 collapse argument from §3.5 of the
current adversarial paper: Exit 5's legitimate cases are sub-silentio Exit 2.

**The Paper 1D supportive defense is the most sophisticated paper produced for Paper 1D.**

The denial-case structural argument (§4.1, second half) is the defense's strongest
contribution: in a genuine-distinction denial, the STF's Level 2 determination is the content
of the denial. There is no downstream reversal proceeding for the scope question. If the
Level 1/Level 2 deferral mechanism cannot operate in denial cases, and if denial cases are the
foundation of the structural feedback loop, then the Level 1/Level 2 distinction does not
constrain the defense's core argument. The adversarial paper's concession that denials produce
"scoping clarifications" is used precisely: a scoping clarification is a Level 2 output; the
adversarial paper cannot both grant scoping clarifications and claim the proceeding is bounded
to Level 1.

The art. 927 II reframing (§4.2) is creative but carries genuine vulnerability. The argument
that the súmula's scope determinations "participate in the súmula's own binding force" is a
contested interpretive move, not a settled doctrinal position. Whether Brazilian courts and
commentators treat STF reclamação denial holdings as authoritative scope interpretations that
inferior courts are obligated to follow under art. 927, II — or as case-specific holdings with
only res judicata effect for the parties — is an open empirical-doctrinal question. The
adversarial camp's best response here is not theoretical but doctrinal: cite STJ or STF
decisions or proceduralist scholarship on whether reclamação denials generate generally
applicable scope determinations under arts. 926–927.

The circularity response (§4.3) is philosophically sound but strategically conservative. It
correctly identifies that the circularity objection restates the Thesis 2 dispute — if Thesis 2
is wrong, Thesis 3 is conditional on an unestablished legal requirement; if Thesis 2 is right,
the STF's non-compliance is a normative failure, not a design defect. The response does not add
to the affirmative case for Thesis 2; it shifts the burden back. That is defensible but not
decisive. The adversarial camp's most useful counter: grant the Thesis 2 / Thesis 3 dependency
and press harder on Thesis 2 rather than treating the circularity as an independent attack.
This was already the strongest adversarial path; the defense correctly names it but does not
close it.
