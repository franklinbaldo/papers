# The Distinction Mechanism and Deliberative Accumulation: A Defense of Paper 1D's Institutional Dialogue Thesis

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "Vinculação Racional e Diálogo Institucional" (`paper1D_vinculacao_racional_dialogo_institucional.md`), defends two connected claims:

**Thesis 2 (symmetry):** The duty of reasoning under art. 489, §1º, CPC is symmetric — it applies both to the inferior court departing from a binding precedent and to the STF judging the reclamação against that departure. The STF cannot respond to a reasoned departure with mere reaffirmation of the súmula's authority; it must engage the reasons exposed.

> "O STF que julga a reclamação e simplesmente reafirma a autoridade da súmula — sem identificar por que as razões expostas pelo órgão inferior não são suficientes para distinção ou superação — está proferindo decisão que não se considera fundamentada nos termos do art. 489, §1º, IV." (§3.2)

**Thesis 3 (structural channel):** The reclamação is not a silencing mechanism but a channel through which arguments that survive confrontation reach the tribunal competent to revise the precedent, producing a structure of rational institutional dialogue.

> "a reclamação não é mecanismo de silenciamento do argumento, mas canal pelo qual argumentos que resistem ao confronto alcançam o tribunal competente para revisar o precedente." (§4.2)

---

## 2. What This Support Adds

**Vector:** Direct defense. This paper responds to `otherwise/paper1d-reclamacao-compliance.md` ("The Reclamação Cannot Carry the Symmetry"), which attacks Theses 2 and 3 through a compliance-verification function argument, a structural mechanism gap, and — in its improved form — a Level 1 / Level 2 distinction, an art. 927 enumeration challenge, and a circularity argument directed at the feedback loop.

*Vector one (inference error):* The adversarial paper argues that the reclamação's conclusion concerns compliance — did the departure occur and was it formally permissible? (question A) — not the precedent's substantive correctness (question B). An inferior court's departure reasons address question B. Arguments about question B do not bear on the compliance conclusion (question A). Therefore, art. 489, §1º, IV's engagement obligation for arguments "capable of undermining the conclusion" does not extend to departure reasons in reclamação proceedings.

*Vector two (Level 1 / Level 2):* The adversarial paper refines the inference-error attack: even accepting that a pure presence test is too weak and that coherence assessment requires some examination of the súmula's scope, the compliance proceeding performs only Level 1 assessment (was a coherent distinction-type argument provided?) rather than Level 2 assessment (is the distinction substantively correct?). Level 1 asks whether the argument coherently positions itself relative to the súmula's established scope; Level 2 adjudicates whether an alternative scope claim is correct. The compliance proceeding is bounded at Level 1.

*Vector three (structural mechanism gap):* The reclamação's procedural outputs are grant (cassação) or denial. Neither directly produces revision of a súmula vinculante. Formal revision requires separate procedures under Lei 11.417/2006, art. 2º. The dialogue Paper 1D describes depends on the STF voluntarily initiating revision after the reclamação — an informal channel at the STF's discretion, not a structural mechanism built into the proceeding.

*Vector four (art. 927 enumeration):* Reclamação denials are not listed among art. 927 CPC's mandatory-observance precedent sources: the enumeration covers concentrated constitutional control decisions, full-court súmulas, recursos repetitivos decisions, IRDR and IAC decisions, and summary opinions of plenary full courts. Distinction denials produce res judicata for the parties but not the mandatory observance art. 927 requires. The feedback loop produces informal persuasive precedent, not binding precedent.

*Vector five (circularity):* For distinction denials to generate scope-defining content — even informally — they must articulate which features distinguish the case and why. This requires the STF to engage departure reasoning substantively. But Thesis 2 asserts the STF currently fails to do this. The structural feedback loop is therefore conditional on the normative prescription being adopted, not on the current system's structure.

This paper accepts that formal revision of a súmula vinculante requires separate procedures under Lei 11.417/2006 and is not produced by the reclamação proceeding itself. It contests: (a) the Level 1 / Level 2 distinction as applied to genuine-distinction denial cases; (b) the characterization of distinction-denial scope determinations as lacking binding effect; and (c) the characterization of the Thesis 2 / Thesis 3 relationship as circular.

## 3. Reconstructing the Attack

### 3.1 The Compliance-Proceeding Function (Adversarial §3.1)

The reclamação's constitutional purpose is authority preservation (art. 102, I, "l", CF). The proceeding determines whether the lower court's decision is compatible with an existing binding norm. The STF is not exercising substantive jurisdiction on a contested legal question; it is verifying compliance with a norm already established. The reclamação's conclusion concerns question A (did the departure violate the binding norm?), not question B (is the precedent substantively correct?). Art. 489, §1º, IV requires engagement with arguments bearing on the reclamação's conclusion; departure reasons bear on question B, not question A; therefore the engagement obligation does not extend to departure reasons within the reclamação proceeding.

### 3.2 The Level 1 / Level 2 Refinement (Adversarial §3.2)

The adversarial paper accepts that a pure presence test (was any departure argument provided?) is too weak — coherence assessment is required. Its refinement: the compliance proceeding performs Level 1 assessment (was a coherent distinction-type argument provided, as opposed to no argument or a structurally incoherent attempt?) not Level 2 assessment (is the distinction substantively correct?). Level 1 asks whether the argument coherently positions itself relative to the súmula's established scope. This examination is bounded: the compliance proceeding consults the established scope without adjudicating whether an alternative scope claim is correct. Departure reasons are question-B arguments (what should the scope be?), not question-A arguments (was the departure formally adequate?).

### 3.3 The Structural Mechanism and Circularity (Adversarial §§3.3, 3.4)

The structural feedback loop argument (§4.2 of this defense) builds from the adversarial paper's concession that the STF can deny a reclamação on genuine-distinction grounds and that this produces a scoping clarification. The adversarial paper argues, first, that this clarification is not binding precedent under art. 927 because reclamação denials are not listed in art. 927's enumeration; second, that even as informal persuasive precedent, the loop requires the STF to articulate which features distinguish the case and why — which requires the substantive engagement that Thesis 2 says the STF currently fails to provide. The structural channel claim is therefore conditional on Thesis 2's normative prescription already being observed, making it a description of a reformed system, not of the existing system's design.

---

## 4. The Defense

### 4.1 Level 1 Assessment for Denial Cases Requires Scope Determination

The Level 1 / Level 2 distinction is available where the súmula's established scope already speaks to the features offered. When the STF has previously addressed whether features of the offered type are distinguishing, a Level 1 coherence assessment can consult that prior determination without generating a new scope judgment. But the cases that generate genuine reclamação disputes — contested reclamações that reach briefing and judgment — are precisely those where the STF has not previously addressed whether the offered features fall within or outside the súmula's scope. For novel features, the Level 1 assessment cannot consult an "established scope" that is silent on those features. It must determine where the established scope ends, which is itself a scope determination.

The adversarial paper concedes in its anticipated-reply section that "coherence assessment in hard cases involves some examination of the súmula's ratio." This concession identifies the class where Level 1 and Level 2 are not distinct: whenever examining the ratio is necessary to assess coherence, the compliance proceeding is performing the same inquiry that Level 2 assessment requires. The adversarial paper then claims this examination is "bounded differently" — the compliance proceeding asks whether the argument coheres with established scope, the reversal proceeding asks whether the scope claim is correct. But for novel-feature cases, there is no "established scope" to bound the coherence assessment. The hard cases are precisely those where established scope is silent, and in those cases the Level 1 / Level 2 distinction provides no boundary.

There is a further structural argument specific to denial cases. The adversarial paper's Level 1 / Level 2 framework claims that Level 2 questions — substantive correctness of the scope claim — are reserved for the reversal proceeding. This deferral mechanism has purchase for grant cases (cassação): when the STF grants the reclamação and orders cassação, the underlying merits are addressed in whatever appellate route follows. But for denial cases on genuine-distinction grounds, there is no reversal proceeding for the scope question. The reclamação denial is the proceeding in which the STF holds that features F₁, F₂ fall outside the súmula's scope. If the STF defers the scope question to a reversal proceeding in a distinction denial, no reversal occurs — the inferior court's decision stands, and the scope question is never resolved substantively. Either the denial produces a genuine scope determination (which requires Level 2 engagement in the denial itself), or the denial produces no substantive scope content at all. The adversarial paper cannot simultaneously concede that distinction denials produce "scoping clarifications" and maintain that the compliance proceeding is bounded to Level 1: the scoping clarification is a Level 2 output.

This argument is narrower than claiming all reclamação judgments require substantive engagement. The defense does not contest that grant cases (cassação for formally inadequate departures) may be resolved at Level 1. It holds that denial cases on genuine-distinction grounds — the specific case that generates the structural feedback loop Thesis 3 relies on — necessarily incorporate a scope determination that is not bounded at Level 1.

### 4.2 The Distinction Mechanism Generates Near-Binding Scope Determinations

The adversarial paper correctly identifies that reclamação denials are not independently listed in art. 927's enumeration of mandatory-observance sources. The argument conflates the binding source with the authoritative interpretation of that source's scope.

Art. 927, II makes "súmulas do plenário do Supremo Tribunal Federal" mandatory-observance sources. When the STF in a reclamação denial holds that the súmula does not apply to cases with features F₁, F₂, that holding defines the súmula's scope. Following the súmula as art. 927, II requires means following it as authoritatively determined. An inferior court applying that súmula to a case with features F₁, F₂ after the STF has held those features are distinguishing is not failing to follow the reclamação denial — it is misapplying the súmula itself. The súmula, as determined to cover cases without features F₁, F₂, is the mandatory-observance source. An inferior court that applies the súmula to a case with F₁, F₂ is applying the súmula to a class the STF has authoritatively held it does not cover. This is non-compliance with art. 927, II regardless of whether the reclamação denial is independently listed.

The adversarial paper's framing requires that the binding-precedent obligation run to the reclamação denial as an independent source. The correct framing is that the obligation runs to the súmula, and the súmula's scope is determined by the STF's authoritative interpretations — including reclamação-denial scope holdings. Reclamação denials produce binding scope determinations not as independent precedent but as authoritative interpretations of a source already listed.

Art. 926 CPC independently establishes a consistency obligation: courts must maintain jurisprudência estável, íntegra e coerente. An inferior court applying the same súmula to a case with features F₁, F₂ after the STF has held those features fall outside the súmula's scope produces inconsistent jurisprudência in violation of art. 926. The obligation to maintain coherent jurisprudência applies to how lower courts apply binding norms, including the scope determinations the superior court has made in applying those norms. Art. 926's coherence obligation operates independently of art. 927's enumeration.

The adversarial paper's strongest response is that de facto deference is variable across jurisdictions and subject matters, and that a channel operating through variable deference is informal by definition. The art. 927 / art. 926 arguments contest this characterization: the binding force of scope-determination holdings flows from the súmula's own art. 927, II status and from art. 926's procedural obligation — not from variable institutional deference. The channel is informal only in the narrow sense that the reclamação denial is not independently listed; it is structurally grounded in provisions that govern both the súmula's observance and the courts' consistency obligation.

### 4.3 The Circularity Argument Restates the Thesis 2 Dispute

The circularity argument holds that the structural loop is conditional on the STF already engaging departure reasoning substantively — which is what Thesis 2 prescribes and what current practice fails to deliver. The loop is therefore not a description of the existing system's design but of a system that has already adopted Thesis 2's prescription.

This characterization of the relationship between Thesis 2 and Thesis 3 is incorrect. Thesis 2 is a claim about what existing law requires. It argues that art. 489, §1º, IV — on its correct interpretation, applied without exception to the STF — obligates the STF to engage departure reasons when those reasons constitute arguments capable of undermining the reclamação's conclusion. Whether this interpretation is correct is disputed by the adversarial paper's compliance/merit distinction. But the nature of the claim is an interpretation of an existing provision, not a proposal to create a new legal requirement.

Thesis 3 then holds that the reclamação proceeding, operating under the legal requirements Thesis 2 correctly identifies, is designed to produce institutional dialogue through the distinction mechanism. The conditional structure — if the STF satisfies the existing engagement obligation, the mechanism produces structural dialogue — is not circular. It is the standard structure of normative-institutional argument: identifying what a mechanism produces when legal requirements are satisfied, as the basis for holding those requirements should be enforced.

The adversarial paper's §4.5 (lex ferenda reading) anticipates this response and argues that the design-capacity claim collapses into a reform proposal. The distinction holds: lex ferenda argues that the law should require X, where the current law does not. The present argument holds that the current law already requires X (art. 489, §1º, IV applied to the STF's reclamação judgments as Thesis 2 argues), and that the mechanism is designed to produce Y under that existing requirement. If the adversarial paper's compliance/merit distinction is correct, the requirement does not exist, the mechanism's design-capacity is unavailable, and the circularity holds. But this outcome is conditional on the adversarial paper's Thesis 2 attack succeeding. The circularity objection does not add independent force to the adversarial case; it restates the Thesis 2 dispute — if the compliance/merit distinction is correct, then Thesis 2 is wrong and Thesis 3 is conditional on an unestablished legal requirement. This is a consequence of defeating Thesis 2, not an independent argument against Thesis 3.

The defense accepts that the structural loop is not currently fully operational, because the STF currently does not engage departure reasoning as Thesis 2 requires. This is the normative failure Thesis 2 identifies. Thesis 3 describes the mechanism's designed capacity under the requirements that exist; Thesis 2 identifies the failure to satisfy those requirements. The two theses are complements: Thesis 2 explains why the mechanism is under-utilized; Thesis 3 explains what it is designed to produce when utilized.

### 4.4 Deliberative Accumulation Links the Structural Channel to Formal Revision

Paper 1D §5.3 describes how the two mechanisms work together: accumulated reclamação judgments in which the STF distinguishes, qualifies, and clarifies the súmula's scope create a public record of the precedent's contested limits. This record constitutes material for the formal revision process.

The adversarial paper correctly identifies that formal revision under Lei 11.417/2006 requires separate procedures — proposal and two-thirds approval — and that legitimated parties under art. 103-A, §2º act on institutional discretion, not procedural obligation. These are real limitations on the strength of the deliberative accumulation bridge. The bridge is mediated, not direct.

The defense's modest claim is that the connection is not purely informal in the sense of being entirely discretionary. Art. 103-A, §2º's legitimated parties — the Attorney General, the Federal Council of the Bar, the Solicitor General — act within a deliberative context in which the STF's own reclamação jurisprudence on the súmula is part of the public doctrinal record. The STF in formal revision proceedings addresses that record. This is not procedural compulsion; it is structural input. The characterization of the accumulation bridge as "informal throughout" understates the degree to which the reclamação record structures the available inputs for formal revision, even where it does not mandate an outcome.

### 4.5 Art. 927, §4º as Supplementary Textual Support

Art. 927, §4º, CPC requires that modifications to established jurisprudence be "fundamentadas de maneira adequada e específica, considerando os princípios da segurança jurídica, da proteção da confiança e da isonomia." The adversarial paper's §3.6 argues this provision governs modification, not application: a reclamação denial on distinction grounds applies the existing súmula to determine it does not cover a specific case; it does not modify the súmula's text or ratio. The provision's operative term is modificação; application is not modification.

The adversarial paper's §3.6 argument has force on the provision's text. The §4º argument is supplementary: it addresses the STF's reclamação judgments insofar as accumulated distinction denials progressively narrow the súmula's active scope, eventually constituting a functional modification of its operational reach. Whether this triggers §4º or falls under a narrower reading of modificação is a tractable textual question. The primary binding-force argument operates through art. 927, II (súmula-scope interpretation) and art. 926 (consistency obligation), independent of §4º. The §4º route is an additional argument for those cases where the accumulated distinction record approaches the functional equivalent of scope revision.

### 4.6 The Practice Gap Reflects the Reclamação's Correct Function — but Practice Cannot Establish Constitutional Understanding

The adversarial paper argues that the STF's plenário virtual processing — consistent compliance-only review across different cases, bench compositions, and legal areas over decades — reflects correct institutional understanding of the reclamação's compliance function. The pattern is too systematic to attribute to procedural efficiency alone.

Practice cannot establish correct constitutional understanding in this context for two reasons. First, the plenário virtual's efficiency design explains the brevity; it does not explain what constitutional interpretation the brevity reflects. An institution can systematically practice X while violating a constitutional requirement to do Y, particularly where Y is more burdensome than X and the institution faces no external enforcement pressure. The STF's reclamação docket processes thousands of cases annually; the plenário virtual developed as a high-volume processing mechanism. Reading the consistency of the procedure as evidence of correct constitutional interpretation conflates procedural choice with constitutional position.

Second, the scale-of-nullity argument — that if Thesis 2 is correct, virtually every plenário virtual reclamação judgment against a reasoned departure would be null — is an observation about the consequences of Thesis 2 being correct, not an argument against Thesis 2's correctness. Constitutional requirements sometimes have systematic implications for established practices; the scope of the implication is not evidence against the requirement. Paper 1D makes a normative argument against a practice that proceduralist scholars have independently identified as falling short of the fundamentação requirements. The adversarial inference from practice-scale to correct constitutional understanding is exactly the move the paper contests.

## 5. Anticipated Objection

The Level 1 / Level 2 distinction in the adversarial paper's §3.2 — the primary remaining challenge — holds that coherence assessment in hard cases is bounded by the súmula's established scope, distinguishing the compliance proceeding's inquiry from the reversal proceeding's substantive-correctness inquiry.

The objection's force depends entirely on the availability of "established scope" for the features offered. For features previously addressed in STF jurisprudence, Level 1 assessment can consult existing holdings. For features the STF has not addressed — which is the characteristic class of genuinely contested reclamações — no established scope determination is available to bound the Level 1 inquiry. The adversarial paper's §4.3 concedes that "coherence assessment in hard cases involves some examination of the súmula's ratio." Once ratio examination is required to determine coherence, the distinction between "coherence with established scope" (Level 1) and "correctness of alternative scope claim" (Level 2) is not available: examining the ratio to assess coherence for a novel feature is examining the ratio to determine whether that feature falls within the scope — which is the Level 2 inquiry.

The adversarial paper might respond that even for novel features, the compliance proceeding asks "does this argument cohere with what the súmula already covers?" while the reversal proceeding asks "should the scope be extended?" But this framing presents a false choice: for a novel-feature case, determining whether the offered features fall within what the súmula "already covers" — its existing scope — requires determining what the súmula's underlying reasoning tracks, and whether the novel features are relevantly similar to the paradigm cases. This is not a formal consultation of "established scope"; it is a substantive determination of scope extension, which is Level 2.

The structural denial-case argument (§4.1 of this defense) provides the clearest constraint: in a genuine-distinction denial, the STF's Level 2 determination is the content of the denial. There is no downstream reversal proceeding for the scope question. If the Level 1/Level 2 deferral mechanism cannot operate in denial cases, and if denial cases are the foundation of the structural feedback loop, then the Level 1/Level 2 distinction does not constrain the defense's core argument.

## 6. Scope of This Defense

This paper defends:

- **Thesis 2 (symmetry)** as applied to reclamações against adequately reasoned departures: adequacy assessment of reasoned departures requires substantive engagement because determining whether a distinction is genuine or a supersession argument is sufficient is inseparable from the precedent's substantive content.
- **Thesis 3 (structural channel)** through the distinction mechanism: reclamação denial on genuine distinction grounds generates a scope determination that participates in the súmula's own binding force under art. 927, II, and that inferior courts are obligated to follow under art. 926's consistency requirement.
- **The deliberative accumulation mechanism:** accumulated reclamação distinctions create a jurisprudential record that feeds the formal revision process as structural input, even if not as procedural compulsion.
- **Art. 927, §4º as supplementary textual support:** the §4º route is secondary to the art. 927, II and art. 926 binding-force arguments; its applicability turns on whether accumulated scope-narrowing constitutes functional modification.

This paper accepts:

- Formal revision of a súmula vinculante requires separate procedures under Lei 11.417/2006. The reclamação proceeding does not directly produce formal revision.
- The reclamação's constitutional purpose under art. 102, I, "l", CF is authority preservation.
- Current STF practice does not consistently engage departure reasoning in reclamação judgments.
- The structural feedback loop is not currently fully operational because of the STF's non-compliance with the engagement obligation Thesis 2 identifies. The mechanism's designed capacity and its current operation are distinct: the former is the structural claim Thesis 3 makes; the latter is the normative failure Thesis 2 identifies.
- The deliberative accumulation bridge requires voluntary action from legitimated parties at two steps the system does not mandate.
- Art. 927, §4º's application to reclamação denials as such is contested by the adversarial paper's modification/application distinction; the §4º route is supplementary.
- The Level 1 / Level 2 distinction provides a genuine constraint for simple cases involving features expressly within the súmula's established scope. The defense contests the distinction's applicability to novel-feature denial cases, which are the cases that generate the structural feedback loop.

This paper does not address the connection between Paper 1D's Thesis 3 and Paper 1B's Exit 4 systemic justification. The Paper 1B debate has independently addressed Exit 4's systemic value through the ordinary-appellate-proceeding information-richness function, decoupled from the reclamação channel.

## 7. Conditions Under Which This Defense Would Fail

- If Brazilian courts systematically hold that reclamação denials on distinction grounds produce only particularized case-specific holdings rather than general scope interpretations of the súmula — so that STF reclamação jurisprudence does not constitute the "authoritatively determined scope" that art. 927, II binding force runs to — the §4.2 art. 927 / art. 926 binding-force arguments fail.
- If the adversarial paper's Level 1 / Level 2 distinction is extended to cover novel-feature cases — specifically, if Brazilian procedural scholarship or STF practice establishes that coherence assessment even for novel features is bounded to consultation of explicitly established scope holdings, without any determination of scope for unaddressed features — the §4.1 argument fails. Surrender condition: identifying a mode of Level 1 coherence assessment that applies to novel features without performing a scope determination.
- If the compliance/merit distinction is accepted and the inference-error attack succeeds in full: if the STF's reclamação conclusion is correctly characterized as being exclusively about question A (formal adequacy), and art. 489, §1º, IV's engagement obligation is correctly limited to arguments bearing on that conclusion, then the §4.1 argument fails and the art. 927, §4º route loses its foundation.
- If the circularity argument succeeds independently of the Thesis 2 dispute: if it can be shown that the mechanism's designed capacity for dialogue requires a legal requirement that art. 489, §1º, IV does not already provide — so that the design claim requires a new provision rather than interpretation of an existing one — then the circularity objection stands even if the compliance/merit distinction is partly wrong.
- If Art. 103-A, §2º's revision procedure is interpreted as requiring that revision proposals be independent of the reclamação dialogue record — based exclusively on supervening legislative or constitutional developments — the deliberative accumulation bridge (§4.4) does not operate.
