# The Distinction Mechanism and Deliberative Accumulation: A Defense of Paper 1D's Institutional Dialogue Thesis

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "Vinculação Racional e Diálogo Institucional" (`paper1D_vinculacao_racional_dialogo_institucional.md`), defends two connected claims:

**Thesis 2 (symmetry):** The duty of reasoning under art. 489, §1º, CPC is symmetric — it applies both to the inferior court departing from a binding precedent and to the STF judging the reclamação against that departure. The STF cannot respond to a reasoned departure with mere reaffirmation of the súmula's authority; it must engage the reasons exposed.

> "O STF que julga a reclamação e simplesmente reafirma a autoridade da súmula — sem identificar por que as razões expostas pelo órgão inferior não são suficientes para distinção ou superação — está proferindo decisão que não se considera fundamentada nos termos do art. 489, §1º, IV." (§3.2)

**Thesis 3 (structural channel):** The reclamação is not a silencing mechanism but a channel through which arguments that survive confrontation reach the tribunal competent to revise the precedent, producing a structure of rational institutional dialogue.

> "a reclamação não é mecanismo de silenciamento do argumento, mas canal pelo qual argumentos que resistem ao confronto alcançam o tribunal competente para revisar o precedente." (§4.2)

---

## 2. What This Support Adds

**Vector:** Direct defense. This paper responds to `otherwise/paper1d-reclamacao-compliance.md` ("The Reclamação Cannot Carry the Symmetry"), which attacks Theses 2 and 3 through seven sequential vectors:

*Vector one (inference error):* The reclamação's conclusion concerns compliance — did the departure occur and was it formally permissible? (question A) — not the precedent's substantive correctness (question B). An inferior court's departure reasons address question B. Arguments about question B do not bear on the compliance conclusion (question A). Therefore, art. 489, §1º, IV's engagement obligation for arguments "capable of undermining the conclusion" does not extend to departure reasons in reclamação proceedings.

*Vector two (Level 1 / Level 2):* Even accepting that a pure presence test is too weak and coherence assessment requires some examination of the súmula's scope, the compliance proceeding performs only Level 1 assessment (was a coherent distinction-type argument provided?) rather than Level 2 assessment (is the distinction substantively correct?). Level 1 asks whether the argument coherently positions itself relative to the súmula's established scope; Level 2 adjudicates whether an alternative scope claim is correct.

*Vector three (structural mechanism gap):* The reclamação's procedural outputs are grant (cassação) or denial. Neither directly produces revision of a súmula vinculante. Formal revision requires separate procedures under Lei 11.417/2006, art. 2º. The dialogue Paper 1D describes depends on the STF voluntarily initiating revision after the reclamação — an informal channel at the STF's discretion, not a structural mechanism built into the proceeding.

*Vector four (art. 927 enumeration):* Reclamação denials are not listed among art. 927 CPC's mandatory-observance precedent sources. Distinction denials produce res judicata for the parties but not the mandatory observance art. 927 requires. The feedback loop produces informal persuasive precedent, not binding precedent.

*Vector five (circularity):* For distinction denials to generate scope-defining content — even informally — they must articulate which features distinguish the case and why. This requires the STF to engage departure reasoning substantively. But Thesis 2 asserts the STF currently fails to do this. The structural feedback loop is conditional on the normative prescription being adopted, not on the current system's structure.

*Vector six (ordinary-appellate-route, PR #84):* The denial-case structural argument misidentifies where the Level 2 proceeding is located. The Level 2 proceeding — substantive review of whether the inferior court's scope claim was correct — is ordinary appellate review of the inferior court's underlying decision, not a proceeding within the reclamação framework. The distinction denial communicates Level 1 output — what argument-types are formally adequate — not Level 2 scope information — what facts fall substantively outside the súmula's coverage.

*Vector seven (participation-inference critique, PR #84):* The art. 927, II / art. 926 binding-force argument relies on an inference — that every STF holding about a listed source inherits the source's mandatory-observance status — that would nullify art. 927's enumeration's limiting function. Art. 926 generates consistency obligations (quality standard) without elevating persuasive authority to mandatory-observance status.

This paper accepts that formal revision of a súmula vinculante requires separate procedures under Lei 11.417/2006; that the reclamação's constitutional purpose under art. 102, I, "l", CF is authority preservation; that current STF practice does not consistently engage departure reasoning in reclamação judgments; that the structural feedback loop is not currently fully operational; and that the Level 1/Level 2 distinction provides genuine constraint for cases where the súmula's established scope expressly addresses the offered features. It contests: (a) the Level 1/Level 2 distinction for denial cases involving novel features, and the ordinary-appellate-route response for the pure-state-law case class; (b) the participation-inference critique as directed at the defense's actual binding-force argument; and (c) the characterization of the reclamação as exclusively a compliance-verification mechanism.

## 3. Reconstructing the Attack

### 3.1 The Compliance-Proceeding Function (Adversarial §3.1)

The reclamação's constitutional purpose is authority preservation (art. 102, I, "l", CF). The proceeding determines whether the lower court's decision is compatible with an existing binding norm. The STF is not exercising substantive jurisdiction on a contested legal question; it is verifying compliance with a norm already established. The reclamação's conclusion concerns question A (did the departure violate the binding norm?), not question B (is the precedent substantively correct?). Art. 489, §1º, IV requires engagement with arguments bearing on the reclamação's conclusion; departure reasons bear on question B, not question A; therefore the engagement obligation does not extend to departure reasons within the reclamação proceeding.

### 3.2 The Level 1 / Level 2 Refinement and Ordinary-Appellate-Route Response (Adversarial §§3.2, 4.3)

The adversarial paper accepts that a pure presence test (was any departure argument provided?) is too weak — coherence assessment is required. Its refinement: the compliance proceeding performs Level 1 assessment (was a coherent distinction-type argument provided, as opposed to no argument or a structurally incoherent attempt?) not Level 2 assessment (is the distinction substantively correct?). Level 1 asks whether the argument coherently positions itself relative to the súmula's established scope. This examination is bounded: the compliance proceeding consults the established scope without adjudicating whether an alternative scope claim is correct. Departure reasons are question-B arguments (what should the scope be?), not question-A arguments (was the departure formally adequate?).

In PR #84, the adversarial paper extends this analysis to denial cases: the denial-case structural argument misidentifies where the Level 2 proceeding is located. The Level 2 proceeding for the scope question — whether the inferior court's substantive scope claim was correct — is ordinary appellate review of the inferior court's underlying decision on the case's merits. The compliance denial does not foreclose ordinary appellate routes; the Level 2 question is addressed in those routes. The scoping clarification from a distinction denial is Level 1 output: it communicates what types of argument are formally adequate distinction attempts for this súmula, not whether the inferior court's substantive scope claim was correct. Level 1 scoping (what argument-types are adequate?) and Level 2 scoping (what facts are outside the súmula's coverage?) address different questions.

### 3.3 The Structural Mechanism and Circularity (Adversarial §§3.3–3.5)

The structural feedback loop argument builds from the adversarial paper's concession that the STF can deny a reclamação on genuine-distinction grounds and that this produces a scoping clarification. The adversarial paper argues, first, that this clarification is not binding precedent under art. 927 because reclamação denials are not listed in art. 927's enumeration; second, that even as informal persuasive precedent, the loop requires the STF to articulate which features distinguish the case and why — which requires the substantive engagement that Thesis 2 says the STF currently fails to provide. The structural channel claim is conditional on Thesis 2's normative prescription already being observed.

### 3.4 The Participation-Inference Critique (Adversarial §§3.4, 4.4)

The adversarial PR #84 extension argues that the art. 927, II / art. 926 binding-force argument relies on an inference — that every STF holding about a listed source inherits the source's mandatory-observance status — that would nullify the enumeration's limiting function. If the inference held, any STF determination about any listed source would automatically carry that source's binding status, making the enumeration's limiting role ineffective. Separately: art. 926 generates a consistency obligation (quality standard) that inferior courts satisfy by following or by providing adequate reasons for departing; it does not elevate persuasive authority to mandatory-observance status.

## 4. The Defense

### 4.1 Level 1 Assessment for Denial Cases Requires Scope Determination

The Level 1 / Level 2 distinction is available where the súmula's established scope already speaks to the features offered. When the STF has previously addressed whether features of the offered type are distinguishing, a Level 1 coherence assessment can consult that prior determination without generating a new scope judgment. But the cases that generate genuine reclamação disputes — contested reclamações that reach briefing and judgment — are precisely those where the STF has not previously addressed whether the offered features fall within or outside the súmula's scope. For novel features, the Level 1 assessment cannot consult an "established scope" that is silent on those features. It must determine where the established scope ends, which is itself a scope determination.

The adversarial paper concedes in its anticipated-reply section that "coherence assessment in hard cases involves some examination of the súmula's ratio." This concession identifies the class where Level 1 and Level 2 are not distinct: whenever examining the ratio is necessary to assess coherence, the compliance proceeding is performing the same inquiry that Level 2 assessment requires. The adversarial paper then claims this examination is "bounded differently" — the compliance proceeding asks whether the argument coheres with established scope, the reversal proceeding asks whether the scope claim is correct. But for novel-feature cases, there is no "established scope" to bound the coherence assessment. The hard cases are precisely those where established scope is silent, and in those cases the Level 1 / Level 2 distinction provides no boundary.

There is a further structural argument specific to denial cases. The adversarial paper's Level 1 / Level 2 framework claims that Level 2 questions — substantive correctness of the scope claim — are reserved for the reversal proceeding. In PR #84, the adversarial paper locates this Level 2 proceeding in ordinary appellate review of the inferior court's underlying case. This response has structural limits for an identifiable case class that the adversarial paper's own assessment section acknowledges without resolving.

Súmulas vinculantes under art. 103-A, CF apply to courts deciding constitutional questions. A decision by an inferior court can engage a súmula vinculante — one governing procedural rights with constitutional foundations, for example — while the underlying substantive dispute involves exclusively state law with no independent federal or constitutional question sufficient to ground a recurso extraordinário to the STF or a recurso especial to the STJ. In those cases, no extraordinary appellate route exists for the underlying case. The reclamação denial is the last federal-court proceeding addressing the scope question for those facts.

The ordinary-appellate-route response fails in this case class. No Level 2 proceeding can occur. The adversarial paper's own assessment section acknowledges the class ("pure-state-law cases with no constitutional question for an RE") and maintains that even there, the distinction denial communicates only Level 1 information. But this claim produces consequences the adversarial paper does not address.

If the distinction denial communicates only Level 1 information — argument-type adequacy, not substantive scope coverage — then for the pure-state-law case class, the súmula's coverage of fact-configurations with features F₁, F₂ is permanently indeterminate: deferred to a Level 2 proceeding that cannot occur. Future inferior courts applying the same súmula to cases with features F₁, F₂ have no guidance on whether those features constitute a genuine distinction — only that someone's argument that they do was formally adequate. Argument-type adequacy is a property of the argument's form; it does not establish whether the feature combination falls inside or outside the súmula's scope. Repeated Level 1-only denials can accumulate without producing a single substantive scope determination for the pure-state-law case class.

This consequence is incompatible with art. 103-A, CF's purpose. Súmulas vinculantes are constitutionally designed to produce uniform application of constitutional norms across all courts. A súmula whose coverage is indefinitely indeterminate for a category of cases — with no proceeding available to resolve the substantive question — does not achieve uniform application. The adversarial paper's Level 1 characterization of distinction denials, taken to its structural conclusion for the pure-state-law case class, makes the súmula's coverage depend on the availability of extraordinary appellate routes in each case, producing variable rather than uniform coverage across the case-type distribution.

This argument is narrower than claiming all distinction denials must produce Level 2 output. It holds that for cases where no extraordinary appellate route exists — where the reclamação denial is the last proceeding on the scope question — the Level 1/Level 2 deferral mechanism cannot operate, and either the denial produces a scope determination or the súmula's coverage is permanently indeterminate for that case class. The adversarial paper has not provided a mechanism that avoids this consequence while maintaining the Level 1 characterization for the pure-state-law case class.

### 4.2 The Distinction Mechanism Generates Near-Binding Scope Determinations

The adversarial paper correctly identifies that reclamação denials are not independently listed in art. 927's enumeration of mandatory-observance sources. The argument conflates the binding source with the authoritative interpretation of that source's scope.

Art. 927, II makes "súmulas do plenário do Supremo Tribunal Federal" mandatory-observance sources. When the STF in a reclamação denial holds that the súmula does not apply to cases with features F₁, F₂, that holding defines the súmula's scope. Following the súmula as art. 927, II requires means following it as authoritatively determined. An inferior court applying that súmula to a case with features F₁, F₂ after the STF has held those features are distinguishing is not failing to follow the reclamação denial — it is misapplying the súmula itself. The súmula, as determined to cover cases without features F₁, F₂, does not cover this case. This is non-compliance with art. 927, II regardless of whether the reclamação denial is independently listed.

Art. 926 CPC independently establishes a consistency obligation: courts must maintain jurisprudência estável, íntegra e coerente. An inferior court applying the same súmula to a case with features F₁, F₂ after the STF has held those features fall outside the súmula's scope produces inconsistent jurisprudência in violation of art. 926. The obligation to maintain coherent jurisprudência applies to how lower courts apply binding norms, including the scope determinations the superior court has made in applying those norms. Art. 926's coherence obligation operates independently of art. 927's enumeration.

**The art. 103-A, §3º, CF textual anchor.** The defense has previously grounded the binding-force argument in art. 927, II and art. 926. Art. 103-A, §3º, CF provides independent constitutional support that the debate has not yet engaged. The provision authorizes the STF in reclamação proceedings to "determinará que outra seja proferida com ou sem a aplicação da súmula, conforme o caso." Two features bear directly on the scope-determination question.

First, "conforme o caso" — according to the case — specifies that the STF's determination tracks the specific case's features, not merely the form of the departure argument. This is a case-specific factual determination about whether the súmula's coverage extends to the presented facts. A purely formal compliance proceeding that asks only "was the departure formally adequate?" does not require a case-specific inquiry; it asks whether the argument's structure satisfies a form requirement. "Conforme o caso" indicates the proceeding includes a determination that varies with the case's features — which is a scope determination, not a form check.

Second, the alternative "com ou sem a aplicação da súmula" specifies that the proceeding's output resolves whether the súmula applies to this case. A formal-compliance proceeding produces a binary: compliant (denial) or non-compliant (grant + cassação). Art. 103-A, §3º produces a ternary for granted reclamações: apply the súmula, do not apply the súmula, or determine what another decision requires — all tracked "conforme o caso." The converse determination in denials — that the súmula does not need to be applied because the departure was legitimately distinguishing — is the scope determination the Level 1 characterization denies.

This constitutional text establishes that the reclamação is constitutionally designed to include a scope-determination component. The Level 1 characterization — distinction denials produce type-level information about argument adequacy, not substantive scope holdings — is in tension with a constitutional provision that makes the proceeding's output a case-specific applicability determination. Art. 103-A, §3º, CF applies specifically to súmulas vinculantes; the constitutional anchor is strongest for that sub-class within art. 927's enumeration.

**Reformulating the binding-force argument without the participation inference.** The adversarial paper's participation-inference critique targets the formulation: "every STF holding about a listed source inherits the source's mandatory-observance status." This formulation, if correct, would nullify the enumeration — any determination about any listed source would carry its binding status. The defense does not require this formulation.

The defense's argument is a content relationship, not a participation relationship. The súmula is the art. 927, II mandatory-observance source. The súmula has scope — it covers some fact-configurations and not others. The STF's authoritative adjudication of which fact-configurations fall within the súmula's scope is the content of the súmula as a binding norm. When the STF in a reclamação proceeding determines that features F₁, F₂ place a case outside the súmula's coverage, the STF is specifying what the súmula covers — determining the content of the art. 927, II source, not creating a new independent source. No new item is added to art. 927's enumeration. The súmula remains the single mandatory-observance source; the STF's scope adjudications specify that source's operative coverage.

An inferior court that subsequently applies the súmula to a case with features F₁, F₂ after the STF has determined those features are distinguishing is not failing to observe a new independent source — it is misapplying the súmula itself. The misapplication violates art. 927, II with respect to the súmula's actual content: the súmula, as authoritatively determined to cover cases without features F₁, F₂, does not cover this case.

The adversarial paper's nullification concern does not arise for this formulation. Nullification would occur if every STF case-specific determination were treated as a new independently listed source — adding items to art. 927's enumeration with each reclamação judgment. The content formulation does not add sources; it specifies the content of the existing listed source. The enumeration's limiting function is not affected: art. 927, II lists the súmula; the STF's scope adjudications are specifications of what the listed source covers, not additional list items. An inferior court that violates the STF's scope determination violates art. 927, II with respect to the súmula (misapplying the source), not art. 927 with respect to an unlisted source.

### 4.3 The Circularity Argument Restates the Thesis 2 Dispute

The circularity argument holds that the structural loop is conditional on the STF already engaging departure reasoning substantively — which is what Thesis 2 prescribes and what current practice fails to deliver. The loop is therefore not a description of the existing system's design but of a system that has already adopted Thesis 2's prescription.

This characterization of the relationship between Thesis 2 and Thesis 3 is incorrect. Thesis 2 is a claim about what existing law requires. It argues that art. 489, §1º, IV — on its correct interpretation, applied without exception to the STF — obligates the STF to engage departure reasons when those reasons constitute arguments capable of undermining the reclamação's conclusion. Whether this interpretation is correct is disputed by the adversarial paper's compliance/merit distinction. But the nature of the claim is an interpretation of an existing provision, not a proposal to create a new legal requirement.

Thesis 3 then holds that the reclamação proceeding, operating under the legal requirements Thesis 2 correctly identifies, is designed to produce institutional dialogue through the distinction mechanism. The conditional structure — if the STF satisfies the existing engagement obligation, the mechanism produces structural dialogue — is not circular. It is the standard structure of normative-institutional argument: identifying what a mechanism produces when legal requirements are satisfied, as the basis for holding those requirements should be enforced.

The adversarial paper's §4.5 (lex ferenda reading) anticipates this response and argues that the design-capacity claim collapses into a reform proposal. The distinction holds: lex ferenda argues that the law should require X, where the current law does not. The present argument holds that the current law already requires X (art. 489, §1º, IV applied to the STF's reclamação judgments as Thesis 2 argues), and that the mechanism is designed to produce Y under that existing requirement. This is a claim about the existing system's design under correctly-interpreted requirements, not a reform proposal. If the adversarial paper's compliance/merit distinction is correct, Thesis 2 is wrong and the circularity holds — but that outcome is a consequence of defeating Thesis 2, not an independent argument against Thesis 3.

The defense accepts that the structural loop is not currently fully operational, because the STF currently does not engage departure reasoning as Thesis 2 requires. This is the normative failure Thesis 2 identifies. Thesis 3 describes the mechanism's designed capacity under the requirements that exist; Thesis 2 identifies the failure to satisfy those requirements. The two theses are complements: Thesis 2 explains why the mechanism is under-utilized; Thesis 3 explains what it is designed to produce when utilized.

### 4.4 Deliberative Accumulation Links the Structural Channel to Formal Revision

Paper 1D §5.3 describes how the two mechanisms work together: accumulated reclamação judgments in which the STF distinguishes, qualifies, and clarifies the súmula's scope create a public record of the precedent's contested limits. This record constitutes material for the formal revision process.

The adversarial paper correctly identifies that formal revision under Lei 11.417/2006 requires separate procedures — proposal and two-thirds approval — and that legitimated parties under art. 103-A, §2º act on institutional discretion, not procedural obligation. These are real limitations on the strength of the deliberative accumulation bridge. The bridge is mediated, not direct.

The defense's modest claim is that the connection is not purely informal in the sense of being entirely discretionary. Art. 103-A, §2º's legitimated parties — the Attorney General, the Federal Council of the Bar, the Solicitor General — act within a deliberative context in which the STF's own reclamação jurisprudence on the súmula is part of the public doctrinal record. The STF in formal revision proceedings addresses that record. This is not procedural compulsion; it is structural input. The characterization of the accumulation bridge as "informal throughout" understates the degree to which the reclamação record structures the available inputs for formal revision, even where it does not mandate an outcome.

### 4.5 Art. 927, §4º as Supplementary Textual Support

Art. 927, §4º, CPC requires that modifications to established jurisprudence be "fundamentadas de maneira adequada e específica, considerando os princípios da segurança jurídica, da proteção da confiança e da isonomia." The adversarial paper's §3.6 argues this provision governs modification, not application: a reclamação denial on distinction grounds applies the existing súmula to determine it does not cover a specific case; it does not modify the súmula's text or ratio. The provision's operative term is modificação; application is not modification.

The adversarial paper's §3.6 argument has force on the provision's text. The §4º argument is supplementary: it addresses the STF's reclamação judgments insofar as accumulated distinction denials progressively narrow the súmula's active scope, eventually constituting a functional modification of its operational reach. Whether this triggers §4º or falls under a narrower reading of modificação is a tractable textual question. The primary binding-force argument operates through art. 927, II (súmula-scope interpretation), art. 926 (consistency obligation), and art. 103-A, §3º, CF (constitutional design of the proceeding's output), independent of §4º. The §4º route is an additional argument for those cases where the accumulated distinction record approaches the functional equivalent of scope revision.

### 4.6 The Practice Gap Reflects the Reclamação's Correct Function — but Practice Cannot Establish Constitutional Understanding

The adversarial paper argues that the STF's plenário virtual processing — consistent compliance-only review across different cases, bench compositions, and legal areas over decades — reflects correct institutional understanding of the reclamação's compliance function. The pattern is too systematic to attribute to procedural efficiency alone.

Practice cannot establish correct constitutional understanding in this context for two reasons. First, the plenário virtual's efficiency design explains the brevity; it does not explain what constitutional interpretation the brevity reflects. An institution can systematically practice X while violating a constitutional requirement to do Y, particularly where Y is more burdensome than X and the institution faces no external enforcement pressure. Reading the consistency of the procedure as evidence of correct constitutional interpretation conflates procedural choice with constitutional position.

Second, the scale-of-nullity argument — that if Thesis 2 is correct, virtually every plenário virtual reclamação judgment against a reasoned departure would be null — is an observation about the consequences of Thesis 2 being correct, not an argument against Thesis 2's correctness. Constitutional requirements sometimes have systematic implications for established practices; the scope of the implication is not evidence against the requirement. Paper 1D makes a normative argument against a practice that proceduralist scholars have independently identified as falling short of the fundamentação requirements. The adversarial inference from practice-scale to correct constitutional understanding is exactly the move the paper contests.

## 5. Anticipated Objection

The central remaining challenge is the adversarial paper's ordinary-appellate-route response to the denial-case Level 2 argument. The defense's §4.1 extension identifies a structural limit in the pure-state-law case class. The adversarial paper might respond: "Even in pure-state-law cases, the distinction denial communicates the STF's judgment that the departure argument was formally adequate — this is the only output a compliance proceeding can produce. The coverage-indeterminacy consequence follows from the compliance proceeding's constitutional scope constraint, not from a flaw in the Level 1 characterization. Expanding the reclamação to produce Level 2 scope determinations for this case class would require constitutional or legislative authorization; coverage indeterminacy is a structural consequence that cannot be avoided by interpreting the denial as Level 2 output."

This response is structurally honest. Coverage indeterminacy may be the correct outcome under a strict compliance-proceeding characterization. The defense's art. 103-A, §3º, CF argument (§4.2) provides the constitutional-design counter: "conforme o caso" indicates the proceeding already includes a case-specific applicability determination as part of its constitutional structure. Whether "conforme o caso" governs the case-specific applicability determination or governs only the remedy's form (the new decision is issued with or without the súmula, depending on whether the reclamação was for contrariar or indevida aplicação) is the tractable textual question that resolves whether the constitutional anchor has force for the Level 2 output claim.

The adversarial paper's most available next move on the textual anchor is precisely this interpretation of "conforme o caso": arguing that the provision specifies the remedy's form rather than establishing a feature-level applicability determination function. Under that reading, "com ou sem a aplicação da súmula, conforme o caso" means the remedy issued depends on which type of violation was found, not that the STF performs a case-specific determination of whether the súmula applies to the presented features. If this reading is correct, the constitutional anchor's force for the Level 2 output claim is weakened, and the coverage-indeterminacy argument must rest on the structural incompatibility with art. 103-A's vinculante purpose rather than on the provision's textual design.

## 6. Scope of This Defense

This paper defends:

- **Thesis 2 (symmetry)** as applied to reclamações against adequately reasoned departures: adequacy assessment of reasoned departures requires substantive engagement because determining whether a distinction is genuine is inseparable from the precedent's substantive content.
- **Thesis 3 (structural channel)** through the distinction mechanism: reclamação denial on genuine distinction grounds generates a scope determination that participates in the súmula's own binding force under art. 927, II, and that inferior courts are obligated to follow under art. 926's consistency requirement. For súmulas vinculantes, art. 103-A, §3º, CF's "conforme o caso" language provides constitutional support for the scope-determination function.
- **The pure-state-law case gap:** For cases with no extraordinary appellate route, the adversarial paper's ordinary-appellate-route response produces permanently indeterminate súmula coverage — a consequence incompatible with art. 103-A's vinculante purpose.
- **The content-vs-participation reframing:** The binding-force argument does not rely on a participation inference that would nullify the enumeration; it relies on the content relationship between the súmula as a binding source and the STF's authoritative specifications of what that source covers.
- **The deliberative accumulation mechanism:** accumulated reclamação distinctions create a jurisprudential record that feeds the formal revision process as structural input, even if not as procedural compulsion.

This paper accepts:

- Formal revision of a súmula vinculante requires separate procedures under Lei 11.417/2006.
- The reclamação's constitutional purpose under art. 102, I, "l", CF is authority preservation.
- Current STF practice does not consistently engage departure reasoning in reclamação judgments.
- The structural feedback loop is not currently fully operational. The mechanism's designed capacity and its current operation are distinct: the former is the structural claim Thesis 3 makes; the latter is the normative failure Thesis 2 identifies.
- The deliberative accumulation bridge requires voluntary action from legitimated parties at two steps the system does not mandate.
- Art. 927, §4º's application to reclamação denials as such is contested by the adversarial paper's modification/application distinction; the §4º route is supplementary.
- The Level 1/Level 2 distinction provides genuine constraint for simple cases involving features expressly within the súmula's established scope.

This paper does not address the connection between Paper 1D's Thesis 3 and Paper 1B's Exit 4 systemic justification. The Paper 1B debate has independently addressed Exit 4's systemic value through the ordinary-appellate-proceeding information-richness function, decoupled from the reclamação channel.

## 7. Conditions Under Which This Defense Would Fail

- If Brazilian courts systematically hold that reclamação denials on distinction grounds produce only particularized case-specific holdings rather than general scope interpretations of the súmula — so that STF reclamação jurisprudence does not constitute the "authoritatively determined scope" that art. 927, II binding force runs to — the §4.2 art. 927, II binding-force argument fails.
- If art. 103-A, §3º, CF's "conforme o caso" language is interpreted as governing the remedy's form — the new decision is issued with or without the súmula depending on whether the violation was contrariar or indevida aplicação — rather than establishing a case-specific applicability determination function, the constitutional anchor's force for the Level 2 output claim is limited. The §4.1 pure-state-law case argument would then need to rest on the structural incompatibility with art. 103-A's vinculante purpose rather than on the constitutional provision's textual design.
- If Brazilian procedural scholarship or STF practice establishes that the pure-state-law case class is properly handled through accumulated Level 1-only reclamação jurisprudence — that coverage indeterminacy for cases without extraordinary appellate routes is a tolerated design feature rather than a systemic flaw — the §4.1 structural incompatibility argument loses force.
- If the adversarial paper's Level 1 / Level 2 distinction is extended to cover novel-feature cases — specifically, if Brazilian procedural scholarship or STF practice establishes that coherence assessment even for novel features is bounded to consultation of explicitly established scope holdings — the §4.1 novel-feature argument fails.
- If the compliance/merit distinction is accepted in full: if the STF's reclamação conclusion is correctly characterized as being exclusively about question A (formal adequacy), and art. 489, §1º, IV's engagement obligation is correctly limited to arguments bearing on that conclusion, then the §4.1 argument fails and the art. 103-A, §3º anchor is insufficient to rescue the Thesis 2 claim.
- If the circularity argument succeeds independently of the Thesis 2 dispute: if it can be shown that the mechanism's designed capacity for dialogue requires a legal requirement that art. 489, §1º, IV does not already provide — so that the design claim requires a new provision rather than interpretation of an existing one — then the circularity objection stands even if the compliance/merit distinction is partly wrong.
- If Art. 103-A, §2º's revision procedure is interpreted as requiring that revision proposals be independent of the reclamação dialogue record — based exclusively on supervening legislative or constitutional developments — the deliberative accumulation bridge (§4.4) does not operate.
