# The Non-Autonomy of Embargos Cognition: A Scoped Defense of Paper 1A's §5.3

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "Embargos de Declaração no CPC 2015: Escopo Amplo dos Vícios e Infringência como Consequência" (`paper1A_embargos_declaracao.md`), defends two related theses about embargos de declaração under the CPC 2015.

**Thesis 1 (scope):** The mode of defect — obscurity, contradiction, omission — determines what is curable by embargos, not the subject matter on which the defect falls. A merit-based omission under art. 489, §1º, IV (failure to address an argument capable of undermining the conclusion) is a curable defect under art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II.

**Thesis 2 (infringement effects):** Modification of the embargoed decision is not an autonomous claim requiring a subsidiary request. When the court's integration of the omission implies modification, modification follows as a contingent procedural consequence of the integrative activity — not a separate petitum.

Both theses rest on a structural claim in §5.3: when embargos address an art. 489, §1º, IV violation, "não há nova cognição autônoma" — the court completes its reasoning path rather than generating a new independent judgment.

This defense responds in §4.9: the register distinction is a formally valid logical observation but fails as a structural resolution because the adversarial paper's mechanism is the only bridge between cognitive description and constitutional concern — a formal distinction between registers requires independent constitutional content on the Type A side, which the paper acknowledges does not exist; condition (iii) is resolved as meaning uniquely determined, making direction-without-uniqueness cases Type B under both readings and reducing the vocabulary dispute to cases where the readings already converge; and the bilateral doctrinal gap is accepted honestly — the structural arguments support the jurisdictional-allocation reading's conclusion, but primary sources establishing the constitutional characterization are absent on both sides.

## 3. Reconstructing the Attack

### 3.1 The Cognition Constraint

The adversarial paper distinguishes two cognitive modes. *Recall cognition:* the court makes explicit what its decision already implicitly decided — no new substantive judgment is formed, only existing judgment is surfaced. *Generative cognition:* the court reaches a substantive judgment not previously reached. For genuine §489, §1º, IV omissions — arguments past the implicit rejection filter — three steps establish generative cognition: (1) determining whether the argument is capable of undermining the conclusion — a substantive legal evaluation; (2) engaging the argument on its merits — deliberation on a question not previously addressed; (3) determining whether this engagement changes the result — a merit judgment not logically entailed by the original decision.

### 3.2 The Constraints-Determination Gap

The adversarial paper argues that the constraints on embargos deliberation (fixed precedents, fixed factual findings, closed record, no new theories, no scope expansion) narrow but do not determine the outcome within the constrained space. For §5.3's "autônoma" to have substantive content, the constraints must determine the outcome, not merely bound it. The statute's definitional requirements compound the problem: art. 489, §1º, IV selects for arguments "capable of undermining the conclusion in principle" — arguments not obviously determined by the existing framework, otherwise they would have been addressed or implicitly rejected.

### 3.3 The Three-Prong Counter-Reply to the Approach-Freedom Operationalization

In response to the supportive claim that "autônoma" means approach-free rather than outcome-pre-determined, the adversarial paper advanced three objections:

**Objection 1 — functional doctrinal purpose:** §5.3 exists to answer the "terceira instância" objection. The operative test in Brazilian civil procedure for "terceira instância" is functional — does the court produce a new first-time substantive determination on a previously undecided merit question? This test does not track approach freedom. The limited-remand comparison: a court instructed to determine damages within an established liability framework lacks approach freedom by any measure, yet produces unambiguously new merit cognition. Approach freedom is therefore the wrong criterion for the "terceira instância" concern.

**Objection 2 — outcome-indeterminacy concession:** The approach-freedom defense accepted that "constraints narrow without determining outcomes" and that the embargos court may affirm or reverse depending on how it engages the omitted argument. This concedes that the court is forming new outcome-determining merit judgments not entailed by the original decision. §5.3's claim is about the *product* of embargos activity (no new substantive determinations), not merely the *process* (approach-constrained deliberation). Once outcome indeterminacy is conceded, the product-level claim is surrendered.

**Objection 3 — no doctrinal grounding:** No Brazilian procedural statute, doctrine, or jurisprudence operationalizes "cognição autônoma" as approach freedom. The operationalization is a philosophical import requiring a textual or doctrinal basis in Brazilian procedural law that has not been supplied.

### 3.4 The Three Counter-Arguments to the Jurisdictional-Allocation Reading

The adversarial paper's §3.7 challenges the jurisdictional-allocation reading (§4.6) with three arguments.

**The competence/exercise equivocation.** The reading holds that the proceeding "assumed jurisdiction" over D when D was raised and the art. 489, §1º, IV obligation was triggered. The adversarial counter argues this conflates jurisdictional *competence* (the authority and duty to decide D) with jurisdictional *exercise* (the formation and expression of a judgment on D). A §489, §1º, IV violation is definitionally the failure to exercise — competence and obligation were present, exercise was absent. If triggering the obligation constitutes "assumed jurisdiction," every decisive argument raised in any proceeding has been "previously decided" in the jurisdictional sense — embargos could never constitute "terceira instância" for any raised argument. That conclusion is what the adversarial attack contests; the reading presupposes it rather than establishing it.

**The infringement-effect problem.** For modification-implying embargos, the original proceeding reached dispositif R; the embargos reach R' ≠ R. A "completion" that changes the outcome is not a completion in the relevant sense. The proceeding has changed its result by deliberating on an argument the original proceeding omitted — which is the functional structure of appellate review, regardless of the "internal remedy" label or the prior obligation to address D.

**Form designation cannot settle the functional test.** The functional test exists because the prohibition would be circumvented by legislative labeling if form designation were sufficient. Art. 1.022's "internal remedy" characterization is a legislative form decision that the functional test is designed to look past. If the line cannot be drawn by cognitive act occurrence and cannot be drawn by form designation, its content must be identified in the activity itself: first exercise of decisional authority on a question not previously decided in the exercise sense.

## 4. The Defense

### 4.1 Accepting and Limiting Objection 1: The Limited-Remand Comparison

The limited-remand comparison is accepted as successfully attacking the general approach-freedom operationalization of §5.3's "autônoma." The objection is correct: both limited-remand courts and embargos courts lack approach freedom; only one is standardly regarded as producing new merit cognition; approach freedom cannot draw the line §5.3 requires.

The question is whether the limited-remand comparison also succeeds against §5.3's defense for Type A violations specifically. It does not, because the relevant distinction is not approach-constrained versus approach-free but **first-time determination of a newly assigned question** versus **remedial deployment of prior commitments to an already-obligated question**.

A court executing a limited remand for damages:
- Was assigned a question it had not previously addressed in the proceeding — damages were set aside for later determination
- Has no prior obligation in the same proceeding to determine the specific damages amount
- Is making first-time substantive determinations on a question that the prior decision did not commit it to in any specific way — the liability finding establishes that some payment is owed, not what the payment is

A court addressing a Type A embargos violation — binding-precedent, statutory-provision, or internal-consistency omission at the determinate end:
- Failed to discharge an obligation already triggered by art. 489, §1º, IV: the decisive argument was before the court; the court was required to address it; the omission was a failure to complete an already-obligated task
- Had prior commitments in the same decision that determine the answer to the omitted argument: the binding precedent it accepted, the statutory provision it endorsed, the factual findings it made
- Is deploying those prior commitments to a question its own reasoning structure was already committed to answering

The "terceira instância" functional test asks whether the court is producing a new first-time substantive determination on a *previously undecided* merit question. For the limited-remand damages question, this condition is satisfied: the question was genuinely not decided by the prior proceeding. For Type A embargos, the condition is not satisfied in the operative sense: the court's prior commitments determine the answer to the omitted argument; what is new is the deployment, not the determination. The court is not deciding what its framework says for the first time — its framework already says it — the court is completing a step it was already required to complete and that its own reasoning structure had the resources to answer.

This distinction preserves the Type A case from the limited-remand comparison without relying on approach freedom. The relevant axis for the "terceira instância" concern is prior commitment versus fresh judgment, not approach-constrained versus approach-free.

### 4.2 Limiting Objection 2: Outcome Indeterminacy for Type A Violations

The approach-freedom defense accepted outcome indeterminacy within the constrained space in general terms. This concession applies to Type B violations, not to Type A.

For Type B violations — where the court's existing framework genuinely leaves the omitted argument's resolution open — the embargos court does form new outcome-determining judgments, and the adversarial paper's attack holds for this category. §5.3 overclaims if read as a universal claim covering all §489, §1º, IV violations.

For Type A violations, the constrained space is not genuinely open in the relevant sense. The court's prior commitments determine the answer: applying Súmula X to argument D is determined by the court's acceptance of Súmula X and its application to parallel arguments A, B, and C in the same decision; applying the statutory provision to the decisive factual configuration is determined by the court's acceptance of the provision as governing; resolving the internal tension in the court's findings is determined by the comparative weight the court's own stated reasoning assigns to the conflicting elements. The court may err in deployment — it may fail to see what its framework commits it to — but error is not indeterminacy. The determinate structure of the prior commitments is what makes the omission an omission rather than a gap.

The adversary's product-level argument — that once outcome indeterminacy is accepted, the no-new-determinations claim is surrendered — depends on outcome indeterminacy being present. For Type A violations, it is not present in the sense that matters: the court's framework commits it to one answer on the argument, and the embargos court's role is to state that commitment, not to choose among legitimate alternatives. What is contingent is whether the court gets the deployment right; what is not contingent is what the framework determines the deployment should produce.

### 4.3 Addressing Objection 3: Doctrinal Grounding for the Scoped Defense

The doctrinal-grounding objection targeted the approach-freedom operationalization specifically. The scoped defense does not primarily rest on approach freedom. For Type A violations, the non-autonomy claim rests on the structure of the violation itself, grounded in the statutory text.

Art. 489, §1º, IV defines the relevant omissions as arguments "capable of undermining the conclusion in principle" (*em tese*). The em tese qualifier establishes that the argument's relevance is assessed within the court's adopted analytical framework: would this argument, engaged within the framework the court has committed to, be capable of changing the result? For Type A violations, the answer is yes precisely because the court's framework commits it to an answer on the argument that differs from the result it reached — the omission occurred within a framework structure that had the resources to address the argument. The violation is the court's failure to deploy a framework already adopted against an argument already before it.

Art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II classifies this failure as omissão for embargos purposes. The remedy is integrative: the court completes the decision by deploying the framework to the argument it omitted. This integrative characterization supports the non-autonomy claim without requiring approach freedom as the operative concept: the court is completing a deployment its existing commitments required, not forming a position where its framework was silent.

The grounding is the conjunction of art. 489, §1º, IV's em tese requirement (the framework must be capable of engaging the argument) and the implicit rejection doctrine's structural-deployment condition (the framework must have actually been deployed to the argument's structure for implicit rejection to apply). Together they define the Type A residual: cases where the framework would engage the argument, the framework is capable of determining the answer, but the structural reasoning stopped before deployment. The embargos remedy for this residual is framework completion, and completion is non-autonomous in the relevant sense — not because the court is approach-constrained, but because it is not forming a new position on the argument's resolution.

### 4.4 The Implicit Rejection Doctrine: Structural Deployment Distinction

The adversarial paper argues the implicit rejection doctrine belongs on its side: it removes from the §489, §1º, IV category the cases where §5.3 holds (recall cognition, where the court's reasoning structure already disposed of the argument), leaving the genuinely omitted arguments disproportionately Type B under the selection effect.

This reversal depends on implicit rejection applying to all Type A cases. It does not, because implicit rejection requires structural deployment, not merely framework availability.

Implicit rejection (*rejeição implícita*) applies when the court's structural reasoning logically disposes of the argument — when the logic of the court's stated analysis extends to cover the argument without explicit mention. A court that accepts and applies Súmula X to arguments A, B, and C in the same decision, while failing to address argument D (which falls under the same ratio), has not implicitly rejected D: the structural reasoning applied the súmula to A, B, C; it did not extend to D. The court has the framework (committed to Súmula X) and the framework determines the answer, but the structural deployment stopped before D. This is a genuine Type A omission: framework-determined, not implicitly rejected, surviving past the filter.

The adversarial reversal establishes that the implicit rejection doctrine removes Type A cases where structural reasoning reached the argument; it does not remove Type A cases where reasoning possessed the framework but failed to deploy it. The residual category of genuine §489, §1º, IV violations contains Type A violations (structural-deployment gap, determinate framework answer) alongside Type B violations (framework-open questions). The selection effect arguments are structural predictions without established empirical basis; neither side has data on the actual distribution of violation types in Brazilian appellate practice.

### 4.5 Thesis 2: Contingent Procedural Consequence

The adversary correctly identifies that "caso o acolhimento implique modificação" in art. 1.024, §3º is conditional grammar. Modification depends on what integration produces; the "automatic consequence" characterization overstated the theoretical claim. The revised account: modification is a *contingent procedural consequence* — contingent on what the court's engagement with the omitted argument produces; procedural in that, once the court determines integration implies modification, no separate petition from the embargante is needed.

This revision does not affect the practical recommendation. The adversary acknowledges that the practical proposal — substituting the traditional subsidiary formula — survives regardless of the logical characterization. The embargante petitions for sanação do vício; if integration implies modification, modification follows from the court's integrative activity without a separate request. The traditional formula ("subsidiariamente, requer-se a atribuição de efeitos infringentes") incorrectly positions modification as a separate petitum rather than a contingent procedural consequence of integration.

### 4.6 The Jurisdictional-Allocation Reading Answers the Three-Vector Attack

The adversarial counter-reply to the Type A scoped defense adds three mechanism-level vectors, all targeting the distinction between *remedial deployment of prior commitments* and *first-time determination of a previously undecided question*. The three vectors share a structural premise: the "terceira instância" functional test tracks *cognitive formation* — whether a court has previously formed and expressed a judgment on the specific argument. If the test tracks formation, then (1) framework-determinacy does not satisfy "previously decided" because the court formed no judgment; (2) even easy application to D constitutes a first formation; and (3) specificity of prior commitments does not prevent that formation from being new. All three vectors depend on the shared premise.

The jurisdictional-allocation reading contests the shared premise. The "terceira instância" prohibition targets the structural duplication of *proceedings* — the assignment of the same merits question to an additional jurisdictional forum. It asks whether a new proceeding is assuming fresh jurisdiction over a question that a prior proceeding was already competent and obligated to decide. It does not ask whether the court has previously formed and expressed a judgment at the judgment-formation level.

Two structural features of the "terceira instância" concern support the jurisdictional-allocation reading over the cognitive-formation reading. First, every proceeding in which a question of law is applied to a case involves cognitive steps performed "for the first time" at that tier. A second-degree appellate court performs analysis it has not previously performed; it does not thereby become a "terceira instância." The relevant structural question is whether the proceeding is a *new tier* in the adjudicatory hierarchy, not whether it generates new cognitive outputs within an already-assumed tier. Second, art. 1.022 CPC positions embargos de declaração as an internal remedy within the original proceeding, addressed at the same court and directed at the same decision. Embargos do not establish new appellate jurisdiction; they are a mechanism within the original proceeding for completing its already-obligated judicial task.

**Vector 1 — framework-determinacy vs. prior determination.** The adversarial counter argues that "previously undecided" tracks whether the court formed a prior judgment on D; framework-determinacy does not satisfy this condition because the court's expression of the judgment, not the framework's logical implications, is the exercise of judicial authority.

Under the jurisdictional-allocation reading, "previously decided" refers to whether the proceeding has *assumed jurisdiction* over D, not whether the court has *explicitly formed and expressed* a judgment on D. The proceeding assumed jurisdiction over D when D was raised and fell within the art. 489, §1º, IV obligation. That obligation to engage D was triggered when D was raised as a decisive argument; the court's failure to discharge the obligation was the violation, not the mere absence of a prior explicit judgment. Embargos completing that discharge operate within the original proceeding's already-assumed jurisdiction over D — they do not constitute a new proceeding's first exercise of jurisdiction. The formation and expression of the judgment is the completion of an already-existing jurisdictional obligation, not the initiation of a new one.

The adversarial claim that "the framework's logical implications do not constitute the exercise of judicial authority" is correct if the question is whether a judgment has been expressed. It is not the operative question if the prohibition tracks jurisdictional assumption, which precedes and is independent of the court's act of expression.

**Vector 2 — easy application is still first application.** The adversarial counter argues that even in determinate cases, the embargos court performs cognitive steps not previously performed for D: identifying D's configuration within the framework, checking for qualifications, forming the judgment that the implications apply. The structural-deployment condition confirms this: running the framework to D is the new cognitive act the prior reasoning failed to perform.

This argument is correct at the cognitive level and irrelevant at the jurisdictional level. The question is not whether cognitive steps are being performed for the first time; it is whether the proceeding is asserting a new jurisdictional claim. Every proceeding performs new cognitive acts in applying its framework to the case before it; the "terceira instância" prohibition does not turn on the occurrence of such acts but on whether the proceeding is a new forum acquiring new jurisdiction. The structural-deployment condition identifies cases where the court failed to deploy its framework to D; embargos cure that failure within the same proceeding's already-assumed jurisdiction. Running the framework to D for the first time within an already-assumed scope is not the same as a new proceeding first exercising jurisdiction over D.

**Vector 3 — the same-proceeding / different-proceeding distinction sustains the argument.** The adversarial counter presses the specified-formula damages remand: type of harm specified, measure formula specified by the prior ruling — yet quantum determination is universally treated as new merit cognition. If specificity were the axis, this case should come out differently.

The response does not rest on specificity alone. It rests on a conjunction the specified-formula case lacks. In that case: (i) the formula was established by a *prior proceeding* that (ii) explicitly deferred the quantum question — it was *not within the prior proceeding's scope*. The remand assigns quantum to a *new proceeding* that is exercising jurisdiction over it for the first time. The universal procedural consensus that this constitutes new merit cognition is explained by the new proceeding's first-time jurisdictional exercise over a question the prior proceeding explicitly excluded from its scope.

For Type A embargos: (i) the prior commitments were established *within the same proceeding* that failed to address D, and (ii) D was within that proceeding's art. 489, §1º, IV scope when raised — no exclusion and no reallocation occurred. The embargos proceeding is the original proceeding completing its already-assumed jurisdiction over D, not a new forum first assuming jurisdiction over a question it did not previously have.

The adversarial counterexample confirms, rather than contests, the jurisdictional-allocation reading: the specified-formula quantum determination is treated as new merit cognition precisely because it is a new proceeding's first exercise of jurisdiction over a question the prior proceeding explicitly excluded. This outcome is consistent with Type A embargos not being "terceira instância" — the original proceeding completing an omission within its already-assumed scope is not a new proceeding exercising new jurisdiction.

### 4.7 Responding to the Three §3.7 Counter-Arguments

The §3.7 counter-arguments target the jurisdictional-allocation reading's operative claim that the "terceira instância" prohibition tracks forum structure rather than cognitive formation. Three structural responses follow.

**On the equivocation argument.** The adversarial argument correctly identifies a distinction: competence and exercise are not the same concept in procedural theory. A §489, §1º, IV violation is defined by the gap between them — the court had competence and obligation but failed to exercise. The question is whether this gap, in the context of same-forum completion of an already-assumed obligation, means that embargos perform the "first exercise" in the sense the prohibition targets.

Two responses taken together.

The reductio — "embargos can never constitute 'terceira instância' for any raised argument" — requires the reading to hold that triggering the obligation alone constitutes jurisdiction assumed. The reading does not make this claim. The relevant conjunction for the "assumed jurisdiction" characterization is: (i) D was raised within the proceeding as a decisive argument; (ii) the art. 489, §1º, IV obligation was triggered; (iii) the proceeding's framework had already committed to an answer on D; and (iv) the embargos remedy operates within the same proceeding. Together, these conditions ground the claim that the proceeding had assumed jurisdiction over D in the operative sense for Type A cases. The reductio removes conditions (iii) and (iv), applying as though (ii) alone sufficed. It does not engage the reading as stated.

The more fundamental point: the competence/exercise distinction was developed against a background in which obligation-triggering and exercise are simultaneous. In ordinary appellate proceedings, a court given a question proceeds to decide it — the triggering of jurisdictional authority and its exercise occur in the same adjudicatory act. The "terceira instância" prohibition operates against this normal background: when it asks whether a court has "previously decided" a question, the implicit assumption is that a court that had the question and had the authority addressed it. A §489, §1º, IV violation is abnormal precisely because it separates obligation from exercise — the court had the question, had the obligation, and failed to discharge it. The question for the prohibition is whether this abnormality (a) creates a jurisdictional gap inviting new-forum treatment, or (b) creates a cognizable defect within existing jurisdiction that the same forum must cure. The jurisdictional-allocation reading argues (b): the failure of exercise is the violation, and embargos cure the violation within the original forum's already-assumed scope.

The statutory design supports reading (b). Art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II classifies §489, §1º, IV failures as *omissão* curable by embargos; art. 1.024, §3º anticipates that cure may imply modification of the embargoed decision. Together, these provisions design a mechanism that presupposes outcome-changing completion of omitted cognition within the original proceeding without creating a "terceira instância" problem. Under reading (a) — the exercise-exclusive reading — this design constitutes a prohibited mechanism: outcome-changing first exercise of authority by a court that had previously omitted it. The statutory scheme would be internally inconsistent with the constitutional prohibition it was designed to accommodate. The jurisdictional-allocation reading renders the scheme coherent: same-forum cure of a §489, §1º, IV defect does not constitute a "terceira instância" exercise because the original proceeding's obligation already covered D.

The adversarial argument is correct that competence differs from exercise. The contested question is whether the "terceira instância" prohibition constitutively tracks exercise in the strict sense, or whether the structural conjunction the jurisdictional-allocation reading proposes — same proceeding, pre-existing obligation, framework commitment — determines the operative jurisdictional characterization. This is a doctrinal question that neither side has resolved with primary sources. The statutory-coherence argument grounds the jurisdictional-allocation reading at the level of structural interpretation.

**On the infringement-effect argument.** The adversarial paper holds that modification-implying embargos — reaching R' ≠ R — are functionally equivalent to appellate review: the proceeding changed its outcome by deliberating on an omitted argument.

This argument is in direct tension with the adversarial paper's own §3.2 concession. The §3.2 concession: "For the clearest cases within the binding-precedent category, the constraints do determine the outcome... The deliberation required is recognition, not formation of a new position. This is recall cognition, and the attack does not reach it." These conceded cases may involve modification — the original decision that failed to apply the controlling súmula to argument D may have produced a dispositif that changes when D is addressed in embargos. The infringement-effect argument, applied generally to all modification-implying embargos, captures even those conceded cases.

The adversarial paper cannot simultaneously maintain that (a) modification-implying embargos are generally functionally equivalent to appellate review, and (b) modification-implying embargos in the clearest Type A cases are recall cognition rather than functionally equivalent to appellate review. Maintaining (b) while allowing for modification requires identifying what distinguishes the conceded cases from the contested ones — a criterion beyond modification itself.

The only criterion the present debate has produced is the one the jurisdictional-allocation reading proposes: the conceded clearest Type A cases involve the same forum completing an obligation its framework predetermined — no new jurisdictional exercise occurs even when modification results. This is the Type A/framework-determination condition. If this criterion explains the adversarial paper's own §3.2 concession, it simultaneously grounds the jurisdictional-allocation reading's distinction for the full range of Type A embargos: modification in Type A cases is same-forum correction of a same-forum defect, not new-forum first jurisdictional exercise. The infringement-effect argument, applied consistently to the adversarial camp's own concession, requires the jurisdictional distinction it was intended to replace.

**On the form-designation argument.** The adversarial paper holds that art. 1.022's "internal remedy" designation is a legislative form label the functional test is designed to look past; if the prohibition cannot rest on form labels, the reading cannot rest on the "internal remedy" characterization.

The jurisdictional-allocation reading does not primarily rest on the label. It rests on structural features that the label reflects: the same court, the same proceeding, an obligation triggered within that proceeding, a framework that committed to the answer within the proceeding, and no new forum assuming new jurisdiction. These structural features would ground the reading even absent art. 1.022's characterization.

The functional test's purpose — to look past labels to structural reality — applies where a label circumvents the prohibition: where a legislature labels a genuinely new appellate mechanism "internal to the prior proceeding" to escape the "terceira instância" concern. A genuinely new mechanism lacks the structural features the label claims: it introduces a new forum, a new jurisdictional claim, a proceeding that did not previously have the question. The functional test correctly identifies the label as misleading when the structural reality contradicts it. For Type A embargos, the structural features are present independently of the label: same court, same proceeding, pre-existing obligation, no new forum. The functional test directed at structural reality — rather than form — reaches the same conclusion the label does, because the structural reality independently supports it.

### 4.8 Responding to the Round 8 Extensions: Asymmetric Use of Framework-Determinacy, the Statutory-Coherence Concession, and Mechanism Specificity

The adversarial round 8 (§3.8 of the adversarial paper) advances three arguments against the round 7 responses. None alters the defense's conclusions.

**On condition (iii) and the adversarial paper's own use of framework-determinacy.**

The §3.8 argument accepts that the four-part conjunction correctly answers the equivocation reductio — triggering the art. 489, §1º, IV obligation alone is insufficient; all four conditions are required. It then identifies that condition (iii) — "the proceeding's framework had already committed to an answer on D" — spans three readings: framework availability (too weak; virtually all §489, §1º, IV violations satisfy it), framework deployment (the implicit rejection level; excludes all genuine omissions from the conjunction's scope), and framework-determinacy (one and only one answer consistent with the framework's adopted positions, even though the court's reasoning stopped before D). Framework-determinacy, the adversarial paper argues, is not a recognized procedural criterion for "assumed jurisdiction." The conjunction "redescribes the Type A case" in jurisdictional vocabulary without procedural grounding.

Two responses.

First, the adversarial paper's own §3.2 employs framework-determinacy as the operative criterion for delimiting the attack's scope. The §3.2 language — "for the clearest cases within the binding-precedent category, the constraints do determine the outcome... This is recall cognition, and the attack does not reach it" — uses outcome-determination-by-constraints as the criterion that puts Type A cases outside the attack. This is precisely reading (c) of condition (iii). A paper that uses framework-determinacy to establish what the cognition-constraint attack does not reach cannot simultaneously characterize that same criterion as procedurally insufficient to ground the jurisdictional-allocation reading. The asymmetry requires justification. The available justification would be: the attack operates at the cognitive level; the prohibition operates at the constitutional level; these require different criteria. But the adversarial paper presents the generative-cognition mechanism as the basis for the "terceira instância" concern — the mechanism bridges the cognitive and constitutional levels. If the mechanism fails to reach Type A because constraints determine the outcome, and if the mechanism is the basis of the constitutional concern, then the constitutional concern similarly fails to reach Type A where constraints determine the outcome. The criterion and its constitutional application are not on separate registers.

Second, the §3.8 argument decomposes condition (iii) in isolation from the four-part conjunction. The jurisdictional-allocation reading does not claim that framework-determinacy by itself constitutes "assumed jurisdiction." The claim is that the conjunction of all four conditions captures the structural configuration that distinguishes same-forum completion of an already-assumed obligation from new-forum first exercise of jurisdiction. When D was raised as a decisive argument (i), the art. 489, §1º, IV obligation was triggered (ii), the framework committed to an answer on D (iii), and embargos operate within the same proceeding (iv) — no new forum has assumed fresh jurisdiction over a previously unassumed question. The procedural grounding the adversarial paper demands from condition (iii) alone is not what the reading requires. Asking whether condition (iii) independently constitutes "assumed jurisdiction" misreads the conjunction's architecture; the jurisdictional force is collective.

**On the statutory-coherence argument's accepted conclusion.**

The §3.8 argument identifies the statutory-coherence argument as "type-insensitive" — it treats the exercise-exclusive reading as prohibiting modification-implying embargos categorically, when that reading is type-sensitive: it reaches Type B (generative cognition, framework-open) and not Type A (recall cognition, framework-determined). Art. 1.024 §3º can be coherently confined to Type A legitimately-granted recall-cognition embargos; the provision's conditional grammar ("caso o acolhimento ... implique modificação") does not itself require covering Type B cases to remain internally consistent.

This response accepts the statutory-coherence argument's constitutional conclusion. The argument was designed to establish that the CPC 2015 design — arts. 1.022 + 1.024 §3º together — presupposes that modification-implying embargos are constitutionally permissible for some class of grants. The adversarial §3.8 concedes this: under the type-sensitive exercise-exclusive reading, Type A modification-implying embargos are legitimately grantable and the provision applies to them without incoherence. Whether the constitutional demarcation is described as "the exercise-exclusive reading is type-sensitive" or "the jurisdictional-allocation reading holds that Type A cases don't constitute 'terceira instância'" differs in vocabulary but not in constitutional content. Both formulations conclude that Type A recall-cognition modification-implying embargos do not raise "terceira instância" concerns under the CPC 2015 design.

The adversarial paper's §3.8 therefore accepts the constitutional demarcation the statutory-coherence argument was designed to establish. The remaining dispute concerns which conceptual vocabulary correctly describes that demarcation — exercise of decisional authority in cognitive type (adversarial) vs. jurisdictional forum allocation (supportive). Neither side has produced primary doctrinal authority resolving this vocabulary dispute; surrender conditions 5-7 remain open for both sides on that question. But the statutory-coherence argument's practical function — demonstrating that the CPC 2015 design coheres with a constitutional permission for Type A modification-implying embargos — is confirmed by the adversarial paper's own domain-restriction acceptance.

**On mechanism specificity and the §3.2 scope statement.**

The §3.8 response to the internal-tension argument correctly identifies that the §3.2 concession is formally a scope statement about the cognition-constraint attack mechanism: "the attack does not reach Type A" means the generative-cognition mechanism has no purchase there, not that Type A cases are constitutionally unproblematic. Type A cases could raise "terceira instância" concerns through a mechanism the adversarial paper has not addressed.

This formal response is available. Its substantive weight after eight rounds of bilateral exchange is limited. The adversarial paper has developed one mechanism across seven rounds as its complete basis for the "terceira instância" concern with respect to §489, §1º, IV violations: the formation of new first-time substantive determinations not entailed by prior decisions. The paper's §5 states its scope as directed at the cognition-constraint mechanism specifically. No alternative mechanism appears in any round.

When a paper has developed a single mechanism as its exclusive basis for a constitutional concern, and its §3.2 concedes that mechanism has no purchase in Type A cases, the claim that unspecified alternative mechanisms might reach Type A is a logical reservation of a position not yet argued. The formal availability of future arguments does not constitute a present argument. Under the bilateral exchange's accumulated record, the burden falls on the adversarial paper to articulate what mechanism makes Type A recall-cognition modification-implying embargos a "terceira instância" problem. Until that mechanism is specified and developed, the §3.2 concession — read in the context of the adversarial paper's complete reliance on the generative-cognition mechanism — implies that the concern the paper articulates does not extend to Type A cases. The §3.8 response holds a place in the argument structure; it does not fill it.

### 4.9 Round 10 Responses: The Register Distinction Has Substantive Limits; Condition (iii) Resolved as Unique Determination; Bilateral Gap Accepted

#### 4.9.1 The register distinction is formally valid but structurally insufficient

The §3.9 asymmetry response distinguishes two logical roles for framework-determinacy: a sufficient condition for the attack mechanism's inapplicability in §3.2 (cognitive description), and a rejected constitutional criterion in §3.8 (jurisdictional inquiry). The formal claim — that using X as a sufficient condition in one inquiry does not commit a paper to treating X as a criterion in a different inquiry — is correct as stated.

The formal correctness does not settle the structural question. The distinction between registers carries the required weight only if the constitutional inquiry at §3.8 has content independent of the cognition-constraint mechanism. The paper's §5 scope statement, and the accumulated record of nine bilateral rounds, establish that the cognition-constraint mechanism is the adversarial paper's sole developed basis for the "terceira instância" concern with respect to §489, §1º, IV violations. §3.9 itself confirms this: "the adversarial paper has not produced a mechanism for Type A." No alternative mechanism appears anywhere in the paper's developed argument.

When the mechanism is the exclusive bridge between the cognitive description and the constitutional concern, distinguishing the register in which framework-determinacy appears does not break the bridge — it only describes where on the bridge the distinction is being drawn. The argument structure is:

1. Framework-determinacy → recall cognition (§3.2, sufficient condition)
2. Recall cognition → attack mechanism inapplicable (§3.2)
3. Attack mechanism is the basis for the constitutional concern (§5 scope, nine rounds)
4. Therefore: Framework-determinacy → constitutional concern does not arise (transitivity through the mechanism bridge)

The §3.9 response correctly notes that step 4 requires treating the sufficient condition in step 1 as a criterion for the independent inquiry in step 3. The response blocks step 4 by asserting that §3.2 and §3.8 operate in different registers. But this block is available only if step 3's "is the basis" is qualified — if some other basis for the constitutional concern exists on the Type A side that operates outside the mechanism. The adversarial paper has no such basis. Without independent constitutional content on the Type A side, the register distinction defers the asymmetry rather than resolving it: the formal distinction is correct, but the substantive gap it identifies (the absence of a Type A mechanism) is precisely what the supportive camp's mechanism-specificity argument targeted. §3.9 acknowledges the gap accurately and labels it a bilateral doctrinal gap — which is accepted, as addressed below — but the acknowledgment confirms rather than resolves the structural situation. The formal distinction between logical roles cannot substitute for developed argument on the constitutional side.

#### 4.9.2 Condition (iii) is operationalized as unique determination; direction-without-uniqueness cases are Type B

The §3.9 vocabulary-frontier argument correctly identifies that condition (iii)'s "committed to an answer" has two candidate readings: uniquely determined (one and only one answer consistent with adopted framework positions) or merely directed (the framework provides direction without uniquely determining the specific answer). The readings diverge for direction-without-uniqueness cases.

The supportive defense resolves the operationalization: "committed to an answer" means uniquely determined. This is the operationalization the Type A scoped defense has required throughout — the defense is that prior commitments *determine* the answer, not merely that they orient the inquiry. A framework that provides direction without unique determination leaves the specific answer open among consistent alternatives; the court choosing among those alternatives exercises genuine deliberative discretion. That exercise is generative cognition. Direction-without-uniqueness cases are Type B under the exercise-exclusive/type-sensitive reading.

Under this resolution, the two readings converge for direction-without-uniqueness frontier cases: both classify them as Type B, and both agree the concern applies. The exercise-exclusive reading classifies them as Type B because the court's framework is genuinely open on the specific answer; the jurisdictional-allocation reading reaches the same classification because condition (iii) is not satisfied — the proceeding's framework did not commit to a unique answer on D, so the conjunction's conditions are not jointly met and the "assumed jurisdiction" characterization does not apply.

The vocabulary dispute after this resolution: both readings classify clear Type A cases (uniquely determined) as permissible; both classify Type B cases (framework-open, including direction-without-uniqueness) as raising the concern. The remaining dispute is descriptive, not classificatory: *why* Type A cases are permissible — whether because the embargos court deploys prior commitments without first-time judgment (exercise-exclusive/type-sensitive) or because the proceeding already assumed jurisdiction over D (jurisdictional-allocation). The two descriptions reach the same outcome for every identifiable case class. The surrender condition added in §6 item 8 — demonstrating that the vocabulary dispute is nominal across all identifiable classes including the frontier — is substantially satisfied by this operationalization. What remains is the bilateral doctrinal question: which vocabulary correctly captures the constitutional test's operative criterion. That question does not determine outcomes across the known case population; it determines the preferred constitutional characterization.

#### 4.9.3 The bilateral doctrinal gap is accurate and accepted

The §3.9 acknowledgment — neither the adversarial nor the supportive camp has produced primary Brazilian procedural sources (statutory text, doctrine, or jurisprudence) establishing whether the "terceira instância" functional test constitutively tracks cognitive formation or jurisdictional allocation — is accurate and accepted.

The structural arguments developed over ten rounds establish the interpretive options clearly. The jurisdictional-allocation reading is structurally coherent, explains the adversarial paper's own §3.2 concession's implications, and is consistent with the statutory design of arts. 1.022 and 1.024 §3º (§4.7, §4.8). The exercise-exclusive/type-sensitive reading is also structurally coherent and correctly identifies the functional character of the embargos court's deliberative activity. Both readings classify the same case population identically after the condition (iii) operationalization above. Neither side has located the primary source that would resolve the constitutional characterization.

The bilateral gap means the debate cannot be resolved by structural argument alone, and this is the honest position at round 10. It does not resolve in the adversarial direction — the structural case for Type A permissibility is intact and acknowledged by the adversarial paper's type-sensitivity acceptance (§4.8). It does not resolve in the supportive direction on the vocabulary question — the doctrinal characterization remains open. The structural arguments available on both sides have been developed; what remains is primary-source research in Brazilian civil procedure scholarship and STF/STJ jurisprudential practice on the "terceira instância" functional test's operative criterion.

## 5. Scope and Concessions

### 5.1 Scope of This Defense

This paper defends §5.3's "não há nova cognição autônoma" for Type A violations: those where the court's prior commitments in the same decision *uniquely determine* the answer to the omitted argument. The paradigm categories are binding-precedent omissions, statutory-provision omissions, and internal-consistency omissions at the determinate end of the spectrum. Direction-without-uniqueness cases — where the framework provides direction but not a unique answer — are Type B and outside this defense's scope. For genuine Type A violations, embargos require remedial deployment of prior commitments to complete an already-obligated reasoning path — not first-time determination of previously undecided questions. This paper defends §5.3 against: the cognition-constraint attack (§§3.1–3.2 of the adversarial paper); the three-prong counter-reply to the approach-freedom operationalization (§3.2, rounds 2–4); the implicit rejection doctrine reversal (§§3.4–3.5); the §489, §1º, IV scope and Thesis 2 challenges (§§3.3, 3.6); the three mechanism-level vectors against the Type A scoped defense (§3.2, round 6); the three §3.7 counter-arguments to the jurisdictional-allocation reading (competence/exercise equivocation, infringement-effect problem, form-designation objection); the round 7 supplements (equivocation reductio refutation, statutory-coherence argument, infringement-effect internal tension from §3.2 concession); the round 8 extensions (§3.8): condition (iii) equivocation relocation via framework-determinacy characterization, statutory-coherence type-sensitivity, and §3.2 concession scope statement; and the round 10 arguments (§3.9): register distinction between §3.2 sufficient condition and §3.8 constitutional criterion, direction-without-uniqueness vocabulary frontier, and bilateral doctrinal gap acknowledgment. All from the current version of `otherwise/ed-cognition-constraint.md`.

§5.3 is not defended as a universal claim. For Type B violations, where the court's framework is genuinely open on the omitted argument, embargos do require first-time cognition that the adversarial attack correctly characterizes as generative. The adversary has established that §5.3 overclaims universally stated.

### 5.2 Concessions

This defense accepts:
- The limited-remand comparison succeeds against approach freedom as a general criterion for "autônoma."
- §5.3 overclaims as a universal claim covering all §489, §1º, IV violations. The adversarial attack is correct for Type B violations.
- Modification is a contingent procedural consequence of integration, not a logical entailment of the prior decision.
- Generative cognition occurs when embargos address genuine §489, §1º, IV omissions where the framework is open. The recall/generative taxonomy is accepted.
- The paper's own illustration — the reenquadramento case — is a Type B case. §5.3 does not hold for that case on any of the defenses offered here.
- Direction-without-uniqueness cases are Type B. Condition (iii)'s "committed to an answer" means uniquely determined; where the framework provides direction without unique determination, the case is framework-open and the adversarial attack applies.
- The competence/exercise distinction is accepted as valid in procedural theory: jurisdictional competence is distinct from jurisdictional exercise. What is contested is whether, in the context of same-forum completion of a pre-existing obligation within the same proceeding, the "terceira instância" prohibition constitutively tracks exercise or tracks the structural conjunction the jurisdictional-allocation reading proposes.
- Neither side has produced primary sources establishing which reading Brazilian procedural doctrine adopts. The statutory-coherence argument (§4.7) is structural, not doctrinal. The bilateral doctrinal gap is the accurate characterization of the state of the record at round 10.
- The adversarial paper's register distinction (§3.9) is formally correct as a logical observation. What it cannot do is supply the independent constitutional content on the Type A side that would be required for the distinction to carry its intended structural weight.

## 6. Anticipated Objection

The revised defense will face the following challenge: the limited-remand comparison applies to Type A embargos as well, including under a specified-formula variant. A damages remand within a specified statutory formula (type of harm designated, measure formula stated in the prior ruling) commits the remand court to a specific type and measure of harm — not merely abstract liability in principle. Under such a formula, the remand court deploys a specific prior commitment to the facts before it. Procedural consensus still treats quantum determination as new merit cognition. If this specific-formula remand is treated as new cognition, specificity of prior commitments cannot be the distinguishing axis.

The defense does not rest on specificity alone. It rests on a conjunction the specified-formula remand lacks. In the specified-formula remand: (i) the formula was established by a *prior proceeding* that (ii) explicitly excluded the quantum question from its scope and assigned it to a new forum. The remand proceeding is assuming jurisdiction over quantum for the first time — a genuinely new jurisdictional exercise. The universal procedural treatment of quantum determination as new merit cognition reflects this first-time jurisdictional exercise, not merely the formula's level of specificity.

For Type A embargos, both conditions are reversed: (i) the prior commitments were established within the *same proceeding* that omitted D, and (ii) D was within that proceeding's art. 489, §1º, IV scope when raised — it was not excluded or deferred. No new jurisdictional exercise occurs. The embargos remedy completes the original proceeding's already-assumed obligation to address D. This jurisdictional difference is what sustains the distinction where specificity alone does not: the same-proceeding completion of an already-assumed obligation is structurally distinct from a new-proceeding's first assumption of jurisdiction over an explicitly deferred question, regardless of how specifically the prior commitment was framed.

## 7. Conditions Under Which This Defense Would Fail

- If Type A violations are rare in Brazilian appellate practice — if empirical data establishes that genuine §489, §1º, IV violations reaching embargos are predominantly Type B — the scoped defense applies to a minority of the remedy's actual domain, weakening §5.3's practical force.
- If the em tese standard in art. 489, §1º, IV is interpreted without reference to the court's specific adopted framework — if capability of undermining the conclusion is assessed abstractly rather than against the court's specific commitments — the Type A/Type B distinction cannot be grounded in the statutory text's structure.
- If Brazilian procedural doctrine or jurisprudence establishes that the "terceira instância" functional test tracks cognitive formation rather than jurisdictional allocation — that is, that the first explicit formation of a judgment on argument D in embargos constitutes a new first-time exercise of judicial authority regardless of whether the proceeding had already assumed jurisdiction over D — the §4.6 response fails and all three adversarial vectors in §3.2 are restored. Under this reading, the same-proceeding/different-proceeding distinction would not carry the legal weight the defense assigns it.
- If Brazilian procedural doctrine or jurisprudence establishes that the "terceira instância" prohibition constitutively tracks exercise of decisional authority in the strict sense — so that the prior triggering of the art. 489, §1º, IV obligation within the proceeding has no bearing on whether embargos constitute a first exercise — the §4.7 equivocation response fails. The statutory-coherence argument would then require reconciliation with that doctrinal authority.
- If the adversarial paper establishes a criterion, independent of the jurisdictional-allocation distinction, that explains why modification-implying embargos in the clearest Type A cases (conceded in §3.2 of the adversarial paper as recall cognition) do not constitute functional equivalence to appellate review, the §4.7 infringement-effect response fails. The response rests on the adversarial paper's §3.2 concession creating an internal tension resolvable only by the jurisdictional distinction; if that tension can be resolved differently, the response loses its footing.
- If the structural-deployment condition in §4.4 is shown to be inapplicable in Brazilian embargos practice — if courts consistently treat framework possession as sufficient for implicit rejection — the residual Type A category would be smaller than assumed.
- If Brazilian procedural doctrine or jurisprudence classifies Type A embargos within the "terceira instância" concern — holding that even same-proceeding completion of an already-assumed obligation constitutes a prohibited third instance — the §4.1, §4.6, and §4.7 arguments are overridden by doctrinal authority regardless of their structural coherence.
- If the adversarial paper articulates a specific mechanism — independent of the generative-cognition mechanism — by which Type A recall-cognition modification-implying embargos raise "terceira instância" concerns, the mechanism-specificity response in §4.8 fails and the §3.2 concession's implied scope would require reassessment.
- If the conjunction's jurisdictional force is shown to depend entirely on condition (iii) independently constituting "assumed jurisdiction" — i.e., if the four conditions are individually necessary but the conjunction's combined structural effect is not greater than the sum of each condition's independent effect — the conjunction-level response in §4.8 fails and the adversarial paper's condition (iii) analysis would require a direct answer.
- If the adversarial paper shows that the exercise-exclusive reading's type-sensitivity (accepting Type A legitimacy) and the jurisdictional-allocation reading are not equivalent in constitutional content — that they reach the same conclusion about Type A permissibility through routes with different legal implications — the statutory-coherence concession analysis in §4.8 would fail to establish shared constitutional ground and the vocabulary dispute would become substantive.
- If an adversarial mechanism for the "terceira instância" concern in Type A cases is articulated and developed — one that does not run through the generative-cognition argument — the register distinction in §3.9 would acquire substantive force, and the §4.9.1 response would need to engage the independent mechanism on its own terms.
- If direction-without-uniqueness cases are shown to constitute the predominant subclass of what this defense classifies as Type A in actual Brazilian appellate practice — i.e., if courts frequently hold precedents that direct but do not uniquely determine the answer to decisive omitted arguments — then the scoped defense would apply to a narrower population than assumed, weakening §5.3's practical significance even for the defended class.
