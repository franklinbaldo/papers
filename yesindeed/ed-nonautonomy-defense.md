# The Non-Autonomy of Embargos Cognition: A Defense of Paper 1A's §5.3 Against the Cognition Constraint Attack

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "Embargos de Declaração no CPC 2015: Escopo Amplo dos Vícios e Infringência como Consequência" (`paper1A_embargos_declaracao.md`), defends two related theses about embargos de declaração under the CPC 2015.

**Thesis 1 (scope):** The mode of defect — obscurity, contradiction, omission — determines what is curable by embargos, not the subject matter on which the defect falls. A merit-based omission under art. 489, §1º, IV (failure to address an argument capable of undermining the conclusion) is a curable defect under art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II.

**Thesis 2 (infringement effects):** Modification of the embargoed decision is not an autonomous claim requiring a subsidiary request. When the court's integration of the omission implies modification, modification follows as a consequence of the integrative activity.

Both theses rest on a structural claim in §5.3: when embargos address an art. 489, §1º, IV violation, "não há nova cognição autônoma" — the court completes its reasoning path, not generates a new independent judgment.

---

## 2. What This Support Adds

**Vector:** Direct defense. This paper responds to `otherwise/ed-cognition-constraint.md`, which argues that §5.3's claim is false for the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case: engaging an omitted decisive argument requires generative cognition — new substantive deliberation on questions not previously addressed — and not merely recall of what was already decided. The defense accepts the adversary's recall/generative taxonomy and accepts that generative cognition occurs in cases where implicit rejection is unavailable. It contests the implication: the §5.3 claim targets *autonomy*, not *generativity*. Embargos cognition is non-autonomous even when generative, because the new deliberation is constrained by the existing decision's legal framework, factual findings, identified precedents, and scope of decided issues. The attack succeeds against a narrower version of §5.3 — "no new cognition whatsoever" — but not against the version the paper actually defends: "no new cognition unconstrained by the existing decision's structure."

---

## 3. Reconstructing the Attack

The adversarial paper (`otherwise/ed-cognition-constraint.md`) identifies a structural claim that both theses share: §5.3's assertion that embargos addressing a §489, §1º, IV omission involve completion of an existing reasoning path rather than autonomous new judgment.

**The recall/generative distinction.** Two cognitive modes: *recall cognition* — the court makes explicit what its decision already implicitly decided, with no new substantive judgment formed — and *generative cognition* — the court reaches a substantive judgment not previously reached. The adversary argues that for the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case, three steps establish generative cognition: (1) determining whether the omitted argument qualifies as decisive — a substantive legal evaluation; (2) engaging the argument on its merits — new deliberation on a question not previously addressed; (3) determining whether this engagement changes the result — a new merit judgment whose outcome is not entailed by the original decision. None of these steps, the adversary argues, is recall.

**The implicit rejection doctrine.** Brazilian courts have developed *rejeição implícita*: a court that reaches a conclusion logically inconsistent with an argument has implicitly engaged and rejected it, even without explicit mention. Under this doctrine, the paper's §5.3 defense holds only for cases where implicit rejection is available — where the court's reasoning already, structurally, disposed of the argument. For genuine omissions where implicit rejection is unavailable, the court must form a new position. The paper does not engage this doctrine, which defines the boundary between recall-cognition and generative-cognition cases.

**The automatic-consequence claim.** Art. 1.024, §3º uses "caso o acolhimento implique modificação" — a conditional, not a statement of sequence. Modification depends on the outcome of the court's integration; it is not logically entailed by the existence of the omission. The paper's §3.4 tacitly confirms this by acknowledging that some embargos are granted without modification.

**The Type A / Type B response.** The adversary anticipates the strongest possible reply: limiting Thesis 1 to omissions where the court's existing framework determines the answer (Type A), conceding the cognition constraint for Type B cases where genuine open questions remain. This reply does not suffice because: (a) the paper does not make this limitation, presenting its illustration as a Type B case while asserting a general thesis; and (b) the Type A/Type B classification is not administrable within the embargos framework — determining whether an omission is Type A or Type B requires assessing whether the court's framework resolves the argument, which is itself the generative cognition the reply was meant to circumvent.

## 4. The Defense

### 4.1 Generativity Is Not Autonomy

The adversary establishes that for §489, §1º, IV omissions where implicit rejection is unavailable, the court forms new judgments not previously made. This is accepted. The adversary does not establish that these new judgments are *unconstrained by the existing decision's structure* — which is what "autônoma" in §5.3 targets.

Define autonomous cognition in the embargos context: the court is free to adopt a legal theory not previously raised in the proceedings, invoke controlling precedents it did not previously identify, make factual inferences unrestricted by the original decision's findings, or reconsider issues it already resolved. De novo appellate review and rescisória proceedings are paradigm cases: the reviewing court approaches the matter with an independent framework, unconstrained by the reasoning or conclusions of the lower court.

Embargos cognition lacks these freedoms even when generative. When the court engages an omitted decisive argument in embargos, the new deliberation is bounded along multiple dimensions simultaneously:

- The court must apply the controlling precedents it already identified — it cannot adopt a different interpretive standard than the one its original decision committed to
- The court's factual findings are fixed; embargos cognition operates on those findings, not on an independent reconstruction of the facts
- The evidentiary record is closed at the time of the original decision; no new material enters
- Issues the court already resolved are outside the embargos scope — only the specific gap identified is addressed
- The court cannot invoke a legal theory not raised by the parties in the original proceeding

These constraints collectively bound the space over which the new deliberation ranges. Whether the argument, engaged within this bounded space, changes the result is contingent — accepted, and the §4.4 qualification of Thesis 2 follows from it. But the deliberation producing that contingent outcome is not autonomous: it extends a predetermined framework to a previously unaddressed question rather than independently reconstructing the case.

The adversary's three steps each operate within these constraints. Determining whether the omitted argument is decisive asks whether it could affect the result *within the court's already-adopted legal framework*, not in the abstract. Engaging the argument requires reasoning from the precedents and standards the court already endorsed. Determining whether engagement changes the result depends on the argument's fit with the court's existing structure — not on an independent re-weighing. Each step is new deliberation; none is unconstrained deliberation.

### 4.2 The Implicit Rejection Doctrine as Cognitive Taxonomy

The adversary raises the implicit rejection doctrine as a limitation the paper ignores. The doctrine does more than limit Thesis 1: it provides a cognitive taxonomy that maps directly onto the non-autonomy claim.

When implicit rejection applies — when the court's reasoning logically disposes of the argument without explicit engagement — the omitted argument is not genuinely omitted in the cognitive sense. The court has a position; embargos only require stating it explicitly. This is recall cognition: nothing new is formed, only retrieved.

When implicit rejection does not apply — when the court's reasoning is genuinely silent on the argument, neither explicitly nor structurally engaging it — the embargos court must form a new judgment. This judgment is constrained generative cognition: bounded by the existing decision's framework in the ways §4.1 specifies.

In both categories, the cognition is non-autonomous. Category A (implicit rejection available) requires no new judgment at all. Category B (implicit rejection unavailable) requires new judgment bounded by the existing decision's structure. Neither gives the court freedom to conduct an independent re-examination of the case.

The paper's §5.3 claim applies to category B cases: even when genuine engagement is required, the court does not conduct autonomous new review. This is the operative distinction between embargos and a "third instance" — the concern that the paper's §5.3 was designed to address. The implicit rejection doctrine, rather than undermining §5.3, provides the threshold that operationalizes it: cases where implicit rejection is available are recall cognition; cases where it is not available are constrained generative cognition. Both are non-autonomous.

The paper's gap is in presentation, not in the substantive claim: incorporating the implicit rejection doctrine would clarify where the non-autonomy argument operates without revising its content.

### 4.3 The Art. 489, §1º, IV Threshold and the Administrability Objection

The adversary argues that the Type A/Type B distinction is not administrable because determining whether an omission is Type A or Type B requires generative cognition to apply — specifically, assessing whether the court's existing framework resolves the omitted argument.

This objection proves too much. Every application of a controlling legal standard to a specific factual configuration requires judgment about whether the standard covers the configuration. If this judgment constitutes generative cognition sufficient to defeat the non-autonomy claim, then no precedent-based legal reasoning qualifies as non-autonomous — including all the cases that the paper correctly identifies as recall cognition. Courts apply established precedents to new factual configurations in every case; this is the normal operation of precedent, not the kind of autonomous re-judgment the non-autonomy claim is designed to exclude.

The art. 489, §1º, IV threshold operationalizes the Type A/Type B inquiry: is the omitted argument "em tese capable of undermining the conclusion"? This asks whether the argument, engaged within the court's already-adopted framework, could change the result. The assessment is bounded by the framework — it asks about fit with the court's existing standards, not about what some different legal theory might produce. Applying this threshold is extension of the framework, not abandonment of it.

The administrability concern identifies genuine difficulty in case-by-case application. It does not show that all §489, §1º, IV omissions collapse into a single category of unconstrained re-judgment, or that the recall/generative distinction has no operative content within the embargos perimeter.

### 4.4 Thesis 2: Separating the Practical Claim from the Theoretical Characterization

The adversary correctly identifies that "caso o acolhimento implique modificação" in art. 1.024, §3º is conditional grammar: modification depends on what integration produces, not on a predetermined sequence. The theoretical characterization "modification is automatic consequence" overstates the claim.

Thesis 2's practical recommendation survives this refinement. The adversary acknowledges this directly: "the paper's practical proposal (substituting the traditional formula) could survive; only the theoretical characterization of modification as automatic would require revision."

The practical claim is: the embargante does not need to formulate a separate petitum for modification. Art. 1.024, §3º addresses a contingent consequence that may emerge when the court's integration activity is complete; it does not condition that consequence on the embargante's having petitioned for it. The embargante's petition is for sanação do vício. If that integration implies modification, modification follows from the court's activity.

The traditional formula ("subsidiariamente, requer-se a atribuição de efeitos infringentes") incorrectly positions modification as an item the embargante must separately request — treating as a contingent claim what is, in law, a contingent consequence of integration. This imprecision is the target of Thesis 2. Reading Thesis 2 as the practical claim rather than the overstated theoretical claim, the adversary's grammatical point about "caso" refines the account without defeating the recommendation.

## 5. Anticipated Objection

The most direct challenge to the constrained-generativity defense: if the embargos court can reach different conclusions depending on how it engages the omitted argument, the practical effect is re-litigation of the merits, regardless of whether the deliberation is characterized as "constrained." Courts can invoke the "completing the reasoning path" framing as cover for substituting a different outcome — and the constraints identified in §4.1 provide no external enforcement mechanism.

This objection conflates a risk of procedural misuse with the conceptual content of the constraint. The constraints in §4.1 — identified precedents, fixed factual findings, closed record, decided issues — are legally operative limitations that reviewing courts can apply to distinguish integrative from substitutive activity. The risk that a court will abuse the embargos framework to reach a predetermined different conclusion is a risk of bad-faith application, not evidence that the framework's constraints are without conceptual substance.

The adversary's deeper concern — that modification may follow from the embargos court's genuine new deliberation — is accepted and is absorbed by the §4.4 refinement of Thesis 2. The adversary does not claim embargos should be confined to cases where modification cannot follow; the adversary claims modification is contingent, not predetermined. This is correct and conceded. The defense addresses the further claim: the cognition producing that contingent outcome is, in cases where embargos properly apply, non-autonomous deliberation within a fixed framework — not independent re-examination of the case.

---

## 6. Scope of This Defense

This paper defends:

- §5.3's claim that "não há nova cognição autônoma" by showing that the adversary's attack establishes generativity, not autonomy. Embargos cognition for genuine §489, §1º, IV omissions is constrained generative cognition: new deliberation bounded by the existing decision's structure. This category is non-autonomous in the operationally relevant sense.
- Thesis 1's mode-over-matter principle: the adversary does not contest the textual analysis establishing that art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II extends embargos standing to art. 489, §1º, IV violations. The admissibility claim is intact.
- Thesis 2's practical claim: no separate petition for *efeitos infringentes* is required when integration implies modification. The adversary explicitly acknowledges that this practical recommendation survives.

This paper does not contest:

- That generative cognition occurs when embargos address genuine §489, §1º, IV omissions where implicit rejection is unavailable
- That modification is contingent on the outcome of integration, not predetermined by the omission's existence
- The adversary's identification of the implicit rejection doctrine as a relevant limiting principle requiring engagement
- That the paper's illustration — the reenquadramento case — is a Type B case involving genuine deliberation on an unresolved constitutional question
- The adversary's surrender condition 3: the theoretical characterization of modification as "automatic consequence" should be revised to "contingent consequence of integration, following without a separate petition"

---

## 7. Conditions Under Which This Defense Would Fail

- If "autônoma" in §5.3 is read as synonymous with "generativa" — if any new judgment, however constrained, qualifies as autonomous cognition — the distinction the defense draws has no content, and the attack succeeds on its own terms.
- If the constraints in §4.1 are shown to be effectively non-binding in Brazilian appellate embargos practice — if courts routinely use embargos to revisit controlling precedents, reconstruct factual findings, or reach conclusions for reasons external to the original decision's framework — then the non-autonomy claim is conceptually defensible but without operative purchase in the target legal system.
- If the implicit rejection doctrine occupies a narrower domain than §4.2 assumes — if courts treat more §489, §1º, IV omissions as falling outside implicit rejection than the defense carves out — then the category of constrained-generative embargos is smaller, and the adversary's concern about unconstrained new judgment becomes more pressing for a larger proportion of cases.
- If the adversary's surrender condition 1 is adopted — explicit limitation of Thesis 1 to cases where the court's existing framework determines the answer to the omitted argument — and this limitation is accepted as the correct doctrinal scope by courts or scholars, then constrained-generativity is insufficient and the paper requires the narrowing the adversary demands.
