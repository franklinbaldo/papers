# Frame Stability and the Semantic Proximity Hypothesis: A Cognitive Mechanism for Non-Transitivity in LLM Judges

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "Embedding-Seeded Hierarchical Tournament Ranking" (ESHTR), Section 4, states the **Semantic Proximity Hypothesis**:

> "the incidence of non-transitive preferences in LLM judges is positively correlated with the semantic distance between compared items"

and offers an informal argument grounding this hypothesis in the instability of implicit evaluative frames when compared items are semantically distant.

This paper provides a mechanism vector: an account from the cognitive science of comparative judgment — specifically the literature on contingent weighting and frame-sensitive preference — that independently grounds why the Semantic Proximity Hypothesis should hold. The mechanism is distinct from, and additive to, the bias-amplification account in ESHTR §4 (which focuses on positional and verbosity biases being harder to override when stable criteria are unavailable). The frame-stability account operates at an earlier stage: the evaluative criteria themselves are not stably selected across cross-cluster pairings, so bias-correction applied within any single pairwise comparison cannot prevent cycles arising from criterion inconsistency across three comparisons.

---

## 2. What This Support Adds

**Vector:** Mechanism. The paper proposes a specific causal model — contingent frame construction in comparative judgment — that explains why non-transitivity should be more frequent when compared items are semantically distant. This mechanism is grounded in pre-LLM literature on human preference formation and generates predictions about the *structure* of the non-transitivity (direction-reversing cycles, not random noise) that go beyond the general Semantic Proximity Hypothesis and could be tested in the ESHTR experimental setup.

The support does not repeat the ESHTR §4 argument. It offers independent grounding from a different domain.

---

## 3. The Mechanism

### 3.1 Intransitivity from Contingent Criterion Selection

A foundational result in behavioral decision theory is that preference intransitivity arises not from random error but from systematic changes in decision strategy across pairs (Tversky, 1969). If an agent selects evaluation criterion d₁ to adjudicate between A and B, criterion d₂ to adjudicate between B and C, and criterion d₃ to adjudicate between A and C, then A > B > C and C > A can all be locally correct without any single judgment being unreliable. The cycle is a product of criterion switches across pairs, not of noise within any pair.

The key empirical finding in Tversky (1969) is that such criterion switches are *predictable from the structure of the choice set*: different features are salient in different pair contexts, and the feature that is most discriminating in one pair need not be the most discriminating in another.

This is not a failure of rationality; it is a consequence of the well-documented fact that comparative judgment is context-sensitive. Tversky (1977) showed that similarity judgments between two objects are influenced by which features best discriminate that particular pair: shared features and distinctive features contribute asymmetrically depending on what is being compared. The same structural property applies to preference: the implicit weighting of features shifts with the pair.

### 3.2 Frame Construction and Frame Stability

Define a **comparison frame** as the implicit weighting vector w(A, B) that an evaluator applies when comparing items A and B — encoding which features matter and how much. A comparison frame is **stable** across a triple (A, B, C) if w(A, B) ≈ w(B, C) ≈ w(A, C): the same set of features drives all three pairwise judgments. A frame is **unstable** if the weighting shifts substantially across pairs.

Transitivity is guaranteed when the frame is stable: if the same features are used in all three comparisons and item X consistently outperforms Y on those features, the ranking is consistent. Transitivity is at risk when the frame is unstable: switching from features that favor A-over-B to features that favor C-over-A can produce a cycle even when each individual judgment is accurate under its own frame.

Pairwise embedding proximity does not guarantee triple-level criterion-consistency. A cluster of three items can all be mutually similar in embedding space while still activating different criterion sets across pairings, if items vary asymmetrically across quality dimensions within the cluster. The within-cluster mechanism therefore makes a comparative, probabilistic claim: within-cluster triples exhibit lower criterion-switching rates than cross-cluster triples, not zero criterion-switching. Two structural properties of within-cluster items support this comparative reduction.

First, **quality dimensions in expert legal reasoning tend to be positively correlated within a subject-matter context.** A decision that demonstrates thoroughness in fact-finding also tends to demonstrate coherence in proportionality reasoning, because both reflect the same underlying disposition toward careful analysis — the judge's propensity to reason carefully rather than rely on stock formulas. Maximally asymmetric quality profiles — a decision that excels on one dimension while performing only adequately on others — occur, but are not the modal pattern in a corpus of decisions from the same procedural area. The frequency of criterion-switching triples within a cluster depends on how often items have such asymmetric profiles; positive correlations among dimensions reduce that frequency relative to what a uniform distribution over quality-dimension combinations would predict.

Three objections to the quality-dimension correlation argument require direct response.

*Rubric design and statistical independence.* C1-C5 are defined as independent failure conditions: each criterion identifies a distinct way a decision can be inadequately reasoned regardless of how it performs on the other four. This logical independence does not imply statistical independence in a corpus. The rubric's independence structure reflects completeness — coverage of separable failure modes so that no deficient decision escapes evaluation — not a prediction about the joint distribution of dimension scores in any specific population. Instruments with logically distinct subscales routinely exhibit positive intercorrelations in expert populations, because an underlying performance disposition (analytical care) generates covariance across dimensions even when those dimensions are separately defined. The rubric's design reflects the legislature's recognition that Brazilian appellate decisions can and do fail on specific dimensions while succeeding on others; it does not predict the *frequency* of maximally asymmetric profiles in a corpus. The adversarial paper grounds this argument not in logical structure but in legislative purpose: the CPC amendment enumerated five independent failure conditions because Brazilian appellate decisions were observed to fail on these dimensions independently in practice, establishing cross-dimension divergence as the expected prior for the corpus. The legislative history is relevant but its evidential weight for the corpus-specific claim is limited. It establishes that independent dimensional failures occur in the broad population of Brazilian appellate decisions — a prior for the class. It does not establish their frequency in the specific ESHTR corpus, drawn from a particular jurisdiction, institutional tier, and period. Legislation of this type addresses the existence of problem cases, not their frequency: the CPC amendment would be warranted even if asymmetrically failing decisions constituted a minority of the corpus. The supportive claim is not that such cases are absent; it is that positive correlation from an underlying analytical-care disposition is the *modal* structure of a within-subject-matter corpus of decisions authored by judges in the same procedural area. A prior established from broad population patterns is a starting point for the corpus-specific question, not a resolution of it. Per-item quality-dimension scores — required for Prediction 4 — would measure the correlation structure directly in the ESHTR corpus, resolving the frequency question empirically.

*Case-driven quality-dimension variation.* Different cases prominently exercise different criteria: a case requiring complex ratio identification generates sustained C1 work; vigorously contested material arguments generate sustained C2 work. This produces within-cluster variation in which dimensions receive the most elaborate treatment. Whether this translates into asymmetric quality *scores* — substantially high on one dimension, below average on others — depends on whether judges perform poorly on dimensions the case did not prominently test. Quality of judicial reasoning reflects a judge-level disposition component — propensity toward careful, non-formulaic analysis — that generates positive covariance across a given judge's decisions on all dimensions, in addition to the case-specific demand component that varies with what the case required. Dimensions like C3 (avoiding generic boilerplate) and C5 (dispositif coherence) are properties of writing style rather than responses to party arguments; a judge with a careful analytical disposition applies them regardless of whether the opposing party pressed strong arguments on those dimensions. Case-driven variation establishes that decisions differ in which dimensions are most elaborately addressed; it does not establish that scores on non-focal dimensions are low rather than merely less extensively exercised.

The adversarial paper contests this exemption specifically for C2, arguing that unlike C3 and C5, C2 performance is constrained by what material arguments were available: a case where the losing party's most powerful argument was genuinely unmeritorious produces thin C2 even in the most careful judge, not from a writing-disposition deficit but because there was no strong argument to engage. C2 variation within a cluster therefore reflects both judge-level engagement skill and the case-level quality of the adversarial record — a compound that writing disposition cannot smooth into uniformity. Four responses contest this C2-specific structural distinctness claim.

First, C2 (art. 489, §1º, IV) evaluates analytical *conduct* toward arguments, not the arguments' intrinsic strength. The criterion asks whether the decision addresses arguments capable of affecting the outcome — assessed from the decision's own reasoning. A carefully reasoned explanation of why a raised argument does not clear the decisiveness threshold demonstrates the same engagement quality as elaborate analysis of a strong argument: it correctly identifies the applicable legal standard, characterizes the argument's limits, and applies the standard to show the shortfall. The adversarial paper conflates volume of engagement (more elaborate reasoning when arguments are stronger) with quality of engagement (careful identification and disposal of arguments, whatever their strength). What a calibrated LLM judge assesses under C2 is whether the court reasoned correctly about the arguments raised — not whether the arguments provided opportunity for extensive elaboration. A brief but analytically precise disposal of a weak argument demonstrates C2 quality; a lengthy but mechanical engagement with a strong argument does not. The quality of C2 reasoning is not bounded by the arguments' strength; it is bounded by the judge's analytical care in reasoning about them, which is the same disposition-level variable that governs C3 and C5.

The adversarial reply to this first response operates at the operationalization level: even if C2 is theoretically a conduct measure, LLM judges trained on human preference data acquire a calibration gradient correlated with adversarial record elaboration, because human annotators cannot distinguish "brief analytically precise disposal of a weak argument" from "brief cursory analysis of any argument" by reading the decision text alone. This reply depends on a premise that should be examined: that analytical precision leaves no argument-type-independent textual markers. The premise is stronger than the evidence warrants. In Brazilian appellate decisions, analytically precise disposal of arguments — whatever their strength — produces structural features distinguishable from conclusory disposal in the decision text itself: explicit identification of what the argument claims (beyond merely acknowledging it was raised), citation to the applicable legal standard as the operative criterion for the argument's validity, and a logical statement of why the argument fails under that standard. These features are present or absent regardless of argument strength; an annotator or calibrated LLM judge evaluating conduct quality — "did the court correctly reason about the arguments raised?" — can assess their presence from the decision text without prior knowledge of whether the argument was strong or weak. Evaluating *conduct quality* requires assessing reasoning structure, which is textually observable; evaluating *argument strength* requires prior knowledge of argument character. The adversarial reply conflates these two cognitive tasks. Calibration examples demonstrating high C2 conduct quality in weak-argument contexts — where the court precisely identifies a weak argument's failure under the applicable standard, with specific citation and logical reasoning, and without elaboration proportional to argument strength — would train LLM judges to track argument-type-independent conduct markers rather than elaboration richness. That SC6 (the adversarial paper's surrender condition for this debate) specifies exactly this calibration extension as sufficient to address the concern is relevant: a gap that can be closed by specifying calibration examples is a protocol design insufficiency, not a structural impossibility that infects C2 measurement regardless of how the calibration protocol is constructed.

Second, adversarial record quality effects are not C2-specific: they are correlated across the argument-dependent dimensions C1, C2, and C4 simultaneously. A rich adversarial record — where opposing counsel raised sophisticated arguments about which precedents control, what their ratios establish, and whether a binding precedent applies, is distinguishable, or requires deviation — demands careful C1 reasoning (identifying the ratio of contested precedents) and careful C4 reasoning (correctly applying or distinguishing precedents when their scope is contested) alongside C2. Strong adversarial records create C1, C2, and C4 opportunity as a correlated set, not C2 opportunity alone. This structural observation reframes the profile consequence. Under the adversarial paper's mechanism, a within-cluster quality profile has a two-group internal structure: argument-dependent dimensions (C1, C2, C4), which show correlated variation with adversarial record quality alongside judge disposition, and argument-independent dimensions (C3, C5), which track writing disposition alone. For C2 to generate asymmetric profiles relative to C3/C5 — the asymmetry that would produce criterion-switching — adversarial record quality would need to affect C2 independently of C1 and C4. But the mechanism does not predict that: a case with weak adversarial arguments constrains C1, C2, and C4 opportunity together. The resulting profiles within a cluster are: {high C1, high C2, high C4, high C3, high C5} for cases with demanding adversarial records, and {adequate C1, adequate C2, adequate C4, high C3, high C5} for cases with undemanding ones. Both profiles are internally correlated. The asymmetric structure that generates criterion-switching — {high C2, adequate C1/C4, high C3/C5} paired with {adequate C2, high C1/C4, adequate C3/C5} — would require adversarial record quality to affect C2 while leaving C1 and C4 unaffected, which the adversarial paper's own argument does not establish.

The adversarial reply to this second response distinguishes between-case from within-cluster dynamics: the C1/C4 co-variation argument applies across cases, but within a fine-grained doctrinal cluster C1/C4 variance is compressed by shared precedential context while C2 varies independently with case-specific adversarial records, producing the profile asymmetry the co-variation argument claims cannot arise. This within-cluster distinction rests on an implicit claim about embedding selectivity: that clustering by embedding proximity compresses C1/C4 variation (via shared doctrinal vocabulary and precedential references) while leaving C2 variation relatively uncompressed, because argumentative elaboration richness is orthogonal to embedding proximity in this domain. Dense embedding models trained on legal text encode both doctrinal vocabulary and argumentative discourse structure in the same representational space. Decisions with richer C2 engagement differ textually from those with thinner engagement on the same doctrinal question in ways beyond doctrinal vocabulary alone: they contain more argument-specific reasoning vocabulary, more explicit counter-reasoning structure, more discursive markers of argument identification and disposal — features that embedding models trained on dense legal text represent alongside precedential references and procedural posture. To the extent that embedding proximity clusters by argumentative elaboration richness alongside doctrinal vocabulary, within-cluster C2 variation is also compressed by the clustering step. The adversarial mechanism requires selective compression — doctrinal context compressed, argumentative elaboration not — a selectivity that has not been established for multilingual-e5-large-instruct or its variants in Brazilian legal corpora. Whether within-cluster C2 variance is in fact systematically wider than within-cluster C1/C4 variance — the empirical premise the within-cluster compression argument requires — is directly testable: per-item C1, C2, and C4 dimension scores within fine-grained clusters, the measurement SC6(c) specifies, would determine whether the asymmetry the adversarial mechanism predicts is present in the corpus.

*Routinization and compressed variance.* C3 is susceptible to high-volume formulaic patterns: courts under caseload pressure may develop standing formulas that satisfy C3's form while failing its substance across most decisions in recurring case types. If C3 is systematically formulaic across most cluster members, within-cluster variance on C3 is compressed toward a common floor. A dimension with low within-cluster variance is not the most discriminating feature in pairings between items that are similarly formulaic on C3: by the Tversky (1977) account, pairings are discriminated by the features that differ most between the specific items, and where most items score near the same floor, C3 is not that feature. Routinization therefore reduces C3's criterion-activating role for pairings between comparably formulaic items — it does not generate widespread criterion-switching across those pairings, which require one item to distinctively outperform another on a dimension the pairing's opponent is weaker on. The exception applies to a cluster member that deviates from the formulaic pattern: its C3 distinctiveness becomes the most discriminating feature in pairings where its C3 advantage exceeds the opponent's counter-advantages on other dimensions. This is a localized effect on C3-exceptional items, not a cluster-wide mechanism.

Second, **embedding proximity captures more than subject-matter topic label.** Legal decisions that are embedding-proximate share doctrinal vocabulary, precedential references, procedural posture, and rhetorical conventions. Two decisions in the same embedding neighborhood of administrative disciplinary proceedings do not merely share the category label "administrative sanctions"; they invoke similar precedents, apply similar evidentiary standards, and employ similar procedural structures. This doctrinal micro-context constrains the most salient criterion set for any pair within the cluster. The Tversky (1977) finding that similarity judgments are driven by the most discriminating features of the specific pair implies that within a high-density embedding cluster — where items share fine-grained doctrinal context, not merely a broad topical category — the features that most discriminate within-cluster pairs are drawn from a more constrained criterion repertoire than those that discriminate cross-cluster pairs.

**Across semantic clusters**, items differ in subject matter, normative framework, vocabulary, and reasoning conventions. The features that best discriminate a criminal sentencing decision from a tax injunction ruling are not the same features that best discriminate a tax injunction ruling from a property rights decision. The comparison frame shifts between pairs — not occasionally but as a structural consequence of domain differences. This frame shift creates the structural conditions for direction-reversing cycles: A beats B on Feature₁ (criminal reasoning quality), B beats C on Feature₂ (regulatory analysis depth), and C beats A on Feature₃ (property law precedent handling) — a cycle produced by criterion switching, with each individual judgment locally defensible.

The within-cluster stability claim is therefore comparative: within-cluster triples exhibit lower criterion-switching rates than cross-cluster triples, because positive quality-dimension correlations and doctrinal micro-context jointly constrain criterion-repertoire shifts. The design standard for Phase 1 is relative reduction in non-transitivity incidence — the baseline being all-pairs cross-corpus comparison — not elimination of within-cluster non-transitivity.

### 3.3 Why LLM Judges Inherit This Mechanism

LLM judges trained on human-generated preference data learn the context-sensitivity that characterizes human comparative judgment. An LLM that has been trained or prompted to evaluate "judicial quality" will have acquired, from legal text and human annotations, that:

- criminal reasoning quality is judged on criteria specific to that domain (evidentiary standards, sentencing proportionality, constitutional rights compliance);
- administrative reasoning quality invokes a different criterion set (abuse of discretion, proportionality of sanction, due process compliance);
- contract law quality invokes yet another (formation analysis, breach materiality, remedy appropriateness).

This domain-specific criterion mapping is *not* a bias to be corrected: within-cluster, it produces more reliable judgments by activating criteria that are actually relevant to the domain being evaluated. It becomes a structural problem precisely when cross-cluster comparisons ask the LLM to compare items from different criterion regimes.

The within-cluster mechanism described in this section should not be read as establishing that domain-specific criterion activation at the representation level is incompatible with Phase 3's structural-reading mode. The claim here is specific: domain-specific criterion mapping, acquired through training, produces reliability when compared items share the domain context for which those criteria are appropriate. The question of whether an explicit abstraction instruction can redirect that activation at the output-mapping level — by modulating what the trained representations serve as evidence for in the generated output — is distinct from the within-cluster claim. Training-encoded domain associations and instruction-tuned output mapping operate at different levels of the same model; the former are the raw material the latter acts on. That Phase 2 reliability depends on domain-specific criterion activation does not entail that Phase 3's instruction cannot shift which features determine the comparative judgment at the output level, nor that structural-reading mode and domain-specific feature activation are mutually exclusive in instruction-tuned models. The Phase 3 question is addressed in `yesindeed/phase3-coherence-defense.md §4.6`; within-cluster reliability and Phase 3 tractability are not in structural conflict.

The LLM faces the same contingent weighting problem as a human domain expert asked to compare work across their specialty and adjacent domains: the evaluative frame that makes sense for items in domain A is not the same as the frame that makes sense for items in domain B, and the frame chosen for the A-vs-B comparison may differ from the frame chosen if the same items appeared in an A-vs-C comparison.

Xu et al. (2025) document that LLM non-transitivity is correlated with positional bias — a result consistent with the frame-instability account. When the comparison frame is unstable, the LLM has no stable criterion set to apply, and surface-level heuristics (position, length) fill the gap. The frame-instability mechanism thus precedes and explains the bias-susceptibility documented by Xu et al.: instability creates the conditions under which biases become decisive.

### 3.4 More Specific Predictions

The frame-stability account generates predictions more specific than the general Semantic Proximity Hypothesis. If non-transitivity arises from criterion switching, then:

1. Cross-cluster non-transitivity should be **structured**: cycles should not be uniformly distributed across all possible item triples but should cluster around triples where the pairwise semantic distances are maximally unequal (one within-cluster pair, two cross-cluster pairs).

2. The direction of cycles should be **predictable from the semantic structure**: if cluster C_i emphasizes Feature₁ and cluster C_j emphasizes Feature₂, then items from C_i should systematically beat items from C_j when the framing activates Feature₁ and lose when Feature₂ is activated — producing a predictable direction for cycles involving items from C_i and C_j.

3. Cross-cluster κ degradation should be **asymmetric across cluster pairs**: cluster pairs that are semantically closer (e.g., two civil law clusters vs. a civil law and a criminal law cluster) should show smaller κ degradation. This is a testable gradient, not a binary within/cross split.

4. Within-cluster non-transitive cycles, to the extent they occur, should be **unstructured relative to quality-dimension asymmetry profiles**: their distribution within clusters should not concentrate at triples where items have maximally asymmetric quality-dimension profiles. Cross-cluster cycles are predicted to be directionally structured (concentrated by domain-criterion mismatch); within-cluster cycles are predicted to be approximately uniformly distributed relative to quality-dimension profiles (reflecting non-systematic criterion variation rather than domain-frame switching). This can be assessed by measuring per-item quality-dimension scores alongside cycle incidence in the ESHTR experimental protocol and testing whether cycle incidence correlates with quality-dimension asymmetry within clusters.

Predictions 1–4 can be assessed in the ESHTR experimental setup (§5.5, §6) with additional analysis beyond the aggregate κ comparison: tracking not just whether κ is lower within-cluster but whether the distribution of non-transitive triples matches the structured between-cluster and unstructured within-cluster patterns the mechanism predicts.

### 3.5 Aggregation Robustness and the Operationalization Proxy

**Bradley-Terry aggregation and residual within-cluster switching.** Even where within-cluster triples exhibit some criterion switching, the Bradley-Terry model aggregates over all pairings within the cluster. If criterion-switching within a cluster is non-systematic — not correlated with a stable directional bias against any particular item — the aggregate Bradley-Terry score over many pairings is a less biased estimator of underlying quality than any individual pairwise comparison. Under positive quality-dimension correlations (§3.2), the highest-quality item in a cluster tends to win more pairings than it loses across the distribution of criteria activated, even when individual pairings may go differently depending on which criterion is most salient for that pair.

The contrast with cross-cluster criterion-switching is structurally important. Cross-cluster switching is directional: the same item (say, a criminal law decision) consistently activates different criteria when paired with opponents from different domains, producing systematic biases that accumulate rather than average out. Within-cluster criterion-switching, predicted by the mechanism to be less directional because the shared doctrinal context constrains the criterion repertoire, contributes variance to pairwise outcomes without producing the systematic directional bias that makes cross-cluster comparison unreliable. Bradley-Terry's value is precisely in reducing variance-without-systematic-bias to a stable aggregate signal — the setting where within-cluster non-transitivity, if non-systematic, can be handled.

A more targeted challenge to the non-systematic assumption proceeds from the Tversky mechanism itself: a decision with a persistently distinctive structural feature — notably extensive fact-finding, an unusually elaborate precedent chain — may activate its associated criterion as the most discriminating dimension in every within-cluster pairing, because that feature is what most distinguishes the decision from each cluster neighbor. Under this pattern, Bradley-Terry would aggregate over a single persistently activated criterion rather than a distribution of criteria, producing a score that reflects performance on one dimension rather than an overall quality signal.

The persistence argument rests on an assumption about pairing structure that requires examination. The Tversky (1977) account identifies the most discriminating feature of a *comparison*, not of either item individually: it is the feature where the difference between the two specific items is largest. Decision A's fact-finding excellence activates fact-finding as the primary criterion in a pairing with opponent B only when A's fact-finding advantage over B exceeds B's counter-advantage over A on every other quality dimension. In a pairing between A and opponent C who is also a strong fact-finder (closely matched with A on that dimension) but distinctly weaker on proportionality analysis (where A holds a comparatively smaller absolute advantage), the most discriminating feature shifts to proportionality — the dimension where the pair differs most. Persistent criterion activation across all of A's within-cluster pairings requires that A's advantage on one dimension exceeds all other dimension advantages relative to *every specific opponent* A faces in the cluster. This is an extreme condition: it holds only when A is a clear outlier on that dimension relative to the entire cluster population, such that every opponent is closer to A on all other dimensions than on the one where A dominates. In clusters with graded quality variation across multiple dimensions — where different items hold modest relative advantages on different dimensions — the most discriminating feature shifts from pairing to pairing depending on each opponent's position, producing pair-specific criterion variation that Bradley-Terry aggregates without directional accumulation. The persistent activation configuration is itself falsifiable: Bradley-Terry scores for items exhibiting it would be more strongly predicted by a single quality dimension than by a multi-dimensional composite, and their cycle involvement would be structured relative to that dimension's activation pattern — the structured pattern that Prediction 4 is designed to detect.

The pair-specificity response and the aggregation defense share a load-bearing premise with the quality-dimension correlation claim: both succeed under strong positive correlation and face pressure under weak correlation. Under strong positive correlation, opponents weaker than A on A's focal dimension are also weaker on other dimensions, so their counter-advantages are modest and A's focal dimension remains the most discriminating feature across the majority of A's cross-strength pairings — producing composition-dependent but quality-valid Bradley-Terry scores. Under weak or zero correlation, opponents weaker on A's focal dimension may be strong on other dimensions, generating counter-advantages that displace the focal dimension as most discriminating in specific pairings. The within-cluster debate therefore has a single empirical pivot: whether quality dimensions in the specific ESHTR corpus exhibit positive correlation. Per-item quality-dimension scores, collected alongside Bradley-Terry rankings as part of implementing Prediction 4, would measure this correlation structure directly and resolve the §3.2, §3.3, and aggregation defense questions simultaneously from a single data collection.

A structural argument from the adversarial paper does not depend on extreme outlier items and addresses the round-robin tournament at the composition level. In a cluster where some items are stronger on fact-finding and others on proportionality reasoning, pairings between two fact-finding-strong items are most discriminated on proportionality — the dimension where they differ — while pairings between two proportionality-strong items are most discriminated on fact-finding. A fact-finding-strong item's pairing schedule in a cluster with many proportionality-strong opponents activates the fact-finding criterion frequently, accumulating wins through that dimension and generating a Bradley-Terry score that reflects, in part, how often its strong dimension is the most discriminating feature — a function of the cluster's composition. This is a genuine property of multi-dimensional quality assessment in tournament settings. What it does not establish is that the resulting scores are invalid quality indicators. Under positive quality-dimension correlation, the item that wins cross-group pairings by activating its focal dimension wins because it is *actually better* on that dimension, and tends to be at least adequate on other dimensions so that same-group pairings (where a different dimension is most salient) do not produce reversals. The composition effect shapes which dimension is most salient per pairing; it does not reverse the quality ordering of items when quality profiles are correlated. For the cluster-composition dynamic to generate cycles — the condition for invalid Bradley-Terry rankings — items strong on one dimension would need to reliably lose to opponents strong on a different dimension while reliably beating opponents similar to themselves: the asymmetric profile structure that positive correlation suppresses. The cluster-composition argument therefore does not constitute an independent attack that survives under positive correlation; it is a restatement of the correlation-sensitive concern in the language of tournament composition. Whether it generates cycles or valid quality orderings depends on the same correlation question that §3.2 and §3.3 pivot on.

**The pre-evaluation operationalization constraint.** An operationalization fully aligned with the mechanism — clustering items so that any triple within a cluster activates approximately the same criterion set — would require knowing, before Phase 2 runs, which quality dimensions are most discriminating for which pairs. This information is not available in advance; it emerges from the evaluation itself. The criterion-set homogeneity that the mechanism identifies as the ideal clustering target is precisely what the ESHTR evaluation is designed to discover: which decisions, compared on which criteria, produce which orderings. Pre-evaluation, the only information available is the text of the decisions, and embeddings are the best available summary of that text for predicting criterion salience. Embedding clustering is a tractable pre-evaluation proxy that reduces criterion-switching relative to random or arbitrary comparison groupings by capturing the semantic context that shapes criterion activation. Whether the reduction is sufficient is an empirical question — one that prediction 4 can inform.

## 4. Anticipated Objection

The mechanism assumes that LLM judges construct locally varying comparison frames that shift with the semantic context of the pair. An alternative model holds that LLMs encode a single high-dimensional quality embedding space in which any two items can be compared by a fixed function of their embeddings, producing preferences that are stable across permutations of the comparison context. On this model, non-transitivity would arise from noise in the quality embedding rather than from criterion switching, and would not be systematically correlated with semantic distance.

The embedded-quality model is consistent with some recent findings showing that larger LLMs produce more consistent pairwise judgments overall. If consistency gains from scale eliminate the criterion-switching mechanism, cross-cluster non-transitivity would not exceed within-cluster non-transitivity, and the Semantic Proximity Hypothesis would be disconfirmed.

This objection underscores that the cognitive science grounding provides *plausibility*, not proof. The mechanism is the best available account given what is known about how LLMs process comparative prompts, but it is not derivable from first principles about LLM internals. The ESHTR experimental protocol (Section 5.5) is the necessary test.

## 5. Scope of This Support

This paper strengthens the theoretical plausibility of the Semantic Proximity Hypothesis through a mechanism independent of the ESHTR paper's own account. It adds:

- a named mechanism (contingent frame construction, grounded in the Tversky intransitivity literature);
- a structural distinction between two sources of non-transitivity (frame instability vs. bias amplification), showing they are additive rather than alternative;
- four experimental predictions derivable from the mechanism (structured cross-cluster cycles, directional predictability, asymmetric cluster-pair gradient, and within-cluster cycle distribution relative to quality-dimension asymmetry) that can be assessed in the same experimental setup as the general hypothesis;
- a comparative account of within-cluster stability: within-cluster triples exhibit lower criterion-switching rates than cross-cluster triples because positive quality-dimension correlations and doctrinal micro-context jointly constrain criterion-repertoire shifts within clusters; Bradley-Terry aggregation provides practical robustness against residual non-systematic within-cluster switching.

This paper does **not** provide empirical evidence for the Semantic Proximity Hypothesis. It does not test LLM judges. The ESHTR experimental protocol remains the required validation. The mechanism argument increases the prior probability that the hypothesis will be confirmed, and its specific sub-predictions give the experiment additional diagnostic power.

The within-cluster stability claim is comparative and probabilistic, not categorical: the paper argues that within-cluster triples exhibit lower criterion-switching rates than cross-cluster triples, not that within-cluster triples are free from criterion switching. The design standard for Phase 1 is relative reduction in non-transitivity incidence, not elimination.

## 6. Conditions Under Which This Support Would Fail

- If the ESHTR experiment shows cross-cluster κ ≤ within-cluster κ (the general hypothesis is disconfirmed), this mechanism paper falls with it.
- If cross-cluster non-transitivity is uniformly distributed rather than structured (cycles are not concentrated around triples with maximal pairwise semantic distance asymmetry), prediction 1 fails, and the criterion-switching account is not supported even if the general hypothesis holds.
- If the distribution of non-transitive cycles shows no predictable directional bias across cluster pairs (prediction 2), the mechanism fails even with the general hypothesis confirmed — pointing to noise-based rather than frame-based non-transitivity.
- If scale eliminates the criterion-switching mechanism (large enough LLMs show no semantic-distance gradient in non-transitivity), the cognitive science grounding does not transfer to frontier models of the kind used in ESHTR's panel.
- If within-cluster non-transitive cycles are found to be *structured* — concentrated at quality-dimension asymmetry boundaries, with items having maximally asymmetric quality-dimension profiles generating cycles more frequently than items with correlated quality profiles — this would indicate that quality-dimension correlations are weak in the corpus. Under this outcome, the within-cluster claim fails even if cross-cluster non-transitivity remains higher, because within-cluster cycles would be qualitatively similar to cross-cluster cycles rather than random noise.
- If item-level persistent criterion activation is confirmed at substantial scale — specifically, if Bradley-Terry scores for more than a small fraction of cluster members are more strongly predicted by a single quality dimension than by a multi-dimensional composite — the non-systematic assumption is empirically rejected for those items and the aggregation argument weakens proportionally. This is detectable in the ESHTR experimental protocol by measuring per-item dimension scores alongside Bradley-Terry rankings and testing whether aggregate rank is better explained by single-dimension standing than by a combined quality signal — the same data collection needed to implement Prediction 4. An analogous test applies at the composition level: if Bradley-Terry scores are more strongly predicted by a quality-dimension scarcity index — how seldom an item's strongest dimension appears in its opponents' profiles — than by the item's own multi-dimensional quality composite, then composition effects rather than individual quality are driving rankings for those items. This would indicate that the cluster-composition dynamic is generating structured score artifacts rather than reflecting valid quality differentiation, and the aggregation argument fails at the composition level even where persistent activation at the individual item level is not observed.
- If per-item C1, C2, and C4 dimension scores within fine-grained clusters show that within-cluster C2 variance is systematically wider than within-cluster C1/C4 variance — controlling for judge disposition by selecting clusters where judicial authorship is similar — this would confirm that embedding proximity selectively compresses doctrinal-context dimensions while leaving argumentative elaboration variation uncompressed, validating the adversarial paper's within-cluster C2 independence mechanism and undermining the fourth response above. Further, if LLM C2 scores under an extended calibration protocol including weak-argument examples remain correlated with adversarial record elaboration richness, this would confirm that the textual markers of analytical precision are not sufficiently argument-type-independent to support a calibration-based resolution of the operationalization gap — and the third response above fails with it.
