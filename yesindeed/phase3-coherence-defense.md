# Phase 3 Is Not a Criterion Substitution: On the Coherence of ESHTR's Global Ranking

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "Embedding-Seeded Hierarchical Tournament Ranking" (ESHTR), Section 3.3, proposes that Phase 3 of the method conducts a cross-cluster championship tournament using the same LLM panel and rubric as Phase 2, with one modification: an additional criterion for **contextual generalizability** — "whether the reasoning quality evident in the decision would transfer to adjacent subject matters."

The implicit claim in the design is that this produces a **unified global quality ranking**: a single ordering of judicial decisions that is coherent with the Phase 2 domain-specific rankings.

This paper responds to the attack in `otherwise/eshtr-phase3-gap.md` (Sections 3.5–3.7 and Section 4), which argues that (a) accepting C1-C5 as domain-general creates a structural dilemma that the defense cannot escape; (b) LLM judges cannot in practice separate domain-specific content from domain-general method; and (c) Phase 3's champion comparators may be artifacts of within-cluster criterion-switching rather than genuine quality peaks.

---

## 2. What This Support Adds

**Vector:** Direct defense. This paper reconstructs the adversarial paper's updated arguments — including the structural dilemma in §3.5, the method/content inseparability claim in §3.6, the champion-circularity argument in §3.7, the asymmetry justification for differential evidential demands (Phase 2 additive/"with grain" vs. Phase 3 suppressive/"against grain"), structural critiques of the calibration protocol (circular quality ground truth; champion-scale gap; variance-source confound), and the three round 5 additions: the input-protocol extension requirement for coverage completeness (party submissions as evaluator input), the SC6(b) naturalistic generalization requirement, and the SC6(3) cross-elaboration extension under prong 2 of the C2 disjunction — and responds to each on its own terms. The core factual move is to show that the dilemma's Horn 1 conclusion rests on a mischaracterization of what ESHTR's hierarchical design was constructed to avoid. The response to the asymmetry justification shows that "suppressive" mischaracterizes what Phase 3 requires under Option (c) and that the LLM's grain includes structural evaluation capabilities that Phase 3 calls on, not only domain-specific criterion associations. The responses to the calibration critiques show that both critiques generate distinguishable predictions within the existing protocol and that the adversarial surrender condition extensions identify valid and testable improvements. On the three round 5 additions: the input-protocol extension is a minor input-specification change, not an architectural redesign; SC6(b-2) naturalistic validation is specified concretely; and the SC6(3) cross-elaboration test is specified as a matched-domain, adversarial-record-differentiated pair construction sharing the expert-assessment infrastructure SC6(3) extension (a) already requires. Round 7 of the adversarial exchange presses three further points: the input-protocol extension introduces a materiality-identification requirement — the LLM must determine which brief arguments cross the threshold of art. 489, §1º, IV ('capazes de, em tese, infirmar a conclusão'), and the elaborate-quality correlation in party briefs (more elaborate arguments more readily treated as material) reintroduces the C2-adjacent elaboration problem at the input level, with SC6(a) calibration required to test the full pipeline including materiality identification, not only coverage ranking given a pre-specified material set; SC6(b-2) is accepted as appropriately specified; and the SC6(3) expert-assessment protocol is challenged as question-begging — specifying that expert assessment be 'independent of elaboration level' presupposes the separability the test is designed to establish, and both available assessment standards (complexity-relative, requiring adversarial-record calibration not specified in the protocol; universal-scale, tracking elaboration directly) reintroduce the adversarial record as a determinant. This defense addresses the round 7 arguments: the materiality-identification challenge is implicit in any C2 evaluation and is addressed by the structure of SC6(b-1) anti-naturalistic calibration pairs, which already test whether the LLM identifies material brief arguments correctly rather than by elaboration; and the question-begging objection is resolved by specifying expert assessment on categorical and ordinal measures — ratio identification precision, material argument engagement completeness, logical entailment validity — whose reference answers are established from the decision text and party brief independently of adversarial record richness. Adversarial round 8 advances three responses: (1) SC6(b-1) anti-naturalistic pairs test coverage-ranking given a construction-embedded material argument set, not the upstream materiality-identification step from novel brief content with no pre-specification — an LLM could pass SC6(b-1) by learning a coverage-over-length heuristic without acquiring genuine materiality-identification from raw brief content; (2) the categorical composite's reference answers require adversarial-record-sensitive construction, and differential inter-rater reliability across adversarial record arms — not global reliability — is the operative diagnostic; (3) the three-measure composite omits C3 and C4, leaving the falsification criterion ambiguous for quality differences on unmeasured dimensions. This defense addresses round 8: SC6(b-1)'s structural limitation is accepted and a supplementary SC6(b-1)-ID calibration test is proposed that directly tests argument-level materiality identification from raw brief content under art. 489, §1º, IV's outcome-dependency standard; the differential inter-rater reliability diagnostic is accepted for both C1 and C2 reference-answer construction, with the prediction that C1 arm-specific reliability is comparable because annotators identify which grounds the court presented as its ratio in the decision text (a post-decision text-reading task) rather than independently determining what the ratio should have been; and the three-measure composite is extended to a five-dimension composite incorporating C4 (decision-to-cited-precedent characterization comparison) and C3 (within-cluster formulaic-language frequency analysis), making the falsification criterion interpretively unambiguous.

## 3. Reconstructing the Updated Attack

### 3.1 The Criterion Substitution Argument

Phase 2 ranks decisions by C1-C5, criteria anchored to domain-specific procedural standards. Phase 3 adds "contextual generalizability" — whether reasoning quality would transfer across domains. The adversarial paper argues that combining these rankings produces a composite of incommensurable criteria, not a unified quality ordering, because a highest-quality Phase 2 decision may score poorly on contextual generalizability if it applies domain-specific statutory formulas.

### 3.2 The Non-Transitivity Recurrence Argument

The adversarial paper argues that contextual generalizability requires cross-contextual judgment that is susceptible to the same criterion-switching mechanism that generates non-transitivity within the ESHTR corpus (§4 of ESHTR). An instruction to "abstract across domains" asks the LLM to bridge precisely the criterion-set differences that produce cross-cluster cycling.

### 3.3 The Structural Dilemma

The adversarial paper (§3.5) presents a dilemma for the prior defense's claim that C1-C5 tests domain-general reasoning method:

- **Horn 1:** If C1-C5 is domain-general, then Phase 3 comparisons under "contextual generalizability" are conceptually equivalent to cross-cluster C1-C5 comparisons. But that, the adversarial paper argues, is "precisely the operation that ESHTR's hierarchical design was constructed to avoid, on the grounds that cross-cluster LLM-panel evaluation produces non-transitive results." Phase 3 then repeats the problem instead of solving it.

- **Horn 2:** If "contextual generalizability" is not simply C1-C5 applied cross-cluster — if it is genuinely different — criterion substitution stands.

The adversarial paper argues the middle position ("C1-C5 at a higher level of abstraction") is unstable: if the instruction changes what is evaluated, it changes the criterion (Horn 2); if it does not, it offers no operational advantage (Horn 1).

### 3.4 Method/Content Inseparability

The adversarial paper (§3.6) argues that LLM judges applying C1-C5 to domain-specific legal texts either:

**(a)** apply domain-specific legal knowledge to identify the ratio, making C1-C5 scores sensitive to domain content in practice, or

**(b)** apply a domain-agnostic template that misses domain-specific load-bearing elements, losing doctrinal precision.

Neither option, the adversarial paper argues, supports the claim that C1-C5 is domain-general in its implementation.

### 3.5 Champion Circularity

The adversarial paper (§3.7) argues that Phase 3 comparators — the Phase 2 cluster champions — may not be genuine quality peaks. If Phase 2 within-cluster rankings are themselves produced by criterion-switching, the champion is whichever decision happened to win the specific pairings the tournament arranged, not necessarily the highest-quality decision under any stable standard. Phase 3's supposed operational advantage (a more homogeneous, quality-selected comparator population) is undermined if Phase 2 cannot be trusted to produce it.

## 4. The Defense

### 4.1 C1-C5 as Reasoning Method

The text of C1-C5 does not support the reading that they test domain-specific content.

- **C1** (art. 489, §1º, V): isolating the *determining grounds* (*fundamentos determinantes*) of invoked precedents — the ratio that generalizes from the specific case — is a reasoning operation that applies across domains in the same form.
- **C2** (art. 489, §1º, IV): engaging arguments capable of affecting the outcome tests whether the judge identified and addressed material challenges to the holding. The criterion does not specify what kinds of arguments count; it tests the cognitive operation of engagement.
- **C3** (art. 489, §1º, III): avoiding generic boilerplate tests whether reasoning is specific to the argument structure at hand, not borrowed from a domain-specific formula bank.
- **C4** (art. 927, §1º): correctly applying, distinguishing, or justifying deviation from binding precedent requires understanding why the precedent holds — its underlying principle — so that its scope can be assessed when facts differ. This operation has the same form regardless of subject matter.
- **C5** (art. 1.022): logical completeness and non-contradiction of the dispositif is domain-agnostic.

C1-C5 test whether reasoning *method* was applied correctly: isolating ratio, engaging material arguments, avoiding formulaic shortcuts, abstracting the right principle from precedent, producing a coherent device.

### 4.2 Content vs. Reasoning-Quality Transferability

The adversarial paper's exemplar counterexample — "a criminal sentencing decision may score poorly on contextual generalizability because criminal sentencing employs technical statutory formulas specific to the Código Penal that do not transfer" — conflates:

- **Content-transferability:** whether the specific statutory rules (art. 59, art. 68 CP, *bis in idem* doctrine) apply in other domains. False; they do not.
- **Reasoning-quality-transferability:** whether the method of reasoning demonstrated — the way the judge applies proportionality, isolates ratio, engages arguments, avoids formulaic shortcuts — would produce quality reasoning in other domains.

ESHTR's Phase 3 criterion asks whether "the reasoning quality evident in the decision would transfer to adjacent subject matters" (§3.3, emphasis added). This is a question about the second property, not the first. A decision that scores highly on C1-C5 demonstrates method quality that is, by the nature of what those criteria test, applicable across domains.

### 4.3 Dissolving the Dilemma

The structural dilemma (§3.3 above) rests on a mischaracterization of what ESHTR's hierarchical design was constructed to avoid.

The adversarial paper states (§3.5) that cross-cluster C1-C5 comparison is "precisely the operation that ESHTR's hierarchical design was constructed to avoid." But ESHTR §3.1 frames the problem as unreliable rankings from *uncontrolled* direct cross-corpus comparison and provides the hierarchical structure as a solution that *confines* cross-cluster comparison to Phase 3 — not that eliminates it. The paper explicitly describes Phase 3 as "a cross-cluster championship tournament" (§3.3). Phase 3 IS the controlled cross-cluster comparison that ESHTR's design enables; it is not a repetition of the problem.

Horn 1's implied conclusion therefore does not follow. Under Horn 1:

- The criterion is the same in Phase 2 and Phase 3 (reasoning method quality). Accepted.
- Phase 3 applies this criterion cross-cluster. Accepted — this is by design.
- This "defeats ESHTR's design rationale." **Not accepted.** ESHTR's design rationale is to make cross-cluster comparison tractable by confining it to Phase 3, where it occurs in a constrained setting (champion-only population; explicit abstraction instruction; small k). Horn 1 describes Phase 3 accurately and is consistent with ESHTR.

The dilemma collapses because "Phase 3 = cross-cluster C1-C5 comparison" is the design's intended output, not a problem the design was meant to avoid. What ESHTR was constructed to avoid is uncontrolled, arbitrary cross-corpus pairings during the main ranking phase — which Phase 2 prevents by confining the bulk of comparison to within-cluster settings.

The remaining question — which the adversarial paper correctly identifies as empirical — is whether Phase 3's structured operationalization of cross-cluster C1-C5 comparison is materially more tractable (lower non-transitivity rate) than uncontrolled cross-cluster C1-C5 application. This is addressed in §4.5.

### 4.4 The Redundancy Objection Does Not Hold

The adversarial paper (§4) argues that if C1-C5 is necessarily domain-general, "the method can simply select the highest-aggregate-C1-C5 decision across all clusters" — making Phase 3 redundant.

Selecting the highest-aggregate-C1-C5 decision across all clusters is not a trivially available operation. It requires cross-cluster comparison of C1-C5 scores — exactly the operation that generates non-transitivity and for which ESHTR's hierarchical structure exists. There is no procedure that produces a global C1-C5 ranking without cross-cluster comparison; Phase 3 is that procedure, structured to reduce the non-transitivity it cannot eliminate. The necessary-implication claim (high C1-C5 → generalizable reasoning method) does not make Phase 3 redundant; it supplies the theoretical grounding for what Phase 3 is trying to identify. Phase 3 remains structurally necessary for the same reason that motivated the hierarchical design.

### 4.5 Why Phase 3's Operationalization Is More Tractable: The Mechanism

The adversarial paper correctly requires empirical evidence that Phase 3 comparisons under the explicit abstraction instruction exhibit lower non-transitivity than unconstrained cross-cluster C1-C5 comparison. A mechanism-level argument establishes the prior.

The Tversky (1977) feature-salience account, accepted by both the adversarial paper and `yesindeed/frame-stability-sph.md` as the correct mechanism for frame-instability-based non-transitivity, identifies what feature salience depends on: the comparison frame. In unconstrained cross-cluster C1-C5 evaluation, the comparison frame is implicit — set by the items themselves. The most salient discriminating features between a criminal sentencing decision and a tax injunction ruling are the domain-specific content features, because those differ most between the items, and they are highly present in the text LLM judges read.

The explicit abstraction instruction in Phase 3 ("abstract from subject-specific vocabulary to underlying argumentation quality," §3.3 ESHTR) functions as a frame-setting mechanism: it names the discrimination axis. Under Tversky's (1977) feature-salience account, this should reduce the salience of domain-specific content features by directing attention to the structural features the instruction specifies — reasoning coherence, argument engagement, non-formulaic application of principles. These are the domain-general features that C1-C5 is designed to target.

The instruction does not eliminate domain-specific feature salience (the decisions' texts still contain it), but it changes the comparison frame from implicit (dominated by the most content-distinctive features) to explicit (directed toward method-quality features). This is the mechanism by which "at a higher level of abstraction" does additional work: it specifies the suppression of domain-specific feature salience as the operationalization strategy, grounded in the same mechanism both sides have accepted. An explicit instruction and an implicit frame do not produce the same comparison, even when the underlying criterion is identical.

The adversarial paper's claim that the middle position is unstable — "if it changes what the LLM judge evaluates, the criterion is different; if it does not change what the judge evaluates, the design offers no advantage" — conflates criterion with operationalization. An instruction can change which features are most salient in the evaluation (the operationalization) without changing what is ultimately being evaluated (the criterion: reasoning method quality). This is a difference between what is assessed and how it is accessed. Instructions that change operationalization without changing criterion are standard in evaluation design.

Whether this theoretical prior is confirmed requires the empirical test the adversarial paper specifies: Phase 3 non-transitivity rates compared to Phase 2 cross-cluster non-transitivity rates under C1-C5, while checking that Phase 3 rankings correlate with Phase 2 cluster-quality rankings within each cluster. The mechanism argument supports the prediction; the test would confirm or disconfirm it.

The adversarial paper (§3.6) has accepted the mechanism argument as providing a "theoretical prior" for Phase 3 tractability, characterizing the prediction as "a mechanism-grounded conjecture that generates a falsifiable prediction... of the same logical type as the Semantic Proximity Hypothesis for Phase 2." This acceptance is significant: the adversarial paper is no longer arguing that Phase 3 tractability is improperly asserted without theoretical grounds, or that the position paper presents it as an architectural guarantee. The adversarial paper's remaining claim is that the mechanism argument does not establish "sufficiency." In the position paper context, no mechanism-grounded conjecture establishes sufficiency before the experiment — that is what experiments are for. Phase 2's Semantic Proximity Hypothesis is in the same position: the mechanism argues it should hold; the experiment confirms or disconfirms it. If the adversarial paper accepts SPH as a warranted prediction at the mechanism-grounded level while pressing the "not sufficient" objection specifically against Phase 3, the asymmetric treatment requires justification. That justification must show Phase 3's mechanism claim depends on an LLM behavior (instruction-modulated structural reading) that is structurally less warranted than Phase 2's mechanism claim (domain-specific criterion mapping from training). The adversarial §§3.7-3.8 attempt this. Whether the attempt succeeds depends on whether the representation/output distinction this defense establishes in §4.6 is adequate — and the calibration protocol in §4.6 specifies the test that would confirm or disconfirm this distinction's practical efficacy.

The adversarial paper (§4, updated) has now provided an explicit asymmetry justification. Phase 2's mechanism is characterized as additive: embedding clustering selects pairings in a context where LLMs' existing criterion-domain associations are uniformly relevant, producing within-cluster consistency without requiring new LLM behavior. This is "with the grain." Phase 3's mechanism is characterized as suppressive: the explicit abstraction instruction must redirect criterion application away from domain-specific feature salience that Phase 2 depends on, requiring the instruction-following layer to work against trained associations. This is "against the grain." The asymmetric evidential demand between Phase 2 and Phase 3 is then presented as justified by this structural difference in behavioral requirements.

Three aspects of this justification are contestable.

First, "suppressive" mischaracterizes what Phase 3 requires under Option (c). Suppression at the representation level would mean the instruction causes the LLM to not activate domain-specific representations when reading domain-specific legal text — an implausible claim that the adversarial paper does not make. What the adversarial paper argues is that suppression occurs at the feature-salience-to-output mapping level: the instruction must prevent domain-specific feature activations from determining what the output evaluates. Under Option (c), this is precisely not suppression: domain-specific textual features serve as evidence *for* structural reasoning properties at the output level, rather than as evaluation targets in themselves. A criminal law decision's proportionality analysis is read as evidence for ratio-isolation quality and argument-engagement depth — not suppressed, but redirected in what it is evidence for. Instruction-tuned LLMs perform this kind of evidential redirection as a primary trained capability: producing output consistent with explicit task specification on top of activated representations is what instruction-following trains for.

Second, the characterization of the LLM's grain is too narrow. The adversarial argument treats the LLM's grain as consisting of domain-specific criterion-domain associations acquired through legal text. But instruction-tuned LLMs have been trained across many domains on tasks involving structural reasoning evaluation: argument quality assessment, logical coherence, discourse entailment, and inferential validity recognition. The instruction "evaluate reasoning structure" activates these structural-evaluation capabilities, which are with the model's grain — they are trained behaviors present in the model's capability repertoire. What the Phase 3 instruction moves against is the domain-specific criterion application that Phase 2 leverages, which is a specific subset of the LLM's capabilities. Both phases select from the LLM's repertoire for specific purposes; neither is simply "with" or "against" the full grain. Phase 2 calls on the model's domain-specific legal evaluation capabilities; Phase 3 calls on the model's domain-agnostic structural-reasoning evaluation capabilities. The latter are not absent from or counter to the model's training.

Third, the additive/suppressive contrast overstates the structural difference. Phase 2 is not purely additive: the embedding clustering imposes a structural constraint on which of the LLM's criterion-domain associations are contextually relevant, and the protocol specifies what the LLM is asked to evaluate (C1-C5). Phase 3 is not purely suppressive: the explicit instruction selects from the LLM's available evaluation schemas and specifies what the output should report. Selection from a capability repertoire is not suppression of non-selected elements. What does remain valid in the asymmetry argument is that Phase 2 leverages a trained, automatic association while Phase 3 invokes instruction-mediated schema selection — and instruction-mediated selection may require more explicit calibration to achieve reliable results. But "different type of mechanism" does not entail "more demanding mechanism." Different requires different evidence; whether it requires asymmetrically more evidence is the empirical question the calibration protocol addresses.

### 4.6 Method/Content Inseparability Is Not Exhaustive

The adversarial paper's Options (a) and (b) for how LLM judges implement C1-C5 are not exhaustive. A third option:

**(c)** LLM judges evaluate the **structure** of reasoning using domain-specific material as evidence, without evaluating the domain-specific content itself as a criterion component. Identifying whether a passage constitutes load-bearing grounds for a decision (C1) requires recognizing logical dependency relationships, not mastery of the specific doctrine. A reader without Brazilian criminal sentencing expertise can assess whether a passage logically necessitates the conclusion or constitutes elaboration. The domain vocabulary is text to be read for structural properties, not a domain knowledge base to be evaluated against.

This is the mode in which comparative law scholars evaluate foreign decisions: they identify ratio structure, coherence of argument engagement, and departure from formula without evaluating whether the specific doctrine is correct. The Phase 3 instruction explicitly positions LLM judges in this mode: abstract from domain vocabulary to argumentation quality.

Option (c) is neither a domain-agnostic template (it reads domain-specific text carefully) nor domain-sensitive evaluation (it does not assess doctrinal correctness). The adversarial paper's dichotomy between these two options misses the third, which is what a careful implementation of Phase 3's rubric aims to produce.

The adversarial paper's failure condition — "If the practical implementation of 'contextual generalizability' in the LLM judge prompt fails to separate content-transferability from reasoning-quality-transferability... the conceptual argument is sound but the implementation does not capture it" — is correctly identified as a prompt-engineering concern. It is a concern, but not a demonstration that the separation is impossible. It is addressable by explicit instruction: evaluate the method of reasoning, not the domain-specific rules applied. This is a tractable calibration problem, not a structural impossibility.

The adversarial paper (§3.7, updated) grounds a further counter in the accepted mechanism. The domain-specific criterion acquisition that produces within-cluster LLM reliability — described in `yesindeed/frame-stability-sph.md` §3.3 as the mechanism by which LLMs evaluate criminal law decisions on criminal law criteria — creates the implementation gap Option (c) must bridge. The Phase 3 instruction to "abstract from subject-specific vocabulary" changes what the LLM claims to evaluate; it does not sever, in the LLM's weights, the associations between domain-specific textual features and the quality criteria those features activate. The mechanism that produces within-cluster reliability and the mechanism that Option (c) must override are, on this account, one and the same.

This counter identifies a genuine implementation challenge. What it does not establish is that representation-level feature activation necessarily flows through to output-level criterion application. These are separated in instruction-tuned LLMs by the instruction-following mechanism that is the defining property of such models. An instruction-following model may activate domain-specific representations when reading criminal sentencing text — recognizing sentencing framework vocabulary, proportionality doctrine, precedential structure — while producing output that reports on the structural properties the instruction specifies, because the instruction modulates the mapping from activated representations to generated output. Instruction-tuning optimizes precisely this mapping: producing outputs consistent with explicit task specification on top of whatever representations are activated by the input. The adversarial argument conflates representation-level feature activation with output-level criterion application as though no instruction-following layer intervenes.

Additionally, Option (c) does not require suppression of domain-specific feature activation. Under Option (c), domain-specific textual features are read as evidence for structural properties — ratio identification, argument engagement quality, precedent application rigour — not as evaluation targets in themselves. The domain-specific vocabulary is the material through which structural properties are assessed, not an obstacle to their assessment. Readers in structural-reading mode activate domain-specific representations to identify the logical dependencies and argument structures that constitute the structural evidence. This is not suppression; it is redirection of what the activated representations serve as evidence for. The adversarial paper treats domain-specific activation and structural-reading mode as incompatible; they are complementary under Option (c).

The calibration gap is real: the current ESHTR calibration protocol (§5.4) does not include a Phase 3-specific test for whether LLM outputs under the abstraction instruction reflect structural rather than domain-specific criterion application. The test is implementable within the existing framework and can be made concrete.

Using decisions from the within-cluster calibration block (§5.4 of ESHTR), which establishes known quality rankings within each cluster, construct a set of **cross-cluster calibration pairs**: decisions from different domain clusters, matched approximately on quality level (both high-quality or both lower-quality, as established by their within-cluster calibration rankings). For each cross-cluster pair, run two evaluation conditions with the same LLM panel:

*Phase 2 condition:* Standard Phase 2 domain-specific rubric (C1-C5, no cross-domain abstraction instruction). Collect per-criterion scores for both decisions and pairwise preference judgments.

*Phase 3 condition:* Phase 3 abstraction instruction ("abstract from subject-specific vocabulary to underlying argumentation quality") applied to the same pair. Collect per-criterion scores and pairwise preference judgments.

Three measurements distinguish structural-reading mode from domain-criterion activation with leakage:

**(1) Cross-cluster κ shift.** Compare inter-judge agreement (Fleiss' κ) across the LLM panel for cross-cluster pairs under Phase 3 versus Phase 2 conditions. If the instruction redirects evaluation toward structural method properties that generate more consistent cross-judge assessments when made explicit, κ should be higher under Phase 3 conditions. *Falsified if:* κ_Phase3\_cross ≤ κ_Phase2\_cross across the calibration set.

**(2) Criterion-profile cross-domain variance.** For each cross-cluster pair, compare the variance of per-criterion score profiles across the two domain decisions under Phase 2 versus Phase 3 conditions. Domain-specific criterion activation (the adversarial claim) drives larger score gaps between decisions from different domains because domain-appropriate criteria differ across domains and penalize the out-of-domain decision. If Phase 3 instructions suppress this effect, per-criterion score variance across the two decisions should decrease under Phase 3 conditions relative to Phase 2 conditions, as both decisions are assessed on the same structural-method dimension. *Falsified if:* criterion-profile variance across domain decisions is not reduced under Phase 3 conditions.

**(3) Quality-discrimination accuracy on mixed-quality pairs.** Construct cross-cluster pairs where one decision ranks higher within its cluster than the other (quality-discrepant pairs). Under Phase 3 instructions, the higher-quality decision should win more often than under Phase 2 instructions, because Phase 3 instructions calibrate the comparison to structural-method quality rather than domain-specific performance — enabling the cross-domain quality distinction that Phase 2 instructions cannot reliably produce. *Falsified if:* winner-accuracy for quality-discrepant cross-cluster pairs is not higher under Phase 3 instructions than Phase 2 instructions applied to the same pairs.

The third measurement directly addresses the adversarial paper's "not sufficient" concern: quality-discrimination accuracy across domains is a direct measure of whether the structural-reading mode produces reliable cross-domain quality distinctions, not only improved inter-judge agreement. If Phase 3 instructions produce higher accuracy on quality-discrepant cross-cluster pairs, the instruction demonstrates the behavioral effect that the adversarial paper characterizes as an aspiration.

To satisfy the adversarial paper's surrender condition §6(3) — which requires evidence that non-transitivity reduction in Phase 3 is attributable in part to the instruction's frame-setting function, not entirely to champion selection — the calibration set should include **non-champion cross-cluster pairs** (decisions that competed but did not win within their clusters) alongside champion pairs. If Phase 3 instructions improve cross-cluster κ on non-champion pairs, the instruction's independent contribution is demonstrated independently of champion-selection effects. *Falsified if:* Phase 3 instruction conditions show no κ improvement over Phase 2 instruction conditions on non-champion cross-cluster pairs, which would indicate that whatever Phase 3 advantage exists is attributable entirely to the champion population's properties rather than the instruction's frame-setting function.

This protocol is implementable within the existing ESHTR evaluation framework: it reuses existing calibration decisions (augmented with cross-cluster pairings), the existing LLM panel infrastructure, and existing metrics (Fleiss' κ, per-criterion scores). The within-cluster quality rankings required to construct matched cross-cluster pairs are produced by the Phase 2 calibration block already in the protocol. The cross-cluster calibration component is an extension of the existing Phase 2 calibration block, applied to cross-domain pairs with an additional instruction condition — no new infrastructure is required.

The adversarial paper raises two structural limits on what this protocol can establish: a circular quality ground truth and a champion-scale gap.

The circular quality ground truth concern holds that measurement 3 (quality-discrimination accuracy) uses within-cluster Phase 2 rankings to identify quality-discrepant pairs, creating a closed loop if Phase 2 rankings are themselves biased by criterion-switching. For the circularity to be fatal, Phase 3 instructions would have to agree with Phase 2 quality orderings specifically by replicating Phase 2's criterion-activation patterns across domain boundaries. If Phase 3 instructions successfully redirect evaluation to structural properties (Option c), Phase 3 and Phase 2 agree for different reasons, through different mechanisms. Measurement 2 (criterion-profile cross-domain variance) directly tests whether this is the case: if Phase 3 agrees with Phase 2's quality orderings while producing systematically reduced criterion-profile variance across domain decisions, the agreement reflects structural evaluation operating consistently across domains — not criterion-activation replication from Phase 2. The circularity concern predicts that Phase 3 agreement is accompanied by unchanged criterion profiles (no variance reduction); the structural-reading defense predicts agreement accompanied by reduced variance. These predictions are distinguishable in the same calibration data, using measurements already specified in the protocol. If measurement 2 shows no variance reduction while measurement 3 shows Phase 3 agreement with Phase 2, the circular quality ground truth concern is confirmed as active; if measurement 2 shows reduced variance alongside measurement 3 agreement, the circularity concern is disconfirmed within the calibration.

Additionally, Phase 2 biases from criterion-switching are pair-context-dependent rather than item-specific: an item wins Phase 2 because a certain criterion dominated its specific within-cluster pairings, not because the item carries a detectable marker that Phase 3 cross-cluster pairings would inherit. For Phase 3 to reproduce Phase 2 biases on entirely different cross-cluster pairings, it would need to independently produce the same biased criterion-activation — which would be distinguishable by measurement 2's criterion-profile analysis.

The adversarial surrender condition extension (a) — independent quality ground truth via expert human judgments rather than Phase 2 rankings — is a valid strengthening of measurement 3. Including it decouples the quality-discrimination test from Phase 2 biases entirely, removing the circularity at its source. This is the correct protocol response to the circular quality ground truth concern, not a reason to abandon measurement 3.

The champion-scale gap concern holds that non-champion calibration does not proxy for champion-scale instruction effects, because in champion decisions method and domain content are most tightly integrated (adversarial §3.8), making structural evaluation hardest at the champion level. The direction of the implied difficulty should be examined.

Tight integration of reasoning method and domain content in champion decisions means structural properties are more fully expressed in the decision's text, not less accessible to evaluation. A champion decision's thoroughgoing argument engagement, careful ratio isolation, and complete logical structuring of the dispositif provides a richer evidentiary base for structural evaluation than a non-champion decision with partial or deficient structural reasoning. Under Option (c) — reading domain-specific text as evidence for structural reasoning properties — the richer the structural content in the text, the more evidence is available to the evaluator. Champion-scale instruction effects may therefore be stronger than non-champion effects, not weaker: the structural signal is clearest when structural properties are most fully realized. Non-champion calibration then provides a lower-bound estimate of the instruction effect, not an upper-bound proxy. The adversarial surrender condition extension (b) — including actual champion decisions in the instruction-independence test — is a valid direct measurement of the instruction effect in the primary comparison population. The supportive argument is that champion results would be at least as favorable as non-champion results; the test would confirm or disconfirm this.

The adversarial surrender condition extension (c) — Phase 3 cross-cluster κ must approach Phase 2 within-cluster κ, not merely exceed Phase 2 cross-cluster κ — is the correct threshold specification for "sufficient attenuation." Phase 3's structural controls aim to produce cross-cluster comparisons of reliability comparable to Phase 2's within-cluster comparisons. Within-cluster κ, already measured in Phase 2 calibration, is the appropriate benchmark. Exceeding the cross-cluster baseline for Phase 2 without instruction is necessary but insufficient; approaching the within-cluster reliability level is the meaningful evidential target. This threshold is testable using measurements already available in the calibration protocol and the Phase 2 calibration block.

The adversarial paper identifies a variance-source confound in measurement 2 that the preceding protocol does not address. Within-pair criterion-profile variance under Phase 2 cross-cluster conditions arises from two sources: (i) domain-specific criterion mapping that differentially weights domain-appropriate quality dimensions, and (ii) pair-specific quality-dimension-profile differences between the two specific decisions, which activate different dominant criteria independently of domain label. Both sources produce within-pair criterion-score asymmetry; measurement 2 as specified measures their sum. When Phase 3 instructions suppress source (i), source (ii) becomes relatively more prominent in the remaining variance. If source (ii) is substantial — which the pair-specificity mechanism makes plausible for high-quality champion comparisons — net variance reduction under Phase 3 may be moderate even when structural-reading mode is fully functioning. The stated falsification criterion — criterion-profile variance not reduced under Phase 3 — correctly identifies zero reduction as consistent with circularity, but moderate reduction is consistent with both full structural-reading mode (source i suppressed; source ii now dominant) and partial structural-reading mode (source i incompletely suppressed). The measurement as specified cannot arbitrate the intermediate case.

The adversarial surrender condition extension (d) — variance-source decomposition — is accepted. Profile-matched cross-cluster pairs and pre-specified hypothesis patterns are both incorporated in the calibration protocol.

**Profile-matched pairs.** The calibration set should include a subset of profile-matched cross-cluster pairs: pairs where the two domain decisions have similar per-criterion score profiles within their respective clusters, as established by Phase 2 per-criterion calibration scores. For such pairs, within-pair criterion-profile differences under Phase 2 conditions are attributable primarily to source (i), because source (ii) is controlled by selection. Phase 3 instructions' suppression of source (i) then produces a clean diagnostic for the matched-pair subset: large criterion-profile variance reduction confirms structural-reading mode; zero reduction confirms circularity. The per-criterion quality profiles required for matching are already available from Phase 2 calibration and do not require additional data collection.

**Pre-specified hypothesis patterns.** The competing hypotheses generate distinguishable result signatures across the three measurements and across matched versus unmatched pairs, specified here prior to data collection:

*Structural-reading mode* (instruction successfully suppresses domain-specific criterion mapping): M1 substantially improved over Phase 2 cross-cluster κ, approaching Phase 2 within-cluster κ; M2 moderate for unmatched pairs (source ii partially compensates for suppressed source i), large for profile-matched pairs (source ii controlled; remaining within-pair variance attributable to residual source-i influence); M3 substantially higher than Phase 2 cross-cluster accuracy.

*Circularity* (Phase 3 reproduces Phase 2 domain-criterion activation patterns): M1 not improved over Phase 2 cross-cluster κ; M2 no reduction for either unmatched or profile-matched pairs; M3 not consistently better than Phase 2 cross-cluster accuracy.

*Partial structural-reading mode* (instruction suppresses source i incompletely): M1 moderately improved but not approaching Phase 2 within-cluster κ; M2 small to moderate reduction for unmatched pairs, moderate but not large for profile-matched pairs; M3 moderately improved.

Source-ii dominance — the case where the instruction fully suppresses source (i) but source (ii) is large, producing moderate unmatched-pair M2 reduction — is detectable through the unmatched/matched M2 contrast rather than as a separate failure hypothesis. When unmatched pairs yield only moderate M2 reduction while profile-matched pairs yield large M2 reduction, source (ii) was substantial in the unmatched set and the instruction was suppressing source (i) fully. This pattern is the signature of structural-reading mode in a corpus where quality-dimension-profile differences are pronounced within cross-domain pairs. It is compatible with structural-reading mode and does not constitute a failure condition.

The matched-pair M2 result is the key diagnostic: it separates structural-reading mode from circularity regardless of source-ii magnitude in the unmatched set. The joint pre-specified signatures across M1, M2 (both unmatched and matched), and M3 convert the three-measurement calibration from a descriptively richer observation into a discriminating test under a pre-specified interpretive framework.

**On the input-protocol extension requirement.** The adversarial paper correctly identifies an input-specification gap: ESHTR §5.4 specifies decision texts as evaluator input; party submissions are not currently included. This gap is accepted. The adversarial framing that this constitutes a non-trivial architectural extension is contested. Brazilian court proceedings operate under Lei nº 11.419/2006 (processo eletrônico); party submissions are publicly accessible components of the digitized case record. For appellate decisions — the primary ESHTR corpus — the relevant party brief is typically one document: the appellant's main brief (razões de apelação, razões de recurso especial) against which the decision's C2 coverage is measured. Providing this document alongside the decision text changes the evaluator's input package, not the panel architecture, rubric, or Bradley-Terry aggregation. An input-specification extension expands the information available to an existing mechanism; it does not change the mechanism. This is architecturally less demanding than a calibration redesign, not more. Practical implementation specifies which party submissions constitute the input: for appellate decisions, the opposing party's main brief; for first-instance decisions, the relevant petição inicial or contestação. SC6(a) Type 2 calibration examples should be constructed using (decision text + party brief) input packages, ensuring that coverage-tracking trained under the complete input specification is what the evaluation applies at ranking time. The coverage-completeness ranking signal survives the input-protocol objection under this specification.

The adversarial round 7 presses that providing the party brief introduces a further processing step: identifying which brief arguments cross the materiality threshold of art. 489, §1º, IV — 'capazes de, em tese, infirmar a conclusão.' The elaborate-quality correlation in party briefs (more elaborate arguments more prominently featured, more readily treated as material) reintroduces the C2-adjacent elaboration challenge at the input level. SC6(a) calibration must therefore demonstrate correct materiality identification from raw brief material, not only correct coverage ranking once a material argument set is given.

Two responses. First, the materiality-identification challenge exists in any C2 evaluation, including the current design before the input extension: even from the decision text alone, the LLM must form a view about which absent arguments would have been material to the conclusion. The party brief makes the evidence for materiality identification explicit rather than requiring it to be inferred from context. The elaborate-quality correlation in brief arguments is not a new confound; it is the same structural confound the C2 debate has analyzed throughout, now visible in the evaluator's input rather than implicit in inferences about absent arguments.

Second, SC6(b-1) anti-naturalistic calibration pairs already test the full pipeline including materiality identification. In anti-naturalistic pairs, the decision with complete coverage of material arguments is shorter than the decision with selective coverage. An LLM that identifies material arguments by tracking brief elaboration would prefer the selective-and-elaborate decision: selective engagement with the most elaborated brief arguments produces a longer decision on those points, while complete coverage of all material arguments (including less elaborate ones) produces a shorter decision. An LLM that correctly identifies material arguments regardless of their elaboration level correctly prefers the complete-coverage decision despite its brevity. Anti-naturalistic SC6(b-1) pairs therefore already test the full pipeline: an elaborate-biased brief reader fails them, and passing them demonstrates that coverage tracking — which requires correctly identifying material arguments from brief context — is not driven by brief elaboration. The adversarial demand that SC6(a) calibration test materiality identification from raw brief material is addressed by this design structure.

The adversarial round 8 counter to the SC6(b-1) pipeline claim is accepted at the structural level. SC6(b-1) calibration pairs are constructed with annotators who know the material argument set and build the pair around it: the LLM's downstream coverage-ranking task receives a pair where the material set is embedded in the construction logic — implicit in which arguments appear and how the decisions are constructed — but never explicitly labeled for the LLM. An LLM could learn from such pairs the general preference "rank by coverage breadth rather than elaboration depth," which would also manifest correct materiality-identification behavior in realistic conditions. Alternatively, it could learn the narrower heuristic "prefer the shorter decision when coverage and length diverge" — which passes SC6(b-1) without requiring general materiality-identification from raw brief content. SC6(b-1)'s design cannot distinguish between these two outcomes.

A supplementary calibration test is required: **SC6(b-1)-ID** pairs, in which the LLM receives a raw party brief with no pre-specified material set and must identify which arguments satisfy art. 489, §1º, IV's threshold — capable, in principle, of affecting the dispositif if accepted — before any coverage ranking. Annotators independently determine the material argument set from the same brief, providing the reference answer. Inter-rater agreement between LLM identification and annotator judgment on the material set is the validation metric.

The materiality threshold under art. 489, §1º, IV is evaluable at the argument-conclusion level without full adversarial record analysis. An argument is material if accepting its conclusion would require a different dispositif. This determination requires reading: (a) what the argument would establish — its premise and conclusion; and (b) what dispositif was reached — the court's holding. An argument whose conclusion contradicts the holding (if accepted, the outcome would change) is material; an argument whose conclusion is consistent with the holding even if accepted is not material. This is an outcome-dependency assessment — premise → dispositif-impact — not a doctrinal-strength assessment. It does not require evaluating whether the argument is legally correct or how demanding the adversarial record was; it requires reading the structural relationship between what the argument claims and what the court decided. SC6(b-1)-ID calibration examples demonstrating clearly-material, clearly-immaterial, and borderline cases from Brazilian appellate brief practice provide the reference frame for LLM materiality identification. *Falsified if:* LLMs calibrated on SC6(b-1)-ID pairs fail to achieve reliable agreement with annotator material-set identification at rates indicating reliable material-argument discrimination from raw brief content.

**On the SC6(b) naturalistic generalization requirement.** The adversarial paper correctly requires that coverage-completeness tracking trained on anti-naturalistic Type 2 pairs (where the complete-but-thin decision is shorter) be validated against naturalistic pairs where coverage and total text volume co-vary positively. SC6(b) accordingly requires two sub-tests. SC6(b-1) is the existing design: anti-naturalistic pairs where the complete decision is shorter — tests coverage tracking when text volume would predict the wrong winner. SC6(b-2) is the required extension: naturalistic cross-coverage pairs where the complete-and-elaborate decision is also longer than the selective-and-elaborate one — tests whether the same judges correctly rank by coverage when text volume and coverage align in the standard positive direction. SC6(b-2) diagnoses whether training on SC6(b-1) installed a coverage-tracking principle (which produces correct rankings in both orientations) or a brevity heuristic (which produces correct rankings only in the reversed-length case and would fail on naturalistic complete-and-longer decisions). *Falsified:* if judges trained on SC6(b-1) fail to consistently identify the longer-and-more-complete decision as higher-ranking in SC6(b-2) pairs, naturalistic generalization is not demonstrated and anti-naturalistic calibration has not installed a general coverage-tracking principle.

The adversarial round 7 accepts SC6(b-2) as appropriately specified. This sub-test is a convergence point: the adversarial camp's SC6(b-2) acceptance forecloses a separate naturalistic-generalization objection and focuses the remaining SC6(b) dispute on the materiality-identification step addressed above.

**On the SC6(3) cross-elaboration extension.** The adversarial paper correctly identifies that SC6(3)'s cross-domain specification does not separately address whether Phase 3 judges rank champions independently of elaboration richness differences arising from adversarial record heterogeneity. Under prong 2 of the C2 disjunction, between-champion elaboration differences in Phase 3 arise from cluster-level adversarial record differences rather than domain-vocabulary differences; the abstraction instruction targets the latter, not the former. SC6(3) extended to govern prong 2 requires a cross-elaboration test alongside its cross-domain test.

A cross-elaboration calibration pair is constructed as follows: two champions matched on doctrinal area (minimizing domain-vocabulary differences), where one has higher total elaboration from a higher-adversarial-record cluster and the other demonstrates higher reasoning-operation quality — assessed by independent expert judgment — from a lower-adversarial-record cluster with correspondingly less elaborate text. Under the Phase 3 abstraction instruction, "argumentation quality" assessed as reasoning-operation quality (ratio isolation, argument engagement precision, logical dispositif structuring) is in principle separable from elaboration level generated by adversarial record richness. A decision that applies systematic ratio isolation and coherent proportionality analysis to undemanding arguments can demonstrate higher reasoning-operation quality than a more elaborate decision that engages demanding arguments with routine structural operations. Whether LLM judges under the Phase 3 instruction correctly separate these properties is the empirical question the cross-elaboration test addresses. The independent expert quality assessment required for cross-elaboration pair construction shares infrastructure with SC6(3) extension (a)'s independent quality ground truth requirement: both require expert assessment of reasoning-operation quality on cross-cluster decisions, independent of text volume and Phase 2 calibration rankings. The cross-elaboration extension adds the adversarial-record-differentiation requirement to the pair selection protocol. *Falsified:* if higher-elaboration champions consistently win quality-discrepant cross-elaboration pairs (quality-discrepancy established by categorical composite scoring as specified below) at rates substantially above chance, the Phase 3 abstraction instruction has not separated argumentation quality from adversarial-record-driven elaboration level, and the cross-elaboration concern under prong 2 remains active.

The adversarial round 7 identifies a design gap in the expert assessment protocol. The specification that expert assessment be 'independent of elaboration level generated by adversarial record richness' presupposes the separability the test is designed to establish. Both available assessment standards reintroduce the adversarial record: the complexity-relative standard (quality assessed relative to adversarial record difficulty) requires calibrating expert quality judgments for cluster-level adversarial difficulty — an operationalization the protocol had not specified, without which experts cannot distinguish genuine quality differences from difficulty-relative performance; the universal-scale standard (quality assessed on a single dimension regardless of context) tracks elaboration directly, since elaboration correlates with quality on any thoroughness-based scale.

The resolution is to specify the expert assessment on categorical and ordinal measures whose reference answers are established from the decision text and party brief independently of adversarial record richness:

**Ratio identification precision** (targeting C1): Did the decision correctly identify the determining grounds of the cited precedent — the grounds necessary for the precedent's holding? Scored ordinally: no isolation (0); precedent identified but determining grounds mischaracterized (1); correct isolation (2). The reference answer is the content of the cited precedent and the decision's characterization of it; it does not depend on the adversarial record's difficulty. A brief decision and an elaborate decision that both correctly identify the same ratio score identically at 2.

**Material argument engagement completeness** (targeting C2): Using the party brief as reference, what proportion of the arguments 'capazes de, em tese, infirmar a conclusão' did the decision address? Scored as a proportion in [0,1]: (material arguments engaged) / (material arguments identified in the brief by expert assessors). Materiality is determined from brief content by expert assessors, not from elaboration level. A decision engaging 4 of 4 material arguments in two paragraphs scores 1.0; a decision engaging 4 of 6 material arguments in twelve paragraphs scores 0.67. Elaboration affects neither numerator nor denominator.

**Logical entailment validity of dispositif** (targeting C5): Are the stated grounds logically sufficient for the dispositif? Binary (0 or 1). Does not depend on adversarial record richness.

These three measures produce reference answers checkable by expert assessors who need not know the adversarial record richness of the cluster the decision came from. Inter-rater reliability is determinable from standard statistics — ordinal κ for ratio identification precision, intraclass correlation for material argument engagement completeness, Cohen's κ for logical entailment validity — without calibrating assessors for adversarial difficulty. The complexity-relative standard problem dissolves: the question is not 'how well given difficulty?' but 'did the decision correctly perform the required reasoning operation?' The universal-scale problem dissolves: an elaborate and a brief decision that both score at the categorical ceiling — correctly identifying the ratio, engaging all material arguments, producing a valid dispositif — are assigned the same quality level, because the ceiling reflects correct performance, not elaboration depth.

Cross-elaboration pairs are constructed from champions matched on doctrinal area and differentiated on adversarial record richness, with expert categorical composite scores assigned before any LLM evaluation runs. A quality-discrepant pair is one where the lower-elaboration (lower-adversarial-record) champion scores higher on the categorical composite than the higher-elaboration (higher-adversarial-record) champion. Under the Phase 3 abstraction instruction, LLM judges should prefer the lower-elaboration champion in quality-discrepant cross-elaboration pairs. The falsification criterion above applies to these pairs: if higher-elaboration champions win quality-discrepant pairs at rates substantially above chance, the abstraction instruction has not separated elaboration from reasoning-operation quality as identified by the categorical composite.

The adversarial round 8 advances two further arguments against the categorical composite as specified in round 8: (1) reference-answer construction for C1 and C2 requires adversarial-record-sensitive analysis, making differential inter-rater reliability across adversarial record arms — not global reliability — the operative diagnostic; (2) the three-measure composite omits C3 and C4, leaving the falsification criterion ambiguous for quality differences on unmeasured dimensions.

**On differential inter-rater reliability.** The adversarial argument is accepted as the correct diagnostic framing. Arm-specific inter-rater reliability for reference-answer construction must be reported separately for high-adversarial-record and low-adversarial-record champion sets, not pooled. Systematic agreement deficits for high-adversarial-record reference answers would confirm that the reference-answer construction step is adversarial-record-sensitive; comparable arm-specific reliability would confirm the record-neutral claim.

For C1 (ratio identification precision), the prediction is that arm-specific reliability is comparable. The reference answer for C1 specifies which grounds the court identified as determinantes in its decision — not which grounds a reasonable court should have found determinante as a matter of law. The decision text fixes the court's own presentation of its ratio; annotators reading the decision identify what the court presented as determinante. A high-adversarial-record case may have more candidate grounds explicitly discussed (because the parties raised more challenges), but the court's own identification of which grounds it found determinante is in the text. The adversarial argument conflates pre-decision difficulty (how contested was it before the court to determine which grounds were determinante?) with post-decision text-reading difficulty (how contested is it for annotators to identify what the court determined?). The court's decision resolves the pre-decision contest; annotators read the resolution. This is a post-decision text-reading task, not an independent ratio-determination task. Whether annotators reliably agree on reading the court's own ratio characterization is an empirical question, and the prediction is that they will achieve comparable reliability across adversarial record arms because the task is the same regardless of how contested the underlying legal question was.

For C2 (material argument engagement completeness), the differential reliability diagnostic is accepted without a stability prediction comparable to C1. The C2 reference answer requires identifying the material argument list from the brief — the same outcome-dependency assessment the SC6(b-1)-ID protocol addresses. Whether this identification is comparably reliable across adversarial record arms depends on whether the materiality determination from brief content is adversarial-record-sensitive in ways the outcome-dependency standard does not control. Arm-specific C2 inter-rater reliability in the cross-elaboration protocol provides the direct evidence.

**On the five-dimension composite.** The three-measure composite's omission of C3 and C4 is accepted as making the falsification criterion ambiguous. The adversarial round 8 proposes specific operationalizations — C4 through a record-neutral precedent-characterization comparison; C3 through a formulaic-language marker count — and both are adopted.

**C4 (precedent application precision):** Does the decision correctly characterize the operative grounds of the cited precedent in its application step? Scored ordinally: (0) the decision's characterization of the cited precedent's grounds does not match the cited precedent text as applied — misquotation, misparaphrase, or structural mischaracterization of what the precedent established; (1) the characterization is accurate but incomplete — some operative grounds are omitted in the application step; (2) correct and complete characterization of the operative grounds in application. The reference answer is the cited precedent's own text, compared cross-textually to the decision's characterization. This is a cross-text comparison task: whether the decision's characterization matches the precedent's text is assessable from those two documents regardless of how contested the underlying question was before the court. A decision from a high-adversarial-record cluster that correctly characterizes the contested precedent's grounds scores 2; one from a low-adversarial-record cluster that mischaracterizes an uncontested precedent scores 0. The scoring does not depend on adversarial record richness.

**C3 (boilerplate avoidance):** Does the decision's reasoning section employ formulaic language that appears near-verbatim across multiple decisions in the cluster? Scored ordinally: (0) three or more phrases of five or more consecutive words appear verbatim in the reasoning section of two or more other decisions in the cluster; (1) one or two such cross-decision recurring phrases; (2) no cross-decision verbatim phrases in reasoning-section context. The reference answer is derived from within-cluster frequency analysis of recurring phrases across the decision corpus texts — a cross-document stylometric task that requires no adversarial record analysis. High-volume processing pressure (C3's primary driver) is detectable from text repetition patterns across the cluster's decisions, independent of what arguments any individual case raised.

The five-dimension composite (C1 ratio identification precision + C2 material argument engagement completeness + C3 boilerplate avoidance + C4 precedent application precision + C5 logical entailment validity) provides reference answers constructable from: C1 — cited precedent text + decision text; C2 — party brief + decision text; C3 — cluster corpus texts; C4 — cited precedent text + decision text; C5 — decision text alone. None requires adversarial record analysis beyond reading the relevant texts. Quality-discrepant cross-elaboration pairs for the SC6(3) cross-elaboration test are identified from the five-dimension composite, assigned before any LLM evaluation runs. The categorical composite protocol additionally reports arm-specific inter-rater reliability separately for C1 and C2 reference-answer construction, with high-adversarial-record and low-adversarial-record arms compared directly. *Falsified if:* arm-specific inter-rater reliability for C1 or C2 reference-answer construction is systematically lower for high-adversarial-record champions than for matched low-adversarial-record champions at a magnitude indicating adversarial-record-sensitive reference-answer construction; or if the five-dimension composite itself fails to achieve reliable expert agreement on C3 or C4 measures (indicating operationalization requires further specification).

### 4.7 Champion Legitimacy Is Supported by Aggregation Robustness

The adversarial paper's champion-circularity concern (§3.7) depends on within-cluster criterion-switching being systematic and directional — consistently advantaging specific items over others in criterion activation. If within-cluster criterion-switching is non-systematic, Bradley-Terry aggregation over the full distribution of pairings produces a valid quality signal even with some criterion-switching in individual pairings.

As analyzed in `yesindeed/frame-stability-sph.md` (§3.5): within-cluster criterion-switching that is non-systematic contributes variance to individual pairings without introducing directional bias against any specific item into the aggregate score. The champion is the decision that wins most pairings across the distribution of criteria activated within the cluster — a signal that averages across criterion activation rather than being determined by which criterion any single pairing happens to activate. This is the structural advantage of Bradley-Terry aggregation over individual pairings.

The circularity concern holds only if within-cluster criterion-switching is systematically correlated with specific item identities — some items consistently trigger the "losing" criterion in their pairings. Prediction 4 from `yesindeed/frame-stability-sph.md` (§3.4) addresses this: within-cluster non-transitive cycles should be unstructured relative to quality-dimension asymmetry profiles if switching is non-systematic; if cycles concentrate at asymmetry boundaries (items with extreme profiles generating cycles more frequently), switching is systematic. Until that structural test indicates directional within-cluster switching, the circularity concern is a possibility rather than an established failure mode, and Bradley-Terry champions are a reasonable quality signal.

The adversarial paper (§3.8) presents a second argument concerning the champion population, distinct from the circularity concern. Even granting that Phase 2 champions are genuine quality peaks, the structural controls do not stabilize Phase 3 because sophisticated domain-integrated reasoning has domain-general and domain-specific method most tightly intertwined. Among champion decisions — selected precisely for the excellence with which they integrate method and content — the method/content separation that Phase 3's structural-reading mode requires is most difficult to achieve. Determining which champion's method is "more transferable," the adversarial paper argues, requires cross-domain bridging that generates non-transitivity precisely among the most sophisticated and domain-integrated examples of legal reasoning.

The argument's premise is correct: in high-quality champion decisions, domain-general reasoning method is expressed through domain-specific doctrinal material in a fully realized way. The implied conclusion — that this intertwining makes structural evaluation harder — does not follow.

Tight integration of method and content makes structural properties more legible in the decision's text, not less. A champion decision that integrates proportionality analysis, ratio isolation, and precedential reasoning at a high level produces a text in which the logical dependencies between claims, the thoroughness of argument engagement, and the rigour of precedent application are extensively exhibited across multiple paragraphs. Structural properties are most visible in decisions where those properties are most fully exercised. By contrast, structural deficiencies are harder to identify in formulaic decisions, because formulaic language conceals whether genuine reasoning occurred. In champion decisions with tight method/content integration, the structural operations that Phase 3 evaluates are demonstrated through the richness of the domain-specific material — providing more, not less, evidence for Option (c)'s structural-reading evaluation.

The "cross-domain bridging that generates non-transitivity" claim also requires examination. What Phase 3 compares is the structural quality of reasoning operations — ratio isolation, argument engagement quality, precedent application — expressed through different domain-specific vocabularies. These operations are domain-general in form (§4.1). The comparison does not ask which champion's domain-specific doctrinal reasoning is superior; it asks which champion's structural reasoning operations are of higher quality as evidenced by domain-specific text. Under the Tversky (1977) feature-salience account, criterion switches in cross-cluster comparisons arise when domain-specific features are the most discriminating dimensions between the compared items, activating different criterion sets across pairings. When the Phase 3 instruction successfully redirects comparison to structural quality — the mechanism-level argument in §4.5 — the most discriminating features between champion decisions are properties of the structural operations they demonstrate, not their domain vocabularies. Criterion-switching at the structural-quality level requires that champions differ more on structural operational quality than on domain-specific content features, which is a less severe cross-cluster switching problem than the unconstrained version. Whether the instruction successfully effects this redirection is, again, the empirical question surrender condition §6(3) addresses.

### 4.8 Non-Transitivity Recurrence Is Attenuated

Phase 3's champion-only population, explicit abstraction instruction, and small k jointly attenuate the criterion-switching mechanism relative to arbitrary cross-cluster comparison. The adversarial paper concedes this is attenuation, not elimination. The mechanism-level argument in §4.5 adds: the attenuation is not ad hoc — it operates through a specific causal pathway (instruction-modulated feature salience, per the Tversky 1977 account) that both sides have accepted as the mechanism for frame-instability-based non-transitivity.

Whether the attenuation is sufficient is the empirical question. The adversarial paper's updated surrender condition §6(3) — evidence that Phase 3 non-transitivity rates are substantially lower than Phase 2 cross-cluster non-transitivity rates while Phase 3 rankings correlate with Phase 2 quality rankings — is the right test. The defense does not claim to provide that evidence; it provides the theoretical basis for why the prediction should be confirmed, and identifies the test that would confirm or disconfirm it.

This paper does **not** address:

- The Phase 2 operationalization attack (Sections 3.1–3.4 of the adversarial paper) — addressed in `yesindeed/frame-stability-sph.md`.

## 6. Conditions Under Which This Defense Would Fail

- If empirical measurements show Phase 3 non-transitivity rates ≥ Phase 2 cross-cluster non-transitivity rates under C1-C5 (the adversarial paper's updated surrender condition §6(3)), the attenuation argument and the operationalization-tractability claim both fail.
- If Phase 3 rankings are poorly correlated with Phase 2 quality rankings within each cluster — i.e., moderate C1-C5 scorers systematically beat cluster champions under contextual generalizability — the claim that both phases measure the same underlying property is empirically disconfirmed.
- If Prediction 4 from `yesindeed/frame-stability-sph.md` (§3.4) confirms systematic, directional within-cluster criterion-switching, the champion-legitimacy argument fails: Phase 2 champions may not be valid quality proxies, and the champion-population advantage of Phase 3 dissolves.
- If the practical implementation of Phase 3's rubric fails to separate reasoning-structure evaluation from domain-content evaluation — if LLM judges under the abstraction instruction consistently penalize domain-specific vocabulary rather than abstracting to structural quality — Option (c) in §4.6 is not realized, and the method/content inseparability concern becomes a binding implementation failure.
- If the Phase 3-specific calibration protocol in §4.6 shows that per-criterion score profiles under the abstraction instruction do not shift relative to Phase 2 domain-specific profiles — the same criterion emphasis patterns appear regardless of the instruction — the representation/output-mapping distinction in §4.6 fails to hold for the LLM panel used, and the instruction-following defense of Option (c) is empirically disconfirmed. Specifically: if (a) cross-cluster κ under Phase 3 instructions does not approach Phase 2 within-cluster κ (not merely exceed Phase 2 cross-cluster κ, which is the minimum bar), (b) criterion-profile variance in profile-matched cross-cluster pairs does not decrease under Phase 3 instructions (the definitive diagnostic for the variance-source confound; §4.6), and (c) winner-accuracy for quality-discrepant cross-cluster pairs is not higher under Phase 3 instructions — all three conditions met — Option (c) is not the LLM's achieved behavior under the Phase 3 rubric. If measurement 3 (quality-discrimination accuracy) is validated using only Phase 2 rankings without independent quality ground truth, and measurement 2 simultaneously shows no criterion-profile variance reduction, the circular quality ground truth concern is active: Phase 3 may be agreeing with Phase 2 through criterion replication rather than structural evaluation, and the protocol alone does not resolve this.
- If the calibration protocol shows no instruction-independent κ improvement on non-champion cross-cluster pairs (i.e., any Phase 3 advantage is attributable entirely to champion-selection, not to the instruction's frame-setting function), the mechanism-level argument for the instruction's contribution in §4.5 is empirically disconfirmed, though the champion-population argument for Phase 3 may still hold partially.
- If judges calibrated on anti-naturalistic SC6(b-1) pairs fail to correctly rank the complete-and-longer decision in naturalistic SC6(b-2) pairs at rates above chance, coverage-completeness tracking has not generalized from calibration examples to naturalistic evaluation conditions, and the coverage-completeness ranking signal requires further calibration development before functioning at ESHTR's full evaluation scale.
- If LLMs calibrated on SC6(b-1)-ID pairs fail to achieve reliable agreement with annotator material-set identification from raw brief content — indicating that the outcome-dependency materiality standard is not operationally learnable under the calibration protocol — the materiality-identification step of the coverage-completeness pipeline requires further specification before the realistic evaluation condition is validated.
- If higher-elaboration champions consistently win quality-discrepant cross-elaboration calibration pairs — where quality-discrepancy is established by the five-dimension categorical composite (ratio identification precision + material argument engagement completeness + boilerplate avoidance + precedent application precision + logical entailment validity) scored independently of adversarial record richness, as specified in §4.6 — at rates substantially above chance, the Phase 3 abstraction instruction has not separated argumentation quality from adversarial-record-driven elaboration level, and the cross-elaboration concern under prong 2 of the C2 disjunction remains unaddressed. If arm-specific inter-rater reliability for C1 or C2 reference-answer construction is systematically lower for high-adversarial-record champions than for matched low-adversarial-record champions, reference-answer construction is adversarial-record-sensitive and the composite's independence claim fails at the construction step rather than the scoring step. If the five-dimension composite proves unreliable on C3 or C4 measures (expert assessors disagree at high rates on boilerplate avoidance or precedent application precision across the cross-elaboration pairs), the operationalization requires further specification before the test can run.
