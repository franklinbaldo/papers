# Defending the Proxy-Devaluation Mechanism: Statutory Compliance Standards and Commitment Displacement in Legal Reputation

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Target paper: `paper1F_reputacao_sistema_juridico.md` — "Reputação como Mecanismo de Coordenação no Sistema Jurídico" (Baldo, 2025).

Thesis:

> "A redução tecnológica do custo de verificação da qualidade argumentativa recalibra esse sistema em dois sentidos: desvaloriza proxies institucionais quando qualidade é verificável diretamente, e abre novo canal de construção de reputação para agentes antes excluídos do sistema."

Adversarial paper engaged: `otherwise/reputation-constitutive-field.md` — "The Constitutive Field Problem: Why Cheaper Verification May Not Devalue Legal Reputational Capital."

---

## 2. What This Support Adds

Vector: direct defense. This paper responds to three adversarial vectors — constitutive capital (§3.1), signaling persistence (§3.2), and network diffusion (§3.3) — on their own terms. The defense shows: (a) the constitutive capital critique is attenuated where the verification standard is statutory compliance for the structural compliance core, and that the mechanism's value in this domain extends to the high-volume, low-review population of proceedings where professional review does not systematically operate, independent of whether it adds detection-value over professional review in cases that review already reaches; (b) the signaling persistence argument is asymmetric across incumbents and new entrants, and that art. 489, §1º, V–VI's requirement of analytical engagement — identification of fundamentos determinantes, specification of distinguishing facts — raises mimicry costs above formal structural presence-of-reference optimization; (c) the network diffusion problem, while genuine for wide propagation, does not eliminate a first-stage local reputation-building channel operating through the Kreps repeated-game mechanism within specific bilateral court-practitioner relationships.

## 3. The Argument

### 3.1 Constitutive Capital and the Exogenous Standard

The adversarial's §3.1 argues that verification technology trained on existing legal production would encode existing capital relations into the quality standard it verifies. The constitutive capital critique holds because what counts as sound legal argument is partly determined by agents recognized as competent judges — and that recognition is capital-structured.

The adversarial anticipates a response and narrows its claim accordingly: the proxy-devaluation mechanism requires verification technology to operate against an exogenous quality standard. It then identifies the criterion: "Field-transforming episodes in legal history have typically involved an external normative shock — legislation, constitutional amendment, international treaty — that imposed standards that existing capital holders could not unilaterally define" (§4).

The paper's verification program meets this criterion. CPC 2015 arts. 489, §1º, V–VI and 927 established obligatory compliance requirements for fundamentação that pre-exist and constrain current field practice. These provisions were themselves the normative shock the adversarial identifies as the exception to constitutive capital stickiness — the paper series (`paper1A` through `paper1E`) is precisely an analysis of the rationality requirements those provisions introduced. Paper 2 (`paper2_pipeline_lean_argdown.md`) operationalizes formal verification against these statutory provisions using a Lean 4 pipeline. This is not technology calibrating against field-relative quality norms; it is technology operationalizing a statutory standard that field practice must comply with.

The adversarial acknowledges this within its own text: "A procedural-validity standard has more independence from capital relations than a peer-assessment standard — CPC 2015 is law, not field consensus" (§3.1). It then responds that "even procedural validity is interpreted through field practice." This is correct for contested margins — what constitutes "adequate engagement" with a precedent's *fundamentos determinantes* involves interpretive judgment at the edges. But the paradigm cases Paper 1A and the series defend are not marginal: omission of a required precedent engagement, absence of a stated statutory provision, internal inconsistency within the fundamentação — these are identifiable by structural textual comparison, not interpretive weighting. For the core class, the exogenous standard is not significantly constituted by field capital.

A further objection presses that where the exogenous standard is clearest, verification technology adds least value: structural compliance violations in the core class are already identifiable through ordinary professional review, making the mechanism redundant in the domain where it operates without constitutive capital interference. This objection correctly identifies a limitation on the mechanism's marginal detection value over professional review in cases where professional review already operates. It does not address the complementary population.

The Brazilian legal docket is not uniformly high-stakes. The principal sources of binding precedent under art. 927 coexist with an enormous volume of individual proceedings at each level where one or both parties lack resources for systematic professional quality review — criminal appellants represented by public defenders carrying hundreds of simultaneous matters, civil defendants without specialized counsel in high-volume collection dockets, administrative proceedings where one party is an underresourced municipal or state body. In these proceedings, structural compliance failures in the core class go undetected not because the exogenous standard is unclear, but because the review cost is prohibitive for the party that would benefit from detection. Systematic automated verification extends detection and deterrence to this population — not because it outperforms professional review in high-stakes proceedings that review already reaches, but because it reaches proceedings where the cost structure currently forecloses review entirely. The inverse relationship between exogenous-standard clarity and mechanism added-value holds within the subset of cases professional review already reaches; it does not characterize the mechanism's value in the larger population where professional review is absent.

This does not fully resolve the constitutive capital problem. It bounds it. Where the verification standard is formal compliance with unambiguous statutory requirements, the constitutive capital critique loses most of its force. Where the standard shades into interpretive quality assessment, the critique regains force. The paper's thesis holds precisely where the standard is statutory — which is where the series locates its central cases — and the mechanism's coverage value in the high-volume, low-review population reinforces the claim that this domain matters at scale.

### 3.2 Signaling Persistence and Commitment Displacement

The adversarial's §3.2 distinguishes two components of institutional proxies under the Spence-Kreps framework: (a) the informational component — the proxy's correlation with quality — and (b) the commitment component — the observable investment record that creates predictable future performance. It argues cheaper output verification disrupts component (a) while leaving component (b) intact.

Two structural points resist this division.

**First, the Kreps framework does not fix the commitment signal to institutional investment.** Kreps's analysis requires only that commitment history be observable by counter-parties. An agent who accumulates verified high-quality outputs across multiple interactions generates an observable commitment history. The observation is not "this agent invested in institutional credentials" but "this agent has consistently produced outputs meeting the formal compliance standard." For repeated-game purposes, this record functions identically to an institutional investment record: it creates predictable future performance and carries reputational accountability. The adversarial's §3.2 assumes that only institutional investment constitutes observable commitment; but Kreps's framework imposes no such restriction. The paper's §4.3 — "mudar o que conta para construir essa reputação: de proxies institucionais para qualidade argumentativa verificável" — is precisely a claim about which commitment vehicle the recalibration transitions between, not a claim that commitment mechanisms disappear.

**Second, the commitment-displacement argument is asymmetric across new entrants and incumbents.** For incumbent practitioners, the adversarial is correct: cheaper output verification diminishes the informational component of proxies while leaving the commitment component (accumulated investment record) intact. The net effect on incumbents is partial, not total. For new entrants, however, the situation is different. New entrants by definition lack the institutional investment record that constitutes the commitment component. What cheaper verification provides is an alternative path to building a commitment record — through accumulated verified outputs rather than through institutional investment. The adversarial's §3.2 analysis addresses the incumbent's position well; it does not address the new entrant's position, which is the democratization half of the paper's claim.

A further objection presses whether formal compliance records constitute mimicry-resistant commitment signals. Kreps's separating equilibrium requires that commitment signals be costly to mimic — specifically, that accumulating the signal requires the same analytical investment that distinguishes high-quality from low-quality agents, rather than optimization for measurable formal markers. Whether this holds depends on what the compliance standard actually tests.

Art. 489, §1º, V–VI specifies obligations not reducible to structural formal completeness. V requires that a decision invoking precedent *identify the fundamentos determinantes* of that precedent and either apply them, *distinguish the case by specifying the distinguishing fact*, or justify overruling. VI requires that a decision invoking a súmula or court holding identify the specific applicable enunciado and explain why it applies. These requirements cannot be satisfied by citation inclusion alone — the decision must locate and engage with what the precedent decided and on what grounds, then establish a specific doctrinal relationship between that holding and the case at hand. Optimizing for these requirements at minimal analytical cost requires producing text that appears to identify fundamentos determinantes and specify distinguishing facts without genuinely doing so — which is harder to sustain across repeated interactions with the same court than across a single isolated filing, because the court accumulates observations about whether the purported distinctions track the cases they claim to distinguish. Mimicry-resistance under these provisions is not guaranteed; it is not reducible to presence-of-reference checking. Whether the pipeline's implementation (Paper 2) tests for engagement rather than presence determines where on this spectrum the standard lands. The statutory text establishes an upper bound on how gameable the standard can be; the implementation determines how close the actual threshold is to that upper bound.

The paper's differential claim survives: cheaper verification changes the competitive position of new entrants relative to incumbents more than it changes the absolute position of either. Proxies lose relative weight for new entrants specifically because an alternative record-building path opens. This is the paper's §4.1 mechanism — "abre novo canal de construção de reputação" — stated as a differential change, not a total replacement.

### 3.3 Network Diffusion and Decision-Text Records

The adversarial's §3.3 identifies three conditions for the democratization prediction: legibility/access of verified quality scores to decision-makers, cross-interaction propagation through the reputation network, and absence of counter-signal investment by existing capital holders. It correctly notes the paper does not establish these conditions.

The response has two parts.

**First, the adversarial conflates non-establishment with non-existence.** The paper's §5.1 claims that cheaper verification "abre canal de construção de reputação para agentes antes excluídos" — it opens a channel, not that the channel operates without friction or at full effectiveness. The paper does not assert that the three diffusion conditions are fully satisfied; it argues that the channel becomes available where it was previously closed. The adversarial's objection reduces the strength of the democratization claim, which is appropriate, but does not eliminate it.

**Second, for formal compliance verification specifically, the diffusion channel has structural features the adversarial does not engage.** When an argument's compliance with art. 489, §1º is formally demonstrable, and a court acts on that compliance (granting or denying a procedural remedy), the quality record is produced in the decision text itself. Judicial decisions are published through official channels (Diário da Justiça, court websites) that are independent of practitioner networks. This is a diffusion path that does not require traversal through the professional relationship channels that existing capital holders dominate.

The counter-signal investment problem (condition c) — high-capital agents investing to shape what counts as verified quality — applies most forcefully to peer-review and reputational-assessment systems. It applies less forcefully to formal statutory compliance records produced in the course of judicial proceedings. An argument's compliance or non-compliance with art. 489, §1º in a given decision is determined by the court, not by peer assessment, and the record is produced in an authoritative document that high-capital practitioners cannot revise.

The adversarial's round 2 response presses the attribution gap: court decisions record compliance findings attributed to case outcomes, not to practitioner identity. A compliance victory enters the official archive as a case outcome — "case X: fundamentação inadequacy found; remand ordered" — not as a practitioner-attributed quality record. Wide network propagation from case outcomes to practitioner reputation requires intermediaries — institutional victory profiles, academic publications, co-counsel networks, professional directories — that are capital-holder-structured.

The attribution gap correctly identifies the binding constraint on wide network propagation. It does not bind the first-stage mechanism.

The Kreps repeated-game framework implies that reputation accumulates through repeated interactions where the same counter-parties observe the same agent's behavior over time. A practitioner who files formally compliant challenges before the same tribunal repeatedly generates an observable compliance track record for the judges and clerks who receive those filings directly — without requiring intermediaries to attribute case outcomes to the practitioner's identity. The court observes the practitioner's arguments across cases; the practitioner's compliance pattern is visible to that court through direct repeated observation. This is not wide reputation propagation; it is local repeated-game reputation accumulation within the bilateral court-practitioner relationship — the same mechanism Kreps identified, operating at smaller geographic and institutional scale.

The paper's democratization claim has two stages: (a) opening a local reputation-building channel for practitioners previously lacking institutional proxies; (b) propagating that reputation through the broader network. The attribution gap constrains stage (b): without practitioner-attributed outcome records reaching reputational network nodes beyond the specific court, compliance records do not accumulate into the networked reputation the paper's §5.1 ultimate prediction envisions. Stage (a) operates through the Kreps mechanism in repeated bilateral court-practitioner interactions, and the attribution gap does not block it.

The mechanism's democratizing effect at stage (a) is localized. For practitioners concentrated in specific courts and dockets — public defenders before their regular tribunals, regional specialists appearing repeatedly before specific administrative bodies, state attorneys before the same appellate chambers — local reputation accumulation through compliance track records provides a new commitment signal channel, independent of institutional affiliation proxies, within those specific relationships. Whether this local reputation propagates into the broader network remains constrained by the attribution gap for stage (b).

## 4. Scope of the Support

This defense strengthens the paper's partial, differential claim for the domain where formal statutory compliance standards are operative. It establishes: (a) the constitutive capital critique is bounded by the exogenous standard for the structural compliance core, and the mechanism's coverage value extends to the high-volume, low-review population where professional review is absent — not just to marginal detection over professional review in high-stakes cases; (b) the commitment-displacement argument is asymmetric, operating for new entrants through an alternative record-building path, and the analytical engagement requirement of art. 489, §1º, V–VI constrains how gameable the compliance record is — with the pipeline's implementation determining how close the realized threshold is to the statutory upper bound; (c) local repeated-game reputation accumulation operates through direct bilateral court-practitioner observation as a first-stage mechanism, independent of the attribution intermediaries that block wide network propagation.

The defense does not establish the full democratization prediction. Cross-interaction propagation beyond specific court-practitioner relationships, and practitioner attribution through the reputational network to its authoritative nodes, remain constrained by the attribution gap. The defense establishes that the mechanism is not zero, that the first-stage channel is not entirely closed, and that the population where the mechanism operates at scale is not empty. It does not establish that the mechanism operates at the systemic, network-wide scale the paper's strongest prediction requires.

The defense does not address Paper 1F's broader reputational analysis (§2–§3) of network proxies, win-history tracking, and credential-based signaling. The adversarial did not attack that descriptive infrastructure; neither does this support extend it.

## 5. Conditions Under Which This Support Would Fail

**(a) The formal compliance standard is shown to be interpretively constituted in the core cases.** If the paradigm categories (precedent omission, statutory provision omission, internal inconsistency) require contested interpretive judgment rather than structural textual comparison in most cases, the exogenous-standard argument loses its grip and the constitutive capital critique regains force across the operative domain.

**(b) Kreps's commitment framework is shown to require institutional investment specifically.** If commitment records require institutional investment (not just observable output history) to function as separating signals — because institutional investment is the only costly signal that cannot be cheaply mimicked across a single interaction — then the commitment-displacement argument fails. This would require showing that accumulating verified quality records across many arguments is cheaply mimicable in a way that institutional credentials are not.

**(c) The decision-text diffusion path is shown to be practically inaccessible to the reputational network.** If courts' formal compliance records do not propagate through the professional network in ways that influence reputational assessments — because practitioners and judges do not track outcomes of formal compliance challenges — then the diffusion path identified here is nominal rather than effective, and the network diffusion problem is not narrowed.

**(d) Structural compliance core violations are shown to be concentrated in proceedings where professional review already operates.** If empirical analysis of the Brazilian legal docket shows that the population of proceedings where structural compliance failures go undetected is small — because high-volume repetitive resource dockets are structurally compliant, or because the parties that would benefit from detection lack standing or interest to raise compliance objections — the coverage-extension argument loses its scale claim. The mechanism would operate at the margin of what professional review already reaches rather than at a distinct population.

**(e) The Lean 4 pipeline tests formal completeness rather than analytical engagement.** If the pipeline's implementation (Paper 2) checks citation presence, statutory reference inclusion, and dispositif non-contradiction without testing whether fundamentos determinantes were identified and whether distinguishing facts were specified, the mimicry-resistance argument fails. Agents optimizing for measurable formal markers would satisfy the pipeline's tests without providing the analytical engagement art. 489, §1º, V–VI requires, and the compliance record would become a gameable proxy of the same type the paper's mechanism is designed to replace.

**(f) Courts do not maintain repeated-game observation of individual practitioner compliance records.** If the courts that receive the highest volume of compliance challenges operate without systematic observation of which practitioners consistently produce correct compliance arguments — because volume, judge turnover, or case-management systems do not permit tracking past interactions with specific practitioners — the local Kreps mechanism does not accumulate. The first-stage democratization requires that courts observe practitioner behavior repeatedly; if the observing party does not retain and use past interaction records, stage (a) fails along with stage (b).
