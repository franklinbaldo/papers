# The Cognition-Constraint Attack Has a Narrow Range: Completing an Established Reasoning Path Is Not Generative Cognition

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "Embargos de Declaração no CPC 2015: Escopo Amplo dos Vícios e Infringência como Consequência" (`paper1A_embargos_declaracao.md`), §5.3, states:

> "a modificação eventual é efeito da integração: a fundamentação corrigida pode levar a dispositivo distinto, **mas não há nova cognição autônoma**."

The paper's two theses:

**Thesis 1 (scope):** The mode of defect — obscurity, contradiction, omission — determines what is curable by embargos de declaração. A §489, §1º, IV violation (failure to address an argument capable of undermining the conclusion) is a curable defect under art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II, CPC 2015.

**Thesis 2 (automatic consequence):** Modification of the embargoed decision is an automatic procedural consequence of granting embargos when cure implies modification — not an autonomous claim requiring a subsidiary petition.

---

## 2. What This Support Adds

**Vector:** Direct defense. This paper responds to the attack in `otherwise/ed-cognition-constraint.md`, which argues that (a) the "no autonomous new cognition" claim in §5.3 is false for the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case the paper invokes throughout, and (b) Thesis 2's "automatic consequence" claim inherits this false premise.

The defense proceeds along five lines: (1) the adversarial paper has used an unrepresentative example to characterize what the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case looks like; (2) the recall-cognition characterization holds for the empirically predominant category of such violations; (3) the implicit rejection doctrine does not remove all Type A cases from the §489 category — it requires structural deployment, not merely framework availability; (4) the selection effect is a structural prediction without established empirical basis, and its court-reliability assumption is in tension with the statutory evidence of Type A omission prevalence; and (5) the automatic-consequence attack conflates logical contingency with procedural mechanism, targeting a claim Paper 1A does not make.

---

## 3. The Argument

### 3.1 The Attack Reconstructed

The adversarial paper distinguishes recall cognition (completing an existing judgment) from generative cognition (forming a new judgment not previously formed). It argues that addressing a previously unaddressed §489, §1º, IV argument requires three steps, each of which is generative:

1. Determining whether the argument is "capable of undermining the conclusion in principle" — a substantive legal judgment about the argument's quality and its relationship to applicable precedents.
2. Engaging the argument on its merits — new deliberation on a question the court had not previously addressed.
3. Determining whether this new engagement changes the result — a merit judgment whose outcome is not logically entailed by the original decision.

The attack is illustrated with Paper 1A's own hypothetical: addressing the STF precedent's reservation ("excluídos os especialistas em educação") requires new constitutional judgments about post-legislative reenquadramento, the scope of the reservation phrase, and whether current official designation governs the accumulability analysis. None of these three judgments, the adversarial paper argues, is recall cognition.

The adversarial counter-response adds two structural arguments beyond the original attack. *The selection effect* (adversarial §3.4) argues that §489, §1º, IV's definitional requirements and the implicit rejection doctrine operate as dual filters selecting against Type A omissions: arguments with determinate framework-outcomes are addressed explicitly (because courts recognize determinacy) or implicitly rejected (because structural reasoning extends to them), so what survives as a genuine omission is disproportionately what the framework left open. *The implicit rejection reversal* (adversarial §3.5) argues the doctrine belongs on the adversarial side: it removes from the §489 category the cases where §5.3 holds (recall-cognition cases where the court has a position), leaving the cases where §5.3 fails (genuine omissions where the court has no position, disproportionately Type B under the selection effect).

The attack also reaches Thesis 2 because if addressing the omitted argument requires generative cognition, whether modification follows is contingent on what that new cognition produces — and "caso" in art. 1.024, §3º is a real conditional, not a presupposition.

### 3.2 The Adversarial Paradigm Is Unrepresentative

The adversarial paper characterizes what a "paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case" looks like by using Paper 1A's illustrative hypothetical: contested constitutional classification of a servant's cargo across a legislative reenquadramento, with an STF precedent containing a disputed reservation phrase. This is a case chosen to display doctrinal complexity. It is not representative of the distribution of §489, §1º, IV violations that Brazilian appellate practice produces.

The empirical center of mass of §489, §1º, IV violations in Brazilian courts involves three recurring categories where the court's existing legal framework determines the answer to the omitted argument:

**Binding-precedent omissions.** The court transcribes a controlling *enunciado de súmula vinculante* or binding rationale in its fundamentação and then fails to run a party's argument through it. The party's argument directly engages the precedent — invoking the same ratio, the same reservation phrase, or the same doctrinal framework the court already accepted. For these cases, the court's existing position on the precedent's applicability commits it to a determinate answer on the omitted argument. Addressing the argument in embargos requires stating what the court's already-accepted framework entails when applied to the argument, not forming a new position.

**Statutory-provision omissions.** A party invokes a statutory provision that unambiguously governs a specific aspect of the case and demonstrates its applicability; the court fails to address it in the fundamentação while reaching a holding that presupposes its non-application. When the provision's meaning is settled and its coverage of the relevant fact pattern is established, the court's failure is to run the analysis through a provision its own jurisprudence treats as controlling. Addressing this in embargos applies an existing legal framework, not develops one.

**Internal-consistency omissions.** A court makes factual findings in its fundamentação that are in tension with each other, and a party's argument directly identifies this tension and shows it is decisive for the conclusion. The inconsistency is already present in the court's own text; addressing the argument requires the court to identify which finding takes precedence and state the implication for the dispositif. The answer is internal to the court's existing reasoning.

In each category, the court's analytical path was interrupted — the argument was before the court and obligated a response under art. 489, §1º, IV — but the path's endpoint is determined by commitments already established. These are the cases Paper 1A's §5.3 describes: no new autonomous cognition, because the cognition required is completion of an existing path.

### 3.3 The Recall/Generative Distinction Does Not Turn on Difficulty

A structural point distinguishes the defense from a mere empirical-range argument. The adversarial paper's three-step analysis of the cognition required to address a §489, §1º, IV omission conflates cognitive effort with cognitive type.

The recall/generative distinction is not about how much analysis is required; it is about whether the court is recovering a position its framework commits it to (recall) or forming a position its framework leaves open (generative). For the binding-precedent category: determining whether a precedent's ratio reaches the party's argument requires examining the ratio, the argument, and the relevant similarities and distinctions. This analysis has steps and requires care. But if the court's existing acceptance of the precedent as governing, its characterization of the ratio in the same decision, and its application of the ratio to other aspects of the case jointly determine how the ratio applies to the party's argument — the court is completing a circuit, not opening a new one. The required analysis is recall cognition even when it involves multiple analytical steps.

Generative cognition arises when the court's framework genuinely leaves the question open — when no combination of the court's prior commitments in the decision determines the answer. The adversarial paper's hypothetical is of this type: whether post-legislative reenquadramento under state law alters the constitutional classification for the art. 37, XVI, "b" purpose is not determined by anything in the court's prior reasoning. The court must form a new position on a question its framework had not previously committed to. This is generative cognition, and the adversarial paper is correct to identify it as such.

But the existence of Type B violations (genuinely framework-open questions) does not establish that the adversarial paper's paradigm case is paradigmatic. The clear majority of §489, §1º, IV violations — the binding-precedent, statutory-provision, and internal-consistency categories — present Type A cases where the court's framework determines the answer. The adversarial paper used Paper 1A's illustrative hypothetical, which was chosen to be doctrinally complex, and treated it as the paradigm. The defense challenges this empirical characterization.

### 3.4 The Implicit Rejection Doctrine Does Not Remove All Type A Cases

The adversarial counter-response argues (§3.5) that the implicit rejection doctrine belongs on the adversarial side: it removes from the §489 category the cases where §5.3 holds (the court's reasoning structure has already disposed of the argument — recall cognition), leaving the cases where §5.3 fails (genuine omissions where the court formed no position, disproportionately Type B under the selection effect).

This reversal depends on the doctrine filtering out all Type A cases among genuine §489 violations. It does not.

The implicit rejection doctrine (*rejeição implícita*) applies when the court's *structural reasoning* logically disposes of the argument — when the logic of the court's stated analysis extends to cover the argument, even without explicit mention. Framework availability is a necessary but not sufficient condition for implicit rejection. The doctrine requires *structural deployment*: the relevant framework component must be present and operating in the decision's reasoning in a way whose logic reaches the unaddressed argument.

Consider the paradigm binding-precedent category: a court accepts and applies Súmula X to arguments A, B, and C in the same decision. Argument D also falls under Súmula X — the same ratio, the same application conditions — but the court's reasoning simply never addresses D. The súmula's application was stated in the context of A, B, C, and the decision's reasoning structure did not extend the analysis to D. This is not implicit rejection: the court's structural reasoning did not reach D. The court has the framework (Súmula X commitment) and the framework determines the answer (D falls under the same ratio as A, B, C), but the structural deployment stopped before D. The result is a genuine §489, §1º, IV omission of the Type A kind: framework-determined answer, absent from the structural reasoning, not implicitly rejected.

The same structure holds for statutory-provision omissions. A court that applies a provision to a specific factual configuration but whose structural analysis doesn't extend to a decisive argument about the provision's application to a related configuration has not implicitly rejected the argument by possessing the provision or by applying it elsewhere. And for internal-consistency omissions: a court whose findings are in tension has not implicitly resolved that tension by articulating the findings that produce it — the structural reasoning contains the materials for a determinate resolution but has not deployed them against the argument that identified the implication.

The adversarial §3.5 treats structural disposal as coextensive with framework availability: if the court's framework determines the answer, the court either addressed the argument explicitly or implicitly rejected it. There is a third category — the court's framework determines the answer, the court had the relevant commitments, and the structural reasoning never reached the argument — producing a genuine Type A omission that survives the implicit rejection filter. This is not a marginal case; it describes the paradigm binding-precedent, statutory-provision, and internal-consistency omissions identified in §3.2.

The adversarial reversal therefore establishes less than it claims. The doctrine removes from the §489 category the Type A cases where the structural reasoning reached the argument; it does not remove the Type A cases where the reasoning possessed the framework but stopped before deploying it against the decisive argument. The residual category — genuine §489 omissions past the implicit rejection filter — contains both Type A cases (structural gap with determinate framework answer) and Type B cases (framework-open questions). The adversarial §3.5 shows the residual contains Type B cases; it does not show the residual is predominantly or exclusively Type B.

### 3.5 The Selection Effect Is a Structural Prediction Without Established Empirical Basis

The adversarial §3.4 introduces the selection effect as a structural argument for why genuine §489, §1º, IV violations skew toward Type B. Courts that face arguments with determinate outcomes under their accepted frameworks tend to address them explicitly or dispose of them through implicit rejection, so genuine omissions of such arguments are filtered out. What survives as a genuine omission is disproportionately what the framework left open.

This argument shares the epistemic status of the empirical range claim it contests. Both are structural predictions about expected distributions; neither is a demonstrated distribution. Section §3.2 identifies structural reasons why Type A cases should predominate at the empirical center. The selection effect identifies structural reasons to expect Type B cases to be disproportionate among genuine omissions. The structural predictions run in opposite directions; neither constitutes the empirical data that would settle the question.

SC3 applies symmetrically: empirical demonstration that Type A predominates despite the selection effect would confirm the empirical range claim; empirical demonstration that Type B predominates despite the arguments of §§3.2–3.3 would confirm the selection effect. The adversarial paper has established a structural mechanism that *would generate* Type B predominance if the mechanism's assumptions hold. It has not established that the assumptions hold.

The critical assumption underlying the selection effect is that courts reliably engage arguments with determinate framework-outcomes — that the combination of determinacy and genuine omission is rare because courts recognize and address arguments whose answers are clear. This is an empirical claim about Brazilian appellate court behavior that the adversarial paper does not document. Brazilian appellate courts operate under documented caseload pressure, and *fundamentação deficiente* — decisions that fail to engage decisive arguments despite possessing the relevant legal framework — is precisely the failure mode that art. 489, §1º, IV and the embargos remedy are designed to address. The statutory design anticipates not just occasional Type B failures but a systematic pattern of omissions significant enough to warrant codification and a specific remedy. A doctrinal device for correcting failures to apply established frameworks does not entail that Type A failures are rare; the statutory context suggests the opposite.

Moreover, the selection effect's implicit rejection component (courts with determinate arguments implicitly reject them through structural reasoning) encounters the same limitation identified in §3.4: implicit rejection requires structural deployment, not just framework availability. Courts can possess determinate framework answers and fail to structurally deploy them against decisive arguments without implicit rejection becoming available. The selection effect's filtering mechanism depends on courts' structural reasoning reliably extending to cover the arguments their frameworks determine; when that extension fails — producing a genuine Type A omission — the selection effect doesn't filter out the Type A case. It leaves it in the residual.

The structural prediction of §3.2 — that binding-precedent, statutory-provision, and internal-consistency omissions are the empirical center — tracks the most commonly litigated categories of §489, §1º, IV violations in Brazilian appellate practice. The adversarial selection effect does not engage the statutory evidence that Type A omissions are prevalent; it reasons from theoretical mechanism to predicted distribution without attending to the institutional context that motivated the mechanism's legislative design.

### 3.6 The Automatic Consequence Defense: Procedural, Not Logical

The adversarial paper attacks Thesis 2 on two grounds: the grammar of art. 1.024, §3º makes modification contingent ("caso... implique"), and Paper 1A's own §3.4 acknowledges cases of non-modifying grants. Both observations are correct. The adversarial paper concludes from them that modification cannot be an "automatic consequence."

The conclusion does not follow because it conflates two distinct questions.

**Whether modification is logically guaranteed** — whether every grant of embargos produces modification. Paper 1A answers no (§3.4: non-modifying grants occur). The adversarial paper correctly notes this. Paper 1A acknowledges it explicitly.

**Whether modification requires a separate procedural petition once integration implies it** — the procedural-mechanics question. Paper 1A's Thesis 2 is about this question, not the first. What the "automatic consequence" thesis claims is that when the court grants embargos and its integration of the decision implies modification, the modification occurs without requiring a subsidiary petition for infringement effects. The "automatic" aspect is procedural: once the court has determined that its integration implies modification, no separate request is needed for the modification to materialize in the dispositif.

The art. 1.024, §3º conditional grammar is consistent with this procedural reading. The provision addresses what happens when the conditional is satisfied — when integration does imply modification — treating the satisfaction of the conditional as the trigger for the embargado's rights. The conditional structure of the provision reflects that modification is not guaranteed by the filing of embargos (correct) while the provision's design confirms that, when modification does follow from integration, it is treated as a consequence of the court's integrative activity, not as a separate judicial act triggered by a separate request. This is exactly the distinction between modification as procedural consequence (Paper 1A) and modification as a separate judicial act requiring a separate petition (the traditional formula the paper contests).

The adversarial paper's argument establishes that modification is contingent on what integration reveals. This is correct and Paper 1A does not deny it. The adversarial paper has not established that modification, when integration reveals it is implied, requires an additional procedural petition to materialize. The attack's premise (logical contingency) does not reach the thesis (procedural automaticity).

### 3.7 The Classification Problem Is Concentrated at the Boundary

The adversarial paper's most precise point against the Type A/Type B distinction (§4 anticipated reply) is that classifying an omission as Type A or Type B is itself a substantive judgment requiring the court to examine its existing reasoning, identify its implications, and determine whether they resolve the argument. For difficult boundary cases, this classification work constitutes generative cognition.

This argument is correct for difficult boundary cases — cases where it is genuinely unclear whether the court's framework commits it to an answer. But the argument's range is the same as the original attack's: it applies to cases where the framework is open or ambiguous, not to the clear center of the Type A category.

For a court that transcribed a binding *enunciado de súmula* and failed to apply it to the party's argument: classifying this as Type A requires determining whether the *enunciado*'s ratio reaches the party's argument. In the standard case — where the party explicitly invoked the *enunciado*, the court accepted it as governing in the same decision, and the party's argument directly engages the precedent's scope — this determination is immediate. No new substantive deliberation is required to identify that the court's accepted framework commits it to an answer on this argument. The classification is obvious; the cognition involved is recognition, not formation.

The adversarial paper has concentrated its analysis on the hard cases at the boundary. Those cases exist. The defense is that they are not the center, and the center is where Paper 1A's thesis operates.

## 4. Anticipated Objection

The adversarial paper's counter-response (§4.2) addresses the empirical range defense directly and offers two reasons it does not suffice. First, the selection effect (§3.4) undermines the empirical characterization. Second, even granting a substantial Type A category, Paper 1A presents a Type B hypothetical as its central illustration without limiting §5.3 to Type A — a general thesis cannot be defended by showing a subset is correct.

Both points are addressed. On the selection effect: §3.5 establishes that the effect is a structural prediction sharing the epistemic status of the empirical range claim it contests; the court-reliability assumption it requires is in tension with the statutory evidence that Type A omissions are prevalent enough to warrant legislative remedy. On the implicit rejection mechanism of the selection effect: §3.4 establishes that the doctrine requires structural deployment, not merely framework availability, and that the Type A omissions that survive the filter are precisely the paradigm cases identified in §3.2.

On the illustration point: this defense has consistently acknowledged that Paper 1A's §5.3 is overstated as a universal claim and operates most securely for recall-cognition and implicit-rejection cases plus the empirical center of Type A genuine omissions. The adversary's second point establishes that the paper should be limited, not that the empirical range defense fails for the category it covers. Establishing that the Type A category is real, non-negligible, and survives both the selection effect (§3.5) and the implicit rejection reversal (§3.4) is the defense's claim — not that §5.3 holds without exception for every §489, §1º, IV violation.

## 5. Scope of This Support

This paper supports Paper 1A by:
- Establishing that the adversarial cognition-constraint attack operates within a narrower range than the adversarial paper's presentation implies.
- Providing three specific categories — binding-precedent, statutory-provision, internal-consistency — that constitute Type A violations resistant to the cognition-constraint attack.
- Showing that the implicit rejection doctrine does not remove all Type A cases from the §489 category: the doctrine requires structural deployment, not just framework availability, and genuine Type A omissions persist where the court's reasoning stopped before deploying a framework-determined answer.
- Showing that the selection effect is a structural prediction without established empirical basis, and that its court-reliability assumption is in tension with the statutory evidence that Type A omissions motivated legislative remedy.
- Distinguishing the procedural and logical senses of "automatic consequence" to show the adversarial attack does not reach the thesis Paper 1A makes.

This paper does **not**:
- Contest the adversarial paper's analysis of Type B cases: for omissions where the court's framework leaves the answer genuinely open, embargos do require generative cognition. The adversarial paper is correct for this category.
- Contest the adversarial §3.2 constraints/determination argument — that argument is directed at the non-autonomy defense and requires a separate response there.
- Establish empirically that Type A constitutes the majority of §489, §1º, IV violations in any specific court or jurisdiction. The empirical claim in §3.2 is a structural characterization of the predominant violation categories; data on actual distributions would confirm or narrow it.
- Contest the adversarial paper's description of the implicit rejection doctrine or its doctrinal accuracy.

## 6. Conditions Under Which This Support Would Fail

1. **If the empirical distribution of §489, §1º, IV violations in Brazilian appellate practice contains a large proportion of Type B cases:** If most actual §489, §1º, IV omissions involve framework-open questions requiring genuine new deliberation, Paper 1A's §5.3 claim ("no autonomous new cognition") would be misleading as a general characterization of the remedy. The defense holds only if Type A is the predominant category in practice.

2. **If the implicit rejection doctrine applies more broadly than §3.4 assumes:** If Brazilian courts routinely treat binding-precedent and statutory-provision omissions as implicitly rejected through structural reasoning — even when the court's analysis addressed the precedent or provision in a different argumentative context — then the structural-deployment/framework-availability distinction narrows. The Type A residual past the filter would be smaller, and the adversarial §3.5 reversal would have greater scope.

3. **If binding-precedent and statutory-provision omissions are routinely less determinate than this paper assumes:** If precedent ratios are genuinely contested in most cases where they are invoked — such that applying the ratio to a new argument requires the court to form a new position on the ratio's scope — then the examples in §3.2 shade into Type B. The adversarial paper's hypothetical would then be representative, not exceptional.

4. **If the procedural/logical distinction in §3.6 is inoperative in Brazilian appellate practice:** If courts consistently treat the absence of a subsidiary petition for infringement effects as a reason to withhold modification, the procedural-consequence thesis fails in practice regardless of its doctrinal support.

5. **If the court-reliability assumption underlying the selection effect is established by data:** If empirical study of Brazilian appellate courts shows that courts reliably engage Type A arguments — that the selection effect's assumed filtering behavior actually occurs at high rates — the §3.5 response weakens. The court-reliability assumption would no longer be an unestablished premise; it would be confirmed evidence that Type A genuine omissions are rare.
