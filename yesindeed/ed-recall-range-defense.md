# The Cognition-Constraint Attack Has a Narrow Range: Completing an Established Reasoning Path Is Not Generative Cognition

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "Embargos de Declaração no CPC 2015: Escopo Amplo dos Vícios e Infringência como Consequência" (`paper1A_embargos_declaracao.md`), §5.3, states:

> "a modificação eventual é efeito da integração: a fundamentação corrigida pode levar a dispositivo distinto, **mas não há nova cognição autônoma**."

The paper's two theses:

**Thesis 1 (scope):** The mode of defect — obscurity, contradiction, omission — determines what is curable by embargos de declaração. A §489, §1º, IV violation (failure to address an argument capable of undermining the conclusion) is a curable defect under art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II, CPC 2015.

**Thesis 2 (automatic consequence):** Modification of the embargoed decision is an automatic procedural consequence of granting embargos when cure implies modification — not an autonomous claim requiring a subsidiary petition.

---

## 2. What This Support Adds

**Vector:** Direct defense. This paper responds to the attack in `otherwise/ed-cognition-constraint.md`, which argues that (a) the "no autonomous new cognition" claim in §5.3 is false for the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case the paper invokes throughout, and (b) Thesis 2's "automatic consequence" claim inherits this false premise.

The defense proceeds along three lines: the adversarial paper has used an unrepresentative example to characterize what the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case looks like; the recall-cognition characterization holds for the empirically predominant category of such violations; and the automatic-consequence attack conflates logical contingency with procedural mechanism, targeting a claim Paper 1A does not make.

---

## 3. The Argument

### 3.1 The Attack Reconstructed

The adversarial paper distinguishes recall cognition (completing an existing judgment) from generative cognition (forming a new judgment not previously formed). It argues that addressing a previously unaddressed §489, §1º, IV argument requires three steps, each of which is generative:

1. Determining whether the argument is "capable of undermining the conclusion in principle" — a substantive legal judgment about the argument's quality and its relationship to applicable precedents.
2. Engaging the argument on its merits — new deliberation on a question the court had not previously addressed.
3. Determining whether this new engagement changes the result — a merit judgment whose outcome is not logically entailed by the original decision.

The attack is illustrated with Paper 1A's own hypothetical: addressing the STF precedent's reservation ("excluídos os especialistas em educação") requires new constitutional judgments about post-legislative reenquadramento, the scope of the reservation phrase, and whether current official designation governs the accumulability analysis. None of these three judgments, the adversarial paper argues, is recall cognition.

The attack reaches Thesis 2 because if addressing the omitted argument requires generative cognition, whether modification follows is contingent on what that new cognition produces — and "caso" in art. 1.024, §3º is a real conditional, not a presupposition.

### 3.2 The Adversarial Paradigm Is Unrepresentative

The adversarial paper characterizes what a "paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case" looks like by using Paper 1A's illustrative hypothetical: contested constitutional classification of a servant's cargo across a legislative reenquadramento, with an STF precedent containing a disputed reservation phrase. This is a case chosen to display doctrinal complexity. It is not representative of the distribution of §489, §1º, IV violations that Brazilian appellate practice produces.

The empirical center of mass of §489, §1º, IV violations in Brazilian courts involves three recurring categories where the court's existing legal framework determines the answer to the omitted argument:

**Binding-precedent omissions.** The court transcribes a controlling *enunciado de súmula vinculante* or binding rationale in its fundamentação and then fails to run a party's argument through it. The party's argument directly engages the precedent — invoking the same ratio, the same reservation phrase, or the same doctrinal framework the court already accepted. For these cases, the court's existing position on the precedent's applicability commits it to a determinate answer on the omitted argument. Addressing the argument in embargos requires stating what the court's already-accepted framework entails when applied to the argument, not forming a new position.

**Statutory-provision omissions.** A party invokes a statutory provision that unambiguously governs a specific aspect of the case and demonstrates its applicability; the court fails to address it in the fundamentação while reaching a holding that presupposes its non-application. When the provision's meaning is settled and its coverage of the relevant fact pattern is established, the court's failure is to run the analysis through a provision its own jurisprudence treats as controlling. Addressing this in embargos applies an existing legal framework, not develops one.

**Internal-consistency omissions.** A court makes factual findings in its fundamentação that are in tension with each other, and a party's argument directly identifies this tension and shows it is decisive for the conclusion. The inconsistency is already present in the court's own text; addressing the argument requires the court to identify which finding takes precedence and state the implication for the dispositif. The answer is internal to the court's existing reasoning.

In each category, the court's analytical path was interrupted — the argument was before the court and obligated a response under art. 489, §1º, IV — but the path's endpoint is determined by commitments already established. These are the cases Paper 1A's §5.3 describes: no new autonomous cognition, because the cognition required is completion of an existing path.

### 3.3 The Recall/Generative Distinction Does Not Turn on Difficulty

A structural point distinguishes the defense from a mere empirical-range argument. The adversarial paper's three-step analysis of the cognition required to address a §489, §1º, IV omission conflates cognitive effort with cognitive type.

The recall/generative distinction is not about how much analysis is required; it is about whether the court is recovering a position its framework commits it to (recall) or forming a position its framework leaves open (generative). For the binding-precedent category: determining whether a precedent's ratio reaches the party's argument requires examining the ratio, the argument, and the relevant similarities and distinctions. This analysis has steps and requires care. But if the court's existing acceptance of the precedent as governing, its characterization of the ratio in the same decision, and its application of the ratio to other aspects of the case jointly determine how the ratio applies to the party's argument — the court is completing a circuit, not opening a new one. The required analysis is recall cognition even when it involves multiple analytical steps.

Generative cognition arises when the court's framework genuinely leaves the question open — when no combination of the court's prior commitments in the decision determines the answer. The adversarial paper's hypothetical is of this type: whether post-legislative reenquadramento under state law alters the constitutional classification for the art. 37, XVI, "b" purpose is not determined by anything in the court's prior reasoning. The court must form a new position on a question its framework had not previously committed to. This is generative cognition, and the adversarial paper is correct to identify it as such.

But the existence of Type B violations (genuinely framework-open questions) does not establish that the adversarial paper's paradigm case is paradigmatic. The clear majority of §489, §1º, IV violations — the binding-precedent, statutory-provision, and internal-consistency categories — present Type A cases where the court's framework determines the answer. The adversarial paper used Paper 1A's illustrative hypothetical, which was chosen to be doctrinal complex, and treated it as the paradigm. The defense challenges this empirical characterization.

### 3.4 The Implicit Rejection Doctrine Supports the Framework

The adversarial paper argues (§3.3) that Paper 1A's failure to engage the *rejeição implícita* doctrine is significant: the doctrine defines the boundary between genuine omissions and implicitly decided arguments, and that boundary is exactly where the recall/generative distinction matters. The paper's thesis requires this boundary to align with its own framework, but the paper ignores the doctrine.

The adversarial reading inverts the doctrine's relevance. The implicit rejection doctrine, properly situated within Paper 1A's framework, supports §5.3 rather than undermining it.

Courts that implicitly reject an argument — whose overall reasoning structure logically disposes of the argument without explicit discussion — have, by the doctrine's logic, already formed a substantive position on it. Embargos for such cases require surfacing what the court already decided. This is recall cognition in its clearest form: the position exists in the decision's reasoning structure; embargos make it explicit. For these cases, §5.3's "no new autonomous cognition" is unqualifiedly correct.

Courts that genuinely omit an argument — where no reading of the decision's structure produces an implicit position — present the core embargos scenario. Among these genuine omissions, the bulk are Type A: the court's legal framework commits it to a determinate answer it simply failed to reach for that argument. §5.3 holds for these cases as well: addressing the argument completes an analytical circuit already established.

The doctrine's significance for Paper 1A's thesis is therefore: it confirms that the implicit-rejection category is one where recall cognition applies most clearly, and it identifies genuine omissions as the set where Paper 1A's §5.3 claim requires empirical support — which §3.2 provides. The implicit rejection doctrine does not expose a gap in the framework; it identifies its clearest application domain.

### 3.5 The Automatic Consequence Defense: Procedural, Not Logical

The adversarial paper attacks Thesis 2 on two grounds: the grammar of art. 1.024, §3º makes modification contingent ("caso... implique"), and Paper 1A's own §3.4 acknowledges cases of non-modifying grants. Both observations are correct. The adversarial paper concludes from them that modification cannot be an "automatic consequence."

The conclusion does not follow because it conflates two distinct questions.

**Whether modification is logically guaranteed** — whether every grant of embargos produces modification. Paper 1A answers no (§3.4: non-modifying grants occur). The adversarial paper correctly notes this. Paper 1A acknowledges it explicitly.

**Whether modification requires a separate procedural petition once integration implies it** — the procedural-mechanics question. Paper 1A's Thesis 2 is about this question, not the first. What the "automatic consequence" thesis claims is that when the court grants embargos and its integration of the decision implies modification, the modification occurs without requiring a subsidiary petition for infringement effects. The "automatic" aspect is procedural: once the court has determined that its integration implies modification, no separate request is needed for the modification to materialize in the dispositif.

The art. 1.024, §3º conditional grammar is consistent with this procedural reading. The provision addresses what happens when the conditional is satisfied — when integration does imply modification — treating the satisfaction of the conditional as the trigger for the embargado's rights. The conditional structure of the provision reflects that modification is not guaranteed by the filing of embargos (correct) while the provision's design confirms that, when modification does follow from integration, it is treated as a consequence of the court's integrative activity, not as a separate judicial act triggered by a separate request. This is exactly the distinction between modification as procedural consequence (Paper 1A) and modification as a separate judicial act requiring a separate petition (the traditional formula the paper contests).

The adversarial paper's argument establishes that modification is contingent on what integration reveals. This is correct and Paper 1A does not deny it. The adversarial paper has not established that modification, when integration reveals it is implied, requires an additional procedural petition to materialize. The attack's premise (logical contingency) does not reach the thesis (procedural automaticity).

### 3.6 The Classification Problem Is Concentrated at the Boundary

The adversarial paper's most precise point against the Type A/Type B distinction (§4, anticipated reply) is that classifying an omission as Type A or Type B is itself a substantive judgment requiring the court to examine its existing reasoning, identify its implications, and determine whether they resolve the argument. For difficult boundary cases, this classification work constitutes generative cognition.

This argument is correct for difficult boundary cases — cases where it is genuinely unclear whether the court's framework commits it to an answer. But the argument's range is the same as the original attack's: it applies to cases where the framework is open or ambiguous, not to the clear center of the Type A category.

For a court that transcribed a binding *enunciado de súmula* and failed to apply it to the party's argument: classifying this as Type A requires determining whether the *enunciado*'s ratio reaches the party's argument. In the standard case — where the party explicitly invoked the *enunciado*, the court accepted it as governing in the same decision, and the party's argument directly engages the precedent's scope — this determination is immediate. No new substantive deliberation is required to identify that the court's accepted framework commits it to an answer on this argument. The classification is obvious; the cognition involved is recognition, not formation.

The adversarial paper has concentrated its analysis on the hard cases at the boundary. Those cases exist. The defense is that they are not the center, and the center is where Paper 1A's thesis operates.

## 4. Anticipated Objection

The adversarial paper's strongest formulation (§4) anticipates the Type A/Type B distinction and gives two reasons it does not suffice: (1) Paper 1A does not make this limitation; (2) the classification itself requires generative cognition.

**On (1):** This support paper argues that no limitation is required for Paper 1A's theses to hold. Thesis 1 (mode-not-matter rule) applies to all §489, §1º, IV violations and is not affected by the cognition-constraint attack — the attack targets §5.3, not the statutory construction argument. Thesis 2's procedural-consequence claim applies when integration implies modification and holds regardless of whether the underlying integration required recall or generative cognition. §5.3's "no autonomous new cognition" claim applies to the empirically predominant category of §489, §1º, IV violations. If Paper 1A's §5.3 is read as overstating universality — as a claim that applies without exception to all §489, §1º, IV violations, including Type B cases — then the adversarial paper is correct that the claim requires qualification. This support paper defends the claim's range, not its universality.

**On (2):** The classification-cognition problem holds for difficult boundary cases. For the clear center of the Type A category — binding-precedent omissions, statutory-provision omissions, internal-consistency omissions with settled provisions — the classification is immediate and requires no substantive deliberation. The adversarial paper's description of the classification problem overstates its scope by the same move it uses in the main attack: treating the hard case as the paradigm.

## 5. Scope of This Support

This paper supports Paper 1A by:
- Establishing that the adversarial cognition-constraint attack operates within a narrower range than the adversarial paper's presentation implies.
- Providing three specific categories — binding-precedent, statutory-provision, internal-consistency — that constitute Type A violations resistant to the cognition-constraint attack.
- Showing that the implicit rejection doctrine supports rather than undermines Paper 1A's §5.3 claim.
- Distinguishing the procedural and logical senses of "automatic consequence" to show the adversarial attack does not reach the thesis Paper 1A makes.

This paper does **not**:
- Contest the adversarial paper's analysis of Type B cases: for omissions where the court's framework leaves the answer genuinely open, embargos do require generative cognition. The adversarial paper is correct for this category.
- Establish empirically that Type A constitutes the majority of §489, §1º, IV violations in any specific court or jurisdiction. The empirical claim in §3.2 is a structural characterization of the predominant violation categories; data on actual distributions would confirm or narrow it.
- Contest the adversarial paper's description of the implicit rejection doctrine or its doctrinal accuracy.

---

## 6. Conditions Under Which This Support Would Fail

1. **If the empirical distribution of §489, §1º, IV violations in Brazilian appellate practice contains a large proportion of Type B cases:** If most actual §489, §1º, IV omissions involve framework-open questions requiring genuine new deliberation, Paper 1A's §5.3 claim ("no autonomous new cognition") would be misleading as a general characterization of the remedy. The defense holds only if Type A is the predominant category in practice.

2. **If binding-precedent and statutory-provision omissions are routinely less determinate than this paper assumes:** If precedent ratios are genuinely contested in most cases where they are invoked — such that applying the ratio to a new argument requires the court to form a new position on the ratio's scope — then the examples in §3.2 shade into Type B. The adversarial paper's hypothetical would then be representative, not exceptional.

3. **If the procedural/logical distinction in §3.5 is inoperative in Brazilian appellate practice:** If courts consistently treat the absence of a subsidiary petition for infringement effects as a reason to withhold modification, the procedural-consequence thesis fails in practice regardless of its doctrinal support. The reading of art. 1.024, §3º would then be an accurate account of the provision's design without describing actual procedural outcomes.

4. **If the adversarial paper's Type A/Type B distinction is not administrable even for clear cases:** If courts in practice are unable to reliably classify a §489, §1º, IV omission as Type A or Type B — even in the clear binding-precedent category — the classification framework provides no workable guidance and §5.3's claim lacks operational content. The recall/generative distinction would remain analytically valid but practically inert.
