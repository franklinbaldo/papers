# Exit 4 as Transparent Contestation: A Defense of Paper 1B Against Inference-Gap, Taxonomy-Incoherence, and Exit-5-Collapse Attacks

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "As Cinco Saídas Legítimas do Tribunal diante de Precedente Vinculante" (`paper1B_cinco_saidas_precedentes.md`), §3.4, defends the following:

> "A superação racional é a saída mais exigente e, ao mesmo tempo, a menos explorada. Ela consiste na superação do precedente pelo próprio tribunal — **inclusive o tribunal inferior ao tribunal-fonte** — mediante apontamento expresso de erro na ratio do precedente."

The thesis: inferior courts have Exit 4 as a legitimate response when facing a binding precedent whose ratio they have identified as erroneous — specifically, departure through transparent and express identification of the error, with submission to the reversal the system will produce.

This paper also defends the taxonomy's internal coherence against the attacks on Exit 5.

## 2. What This Support Adds

**Vector:** Direct defense. This paper responds to `otherwise/paper1b-rational-supersession.md`, which advances five attack vectors: (1) inference error — art. 489, §1º, VI establishes a procedural form condition, not substantive authorization to supersede STF binding precedents; (2) rational binding theory identifies what makes precedential force legitimate, not a decision procedure for who determines when it lapses; (3) constitutional monopoly — art. 102 CF vests the STF with *guarda da Constituição*, which no CPC provision can override; (4) anticipatory overruling from comparative law is predictive, not authoritative, while Exit 4 asserts direct rational authority; (5) Exit 5 coherence — no principled criterion distinguishes legitimate Exit 5 abstention from strategic *afastamento silencioso*.

Round 2 of the adversarial paper pressed three new challenges: (a) *taxonomy incoherence* — Exit 4 on the transparent-contestation account is itself "contrary to binding law," which precludes membership in a taxonomy of *saídas legítimas*; (b) *§4º misreading* — art. 927, §4º governs source-court self-modification, not inferior court departure; (c) *Exit 5 collapse* — the ratio-independence criterion reduces legitimate Exit 5 to sub-silentio Exit 2.

## 3. Reconstructing the Attack

### 3.1 The Inferential Gap (Adversarial §3.1, original)

Art. 489, §1º, VI defines when a departure is *unfounded* (negative sufficiency condition): a decision is not considered reasoned if it "fails to demonstrate the existence of distinction or supersession." By contraposition: a departure with demonstration is not unfounded. The adversarial paper argues this establishes only formal validity — the decision is a "reasoned decision" for purposes of art. 489 — not substantive authority. Art. 927's mandatory binding force ("observarão") is not suspended by the quality of the inferior court's reasoning; it persists until the STF modifies the precedent. The provision tells us *how* to depart (with demonstration); it does not authorize *that* inferior courts may depart with legal effect.

### 3.2 The Rational Binding Theory Does Not Supply Authorization (Adversarial §3.2)

Marinoni's formula — precedent as "razão que não pode ser desconsiderada sem razão mais forte" — characterizes what makes precedential force legitimate. The adversarial paper correctly notes the formula does not specify who determines when the threshold for a stronger reason has been crossed. Even if binding force derives from reasoning quality and a logical defect attenuates the ratio's normative force, the assessment of when the attenuation threshold is crossed requires an authoritative arbiter. The formula supports the inferior court's articulating a challenge; it does not authorize the inferior court to decide the challenge has succeeded.

The adversarial counter-reply adds: the STF's reversal of an Exit 4 departure need not address the departure reasons — it confirms the binding precedent applies and cassas the decision. The STF that reviews and reverses an Exit 4 departure is not "engaging with the challenge" in the relevant deliberative sense; it is making the authorized determination that the challenge does not succeed. The channel function presupposes that Exit 4 activates a discursive obligation; the adversarial paper argues the reversal proceeding does not activate it.

### 3.3 Constitutional Monopoly (Adversarial §3.3)

Art. 102 CF assigns the STF *guarda da Constituição*. For constitutional binding precedents — STF decisions in concentrated control, binding *súmulas* — an inferior court exercising rational supersession exercises a function the CF/88 reserves to the STF. Art. 489, §1º, VI is procedural; it cannot grant what the Constitution allocates elsewhere.

### 3.4 Anticipatory Overruling Is Predictive (Adversarial §3.4)

Anglo-American anticipatory overruling is strategic-predictive: the intermediate court aligns with the trajectory it predicts the supreme court will take, without asserting its own authority to decide the ratio is wrong. Exit 4 requires the inferior court to depart because *it* has identified the error, on the strength of *its own* rational assessment — a different and stronger claim.

### 3.5 Exit 5 Coherence Problem (Adversarial §3.5, original + counter-reply)

The paper defends Exit 5 (legitimate abstention from invocation) while condemning *afastamento silencioso*. The ratio-independence criterion supplied by this archive's first defense paper determines Exit 5 legitimacy by asking: would a court properly applying the binding precedent have been required, in resolving this specific case, to engage the precedent's ratio? The adversarial counter-reply argues this criterion collapses into Exit 2 (distinguishing): every case where Exit 5 is legitimate — where the resolution's grounds are genuinely independent of the precedent's question — is a case where careful distinguishing analysis would have concluded the precedent is inapplicable. Exit 5 on this criterion is sub-silentio Exit 2.

### 3.6 Taxonomy Incoherence (Adversarial counter-reply §3.1)

The adversarial counter-reply presses that the transparent-contestation reconceptualization is self-defeating. The defense accepts that Exit 4 produces a decision "contrary to binding law." A taxonomy titled "cinco saídas legítimas" cannot coherently include a response that the defense itself characterizes as contrary to binding law. The word "legítima" in the legal-procedural context refers to responses the legal system authorizes — not responses that meet formal requirements while being substantively erroneous. The normative difference between Exit 4 and *afastamento silencioso* is a difference in the quality of judicial *conduct*, not a difference in the legal *status* of the outcome. Conduct quality does not determine outcome authorization.

### 3.7 Art. 927, §4º Misreading (Adversarial counter-reply §3.1)

The adversarial counter-reply establishes that art. 927, §4º's grammatical and functional subject is *modificação* — modification of an established norm by the court that holds it. An inferior court departing from an STF or STJ binding precedent is not engaged in *modificação* under §4º; it does not hold those precedents and cannot modify them. Even if §4º were read more broadly, a form requirement cannot create authorization for a practice the substantive law prohibits. The original defense's reliance on §4º as a structural signal is therefore misplaced.

### 3.8 Exit 5 Collapse Elaborated (Adversarial counter-reply §3.5 + round 3 extension)

Exit 2 (distinguishing) applies when the precedent governs the same type of legal question the court faces, but the case facts fall outside the precedent's normatively significant fact-pattern. The ratio-independence criterion identifies Exit 5 legitimacy by asking whether the resolution's grounds engage the same question the precedent's ratio addresses. The adversarial paper argues this is the same determination as distinguishing, made without formal acknowledgment: every legitimate Exit 5 case is one where a careful Exit 2 analysis would have found the precedent inapplicable. Exit 5's legitimate instances are Exit 2 performed informally; its illegitimate instances are *afastamento silencioso*. No genuine third category is established.

Round 3 extends this with a structural comparator: the correct comparison is Exit 5 versus *extended Exit 2 scope*, not Exit 5 versus absence of Exit 5. Exit 2's threshold inquiry already concludes inapplicability in principle; this preliminary type-level step subsumes what Exit 5's ratio-independence criterion does. Exit 5 as a separate category is functionally redundant — it adds the same result (non-invocation) through a slightly different framing — while introducing an independent manipulation vector beyond what the Exit 2 threshold inquiry already carries. The defense's three responses to boundary instability (same risk type, statutory anchoring, Exit 5 vs. its absence) were addressed: the same-risk-type concession accumulates cumulative manipulation vectors; statutory anchoring fails at contested boundaries as the safety recall case shows; the correct alternative to Exit 5 is not its absence but extended Exit 2 threshold scope.

### 3.9 Authorization Asymmetry in the Exit 2/3 Analogy (Round 3, Fourth Ground)

The functional-taxonomy reading holds that "saídas legítimas" classifies recognized response types processed through ordinary channels rather than authorization profiles at the outcome level. Exits 2 and 3 are invoked as evidence: they appear in the "legitimate" category even when individual instances are incorrectly executed and reversed, demonstrating that the taxonomy operates at the response-type level. Exit 4 is of the same kind.

Round 3 presses that this analogy reveals an authorization asymmetry rather than supporting the defense. Exits 2 and 3 carry type-level authorization that Exit 4 lacks. The legal system authorizes distinguishing through art. 1.037, §§9º–13º CPC and presupposes it throughout arts. 926–927. It authorizes recognition of supervening supersession: a court that fails to apply the current binding norm violates art. 927. For Exits 2 and 3, the "recognized response type" and "authorized response type" criteria converge — there are correctly-executed instances that produce valid outcomes; the taxonomy's "legitimate" label tracks this type-level authorization. Exit 4 has no type-level authorization: no provision authorizes inferior courts to depart from binding precedents on the basis of their own assessment that the ratio is wrong; the defense concedes all instances will be reversed.

Under the functional-taxonomy criterion — "recognized response type processed through ordinary channels" — *afastamento silencioso* also qualifies: it too is reversed through ordinary appeal, not nullified. The *invalidade/incorreção* distinction sub-classifies within the unauthorized group (both are unauthorized; Exit 4 is less defective). It does not move Exit 4 into the authorized group alongside Exits 1–3 and 5. Exit 4 satisfies the response-type criterion but not the type-level authorization criterion; Exits 2 and 3 satisfy both.

### 3.10 Subsection V Parallel (Round 3, Fifth Ground)

The subsection III/VI structural disanalogy — that subsection VI is act-specific (governing departure from binding norms) while subsection III is act-neutral (governing any decision) — was offered by the defense to establish that art. 489, §1º, VI's form requirement places departure within the ordinary processing domain rather than among void acts. This establishes that Exit 4 is *ato incorreto*, not *ato inválido* — a point the adversarial paper accepts.

Round 3 introduces a parallel that the disanalogy does not address. Art. 489, §1º, V is also an act-specific form requirement — it governs the specific act of invoking a binding precedent: a decision is not considered reasoned if it invokes a precedent "without identifying its *fundamentos determinantes* nor demonstrating that the case under judgment fits those grounds." This is act-specific targeting of precedent invocation, not act-neutral application to all decisions. Under the defense's logic, subsection V's act-specific character would also place invocation "within the domain of acts subject to reasoning requirements" — but subsection V's form condition does not authorize any particular invocation to produce the outcome the invoking court seeks; a court may satisfy subsection V while invoking the wrong precedent, committing a reversible substantive error. Subsection VI operates identically: meeting its form condition makes the departure *fundamentada* without authorizing the departure to produce the result sought. The act-specific character of VI establishes that Exit 4 is reversible rather than void — category (b) rather than (a) — but does not address the subdivision of (b) into reversible-authorized acts (b1) and reversible-unauthorized acts (b2). Exits 2 and 3 are (b1); Exit 4 is (b2). The disanalogy addresses the (a)/(b) distinction but is silent on the (b1)/(b2) distinction where the authorization question lies.

### 3.11 Round 5: Criterion Clarification and Legislative-History Independence

Round 5 adds a sixth ground to §3.1 of the adversarial paper, responding directly to two arguments introduced in the round 4 defense.

**On the prospective-taxonomy argument.** The round 4 defense argued that "type-level authorization" is not a prospectively determinable criterion: whether a given Exit 2 instance is authorized depends on whether reviewing courts confirm the distinction, which is known only post-review. Round 5 establishes that this addresses the wrong version of the adversarial criterion. The adversarial criterion has always been type-level framework authorization (criterion B): whether the CPC 2015's enacted provisions, as in force before the court acts, authorize the TYPE of act. This is distinct from instance-level retrospective confirmation (criterion A): whether a specific instance will be confirmed by reviewing courts. Criterion (B) is fully prospectively determinable. At the time of decision, a court can check from the CPC's provisions whether distinguishing is authorized as a type (yes — art. 1.037, §§9º–13º confers type-level authorization from enactment), whether recognizing supervening supersession is authorized as a type (yes — art. 927's mandatory structure), and whether departing from a binding precedent by error-in-ratio argument is authorized as a type (no provision). The prospective-taxonomy argument's substantive import — that taxonomy criteria must be prospectively usable — is satisfied by criterion (B) itself. The prospective-taxonomy argument correctly defeats criterion (A); it does not reach criterion (B). Applying criterion (B) to Exit 4 produces the adversarial result prospectively: no enacted provision authorizes the act type.

**On the legislative-history asymmetry.** The round 4 defense argued that the CPC 2015's specification of form for departure in art. 489, §1º, VI resolved the historical contest about departure's legal status, establishing departure as a recognized practice within the framework. Round 5 establishes that art. 489, §1º, VI addresses the fundamentação question and art. 927 addresses the authorization question; these conditions are independently variable. A court can simultaneously satisfy art. 489, §1º, VI (producing a *fundamentada* departure with the required demonstration) and violate art. 927 (departing without modification authority). The historical authorization contest — whether inferior courts can depart — is an art. 927 question, not an art. 489, §1º, VI question. The legislature enacted both provisions simultaneously, addressing different legal questions. Art. 489, §1º, VI specifies form for departure without providing substantive authorization through art. 927 or any parallel provision. The legislature's awareness of the historical authorization contest — proven by the defense's own argument that the contest pre-existed the CPC 2015 — makes the omission of authorization from VI more significant: form-specification for a historically contested practice, without accompanying authorization, reflects the legislature's choice to regulate the form of conduct when it occurs while leaving authorization to the hierarchy provisions governing it.

### 3.12 Round 6: Criterion-Which-Criterion and Doctrinal-Gap Acknowledgment

Round 6 adds a seventh ground to §3.1 of the adversarial paper, responding directly to two arguments introduced in the round 5 defense.

**On the criterion-which-criterion question.** The criterion-clarification concession confirms that both criterion (B) and the functional-taxonomy reading's criterion are prospectively determinable from the CPC's text. Prospective determinability does not discriminate between the two criteria. A court can prospectively determine that Exit 4 has no type-level authorization under criterion (B) — no provision authorizes the type — and can prospectively determine that Exit 4 is within the ordinary processing domain under the functional criterion — it is, as VI's form requirement applies to it. Both criteria are prospectively operable from the same enacted text and reach different conclusions about Exit 4. The criterion-which-criterion question requires affirmative support for whichever dimension "legítima" is claimed to track, not a showing that both criteria are prospectively operable.

**On the two-dimensional decomposition.** Round 6 accepts the decomposition's architectural accuracy: VI addresses dimension (b); art. 927 addresses dimension (a); they are distinct legislative choices for distinct questions. But this precision does not establish why "legítima" tracks dimension (b) rather than dimension (a). The defense claims VI's resolution of dimension (b) supplies the structural signal for the functional-taxonomy reading. Round 6 presses that this names the dimension the defense relies on without arguing for why it is the organizing criterion. Dimension (b)'s resolution places Exit 4 in the ordinary processing domain alongside *afastamento silencioso*; the *invalidade/incorreção* distinction sub-classifies within that domain (Exit 4 less defective); the sub-classification does not bridge Exit 4 into the authorized group alongside Exits 1–3 and 5.

**On the doctrinal gap.** Eleven rounds of bilateral development. No primary Brazilian procedural source. Both readings — authorization-based (dimension a) and domain-status-based (dimension b) — are coherent interpretive options from the CPC's text. Both are prospectively determinable. Neither is established as definitively correct against the other by structural argument alone. Round 6 acknowledges this explicitly as the debate's structural ceiling.

### 3.13 Round 7: Exit 1 Demonstration (Eighth Ground)

Round 7 adds an eighth ground to §3.1 of the adversarial paper, targeting the provision-specificity argument introduced in the round 6 defense.

The provision-specificity argument has the form: (i) the taxonomy classifies response acts; (ii) art. 489, §1º, V and VI govern response acts; (iii) art. 927 governs the antecedent obligation, not the response acts; (iv) therefore the organizing criterion for "legítima" derives from art. 489's response-act classification framework, not from art. 927's authorization framework.

The eighth ground shows that this inference requires a premise the argument does not establish: that "legítima" inherits its organizing criterion from the provision governing the classified acts. Exit 1 tests this premise as the taxonomy's paradigm case.

Exit 1 — correct application of the binding precedent — is the uncontested paradigm "saída legítima." Its normative basis derives from art. 927's "observarão" mandate: courts must observe binding precedents. Art. 489, §1º, V specifies the form of correct Exit 1 performance: invocation must identify the *fundamentos determinantes* and demonstrate that the case fits those grounds. But Exit 1 is "legítima" because art. 927 requires courts to observe binding precedents — not because art. 489, §1º, V recognizes invocation-with-identification as a defined act type within the ordinary processing domain. Applied to Exit 1, provision-specificity produces "Exit 1 is *fundamentada*" — formally correct, but normatively incomplete. *Fundamentada* and *legítima* are derived from distinct provisions and are not equivalent.

The structural implication: if "legítima" tracks art. 927's authorization framework for the paradigm case, it tracks that framework throughout the taxonomy. The provision-specificity inference conflates two distinct questions: (i) which provision governs the form of the classified acts? — art. 489; and (ii) which provision constitutes the normative basis for whether those acts are *legítimas*? — art. 927, as Exit 1 demonstrates. Provision-specificity answers (i) and presents it as answering (ii). Question (ii) is the criterion-which-criterion question. The argument does not answer it; it re-presents (i) in provision-specific vocabulary while leaving (ii) open.

### 3.14 Round 8: Convergence Asymmetry and L2 Non-Autonomy (Ninth Ground)

Round 8 adds a ninth ground responding directly to the round 7 convergence-case/level-distinction compound defense.

**On convergence asymmetry.** The convergence-case observation is accepted as correct: Exit 1 cannot serve as a discrimination test between two co-primary criteria, because both apply simultaneously. But the eighth ground did not advance Exit 1 as a discrimination test. It advanced an asymmetry observation about normative derivation: art. 489's form conditions were designed to specify what correct compliance with art. 927's obligation looks like, so the convergence at Exit 1 is structural rather than coincidental — the form criterion tracks the compliance obligation when that obligation is correctly discharged. An asymmetric convergence (one criterion derivative of the other) cannot be blocked by observing that both criteria apply simultaneously; the asymmetry claim is about the relationship between the criteria, not about which criterion Exit 1 satisfies.

The asymmetry is demonstrated by the hypothetical of a form-compliant unauthorized act: a court that satisfies all art. 489, §1º, V form conditions (identifies *fundamentos determinantes*, demonstrates case fit) while departing from the result would be *fundamentada* in every respect while being unauthorized under art. 927. At such an act, the form criterion and the authorization criterion diverge — *fundamentada* but not *legítima*. If both criteria were co-primary (symmetric), they would converge at all acts satisfying either; if the form criterion is derivative of the authorization criterion (asymmetric), they diverge when the derivative is satisfied without the primary. Exit 4 is the real-case instance of this divergence: *fundamentada* under art. 489, unauthorized under art. 927.

**On L2 non-autonomy.** The level distinction is architecturally accurate — L1 (normative grounding: which provision creates the compliance obligation) and L2 (classification criterion: which response types are recognized exits) are distinct analytical levels. But the claim that L2 operates independently of L1 in this taxonomy is not established. "Saídas legítimas" is a compliance-response taxonomy: a taxonomy of responses to an art. 927 compliance situation. The compliance situation is constitutive of the taxonomy's subject matter — the exits are "saídas" only because there is an art. 927 obligation to navigate. In a compliance-response taxonomy, the classification criterion at L2 is constitutively compliance-relative: "legítima" means compliance-permissible — permissible given the compliance obligation structure — not merely form-recognized as a response type. A response that violates the compliance obligation is not "legítima" in this compliance-relative sense regardless of whether art. 489 recognizes its form within the ordinary processing domain.

The level distinction restates the provision-specificity inference in two-level vocabulary without establishing L2 autonomy. The eighth ground rejected the underlying inference from "art. 489 governs the classified acts" to "art. 489 provides the organizing concept for 'legítima'." The level distinction supplies the vocabulary of "L2 operates independently" in place of the missing premise. For L2 autonomy to hold, the defense must establish one of two things: (a) "saídas legítimas" is a form-classification taxonomy rather than a compliance-response taxonomy — which the paper's own framing of the exits as responses to the art. 927 compliance situation contradicts; or (b) "legítima" in a compliance-response taxonomy means form-recognized rather than compliance-permissible — which is the criterion-which-criterion question the level distinction was supposed to answer, not presuppose. The structural ceiling persists.

## 4. The Defense

### 4.1 "Legítima" in the Taxonomy Tracks Procedural Status, Not Outcome Authorization

The adversarial counter-reply's taxonomy-incoherence argument depends on a specific reading of "legítima": a response is *legítima* only if it produces an outcome the legal system authorizes (a legally valid, not-reversed disposition). On this reading, a response that is procedurally well-formed but substantively erroneous is not "legítima" regardless of its form compliance — it is merely a well-reasoned erroneous response.

Brazilian procedural doctrine does not support this reading as the unique sense of *legitimidade* for judicial conduct.

The relevant distinction is between *invalidade* and *incorreção*. An *ato inválido* (invalid act) is one that the legal system does not process within its ordinary error-correction structure — it may be declared null, may expose the issuing court to extraordinary remedies, and does not produce the ordinary effects of a valid judicial act. An *ato incorreto* (incorrect act) is one that is within the system's ordinary processing structure but produces the wrong outcome on the merits; it is subject to reversal through ordinary appeal. The legal system treats these categories differently in fundamental ways: invalidity triggers different remedies, different time limits, and different jurisdictional paths than incorrectness.

Exit 4 decisions are incorrect, not invalid. A court issuing an Exit 4 departure has not committed a *vício de fundamentação* under art. 489, §1º, VI — it has provided the demonstration that the provision requires. It is subject to reversal, not nullification. The court has not exposed itself to disciplinary sanction or to extraordinary nullification proceedings. Its decision is a *decisão recorrível* (appealable decision) processed normally through the appellate hierarchy.

*Afastamento silencioso* is different. A court committing *afastamento silencioso* has issued a departure without the demonstration art. 489, §1º, VI requires. It is both incorrect (the binding precedent was not observed) and potentially invalid (the fundamental reasoning defect under art. 489, §1º, VI may ground extraordinary nullification). The legal system treats this case differently from Exit 4: the departure is not only reversible but may be challenged as fundamentally defective reasoning.

The taxonomy maps these differences. An exit is "legítima" when it belongs to the set of responses the procedural system processes through ordinary mechanisms — not when it belongs to the set of responses that prevail on the merits. Exits 1, 2, 3, and 5 are responses with no expected reversal exposure on the precedent question (assuming proper execution). Exit 4 is a response with deliberate reversal exposure, procedurally well-formed, that the system processes through ordinary appeal. *Afastamento silencioso* is a response with both reversal exposure AND potential invalidity exposure, that may fall outside the ordinary processing structure.

The adversarial counter-reply's anticipated-reply section predicts this response and characterizes it as "confusing the evaluation of judicial conduct with the legal status of the judicial decision." This charge is answered by the *invalidade/incorreção* distinction: the taxonomy is not about conduct evaluation at all — it is about the procedural status of the decision under the system's own categorization. Exit 4 decisions are *incorretas* but not *inválidas*; this distinction in legal status — not merely in conduct quality — is what "legítima" tracks in the taxonomy's fourth entry.

The adversarial counter-reply's surrender condition 2 maps directly onto this analysis: "If the paper acknowledged that Exit 4 is not 'legitimate' in the same sense as Exits 1–3 and 5 — that it is a form-compliant deviation that is institutionally valuable without being authorized — the taxonomy's incoherence objection would be answered." The analysis above does exactly this: it acknowledges that Exit 4 is "legitimate" in a different sense (procedurally well-formed, within the ordinary processing structure, not invalid) from Exits 1–3 and 5 (authorized to prevail). The taxonomy has two senses of legitimacy, not one, and the taxonomy entry is correctly labeled because it occupies the less-demanding sense without confusion.

The adversarial paper's unified-criterion requirement rests on an undemonstrated premise: that "saídas legítimas" must classify entries by authorization to prevail. A taxonomy can coherently classify entries by a functional criterion — recognized response types to a binding-precedent invocation — without requiring identical authorization profiles across all entries. The adversarial paper asserts that the taxonomy classifies "by normative authorization status" but provides no textual or doctrinal anchor for this reading of the taxonomy's title. The equally available functional reading — the taxonomy classifies what courts do and what the procedural system recognizes as distinct response types — is supported by art. 489, §1º, VI's categorization of departure-with-demonstration as a regulated act within the system's ordinary processing domain.

This functional reading is confirmed by how the taxonomy treats Exits 2 and 3. Exit 2 (distinguishing) is a legitimate exit, but a court that incorrectly distinguishes — concluding facts fall outside the precedent's scope when they do not — issues an *ato incorreto* subject to reversal on the merits. The exit is "legitimate" not because every Exit 2 decision prevails but because distinguishing is a recognized response type that the system processes through defined channels. Exit 3 (recognizing supervening supersession) is analogously conditional: a court that incorrectly identifies supervening supersession issues an *ato incorreto* that will be reversed. Both entries occupy the "legitimate" category as recognized response types, not as unconditional authorizations to prevail. Moreover, the taxonomy's classification of Exits 2 and 3 as "legitimate" cannot depend on knowing that the reviewing court will confirm the classification — the taxonomy operates at the response-type level (what the court does), not at the review-outcome level (what the reviewing court decides). Exit 4 is of the same kind: transparent contestation-with-demonstration is a recognized response type processed through ordinary channels; individual instances are subject to reversal when the identified error is not confirmed — precisely as incorrectly-applied Exit 2 instances are subject to reversal when the distinguishing characterization is not confirmed.

Round 3's fourth ground responds that the Exit 2/3 analogy reveals an authorization asymmetry: Exits 2 and 3 carry type-level authorization that Exit 4 lacks. The legal system authorizes distinguishing via art. 1.037, §§9º–13º CPC; it authorizes recognition of supervening supersession through art. 927's mandatory structure. Exit 4 has no equivalent provision. And under the functional-taxonomy criterion alone — "recognized response type processed through ordinary channels" — *afastamento silencioso* also qualifies (reversed through ordinary appeal, not nullified). The *invalidade/incorreção* distinction only sub-classifies within the unauthorized group.

Two responses.

First, "type-level authorization" is not a prospectively determinable criterion. Whether a given Exit 2 instance is authorized — whether the distinction is valid and will be confirmed — is determined by the reviewing court after the fact. At decision time, no court knows whether its distinction will be confirmed or reversed. The taxonomy must classify response types prospectively, before review, because its function is to guide courts facing binding precedents in real time. A taxonomy organized by "does this type have authorized instances?" cannot function as action-guidance: it classifies types retrospectively based on review outcomes, not prospectively based on what courts are doing. The adversarial paper's "type-level authorization" criterion, applied consistently, would require courts to know reviewers' future outcomes before classifying their own response. This is not what the functional-taxonomy reading proposes, and it is not how doctrinal taxonomies function.

Exit 4's consistent reversal is not a type-level disqualification; it is an instance-level fact about a response type that the functional taxonomy tracks through its scope conditions (§6 of this paper) and failure conditions (§7). The consistent reversal is why Exit 4 occupies the "deliberate reversal exposure" position in the taxonomy — it is structurally asymmetric to Exits 2 and 3 in this respect, and the taxonomy states this asymmetry explicitly. What the taxonomy does not do is bar Exit 4 from the recognized-response-type category on that basis, for the same reason it would not bar incorrectly-executed Exit 2 from that category: the type is classified by what courts do, not by what reviewing courts confirm.

Second, *afastamento silencioso* does not qualify under the functional-taxonomy criterion because it is not a positive response type. Art. 489, §1º, VI defines it negatively: a decision that *fails to demonstrate* the existence of distinction or supersession is not reasoned. The provision names an absence — the failure to provide the required demonstration — not a positive conduct form. A taxonomy of recognized response types classifies what courts do when they respond to a binding-precedent invocation; *afastamento silencioso* describes what courts fail to do. The *invalidade/incorreção* distinction supplements this positive/negative distinction but does not replace it: Exit 4 is a positive act (departure accompanied by demonstration of error); *afastamento silencioso* is a departure by omission (departure without demonstration). The functional-taxonomy criterion's requirement of a positive recognized form excludes *afastamento silencioso* on grounds that precede and are independent of the *invalidade/incorreção* sub-classification.

Round 5 establishes that the adversarial criterion was always type-level framework authorization from enacted provisions (criterion B) — not instance-level retrospective confirmation (criterion A). This criterion clarification is accepted. The round 4 prospective-taxonomy argument correctly showed that criterion (A) — authorization determined by reviewing courts post-review — is not a workable prospective taxonomy criterion. The adversarial camp has clarified that criterion (B) — type-level authorization from enacted CPC provisions, determinable before the court acts — is what it has been deploying throughout. Criterion (B) is prospectively determinable, as round 5 correctly establishes: a court can determine before acting whether enacted provisions authorize distinguishing (yes), whether they authorize recognizing supervening supersession (yes), and whether they authorize inferior-court departure by error-in-ratio argument (no provision). The prospective-taxonomy argument addressed the wrong target.

This acceptance does not settle the core dispute. The contested question is not whether criterion (B) is prospectively determinable — it is — but whether criterion (B) is what "legítima" in the taxonomy tracks. The adversarial criterion (B) asks: does any enacted provision affirmatively authorize this act type? For Exit 4, the answer is no provision does. The defense's functional criterion asks: does the legislature's regulatory treatment of this act type categorize it as within the ordinary processing domain? For Exit 4, the answer comes from art. 489, §1º, VI's act-specific form requirement governing departure-with-demonstration. Both criteria are prospectively determinable from the same statutory text; they produce different results for the taxonomy classification of Exit 4. The criterion clarification demonstrates that criterion (B) is determinable — it does not establish that criterion (B) is what "legítima" requires. That question — which criterion the taxonomy uses — is the central disagreement that the criterion clarification does not resolve.

Round 6 introduces the seventh ground: the two-dimensional decomposition is architecturally accurate but does not argue for why "legítima" tracks dimension (b) rather than dimension (a). The affirmative case for dimension (b) as the organizing criterion is the provision-specificity argument.

The five-exits taxonomy is not a free-standing doctrinal construct organized around whatever criterion the interpreter prefers. It is constructed from the CPC 2015's own framework — specifically art. 489, §1º, V and VI, which together constitute the legislative specification of procedurally recognized response types for courts facing binding precedents. Subsection V governs invocation-type responses: a decision invoking a binding precedent must satisfy V's form requirement to count as *fundamentada*. Subsection VI governs departure-type responses: a decision departing from a binding norm must satisfy VI's demonstration requirement to count as *fundamentada*. The taxonomy's five entries map onto this legislative framework. Exits 1, 2, 3, and 5 involve courts making responses within the framework subsection V prescribes for invocation-type conduct (the court invokes correctly, distinguishes, recognizes supervening supersession, or resolves on grounds fully independent of the precedent's question). Exit 4 involves a court making a departure-type response within the framework subsection VI prescribes for departure-with-demonstration.

A taxonomy of judicial responses organized by the CPC 2015's own classification of those responses draws its organizing concept from the provisions specifically governing the response types — art. 489, §1º, V and VI — not from art. 927, which governs the antecedent obligation. Art. 927 creates binding force and defines who holds modification authority; it classifies courts by their position in the precedent hierarchy, not judicial responses by their procedural type. Art. 489, §1º is the CPC 2015's framework for classifying response types: it defines when a response satisfies the *fundamentação* requirement and, by doing so, recognizes specific judicial acts as distinct response types within the ordinary processing domain. "Saídas legítimas" in a taxonomy organized around what courts DO when facing binding precedents draws on the framework that classifies those acts — art. 489 — not on the framework that creates the antecedent obligation — art. 927.

This is the affirmative structural argument for dimension (b) as organizing criterion: the taxonomy's reference frame is art. 489's response-classification framework, which operates on domain-status principles. Art. 927 creates the taxonomy's subject matter (binding precedents that courts must address); art. 489 creates the framework within which the taxonomy's entries are recognized and classified as distinct procedural acts. "Legítima" in a taxonomy constructed from art. 489's framework tracks what art. 489 recognizes as a well-defined response type within the ordinary processing domain — not what art. 927 authorizes as a type-level outcome.

The adversarial seventh ground correctly notes that the two-dimensional decomposition names the dimension the defense relies on without arguing for why it is the organizing criterion. The provision-specificity argument supplies the missing step: because the taxonomy classifies responses (the domain of art. 489, §1º, V and VI), not the antecedent obligation (the domain of art. 927), the relevant organizing provision is art. 489, not art. 927. This is not a circular inference — it turns on which provision DIRECTLY GOVERNS the subject matter the taxonomy classifies. A taxonomy of invocation-and-departure acts is organized by the provision that governs invocation-and-departure acts; art. 489, §1º, V and VI are those provisions; they operate on domain-status principles; therefore "legítima" tracks domain-status recognition.

Round 6's doctrinal-gap acknowledgment is accepted as the accurate characterization of the structural ceiling. After eleven rounds, neither side has produced a primary Brazilian procedural source establishing what "saídas legítimas" constitutively requires. The provision-specificity argument and the arguments developed throughout this section characterize the functional-taxonomy reading as the more coherent structural interpretation under the available evidence: it draws on the provision that specifically governs the response types the taxonomy classifies; it explains why Exits 2 and 3 are "legítimas" without requiring authorization-to-prevail as the organizing criterion (they are recognized by art. 489's framework, as is Exit 4); and it accounts for *afastamento silencioso*'s exclusion through the positive/negative act distinction that precedes and is independent of any authorization inquiry. This structural case does not substitute for primary authority definitively resolving the criterion-which-criterion question. Both readings remain coherent interpretive options from the CPC's enacted text.

The eighth ground tests the provision-specificity argument against the taxonomy's paradigm case. The demonstration's inferential structure has a gap.

The Exit 1 demonstration uses a convergence case: Exit 1 is a taxonomy entry where both the provision-specificity criterion (art. 489 classifies Exit 1 as a recognized response type) and the adversarial authorization criterion (art. 927 requires Exit 1) apply simultaneously. The adversarial infers from this convergence that art. 927 is the organizing criterion for "legítima" throughout the taxonomy. For the inference to discriminate between the two criteria — to establish art. 927 as the organizing criterion rather than merely one sufficient grounding for Exit 1's legitimacy in the paradigm case — the demonstration must show one of two things: (a) that art. 489's classification criterion does NOT apply to Exit 1, or (b) that there is independent grounds for preferring the art. 927 grounding over art. 489's classification criterion in the paradigm case. The demonstration establishes neither.

On (a): Exit 1 satisfies the provision-specificity criterion. Art. 489, §1º, V recognizes invocation-with-identification as a procedurally classified response type — it specifies the form under which a precedent invocation is *fundamentada*, placing invocation-with-identification within the domain of acts subject to ordinary reasoning requirements. This is the same analysis that places Exit 4 in the ordinary processing domain through art. 489's act-specific form requirements. The provision-specificity criterion applies to Exit 1 as it applies to all five taxonomy entries. The demonstration does not show otherwise.

On (b): the adversarial says "Exit 1 is legítima because art. 927 requires courts to observe binding precedents." This is true. An equally coherent account: "Exit 1 is legítima because art. 489's classification framework recognizes invocation-with-identification as a properly-formed response type in the compliance situation art. 927 creates." These accounts describe different dimensions of the same case without excluding each other. Art. 927 explains why courts must comply with binding precedents; art. 489 classifies which response types count as recognized options for that compliance. The demonstration selects art. 927 as the grounding without showing that art. 489's classification criterion does not independently apply.

The adversarial's *fundamentada/legítima* distinction is accepted: art. 489's form requirements produce a *fundamentada* decision; art. 927's obligation framework constitutes the normative basis for Exit 1's obligatory status. The provision-specificity argument was never claiming *fundamentada* = *legítima* or that art. 489 provides the normative basis for each exit's obligatory status. The provision-specificity argument claims that the CLASSIFICATION CRITERION for "legítima" in the taxonomy — which response types count as recognized exits — is organized by art. 489's response-act classification framework. A decision is *legítima* in the taxonomy when it belongs to the set of response types art. 489's framework recognizes as ordinary processing domain acts for the compliance situation art. 927 creates. The eighth ground accepts that *fundamentada* and *legítima* are distinct predicates derived from distinct provisions — which is correct — but applies this observation at the level of normative grounding rather than at the level of the taxonomy's classification criterion. The provision-specificity argument operates at the latter level.

The taxonomy's function provides structural support for this reading. "Saídas legítimas" classifies response OPTIONS — the recognized ways of responding to a precedent invocation. Art. 927 tells courts they must comply; the taxonomy identifies which exit forms compliance can legitimately take. A taxonomy of exit options is organized around what constitutes a recognized option type — the classification question — not around what makes compliance obligatory — the obligation question. Art. 489's classification framework identifies which response types qualify as recognized option types; art. 927 creates the compliance obligation that frames the situation in which those options become relevant. "Legítima" in a taxonomy of exit options tracks the former classification, not the latter obligation.

The structural ceiling is unchanged: both the art. 927 authorization criterion and the art. 489 classification criterion are coherent interpretive options for what "legítima" constitutively requires in the taxonomy. The Exit 1 demonstration correctly observes that art. 927 grounds Exit 1's obligatory status; it does not establish that this grounding identifies art. 927 as the organizing criterion for the taxonomy's classification concept, because both criteria apply to Exit 1 simultaneously and the demonstration does not discriminate between them. Absent primary doctrinal authority establishing which criterion "saídas legítimas" constitutively requires, the provision-specificity argument remains structurally available.

The ninth ground adds two connected arguments to the adversarial's structural case. Each requires a separate response.

**On convergence asymmetry.** The adversarial's asymmetry claim rests on a design-relationship premise: art. 489 form conditions specify what correct compliance with art. 927 looks like, so art. 489 tracks art. 927, making the form criterion derivative of the authorization criterion. This design-relationship premise is accurate for the authorized exits: art. 489 was designed to specify the form of correct compliance performance for Exits 1, 2, and 3. But design relationship does not establish criterion-subordination. A provision can be designed to specify correct compliance while independently generating classification results for acts that lie outside the authorized domain. Art. 489, §1º, VI produces a classification result for Exit 4 — placing departure-with-demonstration within the ordinary processing domain rather than outside the procedural system entirely — regardless of whether Exit 4 is authorized. That independent classification function is not reducible to specifying how to comply correctly; it addresses the domain-status dimension for an act whose authorization status is separately governed.

The form-compliant unauthorized act hypothetical is intended to demonstrate asymmetry: form can be satisfied without authorization, showing the form criterion is derivative (can be triggered without the primary condition). But a genuinely derivative criterion — one that operates only when the primary is satisfied — could not be satisfied independently. The hypothetical demonstrates the opposite: form conditions can be satisfied independent of authorization. This is evidence of criterion-independence, not of criterion-hierarchy. An "asymmetry" where the derivative criterion is fully satisfiable without the primary is not a derivation; it is an independent regulatory variable. The hypothesis that proves independence cannot simultaneously establish hierarchy.

Exit 4 itself confirms this: it is *fundamentada* under art. 489, §1º, VI (the form condition for departure-with-demonstration is satisfied) and unauthorized under art. 927 (no provision authorizes the departure type). Both criteria apply to Exit 4 independently. If the form criterion were derivative of the authorization criterion, being *fundamentada* without being authorized would be either impossible or unintelligible. It is neither. Two independent criteria that can each be satisfied separately are not in a primary/derivative relationship; they are in an independence relationship. The ninth ground correctly shows that Exit 4 is the real-case instance of form/authorization divergence; it incorrectly characterizes this divergence as asymmetric rather than as bilateral independence.

**On L2 non-autonomy.** The ninth ground's L2 argument moves from the taxonomy's subject matter (responses to an art. 927 compliance situation) to its classification criterion (compliance-permissibility). These are distinct. A taxonomy whose subject matter is a compliance situation can use any criterion to classify the responses within that situation. The adversarial's move requires the premise that a compliance-situation subject matter determines compliance-permissibility as the classification criterion. This premise is a substantive claim about the paper's taxonomy that requires independent support; it is not a logical necessity.

The paper's taxonomy is jointly constituted by two provisions operating at different analytical levels: art. 927 creates the compliance situation that generates the five exits as situations requiring response (the subject matter); art. 489 provides the exit-recognition framework that classifies which responses the CPC 2015 recognizes as distinct response types with defined form conditions (the classification criterion). "Saídas" derives from art. 927's compliance obligation — courts have binding precedents to navigate; "legítimas" derives from art. 489's recognition framework — the CPC identifies which responses are distinct recognized types. The adversarial assumes art. 927 provides both the subject matter and the classification criterion. The provision-specificity argument maintains that the subjects are split: art. 927 provides the compliance situation generating the subject matter; art. 489 provides the classification criterion determining which responses are recognized exits.

The adversarial says L2 is not autonomous in this specific taxonomy because the compliance situation is constitutive of the subject matter. But subject-matter constitution and criterion-provision are distinct questions. Even granting that "saídas legítimas" is a compliance-response taxonomy — a taxonomy organized around responses to an art. 927 compliance situation — the classification criterion can still derive from the provision governing response acts (art. 489) rather than from the provision governing the antecedent obligation (art. 927). What makes a response a *recognized* response type is a question the provision governing response acts (art. 489) answers; what makes the response situation a compliance situation is a question the provision governing the antecedent obligation (art. 927) answers. L2 autonomy holds when the former and the latter are answered by different provisions — which is the functional-taxonomy reading's claim.

The adversarial's alternative — that for L2 autonomy to hold, the defense must show either that "saídas legítimas" is not a compliance-response taxonomy or that "legítima" in a compliance taxonomy means form-recognized — presents a false dilemma. A third option is available: the taxonomy is a compliance-response taxonomy (subject matter defined by the compliance situation) AND uses form-recognition as its classification criterion (because the provision governing response acts, art. 489, provides the recognition framework). These are not incompatible. A taxonomy can have a compliance-situation subject matter and a form-recognition classification criterion. The adversarial's dilemma assumes that compliance-situation subject matter entails compliance-permissibility classification criterion — which is the contested premise.

The structural ceiling acknowledged throughout persists after the ninth ground. Both the convergence-asymmetry argument and the L2 non-autonomy argument reinforce the adversarial structural case without reaching primary Brazilian procedural authority establishing what "saídas legítimas" constitutively requires. The ninth ground's most sophisticated advance is the observation that the compliance situation is constitutive of the taxonomy's subject matter — this is a genuine structural point that sharpens the adversarial case. It does not resolve the criterion-which-criterion question, because the criterion question (what "legítimas" tracks) and the subject-matter question (what the taxonomy is about) remain distinct, and the adversarial must still establish that subject-matter constitution determines classification criterion.

### 4.2 Art. 489, §1º, VI Presupposes the Practice It Regulates

The adversarial counter-reply establishes that art. 927, §4º governs source-court self-modification and does not extend to inferior court departure from superior courts' binding norms. This argument is correct. The original defense's reliance on §4º as a structural signal is withdrawn.

The structural signal comes from art. 489, §1º, VI itself. A negative form requirement — "a decision is not considered reasoned if it fails to demonstrate X" — necessarily contemplates the affirmative case: a decision that demonstrates X is a decision that meets the reasoning standard. The provision would be rendered effectively nugatory if "demonstrating supersession" produced no legal consequence — if a departure with demonstration of supersession were categorically equivalent to a departure without demonstration, there would be no operative sense in which the provision distinguishes the two.

The provision specifies a form for a type of departure (departure with demonstration of supersession). By specifying this form, the CPC 2015 places departures-with-demonstration within the domain of acts subject to ordinary reasoning requirements, not outside the procedural system entirely. This is not a grant of authorization to prevail; it is a categorization decision that places the act within the ordinary domain rather than treating it as categorically prohibited. If departures from binding precedent were categorically prohibited rather than merely incorrect, art. 489, §1º, VI would not specify the form under which they are reasoned — it would prohibit them without a prescribed form.

The adversarial round 2 counter draws a parallel with art. 489, §1º, III: just as case-specific reasoning satisfies subsection III's form requirement without authorizing any case-specifically-reasoned conclusion, departure-with-demonstration satisfies subsection VI's form requirement without authorizing the departure. This parallel fails structurally. Subsection III governs reasoning quality for any decision — it is act-neutral: it specifies when reasoning-that-uses-indeterminate-concepts is defective and applies equally to authorized acts and unauthorized ones, to legally correct decisions and legally erroneous ones. Its presupposition (if case-specific, then not generically defective) says nothing about the act's systemic status; any act can be reasoned generically or specifically regardless of whether it is authorized. Subsection VI is categorically different: it governs a specific act — departure from a binding norm — and specifies the form under which that departure satisfies the *fundamentação* requirement. This act-specific form specification carries an implication that act-neutral quality requirements do not. *Fundamentação* requirements are imposed on acts within the system's ordinary processing domain; an act that is categorically prohibited — void, outside the ordinary structure — does not require *fundamentação* because it is simply null. The specification of a *fundamentação* standard for departure-with-demonstration necessarily implies that departure occupies the domain of acts the system subjects to reasoning requirements. The subsection III parallel, because it concerns act-neutral reasoning quality, carries no such implication about systemic status.

The adversarial paper's general principle — "a form requirement can only regulate practices that are otherwise legally authorized; it cannot create authorization for practices the substantive law prohibits" — is sound as a general principle but does not apply to art. 489, §1º, VI in the relevant way. The provision does not purport to authorize departures; it categorizes them. Acts can be within the legal system's ordinary processing domain (subject to ordinary form requirements, ordinary remedies, ordinary appeal) without being authorized to produce the outcome sought. The form requirement for departure-with-demonstration reflects the CPC 2015's decision to process departures ordinarily, not to prohibit them. That decision is the structural signal Exit 4 relies on.

Round 3's fifth ground introduces a closer parallel. Art. 489, §1º, V is also an act-specific form requirement — it governs precedent invocation specifically, not all decisions: a decision is not considered reasoned if it invokes a precedent without identifying its *fundamentos determinantes* or demonstrating that the case fits those grounds. By the defense's logic, subsection V's act-specific character should also place invocation "within the domain of acts subject to reasoning requirements" in a way that implies something beyond a reasoning condition. But subsection V does not authorize any particular invocation to prevail: a court that satisfies subsection V's form condition while invoking the wrong precedent reasons adequately under subsection V while committing a substantive error. Subsection VI operates identically: meeting its form condition makes the departure *fundamentada* without authorizing the departure to succeed. Both are act-specific reasoning conditions; neither is an authorization condition.

The fifth ground correctly identifies the (b1)/(b2) gap in the subsection III/VI disanalogy. The disanalogy establishes that Exit 4 is reversible rather than void — category (b) rather than (a). It does not distinguish reversible-authorized acts (b1) from reversible-unauthorized acts (b2). Exit 4 is (b2); the subsection III/VI contrast only addresses the (a)/(b) distinction.

The response does not rest on the disanalogy alone. Subsection V confirms that act-specific reasoning conditions do not imply authorization — but it does not reach the more fundamental difference between subsections V and VI. Invocation of binding precedents (§V) was never in contested systemic status: it has always been an expected judicial function, uncontested in doctrine. Departure from binding precedents (§VI) was historically in contested systemic status: before the CPC 2015, reasoning-free departures were treated by some courts as per se nullifiable, by others as merely reversible. The CPC 2015's decision to regulate departure through a form-specific provision — rather than prohibiting it categorically or ignoring it — resolves this contested status by placing departure within the reversible-acts domain. The legislative act of specifying a reasoning form for departure is informationally different from the legislative act of specifying a reasoning form for invocation, because departure's systemic status was contested and invocation's was not. Subsection V regulates an uncontested practice; subsection VI resolves a contested one. This resolution is the structural signal the defense relies on, not subsection V's parallel act-specificity.

The fifth ground's central claim is correct as a formal matter: reasoning conditions and authorization conditions are independently variable; subsection VI establishes the former and is silent on the latter. What the fifth ground does not address is whether the taxonomy's classification criterion is authorization or recognition. The defense's position — contested since the first round — is that "legítima" in the taxonomy's functional reading tracks recognition as a regulated positive response type, not authorization to prevail. On this reading, the subsection V parallel confirms rather than contests the defense: subsection V's act-specific form makes invocation a recognized positive response type; subsection VI's act-specific form makes departure-with-demonstration a recognized positive response type. Neither provision authorizes its governed act to prevail; both recognize the act as belonging to the regulated domain. "Legítima" tracks this recognition, not the downstream authorization question.

Round 5 adds a legislative-history independence argument: the legislature was aware of the historical authorization contest and chose to address only fundamentação through art. 489, §1º, VI, leaving authorization to art. 927's terms. The omission is significant because the legislature had the authorization question before it and did not resolve it through VI; form-specification is not authorization.

The historical contest about departure's legal status before the CPC 2015 had two distinct dimensions: (a) whether inferior-court departure from binding precedents is hierarchically authorized — an authorization question about who holds modification authority; and (b) whether departures-without-demonstration are *inválidas* (subject to nullification and extraordinary challenge) or merely *incorretas* (subject to ordinary reversal through appeal) — a domain-status question about where in the procedural system departure belongs. Before the CPC 2015, both dimensions were genuinely contested. Courts differed about whether reasoning-free departures were per se nullifiable (outside the ordinary processing structure) or merely reversible (within it). Art. 927 addresses dimension (a): it creates binding force and allocates modification authority to source courts, governing hierarchical authorization. Art. 489, §1º, VI addresses dimension (b): by specifying the form under which departure is not-unfounded, it categorizes departure-with-demonstration as within the ordinary processing domain — a *decisão incorreta* subject to ordinary appeal, not a departure potentially exposable to extraordinary nullification.

Round 5 establishes that the legislature was aware of dimension (a) and that VI's omission on dimension (a) is significant. This is accepted: VI is silent on authorization; the defense has never claimed VI provides authorization. But the defense's structural signal operates on dimension (b), not dimension (a). VI resolved dimension (b) by categorizing departure-with-demonstration within the ordinary processing domain. The defense's claim — that "legítima" tracks domain status (ordinary processing domain) under the functional-taxonomy reading — is grounded in VI's resolution of dimension (b). Round 5's argument proves the significant omission on dimension (a); it does not address VI's affirmative resolution of dimension (b). A legislative design in which art. 927 governs dimension (a) while VI governs dimension (b) is consistent with VI's categorization signal being directed at dimension (b) specifically: each provision addresses a different dimension of the same historical contest, and VI's resolution of the domain-status question is neither negated nor absorbed by art. 927's separate address of the authorization question.

### 4.3 The Rational Binding Theory's Channel Function Operates Through Deliberative Accumulation

The adversarial counter-reply correctly establishes that the STF's reversal of an Exit 4 departure does not require engagement with the inferior court's departure reasons: the reversal applies the binding precedent under art. 927's mandatory force without addressing the ratio challenge. The reversal proceeding is not the site of the discursive engagement Exit 4 aims to produce.

The channel function operates at a different level. An Exit 4 departure enters the judicial record as a formally structured challenge to the ratio — available as antecedent material in subsequent proceedings, available as the basis for authorized petitions for reconsideration, and available as part of the deliberative context in which the STF's reclamação and review proceedings are conducted. The discursive obligation Marinoni's formula generates — the STF's obligation to engage challenges to its precedents — is triggered by the accumulation of formally structured challenges that Exit 4 contributes to, not by any individual reversal proceeding.

The adversarial paper's point about authorized procedures is accepted: the proximate mechanism for triggering the STF's deliberative engagement is authorized petitions for reconsideration and review, not the Exit 4 departure itself. Exit 4 contributes to this mechanism by making the strongest possible challenge available in the record from which authorized petitions are constructed. A system in which inferior courts commit *afastamento silencioso* generates no such record; a system in which inferior courts exercise Exit 4 generates the most analytically complete record available for the petitions through which deliberative obligation is properly triggered.

This reading is consistent with the adversarial paper's own account of the channel's proper operation: it concedes that the deliberative response "runs through authorized petitions for reconsideration and review proceedings." Exit 4 is not in competition with those proceedings; it contributes to the quality of what those proceedings receive.

### 4.4 The Constitutional Monopoly Constraint Is Accepted and Defines Exit 4's Domain

The adversarial §3.3 argument is accepted for STF constitutional binding precedents. Art. 102 CF's allocation of constitutional interpretive authority to the STF is a constitutional provision that cannot be modified by art. 489, §1º, VI. An inferior court that exercises Exit 4 against an STF holding on a constitutional question exercises a function the CF/88 reserves to the STF. Exit 4 is unavailable for this class of precedents.

This is a domain restriction, not a defeat. The adversarial paper concedes the position is "less direct" for infraconstitutional STJ precedents. Paper 1B's practical operation lies precisely in the infraconstitutional domain: the STJ's repetitive resources on federal statutory interpretation questions under art. 927 produce the binding precedents with which most inferior courts most frequently interact. For these precedents, art. 105 CF assigns the STJ the function of "unification of federal law interpretation" — but this allocation does not carry the same constitutional exclusivity that art. 102 CF's *guarda da Constituição* creates for the STF on constitutional questions.

The adversarial counter-reply notes that "the same structural argument applies" to the STJ. This is too fast. Art. 102 CF's specific grant of *guarda da Constituição* creates an interpretive monopoly on constitutional questions because the CF/88 itself establishes a hierarchy of interpretive authority for constitutional meaning. Art. 105 CF's grant to the STJ is a jurisdictional grant for legal unification purposes — it does not establish a constitutional monopoly on the meaning of federal statutory law in the same way. Inferior courts that contest an STJ statutory-interpretation precedent are not contesting a constitutionally allocated interpretive function; they are contesting the STJ's current best reading of a federal statute. The statutory reading is revisable; the constitutional allocation of interpretive authority is not.

Exit 4's proper domain is therefore infraconstitutional precedents — primarily STJ interpretive precedents under art. 927, II–V.

### 4.5 The Anticipatory Overruling Comparison Is a Useful Partial Analogy

The adversarial paper is correct that Exit 4 is not identical to Anglo-American anticipatory overruling. Anticipatory overruling is predictive: the intermediate court aligns with the trajectory it expects the supreme court to take. Exit 4 does not require prediction; the inferior court may believe the STF will affirm its prior position while still exercising Exit 4 to create the record of challenge.

But the adversarial paper's conclusion — that the distinction undermines Exit 4 — does not follow. The comparison to anticipatory overruling was invoked in Paper 1B §7.2 as a *parallel* for the existence of transparent departure mechanisms in hierarchical systems, not as an identical analogue. The point is structural: hierarchical precedent systems in mature jurisdictions have recognized that allowing transparent departure, even with certain reversal, serves the system's deliberative health. The form differs across systems; the structural function — creating a record of qualified challenge that the apex court must eventually address — is shared.

Exit 4 is a Brazilian-law adaptation of this structural function: not predictive deference, but transparent challenge under the CPC's own art. 489, §1º, VI framework. Whether this constitutes "anticipatory overruling" in the technical sense is a labeling question; whether it constitutes a constructive participation in the system's error-correction under the terms the CPC makes available is the substantive question.

### 4.6 Exit 5: Precedent-Scope Independence Is Not Reducible to Factual Distinguishing

The ratio-independence criterion determines Exit 5 legitimacy by asking: would a court properly applying the binding precedent have been required, in resolving this specific case, to engage the precedent's ratio? The adversarial counter-reply argues this collapses into Exit 2 because every case where Exit 5 is legitimate is a case where careful distinguishing analysis would have concluded the precedent is inapplicable — making legitimate Exit 5 sub-silentio Exit 2.

This collapse argument rests on an implicit assumption that is false in the relevant class of cases.

Exit 2 (distinguishing) and Exit 5 (legitimate abstention) apply to structurally different situations. Exit 2 applies when the binding precedent covers the *same type of legal question* the court is resolving, but the case's facts fall outside the precedent's normatively significant fact-pattern. The presupposition of distinguishing is that the precedent is applicable in principle to the type of question — it governs cases of this kind — but inapplicable to this particular case given its facts. Distinguishing is a technique for cases where the precedent's ratio and the resolution's grounds are oriented toward the same legal question.

Exit 5 applies when the binding precedent governs a *different legal question* from the one the court is resolving. In this situation, the precedent is not applicable in principle; it does not govern the type of legal question the court faces. No distinguishing analysis is required or available, because distinguishing presupposes the precedent addresses the same type of question with different facts.

A concrete case distinguishes the two. Suppose a binding STJ precedent establishes the limitations period for contractor liability actions under the CDC. A court resolves a dispute about whether a contractor fulfilled its quality-of-performance obligation under the CDC — specifically, whether the work performed met the contractual standard. The limitations precedent governs a legal question about when the action expires; the court is resolving a question about whether the obligation was fulfilled. These are different legal questions: the former concerns temporal admissibility, the latter concerns merits of the obligation's performance. No distinguishing analysis applies — the limitations precedent does not govern the quality-of-performance question. The court resolves the case without the limitations precedent ever becoming the relevant framework. This is legitimate Exit 5.

The adversarial collapse argument would say: careful distinguishing analysis would have concluded the limitations precedent is inapplicable. But this is not what distinguishing does. Distinguishing concludes a precedent is inapplicable *to these facts* while the precedent *governs this type of question*. A case where the precedent doesn't govern the type of question at all requires not distinguishing but a scope determination — identifying that the precedent's ratio addresses a different question than the court faces. This is Exit 5, not sub-silentio Exit 2.

The collapse argument's implicit assumption: for any binding precedent P and any case C, if P's ratio is genuinely independent of C's grounds, then there exists a distinguishing analysis that would have concluded P is inapplicable to C. This assumption holds only when P and C address the same legal question with different facts. It does not hold when P and C address different legal questions — which is precisely the class of cases Exit 5's ratio-independence criterion governs.

The practical consequence is that Exit 2 and Exit 5 generate different reviewing-court inquiries. Exit 2 review asks: given that P addresses the same question as C, do C's facts fall within P's normatively significant fact-pattern? Exit 5 review asks: does P address the same legal question as C? These are distinct inquiries. A court that commits *afastamento silencioso* against P (P governs C's question; C's facts are within P's scope; the court resolved without invoking P) would fail Exit 2 review. A court that legitimately exercises Exit 5 (P does not govern C's question) would pass Exit 5 review. A court that mischaracterizes its question as different from P's to avoid invoking P would fail Exit 5 review (the questions are not genuinely different). No case that passes Exit 5 review is a case that should have been processed through Exit 2 analysis — because Exit 5 review asks whether P governs the question at all, and Exit 2 review presupposes it does.

The adversarial round 2 boundary instability attack targets this analysis at the contested-characterization level: it concedes the structural distinction for clearly separated question types but argues that question-type characterization is a legal determination subject to strategic abstraction-level selection, illustrated by the safety recall case (art. 26, §3º CDC product limitations vs. art. 6, III CDC duty of information). Three responses limit this attack.

First, Exit 2's fact-pattern characterization is subject to the same strategic pressure — courts can mischaracterize facts to distinguish — and the adversarial paper accepts Exit 2 as a legitimate taxonomy entry. The manipulation concern is not distinctive to Exit 5; it is a general feature of precedent-based distinctions that the taxonomy already accepts at the Exit 2 level. Adding Exit 5 does not introduce a new type of manipulation risk; it introduces the same type of risk at a different analytical level. Whether this risk is acceptable is a question about the taxonomy as a whole, not a specific objection to Exit 5 in particular.

Second, the adversarial paper claims question-type characterization is "harder to police" than fact-pattern characterization without demonstrating this claim. Exit 5 review asks whether the precedent's ratio governs the legal question the case turns on — an inquiry anchored in the statutory provision the precedent interprets, which is typically explicit in Brazilian civil law. A precedent interpreting art. 26, §3º CDC addresses a different statutory provision from a question arising under art. 6, III CDC; this statutory anchoring constrains characterization in ways the adversarial paper's "same strategic pressure" framing overlooks. The safety recall case is genuinely ambiguous because both provisions could plausibly apply to the underlying conduct — but this is structural ambiguity in the CDC's drafting, not a distinctive failure of the Exit 5 criterion. Exit 2 faces the same ambiguity in the safety recall case: it also requires determining whether the recall failure triggers art. 26's defect framework or art. 6's information-right framework. "Harder to police at the boundary" applies equally to both exits in this case.

Third, the correct comparison point is Exit 5 versus its absence, not Exit 5 versus a manipulation-free standard. Without Exit 5 as a recognized response type, courts facing genuinely different-question-type cases have only two options: perform a pointless Exit 2 distinguishing analysis for a precedent that does not govern their question, or resolve without engaging the precedent and face potential *afastamento silencioso* characterization. Exit 5 provides a recognized path that is functionally superior in the paradigm cases — where question types are clearly distinct — and no worse than Exit 2 in the boundary cases, where both exits face the same strategic characterization pressures and the same reviewing-court inquiry into whether the characterization is accurate.

Round 3 responds to the third point specifically by proposing a different comparator: Exit 5 versus *extended Exit 2 scope*. Exit 2's threshold inquiry already asks whether the binding precedent is applicable in principle; this preliminary type-level determination subsumes the ratio-independence criterion. Exit 5 as a separate category is functionally redundant while adding a second manipulation vector on top of Exit 2's existing one.

This is a definitional collapse rather than a structural one. If Exit 2's "threshold inquiry" asks whether the precedent's ratio governs the type of legal question the case turns on, then what round 3 calls "extended Exit 2 scope" is structurally identical to what the defense calls Exit 5: same question, same criterion, same reviewing-court inquiry. Absorbing Exit 5 into Exit 2's threshold step does not eliminate the category; it reclassifies it under Exit 2's label without changing what courts must determine or what reviewing courts must ask. The adversarial paper must identify a functional difference between "Exit 2's type-level threshold inquiry" and "Exit 5" — some operation the former performs or outcome it produces that differs from the latter. No such difference has been identified. Both ask: does this precedent's ratio address the type of legal question this case turns on? Both produce the same result: invocation is required (and the court must proceed via Exit 1, 2, or 3) or invocation is not required (Exit 5). Round 3's cumulative-manipulation-vector argument applies equally under either label: if the type-level determination is within Exit 2, it adds a manipulation vector to Exit 2; if it is a separate Exit 5, it adds one there. The manipulation concern provides no grounds for preferring the definitional absorption.

## 5. Anticipated Objection

**The scope limitation still generates a labeling problem.** The adversarial counter-reply's taxonomy-incoherence argument, even if answered by the *invalidade/incorreção* distinction in §4.1, remains troubling as a claim about the paper's title. A taxonomy of "cinco saídas legítimas" in which entry four is "legitimate" in a weaker sense (within the ordinary processing domain, subject to reversal) than entries one, two, three, and five (authorized to prevail, not subject to reversal on this question) carries a risk of misleading readers who take "legítima" uniformly.

This objection has force at the communication level. The response is that the taxonomy's own text — specifically §4.4 and §7.2 of Paper 1B, and the conditions listed in §7 of this paper — already signal the asymmetry. The "legitimacy" of Exit 4 is explicitly conditioned on acceptance of reversal and transparent articulation of the error. Readers who understand the taxonomy know Exit 4 will be reversed; the label "legítima" in that context cannot be read as a claim that the departure will stand. Whether the paper's exposition communicates this clearly enough is a question about the presentation of the taxonomy, not its logical coherence. A cleaner paper would likely label Exit 4 explicitly as "legitimate as to conduct, incorrect as to outcome" — but the logical structure that makes this coherent is available in the paper's own text.

**The Exit 5 different-question criterion is manipulable.** Courts can always characterize their question as "different" from the binding precedent's question to avoid invoking it. This is the same difficulty that attends any application of a legal standard requiring characterization of grounds.

The response: the difficulty is not a defect in the criterion but a general feature of legal reasoning, present equally in Exit 2 (courts can mischaracterize facts to distinguish) and in all of art. 489's reasoning requirements (courts can misstate their grounds). The criterion gives reviewing courts a precise question to ask — does P govern the legal question C's grounds actually address? — that differs from both the exit-2 question (do C's facts fall within P's scope?) and the sincerity question (did the court honestly believe its grounds were independent?). Appellate review is competent to answer this question, as it is to answer the exit-2 question. The manipulability is no greater than elsewhere in the reasoning-adequacy domain.

**The provision-specificity argument conflates reasoning quality with authorization.** Art. 489 governs the quality of reasoning for judicial responses; art. 927 governs whether those responses are authorized. A taxonomy classifying responses by their "legitimacy" asks whether the system authorizes the response type, not whether the response meets reasoning quality standards. The relevant organizing provision for a legitimacy inquiry is art. 927, not art. 489.

The response: the provision-specificity argument does not depend on art. 489 governing authorization. It depends on art. 489, §1º, V and VI governing and classifying specific response types as acts within the ordinary processing domain. These subsections do not merely impose reasoning quality standards; they recognize and regulate specific judicial acts — invocation and departure — as distinct acts within the CPC 2015's procedural framework. A provision that says "a decision is not considered reasoned if the court departs from a binding norm without demonstrating distinction or supersession" (VI) is not only imposing a quality standard; it is recognizing "departure with demonstration" as a specific act type with defined form requirements. This recognition is what places the act in the ordinary processing domain rather than outside it. Art. 927 creates binding force and organizes courts by their position in the hierarchy; art. 489 organizes response acts by their procedural characteristics. A taxonomy of response acts draws on art. 489's response-act framework. Whether that framework generates a "legitimacy" criterion for the taxonomy or only a "reasoning adequacy" criterion is precisely the criterion-which-criterion question — the provision-specificity argument proposes that the response-act recognition function of art. 489 is the more natural reference frame for a taxonomy of "saídas legítimas" than art. 927's authorization function.

**The Exit 1 demonstration shows the provision-specificity argument produces a normatively incomplete account of Exit 1's legitimacy.** The adversarial's eighth ground presses that applying provision-specificity to Exit 1 produces "Exit 1 is *fundamentada*" — accurate but normatively incomplete, since Exit 1's legitimacy derives from art. 927's requirement, not from art. 489's classification.

The response: the provision-specificity argument's account of Exit 1's taxonomy membership is not "Exit 1 is *fundamentada*." The account is: "Exit 1 is classified as a 'saída legítima' because art. 489's response-classification framework recognizes invocation-with-identification as an ordinary processing domain act." This is a claim about the CLASSIFICATION CRITERION for "legítima" — what places a response type in the taxonomy's recognized set — not a claim about Exit 1's normative basis for obligatory compliance. Both predicates apply to Exit 1: it is *fundamentada* when art. 489's form conditions are met, and *legítima* in the taxonomy when it occupies the recognized-response-type set the classification criterion defines. The provision-specificity argument does not claim that art. 489 constitutes the normative basis for Exit 1's obligatory status; it claims art. 489 organizes the taxonomy's classification of response types. The anticipated objection asserts the adversarial criterion as the answer to the criterion-which-criterion question — which is the central dispute — not a defect in the provision-specificity argument's internal consistency.

## 6. Scope of This Defense

This paper defends:

- Exit 4 as a legitimate exit in the sense of a procedurally well-formed departure within the ordinary processing domain of the CPC — *incorreta* but not *inválida* — that contributes to the system's error-correction mechanism by creating the most analytically complete record available for authorized petitions for reconsideration.
- Exit 4 as operative in the infraconstitutional domain (STJ statutory-interpretation precedents under art. 927), with the constitutional monopoly constraint accepted for STF constitutional binding precedents.
- Art. 489, §1º, VI as the presupposition-grounding provision: by specifying the form for a reasoned departure, the CPC 2015 places departures-with-demonstration within the ordinary processing domain rather than categorically excluding them from it. Art. 927, §4º is not part of this argument.
- Exit 5 as a category distinct from Exit 2: legitimate abstention applies when the binding precedent governs a different legal question from the one the court resolves; Exit 2 applies when the precedent governs the same legal question on different facts. These generate different reviewing-court inquiries and cannot be collapsed.

This paper does not contest:

- The adversarial inference-gap argument as applied to Exit 4 as a claim of substantive authorization to produce a legally effective supersession.
- The constitutional monopoly argument for STF constitutional precedents.
- The adversarial paper's correct observation that the STF's reversal judgment of an Exit 4 departure need not engage the departure reasons.
- The adversarial paper's §4º argument: art. 927, §4º governs source-court self-modification and does not provide structural authorization for inferior-court departure from superior courts' norms.
- The adversarial paper's observation that anticipatory overruling, as practiced in Anglo-American systems, is predictive rather than authoritative.
- Round 5's criterion clarification: the adversarial authorization criterion was always type-level framework authorization from enacted CPC provisions (criterion B), not instance-level retrospective confirmation (criterion A). The round 4 prospective-taxonomy argument addressed criterion (A) — the wrong version of the adversarial criterion. Criterion (B) is prospectively determinable from enacted text. This does not concede that criterion (B) is what "legítima" in the taxonomy requires; it concedes that criterion (B) is the adversarial criterion as deployed.
- Round 6's criterion-which-criterion observation: both criterion (B) and the functional criterion are prospectively determinable from the same enacted text; affirmative support is required for whichever dimension "legítima" tracks. This does not concede criterion (B) is the organizing concept; the provision-specificity argument in §4.1 provides the structural case for dimension (b). This concedes that the structural case is not dispositive in the absence of primary doctrinal authority.
- Round 6's doctrinal-gap acknowledgment: eleven rounds of bilateral structural development without a primary source establishing what "saídas legítimas" constitutively requires. Both the authorization-based and domain-status-based readings are coherent interpretive options. This paper characterizes the functional-taxonomy reading as more coherent under available structural evidence, not as definitively established. Neither reading has been confirmed by primary Brazilian procedural authority.
- Round 7's Exit 1 demonstration as an accurate structural observation: Exit 1 is legítima in part because art. 927 requires courts to observe binding precedents, and *fundamentada* and *legítima* are derived from distinct provisions when applied to Exit 1. These observations are accepted. What is contested is the inference from "art. 927 grounds Exit 1's obligatory status" to "art. 927 is the organizing criterion for legítima in the taxonomy" — an inference the §4.1 response argues is not established by the demonstration, because both criteria (art. 927 authorization and art. 489 classification) apply to Exit 1 simultaneously, and a convergence case cannot discriminate between two criteria that both explain the same outcome.
- Round 8's convergence-asymmetry argument: that art. 489 form conditions are designed to specify what correct compliance with art. 927 looks like (the design-relationship premise) is accepted. What is not accepted is the inference from design relationship to criterion-subordination — that because art. 489 was designed to specify correct compliance, the form criterion is derivative of (incapable of independent satisfaction from) the authorization criterion. The form-compliant unauthorized act hypothetical correctly shows that form and authorization can diverge; this demonstrates independence, not hierarchy. A criterion that can be satisfied without the "primary" criterion is not genuinely derivative of it.
- Round 8's L2 non-autonomy argument: that "saídas legítimas" is a compliance-response taxonomy whose subject matter is constituted by the art. 927 compliance situation is accepted. What is not accepted is the inference from compliance-situation subject matter to compliance-permissibility classification criterion — that because the taxonomy's subject matter involves a compliance situation, the classification criterion must be compliance-permissibility rather than form-recognition. Subject-matter constitution and criterion-provision are distinct questions. The provision-specificity argument maintains that art. 489 provides the classification criterion even for a compliance-situation taxonomy.

## 7. Conditions Under Which This Defense Would Fail

- If the *invalidade/incorreção* distinction is rejected as inapplicable to the taxonomy — specifically, if "saídas legítimas" is a technical term in Brazilian procedural doctrine that specifically means "responses that produce valid, non-reversible outcomes" — then §4.1's recharacterization is unavailable, and the taxonomy-incoherence attack succeeds as stated.
- If the presupposition-grounding argument in §4.2 is shown to be illusory — specifically, if art. 489, §1º, VI is correctly read as a form requirement that applies only after authorization exists (not as a form requirement that itself categorizes the act within the ordinary processing domain) — the structural signal the defense relies on is eliminated.
- If Brazilian superior courts (STJ or STF) uniformly treat Exit 4 departures as sanctionable judicial misconduct rather than as incorrect decisions subject to reversal, the *incorreção* (not *invalidade*) characterization is contradicted by the system's institutional response, and the taxonomy's "legitimacy" claim fails.
- If the constitutional monopoly argument extends to infraconstitutional STJ precedents through art. 105 CF's *uniformização* function, which a court determines carries the same constitutional exclusivity as art. 102 CF's *guarda da Constituição*, Exit 4's domain is narrower than §4.4 assumes.
- If a concrete case type is identified where Exit 5's ratio-independence criterion and Exit 2's fact-distinguishing criterion classify the same scenario identically — where P governs the same question as C under any fair description of P's question, yet the court did not invoke P — the distinct-question analysis in §4.6 fails for that class of cases.
- If Paper 1B's own concession in §7.2 (that Marinoni and Mitidiero resist Exit 4 in their own works) is understood as a substantive admission that the Brazilian scholarly consensus on rational binding theory does not support Exit 4, the rational-binding-theory support for Exit 4 is more contested than §4.3 allows.
- If the adversarial camp establishes, with doctrinal authority, that "saídas legítimas" as a term of art in Brazilian precedent scholarship specifically requires type-level authorization from enacted CPC provisions (criterion B in the adversarial paper's sense: the enacted framework must affirmatively authorize the act type before the court acts), the §4.1 functional-reading response fails. The round 5 criterion clarification is accepted — criterion (B) is the adversarial criterion and it is prospectively determinable — so if doctrinal authority establishes criterion (B) as what "legítima" requires, the functional-reading response to the fourth ground fails directly, and no prospective-taxonomy counter is available to rescue it. Under this condition, the provision-specificity argument in §4.1 also fails: even if the taxonomy is constructed from art. 489's response-classification framework, doctrinal authority establishing that "legítima" tracks art. 927's authorization criterion rather than art. 489's domain-status criterion would override the structural inference from provision specificity.
- If primary Brazilian procedural authority — a judicial decision, a doctrinal work, or the CPC 2015's legislative record — establishes that "saídas legítimas" tracks dimension (a) (type-level authorization from the art. 927 binding force regime) rather than dimension (b) (domain-status recognition under art. 489's ordinary processing framework), the provision-specificity argument fails at the source. The provision-specificity argument proposes that the organizing criterion comes from the provision governing response types (art. 489); if primary authority establishes the organizing criterion comes from the provision creating the antecedent obligation (art. 927), the structural inference is overridden regardless of which provision is more specifically directed at response acts.
- If the adversarial camp establishes a functional difference between "Exit 2's type-level threshold inquiry" and the ratio-independence criterion — some step the former performs, or some outcome it produces, that differs from the latter — the round 3 "extended Exit 2" proposal would constitute a genuine structural alternative to Exit 5 rather than a definitional relabeling, and the §4.6 response fails. No such difference has been identified; if one is identified, the Exit 5 category requires separate defense.
- If the adversarial camp produces evidence that invocation of binding precedents (§V) was also historically contested in Brazilian procedural doctrine before the CPC 2015, the §4.2 response to the fifth ground's historical distinction fails. The legislative choice to regulate invocation would then carry the same informational content as the choice to regulate departure, and the defense's asymmetry argument between §§V and VI would not hold.
- If the historical contest about departure's legal status before the CPC 2015 is shown to have been exclusively an authorization contest (dimension (a): whether inferior courts can depart), with no separate contested domain-status dimension (dimension (b): whether departures without demonstration were *inválidas* or merely *incorretas*), then the §4.2 two-dimensional decomposition fails. If only dimension (a) was contested, VI's regulatory treatment addresses only a practice whose domain status was already settled, carrying no independent categorization signal for dimension (b).
- If art. 489, §1º, VI is correctly read as a form requirement that applies only after authorization is established from another source — that the legislature presupposed authorization elsewhere before specifying form in VI — then VI's form specification for departure-with-demonstration carries no independent categorization signal. On this reading, VI would be specifying form within a domain already defined by art. 927's authorization framework, and Exit 4's absence from that framework would preclude VI's form specification from placing it within the ordinary domain. This condition overlaps with the second condition above but applies to the sixth ground specifically: if the round 5 legislative-history independence argument is correct that VI addressed fundamentação only, and this is combined with the reading that VI presupposed existing authorization, then the defense's §4.2 position on dimension (b) fails entirely.
- If the adversarial camp establishes that art. 489's response-act classification criterion does NOT independently apply to Exit 1 — that Exit 1's membership in the "saídas legítimas" set is explained by art. 927's authorization alone, with no independent role for art. 489's framework in classifying it as a recognized response type — the §4.1 response to the eighth ground fails. The response holds that both criteria (art. 927 authorization and art. 489 classification) apply to Exit 1 simultaneously; evidence that art. 489's criterion does not apply to Exit 1 would confirm the adversarial's inference that art. 927 uniquely organizes "legítima" throughout the taxonomy. Alternatively, if an independent argument establishes why the paradigm case's normative grounding in art. 927's obligation identifies art. 927 as the organizing criterion for the taxonomy's classification concept — beyond the observation that art. 927 provides one sufficient explanation of a convergence case — the Exit 1 demonstration would discriminate between the criteria rather than merely selecting one of two simultaneously applicable explanations.
- If art. 489's classification function for unauthorized acts is shown to be purely incidental — that VI's form specification for departure-with-demonstration was designed exclusively to address the case where a court has authorization (defining the form of correct authorized departure) and carries no independent classification signal for unauthorized departures — then the §4.1 response to the ninth ground's convergence-asymmetry argument fails. The response holds that art. 489 generates independent classification results for Exit 4 (placing departure-with-demonstration in the ordinary processing domain) regardless of authorization status; if this independence is denied and VI's form specification is correctly read as presupposing authorization, the criterion-subordination inference from the design-relationship premise succeeds.
- If it is established that the subject-matter constitution of a taxonomy by a compliance situation is sufficient to determine that the taxonomy's classification criterion is compliance-permissibility — specifically, if "saídas" in "saídas legítimas" constrains "legítimas" to mean "compliance-permissible" rather than allowing it to mean "form-recognized" — the §4.1 response to the ninth ground's L2 non-autonomy argument fails. The response holds that subject-matter constitution and classification criterion are distinct analytical questions; if subject-matter determines criterion as a matter of the taxonomy's internal logic, L2 autonomy is unavailable and the adversarial's compliance-response taxonomy framing succeeds on its own terms.
