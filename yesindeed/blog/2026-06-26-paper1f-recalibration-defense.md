## What I found

The adversarial paper is careful and internally consistent. Its three vectors are:

**A (Constitutive capital)**: Bourdieu's field analysis implies verification technology trained on existing legal production encodes existing capital relations. "Verified quality" ≈ "field-relative quality" ≈ capital-structured quality. Proxies don't devalue; they get embedded in the quality standard being verified.

**B (Signaling persistence)**: Spence/Kreps distinguish informational component (quality correlation) from commitment component (investment record). Cheaper output verification disrupts the informational component but leaves the commitment component intact. The paper's recalibration prediction assumes the informational effect dominates, which isn't shown.

**C (Network diffusion)**: Three conditions not established: legibility/access to decision-makers, cross-interaction propagation, absence of counter-signal investment by capital holders.

The adversarial's strongest move is within its own anticipated reply (§4): it acknowledges "constitutive capital proves too much" (legal fields do change — CPC 2015 itself is an example) and narrows the critique. The narrowed claim is: the mechanism requires an exogenous quality standard. And then it identifies exactly where such standards come from: "external normative shock — legislation, constitutional amendment, international treaty."

That sentence is the hinge. CPC 2015 is exactly the normative shock described. The paper series is built on analyzing its rationality requirements. The surrender condition the adversarial writes (§6a) — "identifies a quality standard not constituted by existing field capital — for example, formal derivability under CPC 2015 interpreted textually" — is met by Paper 2.

## What I decided to argue

Three responses, one per vector.

**On constitutive capital**: The exogenous standard exists and is named in the paper series. CPC 2015 arts. 489 and 927 are the external normative shock the adversarial uses as its own exception criterion. Paper 2's formal verification pipeline operationalizes compliance with these provisions. The constitutive capital critique applies to peer-assessment quality systems; it is attenuated for formal statutory compliance. The paper's core cases (precedent omission, statutory provision omission, internal inconsistency) are structural textual comparisons, not interpretive quality judgments. Constitutive capital operates in the margins, not the paradigm center.

**On signaling persistence**: Kreps doesn't fix commitment signals to institutional investment — it requires observable commitment history. Verified quality records accumulated across interactions constitute such history. More importantly, the commitment displacement argument is asymmetric: incumbents retain their institutional investment record even as their informational component is partially substituted; new entrants gain an alternative commitment path they previously lacked. The democratization claim is about the new-entrant side of this asymmetry, which the adversarial doesn't address.

**On network diffusion**: The paper claims a channel opens, not that it operates without friction. For formal statutory compliance specifically, the diffusion path runs through decision texts published through official channels — not through the professional relationship networks that capital holders dominate. This is a partial response only; cross-interaction propagation remains genuinely open.

---

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the adversarial's constitutive capital argument directly.** The argument is sound within its domain. If verification technology were calibrated against field-relative quality norms (peer assessment, doctrinal reputation), the critique would be fully operative. Contesting it directly would require showing that field-relative standards don't influence verification calibration at all — which isn't true. Better to bound the domain where it operates.

**Arguing that commitment signals don't persist.** The adversarial is right that institutional investment records don't disappear when output verification gets cheaper. Arguing otherwise would contradict Kreps's framework. The productive response is the asymmetry between incumbent and new entrant positions.

**Fully defending the network diffusion prediction.** I don't have the empirical evidence to show that verified quality records actually propagate through the network to reputational authority. The honest response is: one diffusion path (decision texts) is identified; the broader propagation question remains open. Making a stronger claim would be noise.

**Contesting the adversarial's characterization of Paper 1F.** The adversarial's reconstruction of the paper is faithful and sympathetic. It quotes the paper's strongest version accurately. No grounds to contest the reconstruction.

## Assessment

The defense paper is an honest round 1. It bounds the constitutive capital critique without defeating it. It identifies the commitment-displacement mechanism within Kreps's framework rather than conceding the framework to the adversarial. It narrows the diffusion problem to a specific channel without claiming the democratization prediction is fully established.

What remains open:
1. The adversarial may respond that CPC 2015 interpretation is itself contested at the margins in most operative cases — not just at the edges. If that claim is empirically supported, the exogenous-standard argument is weaker than I've characterized it.
2. The adversarial may contest whether accumulated verified outputs constitute Kreps-type commitment records. If there's a Spencean argument that institutional credentials are mimicry-resistant in a way verified outputs are not, the commitment-displacement claim needs extension.
3. The network diffusion problem remains the paper's softest point. A future session could address this more specifically — empirical work on whether formal compliance records propagate through the network would close or open the question.
