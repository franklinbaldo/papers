## The three adversarial vectors

**Vector 1 — framework-determinacy vs. prior determination:** The "terceira instância" functional test tracks whether the court has previously formed and expressed a judgment on the specific argument. For genuine §489, §1º, IV violations, the court formed no such judgment — the violation is defined by that absence. Framework-determinacy (the framework implies the answer) is distinct from prior determination (the court has spoken). Only the latter satisfies the "not previously undecided" condition. The prohibition allocates judicial authority; a predictable first-time judgment is still a first-time exercise of judicial authority.

**Vector 2 — easy application is still first application:** Even in the easiest Type A case, the embargos court must determine that D falls within the framework, that no qualifications are triggered, and that the framework's implications produce the determined answer for D. None of these determinations has been made for D. The structural-deployment condition (§4.4 of the supportive paper) is used against the defense: when embargos cure a deployment gap, the court runs the framework to D for the first time.

**Vector 3 — specificity doesn't sustain the boundary:** A damages remand within a specified statutory formula (type of harm specified, measure formula specified) commits the remand court to a specific type and measure — not merely abstract liability. Procedural consensus still treats quantum determination as new merit cognition. Specificity constrains discretion; it does not convert a new judgment into an old one.

All three vectors share a structural premise: the "terceira instância" test tracks cognitive formation — whether a court has previously formed and expressed a judgment — rather than jurisdictional allocation.

## What I argued

**§4.6 — Jurisdictional-allocation reading as unified response:**

The three adversarial vectors depend on reading the "terceira instância" test as tracking cognitive formation. The jurisdictional-allocation reading contests that premise: the prohibition targets the structural duplication of proceedings — the assignment of the same merits question to an additional jurisdictional forum — not the occurrence of cognitive steps within a proceeding that has already assumed jurisdiction.

Two structural features support the jurisdictional-allocation reading. First, every appellate proceeding involves cognitive steps performed "for the first time" at that tier; a second-degree court does not thereby become a "terceira instância." The relevant question is whether the proceeding is a new tier, not whether it generates new cognitive outputs within an already-assumed tier. Second, art. 1.022 CPC positions embargos as an internal remedy within the original proceeding, not an additional appellate tier.

Under this reading:

*Vector 1:* "Previously decided" refers to whether the proceeding assumed jurisdiction over D, not whether the court formed an express judgment. The proceeding assumed jurisdiction over D when D was raised under art. 489, §1º, IV. The obligation to engage D arose at that moment. The formation of the judgment completes an already-existing jurisdictional obligation, not a new one.

*Vector 2:* The cognitive steps performed "for the first time" in Type A embargos are performed within an already-assumed jurisdiction. The "firstness" of the cognitive act does not convert an internal completion step into a new jurisdictional assertion. Every proceeding performs new cognitive acts; the prohibition turns on new forum jurisdiction, not new cognitive outputs.

*Vector 3:* The specified-formula damages remand differs from Type A embargos in two structural respects: (i) the formula was established by a prior proceeding, and (ii) the quantum question was explicitly excluded from the prior proceeding's scope. The remand is a new forum's first jurisdictional exercise over a question the prior proceeding explicitly deferred. For Type A embargos, both conditions are reversed: prior commitments are within the same proceeding, and D was within that proceeding's scope when raised.

**Updated §6 (Anticipated Objection):**

Replaced the specificity-alone response with the same-proceeding/different-proceeding conjunction. The specified-formula variant is now the anticipated objection's strongest form; the response engages it directly by explaining why the universal procedural consensus on quantum determination as new merit cognition confirms rather than threatens the defense: it reflects first-time jurisdictional exercise by a new proceeding, precisely the condition absent in Type A embargos.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the functional test's doctrinal grounding more aggressively.** The adversarial SC5 says the defense must establish through "statutory text, doctrinal authority, or jurisprudential practice" that the "previously undecided" condition is satisfied when the framework determines the outcome even though the court formed no prior judgment. I could have pressed that the adversarial paper itself has not grounded the cognitive-formation reading in Brazilian procedural authority — it is as much a philosophical import as approach freedom was. I decided against leading with this because the jurisdictional-allocation argument is structurally stronger than the absence-of-doctrinal-grounding rejoinder, and because the latter would expose me to the same countercharge.

**Making a direct argument from the art. 1.024, §3º grammar.** The conditional "caso o acolhimento implique modificação" could be read as presupposing that integration does not always generate new determinations — the conditional would be idle if integration always triggered new cognition. This is a textual argument available for the "autônoma" claim. I decided not to include it because it conflates the Thesis 2 argument with the §5.3 argument; the better textual anchor for the Type A defense is already in §4.3 (art. 489, §1º, IV's em tese qualifier and art. 1.022's integrative characterization).

**Contesting whether vector 2 follows from the structural-deployment condition at all.** The adversarial paper uses §4.4 of the supportive paper against itself: the condition says the court failed to run the framework to D; embargos cure this by running it for the first time. I could contest the inference by arguing that "running the framework to D" within the same jurisdiction is not the same as "first determination" in the "terceira instância" sense. But this is already contained within the jurisdictional-allocation response to vector 2; I didn't need a separate argument.

## Assessment

The jurisdictional-allocation reading is the session's primary contribution. It provides a unified answer to all three adversarial vectors rather than replying to each separately, which was important given the shared structural premise they depend on. The reading is conceptually coherent: the "terceira instância" prohibition is about hierarchical structure of adjudication — which proceeding exercises first-instance jurisdiction — not about the cognitive novelty of the steps within a proceeding.

The most vulnerable point: the adversarial camp's best response will be doctrinal, not structural. If it can produce Brazilian procedural scholarship, STF or STJ holdings, or authoritative commentary establishing that "terceira instância" is assessed at the judgment-formation level rather than the proceedings-jurisdiction level, the §4.6 argument loses its doctrinal support. SC5 of the adversarial paper already identified this path: the surrender condition asks for evidence that framework-determinacy satisfies the "previously undecided" condition; the adversarial counter will ask for evidence that jurisdictional-assumption is the right tracking criterion. The symmetry of the doctrinal requirement is acknowledged in the new §7 failure condition.
