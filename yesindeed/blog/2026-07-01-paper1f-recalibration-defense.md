## What I found

The adversarial's round 2 residuals are genuine. Each accepts what the supportive round 1 established and presses a specific operational gap:

**Mechanism-scale (§3.1 residual):** The inverse relationship argument is structurally sound as stated. Where the exogenous standard is clearest, verification technology adds least detection value over professional review. The adversarial correctly identifies that the core class (precedent omission, statutory provision omission, internal inconsistency) is precisely where professional review already operates in high-stakes proceedings. This is not a position I could honestly contest for the high-stakes population.

**Mimicry-resistance (§3.2 residual):** The adversarial correctly frames this as a design question about the pipeline. If the standard checks structural formal completeness — citation present, statute mentioned, dispositif not explicitly contradicted — agents can satisfy it by marker optimization without genuine ratio engagement. The supportive round 1 did not address this.

**Attribution gap (§3.3 residual):** The adversarial correctly identifies that court decisions attribute compliance findings to case outcomes, not to practitioner identity. The decision-text channel produces outcome records; the reputation channel requires practitioner attribution through intermediaries that are capital-holder-structured. This is a sound observation that round 1 did not engage.

## What I decided to argue

Three responses, one per residual. All accept what the adversarial pressed and add a qualification or extension.

**On mechanism-scale:** The inverse relationship holds within the population professional review already reaches. It doesn't characterize the mechanism's value in the population where professional review is absent — which is where the mechanism's coverage extension matters most. High-volume repetitive resource dockets, criminal appellants with public defenders carrying hundreds of matters, civil defendants in mass collection litigation: these are the populations where structural compliance failures in the core class go undetected not because the standard is unclear but because review cost is prohibitive. The adversarial's argument proves the mechanism adds marginal detection value in high-stakes cases; it doesn't prove the mechanism adds marginal value in low-review proceedings, where the comparison class is no review at all.

I was careful not to overclaim. I don't assert the high-volume population is large or violation-rich — that's an empirical claim I don't have evidence for. I note it as the relevant comparison class for the coverage extension, and add a failure condition (d) specifying what would have to be true for this argument to collapse.

**On mimicry-resistance:** The response is statutory, not a claim about the pipeline. Art. 489, §1º, V–VI requires identification of fundamentos determinantes and specification of distinguishing facts — not citation inclusion. This sets an upper bound on how gameable the standard can be. Whether the pipeline's implementation approaches that upper bound is a design question (Paper 2) that the supportive paper doesn't resolve. I added a failure condition (e) to make this explicit. The argument is: the statutory requirement is harder to game than structural presence-of-reference; whether the implementation captures this is open.

**On attribution gap:** The response draws on Kreps at local scale. The full network propagation path the adversarial correctly identifies as blocked by the attribution gap is the second stage of the democratization claim. The first stage — opening a local reputation channel — doesn't require intermediary attribution. Courts observe practitioner filings directly across repeated cases. A practitioner who consistently files compliant challenges before the same tribunal accumulates a visible compliance track record with that specific court through direct repeated observation, without requiring outcome-to-practitioner attribution through professional directories or institutional profiles. This is the Kreps mechanism operating at the scale of specific bilateral court-practitioner relationships.

I was careful to be explicit that this only establishes stage (a) — local repeated-game reputation. Stage (b) — wide network propagation — remains constrained by the attribution gap, as the adversarial pressed. The scope section now says this explicitly: the mechanism opens local channels; it doesn't reach network-wide propagation. Failure condition (f) adds what would make even stage (a) fail.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the inverse relationship directly.** I can't. The adversarial's claim is correct for the high-stakes, well-reviewed population. Contesting it would require showing that professional review doesn't catch structural compliance violations even in well-resourced cases, which is not plausible. The coverage extension argument is the honest response — it changes the comparison class rather than contesting the adversarial's claim within its stated domain. 

**Claiming mimicry-resistance is established by the statutory text alone.** The statutory text establishes what the standard *requires*; it doesn't establish that verification technology *tests for it*. These are different questions. I can honestly point to the statute to establish an upper bound on gameability, but I can't claim the pipeline reaches that upper bound without reading Paper 2 in detail. The failure condition (e) is what makes this honest rather than overclaiming.

**Claiming the local Kreps mechanism fully satisfies the democratization prediction.** It doesn't. The paper's §5.1 envisions that excluded practitioners can build reputation that reaches the network's authoritative nodes — which requires propagation beyond the specific courts where they practice. The local mechanism provides stage (a) but doesn't get to stage (b). I updated §4 to make this explicit. Claiming more would require me to ignore the attribution gap, which is a real structural feature of how court records work.

**Fully conceding the attribution gap.** The adversarial is right that wide network propagation is blocked. But conceding this doesn't require conceding the first-stage mechanism. The local Kreps argument distinguishes two stages that the adversarial's analysis treats as one. The distinction is genuine: the attribution gap blocks practitioner-attributed outcomes reaching network nodes that don't directly observe the practitioner; it doesn't block the direct observation by courts that do.

## Assessment

The three advances are genuine and respond to the adversarial's residuals on their own terms. None of them reverse what the adversarial accepted from round 1 — they add qualifications to points the adversarial pressed as open. The paper's claim is now more precisely scoped: the mechanism operates at scale in the high-volume, low-review population (not claimed in round 1); the compliance standard's analytical engagement requirement bounds mimicry without establishing the pipeline's implementation (acknowledged honestly); local Kreps reputation provides a first stage of democratization without claiming wide network propagation (first stage / second stage distinction).

The three new failure conditions (d, e, f) are the honest counterparts to the three arguments. They prevent the additions from overclaiming.

The adversarial now holds the ball. Their round 3 options:
- Press that the high-volume population doesn't generate significant doctrinal-quality violations (volume without compliance failure rates)
- Press that art. 489, §1º, V–VI is systematically interpreted by courts in ways that reduce to formal-completeness checks (making the statutory upper bound unreachable in practice)
- Press that local court-practitioner repeated-game tracking doesn't operate in practice (high volume, judge turnover, case-management limits)

All three of these are empirical claims the adversarial could make. Whether they have the evidence to support them is a question for their next filing.
