## What I argued

### Taxonomy incoherence: the *invalidade/incorreção* distinction

The adversarial paper assumes "legítima" in the taxonomy means "legally authorized to produce a binding outcome." This reading collapses two distinctions Brazilian procedural law keeps separate: *invalidade* (the act is outside the ordinary processing structure — grounds for nullification, extraordinary remedies, disciplinary exposure) and *incorreção* (the act is within the ordinary structure but wrong on the merits — subject to reversal through ordinary appeal).

Exit 4 decisions are *incorretas*, not *inválidas*. A court exercising Exit 4 has met the art. 489, §1º, VI form requirement; it is not exposed to nullification proceedings or disciplinary sanction; its decision is processed through ordinary appeal. *Afastamento silencioso* involves both *incorreção* AND potential *invalidade* — the form defect under art. 489, §1º, VI is grounds for recognizing fundamental nullity.

"Legítima" in the taxonomy tracks this *invalidade/incorreção* line, not the authorized/unauthorized line. An exit is "legítimo" when it belongs to the set of responses the procedural system processes ordinarily. Exit 4 belongs there; *afastamento silencioso* does not (or does only precariously, given the nullity exposure). The adversarial counter-reply's anticipated reply section says this "confuses conduct quality with decision legal status" — but the *invalidade/incorreção* distinction IS a distinction in legal status, not merely in conduct quality.

This directly engages the adversarial surrender condition 2: "If the paper acknowledged that Exit 4 is not 'legitimate' in the same sense as Exits 1–3 and 5 — that it is a form-compliant deviation that is institutionally valuable without being authorized — the taxonomy's incoherence objection would be answered." The *invalidade/incorreção* analysis does exactly this: it acknowledges two senses of "legítimo" (authorized to prevail vs. within the ordinary processing domain) and shows that Exit 4 occupies the second sense without confusion.

### §4º: accept and replace

The §4º structural-signal argument is withdrawn. The adversarial paper's reading of §4º — that it governs source-court self-modification only — is correct. I dropped §4º from the defense entirely.

The structural signal now comes from art. 489, §1º, VI's own presupposition structure. A negative form requirement ("not reasoned if it fails to demonstrate X") necessarily categorizes the affirmative case ("reasoned if it demonstrates X") as within the provision's domain. The provision specifies a form for departure-with-demonstration. This places departures in the ordinary processing domain rather than categorically excluding them. This is not a grant of authorization to prevail; it is a decision by the CPC 2015 to process certain acts ordinarily rather than as categorically prohibited nullities.

The adversarial principle — "form requirements can only regulate authorized practices" — is sound but inapplicable. The provision doesn't purport to authorize departures; it categorizes them. An act can be within the ordinary processing domain without being authorized to produce the outcome sought. That is precisely what Exit 4 claims: the act is within the domain (ordinary appeal applies); the outcome is not authorized (reversal follows).

### Exit 5: different legal questions vs. different facts

The collapse argument holds that every legitimate Exit 5 case is a case where careful Exit 2 analysis would have concluded P is inapplicable. This holds only when P and C address the same legal question with different facts — which is Exit 2's presupposition, not Exit 5's.

Exit 2 applies when P covers the same type of legal question as C but C's facts fall outside P's scope. Exit 5 applies when P governs a different legal question entirely. These are distinct cognitive operations.

Concrete case: binding STJ precedent on the limitations period for CDC contractor liability actions. Court resolves whether the contractor fulfilled the quality-of-performance obligation. The limitations question (when does the action expire) and the performance question (was the obligation fulfilled) are different legal questions. No distinguishing analysis is available — there is nothing to distinguish. "Careful distinguishing analysis" cannot conclude a precedent is inapplicable to a different legal question; distinguishing is a technique for the same question on different facts, not for different questions.

The adversarial collapse argument's implicit assumption: for every P and C, if P's ratio is genuinely independent of C's grounds, there exists a distinguishing analysis concluding P is inapplicable to C. False when P and C address different questions. Distinguishing requires the precedent to be applicable in principle — which requires the same question, not different facts.

The reviewing-court inquiries are also distinct: Exit 2 review asks whether C's facts fall within P's scope (same question, different facts inquiry); Exit 5 review asks whether P governs C's question at all (scope inquiry). A court that mischaracterizes its question as different from P's fails Exit 5 review — but this is the same manipulability problem Exit 2 faces (courts can mischaracterize facts to distinguish), and the remedy is the same: appellate review of whether the questions genuinely differ.

### Rational binding theory: accept the reversal-judgment limitation, preserve deliberative record

Accepted: the STF's reversal of an Exit 4 departure does not require engaging the departure reasons. The reversal proceeding is not the site of the discursive engagement. The channel function operates through deliberative accumulation: Exit 4 departures enter the record as formally structured challenges available for authorized petitions for reconsideration. The proximate mechanism is those authorized petitions; Exit 4 makes them more analytically complete. This is consistent with the adversarial paper's own account that the channel "runs through authorized petitions."

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the adversarial paper's §4º reading.** The reading is correct. Art. 927, §4º's subject is *modificação* by the holding court. Inferior courts departing from STF/STJ precedents are not engaged in self-modification under §4º. The presupposition-structure argument (§4.2 of the improved paper) is the honest replacement.

**Defending Exit 5 through "named sub-type" route** (one of the options the synthesis identified). The different-legal-questions analysis is more fundamental and better grounded. The named-sub-type route concedes Exit 5 collapses into Exit 2 and argues the labeling is still useful; the different-questions analysis shows the collapse doesn't occur in the first place, which is the stronger position if it holds.

**Adding a new fifth argument on the art. 926 integrity obligation.** Art. 926's integrity obligation runs on all courts; a court applying a precedent it has identified as internally incoherent may violate its own integrity obligation. I considered anchoring Exit 4 in art. 926. Discarded: this argument could support a duty to flag the concern, but not Exit 4 specifically; the art. 489, §1º, VI presupposition argument is cleaner and more directly tied to the provision that specifies the departure form.

## Assessment

The taxonomy-incoherence attack was the most dangerous of the three. The *invalidade/incorreção* distinction is the most intellectually honest answer available — it accepts the narrowed sense of "legítima" for Exit 4 while grounding that sense in a legal-status distinction Brazilian procedural law actually draws. Whether the adversarial routine accepts this or presses that *incorreção* is not "legitimidade" in any jurisprudentially meaningful sense is the debate's next open question.

The Exit 5 different-questions analysis is the substantive contribution of this session. If the collapse argument fails for different-question cases (as argued), Exit 5 survives as a genuine independent category. The adversarial routine's best response is to show that the different-question/different-facts distinction doesn't hold — that for the class of cases the paper has in mind, a careful Exit 2 analysis was always the available technique. The concrete example (limitations period vs. quality-of-performance) is designed to be a case where that response is not available.

The §4º withdrawal is clean and doesn't weaken the defense significantly; the presupposition argument is the better structural ground anyway.
