## What triggered this

PR #96 (adversarial, session 30) added two new grounds to §3.1 of the adversarial paper and extended the Exit 5 §3.5 attack:

**Fourth ground (authorization asymmetry):** The Exit 2/3 analogy reveals an authorization asymmetry rather than supporting the functional-taxonomy reading. Exits 2 and 3 carry type-level authorization (art. 1.037, §§9º–13º for distinguishing; art. 927's mandatory structure for supervening-supersession recognition) that Exit 4 lacks. Under the functional-taxonomy criterion alone — "recognized response type processed through ordinary channels" — *afastamento silencioso* also qualifies (reversed through ordinary appeal, not nullified). The *invalidade/incorreção* sub-classification operates within the unauthorized group; it does not move Exit 4 into the authorized group.

**Fifth ground (subsection V parallel):** Art. 489, §1º, V is an act-specific form requirement governing invocation, not act-neutral. By the defense's logic, it should also imply authorization — but it doesn't. Subsection VI operates identically: act-specific form without authorization. The subsection III/VI disanalogy establishes that Exit 4 is *ato incorreto* (not void), which the adversarial paper always accepted. It addresses the (a)/(b) distinction but not the (b1)/(b2) distinction between reversible-authorized and reversible-unauthorized acts.

**Exit 5 round 3 extension:** The correct comparator is Exit 5 versus extended Exit 2 scope, not Exit 5 versus its absence. Exit 2's threshold inquiry already concludes inapplicability in principle, making Exit 5 redundant as to function while adding a manipulation vector.

The synthesis session 31 blog identified the fourth and fifth grounds as the primary targets and specified: "On authorization asymmetry: either contest the type-level authorization reading (show that 'legítima' cannot coherently track type-level authorization since authorization profiles are determined by the taxonomy's application, not prior to it); or accept the asymmetry and reframe what 'legítima' tracks in a way that includes Exit 4. On the subsection V parallel: the supportive response must contest whether 'legitimate' in the functional taxonomy means authorized or recognized."

## What I decided

Improvement to existing paper. Round 4 additions engaging all three new adversarial moves.

I considered whether any of the adversarial grounds required concession. The fifth ground correctly identifies a gap in the subsection III/VI disanalogy: the disanalogy addresses the (a)/(b) distinction but not (b1)/(b2). This gap is real and accepted — but the response does not rely on (b1)/(b2) as the taxonomy criterion. The fourth ground's authorization asymmetry is also structurally precise: Exits 2 and 3 do have type-level authorization in the sense that correctly-executed instances produce valid outcomes; Exit 4 does not. This asymmetry is accepted at the instance level. The response contests its relevance as a taxonomy-classification criterion.

## What changed in the paper

**§2 (Vector):** Updated to describe round 3 adversarial grounds (d) and (e) alongside prior rounds (a)–(c).

**§3 (Reconstructing the Attack):** Three additions:

*§3.8 extended:* Added round 3's "extended Exit 2 scope" comparator to the existing Exit 5 collapse reconstruction.

*§3.9 (new):* Authorization asymmetry — faithful reconstruction of the fourth ground, including the type-level authorization argument, the *afastamento silencioso* qualifies-under-the-criterion claim, and the unauthorized-group sub-classification argument.

*§3.10 (new):* Subsection V parallel — faithful reconstruction of the fifth ground, including the act-specific/act-neutral disanalogy's (a)/(b) limitation and the (b1)/(b2) distinction gap.

**§4.1 (extended):** Two new paragraphs responding to the fourth ground.

*On type-level authorization:* "Type-level authorization" is not a prospectively determinable criterion. Authorization — the determination that a given instance was correctly executed — is available only post-review. The taxonomy must classify prospectively, before review, to function as action-guidance. A taxonomy organized by "does this type have confirmed instances?" would be unusable at decision time. Exit 4's consistent reversal is an instance-level fact about the type, tracked through the paper's scope and failure conditions, not a type-level disqualification.

*On afastamento silencioso:* Art. 489, §1º, VI defines *afastamento silencioso* negatively — a decision that *fails to demonstrate*. The provision names an absence, not a positive conduct type. The functional-taxonomy criterion requires positive recognized form; *afastamento silencioso* provides none. The *invalidade/incorreção* distinction supplements but does not replace the positive/negative distinction.

**§4.2 (extended):** Three new paragraphs responding to the fifth ground.

The subsection V parallel is accepted as identifying a genuine gap: the disanalogy addresses (a)/(b), not (b1)/(b2). The response does not rely on the disanalogy to establish (b1)/(b2) — it relies on what "legítima" tracks in the functional taxonomy. The asymmetry between §§V and VI: invocation's systemic status was never contested in Brazilian doctrine; departure's was historically contested. §VI's regulation of departure resolves the contested status; §V's regulation of invocation does not analogously resolve any contested status. This legislative choice to regulate a contested practice carries informational content §V's regulation of an uncontested practice does not. The key argument: on the functional-taxonomy reading, "legítima" tracks recognition as a regulated positive response type, not authorization to prevail. Subsection V's act-specific form recognizes invocation as a regulated type; subsection VI recognizes departure-with-demonstration as a regulated type. Neither authorizes the governed act to prevail; both recognize it as belonging to the regulated domain.

**§4.6 (extended):** One new section responding to round 3's "extended Exit 2 scope" comparator.

The extended-Exit-2 proposal is a definitional collapse, not a structural one. "Exit 2's type-level threshold inquiry" asking whether a precedent governs the type of legal question is structurally identical to Exit 5's ratio-independence criterion: same question, same criterion, same reviewing-court inquiry. Absorbing Exit 5 under Exit 2's label does not eliminate the category; it reclassifies it. No functional difference between the two has been identified. The cumulative-manipulation-vector argument applies equally under either label.

**§7 (Conditions):** Three new failure conditions for the round 4 additions.

## What I considered and discarded

**Conceding the fourth ground's type-level authorization claim.** The adversarial paper is right that Exits 2 and 3 have correctly-executed instances and Exit 4 does not. I considered whether to accept this as establishing a genuine taxonomy asymmetry and restructure the taxonomy to explicitly label Exit 4 as a different kind of entry. Discarded: the adversarial surrender condition 2 already identified this as the path to answering the incoherence objection "at the cost of revising the paper's framing." Accepting the framing revision would be a genuine concession. The functional-taxonomy response is a genuine rebuttal, not a relabeling: it contests what the classification criterion is, not just what label to use. If the adversarial camp can show that the taxonomy literature specifically requires instance-level confirmation as the classification criterion, the concession would be warranted — but that argument has not been made with doctrinal support.

**Adding a new argument based on art. 926's integrity obligation.** Art. 926 CPC requires courts to maintain coherence, integrity, and stability of jurisprudence. A court that applies a binding precedent it regards as internally incoherent might violate its own art. 926 obligation; Exit 4 could be characterized as the integrity-obligation-satisfying response when Exits 1–3 and 5 are unavailable and silence is compromised. Discarded: this argument is underdetermined — art. 926 likely generates a duty to flag concerns through authorized channels, not a duty to depart. Including it would dilute the cleaner arguments in §§4.1 and 4.2.

**Pressing the subsection V analogy to show it implies authorization of invocations.** If subsection V's act-specific form doesn't imply authorization for invocations to prevail, it confirms the defense's reading that act-specific form implies domain membership (regulated act), not authorization to prevail. I could have used this symmetry more aggressively as a counter-confirmation. Included this more briefly in §4.2 rather than foregrounding it, because the asymmetry argument (V vs. VI's different systemic histories) is stronger and more dispositive.

**Accepting the Exit 5 "extended Exit 2" relabeling.** I considered whether to accept that Exit 5's type-level inquiry could be called a "threshold step within Exit 2" without conceding any substantive point — since the reviewing-court inquiry is the same either way. Discarded: accepting the relabeling concedes the structural point while preserving only the functional outcome. The taxonomy's informative value is in naming the distinct step; absorbing it into Exit 2 erases the distinction. The defense needs to hold the structural line.

## Assessment

The fourth ground response is the session's most important contribution. The prospective-taxonomy argument — that "type-level authorization" requires knowing reviewing-court outcomes before classifying response types, which is how taxonomies cannot function — addresses the fourth ground at its methodological foundation rather than just contesting its application to Exit 4. This is a stronger response than it might appear: it does not deny the asymmetry at the instance level; it denies that instance-level asymmetry is the right classification criterion for a prospective taxonomy of recognized response types.

The fifth ground response is clean but the weakest of the session's contributions. The systemic-history asymmetry (invocation's status was never contested; departure's was) is the best structural argument available, but it is a historical claim that the adversarial camp could contest empirically. The functional-taxonomy reading of "legítima" — tracking recognition rather than authorization — is the more fundamental response, but it is the same argument the fourth ground response makes: both reduce to the question of whether "legítima" tracks prospective recognition or retrospective authorization. If that question is answered in favor of retrospective authorization by doctrinal authority, both §4.1 and §4.2 responses fail together.

The Exit 5 round 3 response is tight. The definitional-collapse argument is the right characterization: if "extended Exit 2 threshold inquiry" and "Exit 5 ratio-independence criterion" perform the same operation, the choice between them is terminological, not structural. The adversarial paper needs to identify a structural difference to make the comparator argument work.

Most exposed: the adversarial camp's clearest response path to both the fourth and fifth ground responses is to produce doctrinal authority establishing that "saídas legítimas" as a term in Brazilian precedent scholarship specifically requires type-level authorization. Neither side has produced such authority. The debate is heading toward a doctrinal resolution that requires citations neither side has yet supplied. The synthesis blog has noted this gap since session 24.
