## What I argued

### The convergence case gap

The Exit 1 demonstration uses a convergence case: Exit 1 is a taxonomy entry where both the provision-specificity criterion (art. 489 classifies Exit 1 as a recognized response type) and the adversarial authorization criterion (art. 927 requires Exit 1) apply simultaneously. The adversarial infers from this convergence that art. 927 is the organizing criterion.

The inference requires one of two things:
- (a) That art. 489's classification criterion does NOT apply to Exit 1
- (b) Independent grounds for preferring art. 927 as the organizing criterion over art. 489's classification criterion

Neither is established. On (a): Exit 1 clearly satisfies art. 489, §1º, V's classification — it recognizes invocation-with-identification as a procedurally classified response type in the ordinary processing domain. On (b): the adversarial says "Exit 1 is legítima because art. 927 requires it" — true — but an equally coherent account says "Exit 1 is legítima because art. 489's classification framework recognizes invocation-with-identification as a properly-formed response type in the compliance situation art. 927 creates." The demonstration selects art. 927 without ruling out art. 489's criterion.

### The *fundamentada/legítima* clarification

The adversarial correctly notes these predicates are derived from distinct provisions for Exit 1. This is accepted. But the provision-specificity argument was never claiming *fundamentada* = *legítima* or that art. 489 provides the normative basis for Exit 1's obligatory status. The provision-specificity argument claims the CLASSIFICATION CRITERION for the taxonomy's "legítima" predicate is organized by art. 489's response-act classification framework. The demonstration applies the observation about distinct provisions at the normative grounding level, not at the taxonomy classification level. These are different levels of analysis.

### The taxonomy's function

"Saídas legítimas" classifies response OPTIONS — recognized ways of responding to a precedent invocation. Art. 927 creates the compliance obligation; the taxonomy identifies which exit forms compliance can legitimately take. A taxonomy of exit options is organized around what constitutes a recognized option type (classification question), not around what makes compliance obligatory (obligation question). Art. 489's framework identifies which response types qualify as recognized option types; art. 927 creates the compliance obligation that frames the situation. "Legítima" in a taxonomy of exit options tracks the former classification, not the latter obligation structure.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the adversarial's observation that Exit 1 is legítima because art. 927 requires it.** This is correct. Exit 1 is required by art. 927. Contesting the observation would be wrong.

**Arguing that art. 489 is the SOLE basis for Exit 1's legitimacy.** This would overclaim. Exit 1's legitimacy is multiply grounded. The provision-specificity argument's claim is about the taxonomy's classification criterion, not about which provision is the unique basis for any exit's legitimacy.

**Contesting the *fundamentada/legítima* distinction.** The adversarial is right that these are distinct predicates. The response works with this observation rather than against it.

**Accepting the eighth ground as establishing the adversarial position.** The argument doesn't close the criterion-which-criterion question — it applies one criterion to a convergence case. The structural ceiling (both readings coherent, no primary authority) persists.
