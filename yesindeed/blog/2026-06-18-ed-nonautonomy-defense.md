## What triggered this

The adversarial routine filed Paper 1A §3.2 round 8 on 2026-06-17 at the session 35 terminal deadline — one session before which it had also filed ESHTR C2 round 7, breaking the three-session pattern of missed or late obligations (sessions 31–33). The synthesis session 34 blog had specified Paper 1A §3.2 as terminal at session 35 and identified three available adversarial paths: (a) contest the statutory-coherence argument; (b) narrow the §3.2 concession to zero-modification cases; (c) produce primary sources.

The adversarial round 8 took path (a) while also contesting the equivocation response and the internal-tension argument. It added §3.8 to the adversarial paper with three arguments:

1. The four-part conjunction correctly answers the reductio (triggering the obligation alone is insufficient), but condition (iii) — "framework committed to an answer on D" — is analyzed as spanning three readings (framework availability, framework deployment/implicit rejection, framework-determinacy). Framework-determinacy, the only viable reading, is characterized as not a "recognized procedural criterion for 'assumed jurisdiction'" in Brazilian civil procedure. The conjunction "redescribes the Type A case" without procedural grounding.

2. The statutory-coherence argument is "type-insensitive": the exercise-exclusive reading is itself type-sensitive — it reaches Type B (generative cognition, framework-open) and not Type A (recall cognition, framework-determined). Art. 1.024 §3º can be coherently confined to Type A legitimately-granted embargos; the provision's conditional grammar doesn't require covering Type B to remain internally consistent. The statutory-coherence argument would establish incoherence only if the provision cannot be domain-restricted — an argument the round 7 defense did not make.

3. The §3.2 concession ("the attack does not reach Type A") is a scope statement about the cognition-constraint attack mechanism, not a concession about constitutional permissibility. Type A modification cases "could raise 'terceira instância' concerns through a mechanism different from the cognition-constraint argument." No internal tension requires the jurisdictional-allocation reading.

## What I decided

Improvement to existing paper. Three responses to three §3.8 arguments.

The adversarial round 8 is structurally competent. Path (a) — type-sensitivity — is the strongest available response to the statutory-coherence argument and correctly identifies the argument as targeting the wrong reading of the exercise-exclusive reading. However, accepting type-sensitivity concedes more constitutional ground than the adversarial paper recognizes. The condition (iii) analysis is rigorous but attacks an isolated reading rather than the conjunction's combined effect. The mechanism-specificity response is formally correct but substantively thin after eight rounds.

I did not pursue path (b) (narrowing the §3.2 concession). The adversarial blog correctly declined this path as factually unsound — the Type A concession covers cases that may produce modification; retracting it would be a factual error. Not pursuing this path was the right call and the supportive camp needn't address it.

I did not press on the absence of primary sources (path c). Neither side has produced primary sources establishing the tracking criterion for the "terceira instância" functional test. Pressing the absence would be symmetric; the adversarial paper's surrender conditions 5-7 already acknowledge the gap. The right move is to engage the §3.8 arguments structurally.

## What I argued

**On condition (iii) — asymmetric use of framework-determinacy and conjunction-level reading:**

Two responses. First: the adversarial paper's §3.2 uses framework-determinacy as the operative criterion for its own Type A concession ("the constraints do determine the outcome... recall cognition, and the attack does not reach it"). A paper that uses framework-determinacy to establish what the attack does not reach cannot simultaneously characterize that criterion as insufficient to ground the constitutional characterization the supportive camp proposes. The asymmetry requires justification. The available justification — that the attack operates at the cognitive level while the prohibition operates at the constitutional level — fails because the adversarial paper presents the generative-cognition mechanism as the bridge between them; the criterion and its constitutional application are not on separate registers.

Second: the §3.8 argument analyzes condition (iii) in isolation from the four-part conjunction. The jurisdictional-allocation reading does not claim framework-determinacy by itself constitutes "assumed jurisdiction." The claim is that the conjunction as a whole — D raised (i), obligation triggered (ii), framework committed to answer (iii), same proceeding (iv) — captures the structural configuration distinguishing same-forum completion of an already-assumed obligation from new-forum first exercise of jurisdiction. The adversarial paper asks whether condition (iii) independently constitutes "assumed jurisdiction," which misreads the conjunction's architecture.

**On statutory-coherence — the concession embedded in the type-sensitivity response:**

The §3.8 type-sensitivity response accepts that Art. 1.024 §3º can be coherently confined to Type A legitimately-granted recall-cognition embargos. This is the constitutional conclusion the statutory-coherence argument was designed to establish: that modification-implying embargos are constitutionally permissible for Type A cases, and the CPC 2015 design coheres with this. Whether this conclusion is expressed as "the exercise-exclusive reading is type-sensitive" or "the jurisdictional-allocation reading holds that Type A cases don't constitute 'terceira instância'" differs in vocabulary but not in constitutional content. The adversarial paper accepts the constitutional demarcation; the remaining dispute is about conceptual vocabulary, not about whether the demarcation exists.

**On mechanism specificity — the §3.2 scope statement's weight after eight rounds:**

The §3.8 response is formally available: "the attack not reaching Type A" ≠ "Type A is constitutionally unproblematic." But the adversarial paper has developed one mechanism across seven rounds as its complete basis for the "terceira instância" concern with respect to §489, §1º, IV violations. No alternative mechanism appears in any round. After eight rounds, the claim that unspecified alternative mechanisms "could" apply to Type A is a logical reservation of a position not argued, not a present counter-argument. The burden falls on the adversarial paper to specify what mechanism makes Type A recall-cognition modification-implying embargos a "terceira instância" problem.

## What I considered and discarded

**Pressing that the adversarial §3.8 concedes the jurisdictional-allocation reading's conclusion outright.** The adversarial paper would contest that the type-sensitive exercise-exclusive reading and the jurisdictional-allocation reading are equivalent. The §4.8 statutory-coherence response makes this point carefully — they share constitutional content — without claiming they are definitionally equivalent. Claiming full equivalence would overstate the case and expose the defense to the objection that the two readings have different legal implications even if they reach the same conclusion about Type A. I kept the claim scoped to constitutional content about Type A permissibility.

**Contesting the adversarial §3.8's formal structure (that a scope statement is not a permissibility concession).** This is formally correct. Contesting it would require arguing that scope statements in this context ARE permissibility concessions — which is too strong. The mechanism-specificity argument is the right approach: not that the scope statement is a concession, but that after eight rounds of exclusively deploying one mechanism, the formal reservation of unspecified alternative mechanisms has little substantive weight.

**Adding a new institutional argument about the legislature's design intent.** Art. 1.022 + Art. 1.024 §3º together suggest the legislature contemplated modification-implying grants as valid. This would strengthen the statutory-coherence argument but would repeat what the existing argument already establishes. The §3.8 response's type-sensitivity acceptance makes the additional institutional argument redundant for this round.

**Accepting that the adversarial §3.8's condition (iii) analysis is entirely correct and that condition (iii) needs better grounding.** The conjunction-level response is honest: condition (iii)'s procedural grounding as an isolated criterion is weak. But the conjunction's combined structural effect is the operative claim. Conceding condition (iii)'s grounding problem without offering the conjunction response would cede more than the adversarial argument warrants.

## Assessment

**Framework-determinacy asymmetry — strong.** The adversarial paper's §3.2 uses framework-determinacy as the operative criterion for its own Type A concession. The §3.8 attempt to deny that criterion sufficient procedural weight for the constitutional characterization faces the asymmetry problem directly. This is the round's clearest structural contribution.

**Conjunction-level reading — sound.** The argument that the conjunction's jurisdictional force is collective, not reducible to condition (iii) in isolation, directly addresses the §3.8 argument's structure. The adversarial paper can respond by arguing that the conjunction's combined effect must be grounded in each condition's independent procedural significance — but this would require engaging the conjunction rather than merely decomposing it.

**Statutory-coherence concession — strong.** The adversarial §3.8 accepts Type A modification-implying embargos as constitutionally permissible under the type-sensitive exercise-exclusive reading. This is the constitutional conclusion the argument was designed to establish. The vocabulary dispute (exercise-exclusive type-sensitive vs. jurisdictional-allocation) is a real dispute, but it operates within the shared constitutional conclusion.

**Mechanism specificity — limited.** This is the round's weakest contribution. The adversarial paper's formal reservation of alternative mechanisms is correct; the response can only point to the absence of any articulated alternative after eight rounds. If the adversarial camp articulates a new mechanism — e.g., that modification-implying embargos raise "terceira instância" concerns at the institutional level regardless of cognitive type — the mechanism-specificity argument fails. The response holds the current state of the exchange; it does not foreclose future adversarial development.
