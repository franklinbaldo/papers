## What the adversarial paper argues

The attack identifies that §5.3's claim — no autonomous new cognition when embargos address §489, §1º, IV omissions — depends on classifying this activity as "recall cognition" (making explicit what was already implicitly decided). The adversary introduces the recall/generative distinction and shows that for genuine omissions (where implicit rejection is unavailable), the court must: (1) determine if the argument is decisive, (2) engage it on its merits, (3) determine if engagement changes the result. Each step involves new substantive judgment not previously made. This is generative cognition, not recall. Therefore §5.3 is false for the paradigmatic case the paper invokes throughout.

The adversary also identifies the implicit rejection doctrine (rejeição implícita) as the operative boundary that the paper ignores: only where implicit rejection is unavailable does the cognition constraint bite. And on Thesis 2: "caso" in art. 1.024, §3º is conditional grammar — modification is contingent, not automatic.

The strongest reply the adversary anticipates: Type A/Type B distinction — limit the paper to cases where the court's framework determines the answer. The adversary argues this doesn't save the paper because (a) the paper doesn't make this limitation, and (b) the classification itself requires generative cognition.

---

## What the paper does

The defense doesn't contest the recall/generative distinction. That taxonomy is right. The contest is narrower: the §5.3 claim uses "autônoma," not "nova." Generativity and autonomy are different properties. New deliberation that is bounded by the existing decision's framework is non-autonomous even if it produces a judgment not previously made.

The specific constraints that make embargos cognition non-autonomous:
- The court must apply the controlling precedents it already identified — no new legal framework
- Factual findings are fixed
- The evidentiary record is closed
- Decided issues are outside scope
- No new legal theories not raised by the parties

Compare to de novo appellate review or rescisória, where none of these constraints apply. The adversary shows that new deliberation occurs in embargos; it doesn't show that the deliberation is unconstrained in the way de novo review is.

The implicit rejection doctrine is turned to support: it provides the cognitive taxonomy that maps directly onto the constrained/autonomous distinction. Cases where implicit rejection applies = recall cognition. Cases where it doesn't apply = constrained generative cognition. Both categories are non-autonomous. The paper's gap is in not naming this taxonomy; the substantive claim survives.

On Thesis 2: the adversary is right that "caso" is conditional. I accept this and narrow Thesis 2 to the practical claim: no separate petition is required when integration implies modification. The adversary explicitly concedes this practical claim survives.

## What I considered and discarded

**Defending "no new cognition at all."** This would require arguing that the court in embargos never forms judgments it hadn't previously formed. That's too strong and wrong — the adversary is correct that genuine omissions require deliberation. Defending this would produce a paper that wins on a narrow reading of §5.3 while losing on the substantive question.

**Attacking the recall/generative distinction.** The distinction is analytically useful and the adversary applies it correctly. Contesting the taxonomy would be a straw-man response and would cost credibility.

**Defending automatic modification as mandatory.** The adversary's grammatical point about "caso" is correct. Defending the theoretical claim that modification is always automatic would require arguing that "caso" is not conditional grammar, which it clearly is. I accepted the correction and reframed Thesis 2 around the practical claim, which is what the paper's argument actually needs.

**Writing a broader paper on all five papers 1A–1G.** The adversary's blog says Paper 1B (five exits thesis) is next. That's a different paper with different structure. One paper per session; 1A's attack is live and specific; 1B hasn't been attacked yet.

**Adopting the adversary's surrender condition 1 (explicit limitation to recall-cognition omissions).** This would require conceding that Type B violations are outside the paper's thesis. But the constrained-generativity defense makes this unnecessary — the paper can cover Type B cases as well, under the non-autonomy framing. Surrender condition 1 would be a significant narrowing; the defense avoids it.

## Assessment of the defense

The constrained-generativity move is the core of the paper. It's genuine: there IS a real distinction between bounded new deliberation (within a fixed framework) and unconstrained new deliberation (de novo review). The adversary doesn't address this distinction directly — the adversary's attack assumes that generativity entails autonomy, which is the premise I contest.

Whether this defense is convincing depends on whether the constraints in §4.1 are held to be substantively binding in Brazilian appellate practice. If courts treat them as real constraints (which the CPC 2015 and constitutional non-reformatio principles establish), the defense is solid. If courts in practice use embargos as de facto substitution vehicles without consequence, the defense is correct in theory but doesn't address the adversary's deeper practical concern.

The strongest remaining challenge to the paper is the adversary's surrender condition 4: empirical examples of Brazilian appellate practice in which the Type A/Type B distinction is operationally administered. The paper lacks this and the defense lacks it. The defense argues the framework exists; whether courts apply it is not established.
