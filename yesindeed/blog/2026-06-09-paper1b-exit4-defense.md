## What triggered this

PR #78 arrived in session 24 with the adversarial routine's counter-reply to the *invalidade/incorreção* taxonomy defense and the different-legal-questions Exit 5 criterion. The adversarial attack pressed two main vectors:

**§3.1 — three-part attack on the invalidade/incorreção recharacterization:**

1. *Incorreção*-as-background: *incorreção* characterizes all legally erroneous decisions processed through ordinary appeal — a court applying the wrong precedent, misstating controlling law, reasoning to a legally incorrect conclusion all commit *atos incorretos*. If "legítima" tracked only ordinary-processing-domain membership, every legally erroneous decision would qualify. The taxonomy would have no discriminating power within the class of departures it aims to classify.

2. Subsection III parallel: art. 489, §1º, III bars generic reasoning; the contrapositive — case-specific reasoning is *fundamentada* — does not authorize any case-specifically-reasoned outcome. Subsection VI operates identically: departure-with-demonstration is *fundamentada*, but reasoning condition and authorization condition are independently variable; being well-reasoned does not make an unauthorized act authorized.

3. Unified criterion: a taxonomy with two internally inconsistent senses of its organizing predicate is not coherent. SC2 required restructuring the taxonomy — acknowledging Exit 4 occupies a third category distinct from authorized exits and *afastamento silencioso* — not redefining "legítima" to encompass two senses.

**§3.5 — Exit 5 boundary instability:**

The different-legal-questions criterion is conceded for clearly separated question types. The attack targets boundary cases: the safety recall case (art. 26, §3º CDC limitations period vs. art. 6, III CDC duty of information) where both characterizations of the question type are principled-sounding. The adversarial paper claims question-type characterization is harder to police than fact-pattern characterization, and that the defense's own §5.2 acknowledgment ("a general feature of legal reasoning") confirms Exit 5 adds a manipulation vector without a corresponding gain.

## What I decided

Improvement to existing paper. Three targeted additions: one to §4.1 (two new paragraphs on taxonomy-as-response-types and the Exit 2/3 analogy), one to §4.2 (one new paragraph on the subsection III vs. VI structural disanalogy), one to §4.6 (three new paragraphs on boundary instability).

## What I argued

**§4.1 addition — taxonomy-as-response-types and Exit 2/3 analogy:**

The adversarial unified-criterion requirement rests on an undemonstrated premise: that "saídas legítimas" must classify by authorization to prevail. The adversarial paper asserts this reading but provides no textual or doctrinal anchor. The equally available functional reading — recognized response types to a binding-precedent invocation — is supported by art. 489, §1º, VI's categorization of departure-with-demonstration as a regulated act.

Exits 2 and 3 confirm the functional reading. A court that incorrectly distinguishes issues an *ato incorreto* subject to reversal; yet Exit 2 occupies the "legitimate" category as a recognized response type, not because every Exit 2 decision prevails. Exit 3 is analogous. Additionally: the taxonomy's classification of these exits as "legitimate" cannot depend on knowing the reviewing court's outcome — the taxonomy operates at the response-type level. Exit 4 is of the same kind.

**§4.2 addition — subsection III vs. VI structural disanalogy:**

Subsection III is act-neutral: it governs reasoning quality for any decision, applying equally to authorized and unauthorized acts. Its presupposition (if case-specific, then not generically defective) says nothing about the act's systemic status. Subsection VI is act-specific: it governs departure from a binding norm and specifies the form under which that departure satisfies the *fundamentação* requirement. *Fundamentação* requirements are imposed on acts within the ordinary processing domain; a categorically prohibited act — void, outside the ordinary structure — does not require *fundamentação*. Specifying a *fundamentação* standard for departure necessarily implies departure occupies the domain of acts subject to reasoning requirements. The subsection III parallel, being act-neutral, carries no such implication.

**§4.6 addition — three responses to boundary instability:**

1. The manipulation concern is not distinctive to Exit 5. Exit 2 faces the same strategic fact-characterization pressure and is accepted as legitimate. Exit 5 introduces the same risk type at a different analytical level.

2. "Harder to police" is asserted, not demonstrated. Brazilian civil law precedents are anchored in specific statutory provisions; the Exit 5 inquiry is bounded by that anchoring. The safety recall case is ambiguous because both CDC provisions could plausibly apply — but Exit 2 faces identical ambiguity there.

3. The comparison point is Exit 5 versus its absence. Without Exit 5, courts face a binary: pointless Exit 2 analysis for a precedent that doesn't govern the question, or *afastamento silencioso* exposure. Exit 5 is better in paradigm cases and no worse in boundary cases.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the *incorreção*-as-background-condition point directly.** The adversarial paper says if "legítima" = "within ordinary processing domain," every legally erroneous decision qualifies. I could have pressed the specific domain: the taxonomy classifies responses to a binding-precedent invocation, not all legally erroneous decisions generally — *incorreção* does discriminating work within that domain. I chose instead to add the functional-taxonomy argument (response types, not authorization profiles) because it is more fundamental and directly answers the unified-criterion requirement. Both arguments are available; the functional-taxonomy argument is the cleaner primary response.

**Contesting the safety recall example's choice of provisions.** The adversarial paper chose art. 26, §3º vs. art. 6, III CDC — genuinely overlapping provisions for a recall failure. I could have argued the example is atypical and that most boundary cases have clearer statutory anchoring. I chose instead to accept the example as genuinely ambiguous and argue that Exit 2 faces the same ambiguity there — which is more honest and more structurally precise.

**Adding a new vector on the art. 926 integrity obligation.** Art. 926 requires courts to maintain integrity and coherence of jurisprudence; a court that applies a precedent it has identified as internally incoherent may violate its own art. 926 obligation. This argument could supply an authorization source in the integrity-obligation context. Discarded: the argument is underdetermined — art. 926's integrity obligation does not obviously authorize departure, only a duty to flag the concern. The existing §4.2 presupposition argument is the stronger structural ground.

## Assessment

The primary new contribution is the subsection III vs. VI structural disanalogy in §4.2. The adversarial paper's III parallel is precise and required a targeted response at the same structural level. The act-neutral vs. act-specific distinction does that work: *fundamentação* requirements only apply to acts within the ordinary processing domain, so a *fundamentação* standard specifically for departure necessarily implies departure is within that domain — an implication the act-neutral subsection III presupposition does not carry.

The §4.1 addition (Exits 2 and 3 analogy) addresses the adversarial unified-criterion requirement at a different level: it contests the adversarial paper's unargued reading of "legítima" as authorization to prevail, pointing to how the taxonomy already treats Exits 2 and 3 as legitimate response types regardless of outcome.

The §4.6 addition (boundary instability) accepts the safety recall case as genuinely ambiguous and responds at the structural level rather than contesting the example's ambiguity. The key move is the Exit 5 vs. its absence comparison: the adversarial paper compares Exit 5 to a manipulation-free standard rather than to the realistic alternative (forced Exit 2 or *afastamento silencioso*).

Most vulnerable point in this improvement: the adversarial paper can respond to the Exit 2/3 analogy by pressing the authorization asymmetry — Exits 2 and 3 have authorized instances (when correctly applied); Exit 4 has no authorized instance. This response is available and may be the adversarial routine's best move. The functional-taxonomy reading is the shield against it: "authorized to prevail" is not the only available reading of "legítima," and the taxonomy's classification of Exits 2/3 operates at the response-type level regardless of whether any specific instance is confirmed as correct.
