## Decision: Path 2

Three paths were specified in the synthesis session 17 analysis:

- Path 1: Distinguish limited remands from embargos on approach-freedom grounds. Requires showing limited-remand courts retain some approach freedom that embargos courts lack. Closed — the limited-remand description is accurate; both are approach-constrained.

Path 2 is the right choice. The approach-freedom operationalization was a genuine advance — it answered the constraints-determination gap without conceding the core. The adversarial counter-reply engaged it at the right level. The limited-remand comparison is a genuine objection: it correctly shows that approach freedom is not what the "terceira instância" concern tracks. But the comparison does not reach Type A embargos. The relevant distinction is not approach-constrained vs. approach-free; it is first-time-determination vs. remedial-deployment.

## The key argument: remedial deployment vs. first-time determination

The limited-remand court is making first-time determinations on a question newly assigned to it — damages not previously before it in the proceeding, no prior obligation to determine this specific question, no prior commitment specifying the answer. Its constraints are domain-limits (liability is settled) but the judgment within those limits is fresh.

The Type A embargos court is deploying prior commitments to a question it was already obligated to address. The argument was before the court; art. 489, §1º, IV already required the court to engage it; the court's own prior commitments in the same decision — the binding precedent accepted, the statutory provision applied, the factual findings made — determine how engagement must go. The omission was a failure to complete a task the court's own framework already specified.

This distinction tracks what the "terceira instância" functional test is actually about: courts using integrative remedies as vehicles for fresh first-time merit judgments. Limited remands make fresh judgments on questions not previously before the court. Type A embargos complete deployments on questions the court was already committed to answering.

The adversary's "terceira instância" test — "new first-time substantive determination on a previously undecided merit question" — is correctly answered NO for Type A embargos: the question was not previously undecided in the operative sense. The court's prior commitments decided it; the omission was a failure to make that decision explicit.

## What I considered and discarded

**Path 3 (contesting the functional test):** Would require doctrinal support for a structural characterization of embargos that Brazilian procedural authorities don't offer in the relevant form. Discarded.

**Finding Brazilian doctrinal support for approach freedom:** Marinoni-Mitidiero-Arenhart's treatment of embargos as "recurso de integração" addresses integrative vs. substitutive activity but doesn't operationalize "autônoma" as approach-constrained in the relevant technical sense. The lack of doctrinal grounding is genuine. Retiring approach freedom as primary criterion is cleaner.

**Full concession of §5.3:** The adversary has established that §5.3 overclaims universally stated, and that the paper's own illustration is Type B. Full concession would abandon a defensible core. The Type A category is real and survives the limited-remand comparison under the remedial-deployment distinction.

**Contesting that limited-remand courts lack approach freedom:** They do. Disputing the description is factually wrong.

## Assessment

The adversarial three-prong counter-reply is the sharpest attack in this exchange. The limited-remand comparison was not available before the approach-freedom operationalization appeared — it was constructed in direct response — and it is a genuine objection. The outcome-indeterminacy observation lands cleanly on the product-level claim.

Whether this is sufficient depends on whether the adversarial routine can reconstruct the damages determination as remedially specific (liability commits to a specific amount, not just to paying something). That is the live exposure. §6 addresses it; whether the argument holds is for the adversarial routine to test.
