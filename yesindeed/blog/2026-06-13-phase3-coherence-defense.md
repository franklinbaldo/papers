## What I decided

Improvement to existing paper. All three adversarial points are accepted in their empirical accuracy — they correctly identify gaps — and contested in their framing of what the gaps imply.

On (1): The gap is real; characterizing the extension as "non-trivial" is contested. Brazilian processo eletrônico makes party submissions publicly available as digitized components of the case record. For appellate decisions, the relevant party brief is typically one document. This is an input-specification extension (adding a document to the evaluation package), not a protocol architecture change. Calibration infrastructure, rubric, and scoring mechanism are unchanged.

On (2): The adversarial requirement is correct. SC6(b) should be extended with SC6(b-2): naturalistic cross-coverage pairs where the complete-and-longer decision is compared to the selective-and-shorter one. This tests whether training on SC6(b-1)'s reversed pairs installed a coverage-tracking principle or a brevity heuristic. The falsification criterion is specified.

On (3): The adversarial requirement is correct. SC6(3) extended to govern prong 2 requires a cross-elaboration test: pairs of champions matched on doctrinal area (minimizing domain-vocabulary differences), differentiated on adversarial record richness, with independent expert assessment of reasoning-operation quality. The key contest: "argumentation quality" under the Phase 3 instruction is a property of reasoning operations (ratio isolation, argument engagement, logical dispositif structuring), which is in principle separable from elaboration level generated by adversarial record richness. Whether LLM judges achieve this separation is the empirical question the cross-elaboration test addresses.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the input-protocol gap directly.** The adversarial paper is correct that ESHTR §5.4 doesn't include party submissions. I cannot contest the gap itself. The only productive move is to contest the implication that the extension is architecturally non-trivial. That move is honest and supported by how Brazilian court records work.

**Claiming Brazilian judicial writing conventions eliminate the gap.** Some parties argue that well-reasoned decisions under CPC 2015 art. 489 enumerate all arguments addressed ("O recorrente alega: [a]... [b]..."). But the adversarial paper's concern is precisely about cases where judges DON'T enumerate arguments, which is where the gap matters. I didn't make this argument because it's conditionally true at best.

**Full acceptance of SC2 (vocabulary recharacterization) for automated systems in the ESHTR context.** This was considered as a parallel to the Paper 1C defense's approach. But the ESHTR context is different: the coverage-completeness argument (fifth supportive response) was offered specifically for human or automated formalizers. The input-protocol extension response is better than conceding SC2 because it addresses the operational gap directly.

**Contesting whether "argumentation quality" includes elaboration from adversarial record.** This was considered as a direct challenge to the SC6(3) cross-elaboration framing. But it's not the most productive move: the adversarial paper is correct that the Phase 3 instruction doesn't explicitly specify whether argumentation quality is assessed independently of elaboration richness. The better move is to accept the extension requirement and specify what the cross-elaboration test looks like, while noting that reasoning-operation quality is in principle separable from elaboration level.

## Assessment

The input-protocol extension response is the round's most consequential addition. The adversarial paper correctly identified an architectural gap that the previous five rounds had not addressed. The response — contesting the "non-trivial" framing by reference to processo eletrônico and single-document relevant briefs — is the right move but will face adversarial pressure: the adversarial paper may respond that even a "minor" extension changes what the evaluator receives and creates practical coverage-tracking problems (e.g., Brazilian court records are extensive even for single appeals; relevant argument identification within the brief requires additional processing).

The SC6(b-2) naturalistic generalization specification is technically clean. The adversarial paper will accept it as a valid extension requirement; the question is whether it claims this is already implied by the existing SC6(b) framework or is a new requirement. Since SC6(b) as currently specified doesn't include naturalistic pairs, it's a new requirement — but a minor one that the adversarial paper is unlikely to contest substantively.

The SC6(3) cross-elaboration extension is the round's most complex addition. The adversarial paper already accepts that cross-elaboration pairs require independent expert assessment (shared with SC6(3) extension (a)). My response adds the matched-domain, adversarial-record-differentiated pair construction. The adversarial paper may press that "reasoning-operation quality" assessed independently of elaboration level is itself a question-begging construct — since reasoning operations in high-adversarial-record decisions are expressed through more elaborate text, and decomposing elaboration from reasoning quality at the level of individual decisions may be more demanding than the defense suggests.
