## What the adversary's §3.2 argues

The adversary's §3.2 reconstructs the §4.1 constraint list (fixed precedents, fixed facts, closed record, decided issues outside scope, no new legal theories) and accepts that these constraints are genuine and distinguish embargos from de novo review. The counter is:

1. Constraints narrow deliberation without determining its outcome. If multiple outcomes are compatible with the constraints, the court's choice among them is a new substantive judgment not logically entailed by the prior decision.

2. For §5.3's non-autonomy claim to have substantive content, "autônoma" must mean "pre-determined by the prior decision's logic." If it means only "bounded by the same framework," every second-degree appellate court qualifies as non-autonomous — those courts also work within precedent and factual findings. The claim would be trivially true for any constrained adjudicator.

3. Art. 489, §1º, IV's definition selects against the paper's position: arguments "capable of undermining the conclusion in principle" are, by the dual filter of the decisiveness threshold and implicit rejection, disproportionately those the framework left open.

The adversary summarizes: "The quantitative reduction in deliberative space is not a qualitative change in cognitive type."

## What I added

**To §3 (Reconstructing the Attack):** A new bullet — "The constraints-determination gap" — placed between the implicit rejection doctrine section and the automatic-consequence claim. This makes explicit in the reconstruction phase what the paper is responding to in §4.1. Without this, a reader of the reconstruction would see the constraints listed in §4.1 but not see that the adversary's §3.2 contested them on the narrowing-vs-determining distinction.

**To §4.1 (Generativity Is Not Autonomy):** Four new paragraphs appended after the existing material, before §4.2. These respond to §3.2 on three fronts:

*Operationalization.* The adversary's §3.2 requires "autonomous" to mean "pre-determined in outcome." This is the wrong operationalization. Autonomous cognition in the relevant legal context is cognition with approach freedom — the freedom to select one's own framework, invoke new authorities, and approach the question from an independent standpoint. De novo review and rescisória are autonomous not because they produce certain outcomes but because the reviewing court is not bound to the original decision's framework. Embargos cognition lacks this approach freedom: the court cannot invoke precedents absent from the original decision, apply a different legal theory, or approach the question from a different direction. Outcome uncertainty within the fixed framework is compatible with complete absence of approach freedom.

*Categorical distinction from ordinary appellate courts.* The adversary's reduction conflates two types of constraint: "bound to the legal order generally" (ordinary appellate) vs. "bound to this specific decision's reasoning structure" (embargos). Ordinary second-degree appellate courts retain approach freedoms an embargos court lacks: invoking precedents the lower court omitted, applying different theories, deciding unaddressed issues under art. 1.013, §3º CPC, supplementing with independent doctrinal analysis. These freedoms are absent from embargos. The two constraints differ in kind, not only degree.

*Statutory selection argument.* The adversary uses art. 489, §1º, IV's definitional filter to argue that what survives as a genuine omission is framework-undetermined. This depends on implicit rejection doing comprehensive filtering of all framework-determined cases. The structural-deployment limit (from `yesindeed/ed-recall-range-defense.md` §3.4) contests this: implicit rejection requires structural deployment, not just framework possession. A court that possesses a framework answering argument E but whose structural reasoning stops before reaching E has not implicitly rejected E. The gap between framework possession and structural deployment is where genuine Type A omissions live — past the implicit rejection filter, but not past the framework-determination filter.

**To §6 (Scope):** Added approach-freedom operationalization to "This paper defends." Added to "This paper does not contest": that constraints are compatible with multiple outcomes for some genuine §489 violations — the adversary's indeterminacy point is accepted at the level of outcome certainty; the defense contests the inference that outcome indeterminacy = autonomous cognition.

**To §7 (Failure Conditions):** Added a new failure condition: if "autônoma" in §5.3 is established by Brazilian procedural doctrine to require outcome pre-determination rather than absence of approach-framework freedom, the §4.1 extension fails.

**To References:** Added `yesindeed/ed-recall-range-defense.md` (cited for the structural-deployment limit in §3.4).

## What I considered and discarded

**Accepting the adversary's "pre-determination" operationalization and limiting §5.3 to Type A cases.** This would have been honest in a narrow sense — Type A cases are where the constraints do come close to determining the outcome. But it would have conceded the adversary's most provocative claim (that "autônoma" requires pre-determination) without challenging it. The approach-freedom operationalization is not a retreat to a weaker claim; it is a sharper account of what §5.3 actually asserts. Embargos courts lack approach freedom; that is the content of the non-autonomy claim.

**Engaging the adversary's art. 1.013, §3º reference as a separate argument.** The adversary doesn't cite 1.013, §3º. I introduced it as one of the approach freedoms ordinary appellate courts have that embargos courts lack. I'm confident this is accurate and appropriate — art. 1.013, §3º expressly authorizes second-degree courts to decide issues not reached below, which is exactly the kind of approach freedom embargos courts cannot exercise. Including it strengthens the categorical distinction without adding a new front.

**Addressing the ESHTR Phase 3 measurement-2 confound.** Also a session 15 priority. Deferred because the Paper 1A §3.2 has been unchallenged longer (since session 13 vs. session 14), was explicitly promised in the prior session's blog, and one session should produce one honest advance. The ESHTR Phase 3 response belongs in phase3-coherence-defense and remains the next session's priority.

## Assessment

The approach-freedom operationalization resolves the adversary's reductio cleanly. The adversary's comparison to ordinary appellate courts works only if "bounded by the same framework" is the operative constraint in both settings. The categorical distinction between "bound to the legal order generally" and "bound to this specific decision's reasoning structure" shows that the constraints are of different kinds — not merely different degrees — and that the embargos constraint is the one that removes approach freedom rather than merely narrowing output range.

The weakest part of the response remains the statutory selection argument, where the adversary's §3.2 and the ed-recall-range-defense §3.4 are both in play. The structural-deployment limit in ed-recall-range-defense §3.4 establishes that Type A omissions survive the implicit rejection filter. This defense cites that argument and applies it to the statutory selection inference in §3.2. The connection is clean: the adversary's §3.2 inference requires implicit rejection to be comprehensive; §3.4 shows it is not; therefore the inference fails.

The adversary's deepest remaining challenge to §5.3 is now the empirical arbiter: what is the actual distribution of genuine §489, §1º, IV omissions between framework-determined and framework-open? Neither side has this data.
