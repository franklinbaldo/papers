## What I argued

**On vector (1) — the endogenous threshold problem:**

The adversarial paper treats threshold adjustment as cost-free — the STF chooses between option (a) proportional engagement and option (b) proportional threshold raising, with no cost attached to (b). This is the paper's main vulnerability.

Option (b) carries procedural costs that scale with argument quality. Art. 93, IX CF requires reasoned decisions; art. 1022, II CPC allows embargos de declaração for omissão. A formally structured reclamação that explicitly identifies the súmula's ratio, demonstrates departure, and provides a structured supersession/distinction argument creates a record that a conclusory dismissal decision will not adequately address. The minimum adequate dismissal reasoning for a low-quality argument ("súmula X applies, reclamação denied") is not adequate for a high-quality argument without engaging the argument's structure. If the STF dismisses with minimal reasoning, the decision is exposed to embargos de declaração for omissão.

This doesn't block threshold adjustment — it makes adjustment costlier as argument quality increases. The adversarial paper's option (b) is available but not free. This partially constrains the endogenous threshold problem.

I was careful not to overclaim: the adversarial paper's surrender condition 2 (identifying a mechanism that *blocks* threshold adjustment) is only partially satisfied. The procedural constraint binds only if embargos de declaração for omissão in reclamação dismissals produce meaningful review in practice. If the STF dismisses embargos with equally brief reasoning, the constraint is loose. The anticipated objection section acknowledges this directly.

**On vector (2) — historical refutation:**

The adversarial paper's historical evidence (academic criticism of plenário virtual practice hasn't worked) tests the wrong mechanism. Academic criticism of the systemic practice is diffuse; no individual critic has strong incentive to monitor every reclamação judgment. The mechanism Paper 1E invokes operates at the individual-argument level: the party that produced the quality argument has both concentrated incentive and analytical capacity to document and publicize non-engagement.

The adversarial paper anticipates that individual-argument monitoring requires actors "with the incentive and analytical capacity to evaluate whether specific arguments warranted engagement." The argument's producer has both. This doesn't guarantee the monitoring works at scale, but it does establish that the historical evidence doesn't test the mechanism.

**On vector (3) — differential cost reduction:**

I accepted this as the adversarial paper's strongest point. The narrow defense: tools differentially reduce the articulation cost for arguments where the ratio has already been correctly identified by a practitioner with analytical capacity. The mechanism operates for practitioners at the margin — skilled enough to identify the ratio, previously resource-constrained in elaboration. Whether this class is large enough to generate sufficient quality-argument volume is unresolved.

The adversarial paper's noise concern — formally structured analytically incorrect arguments are cheap to produce — is correct and not fully answered. I said so explicitly in the scope section.

**On vector (4) — rapporteur structure:**

Accepted the modeling gap. Contested the inference to fully diffuse reputational costs. Brazilian STF justices are individually attributed in academic literature; rapporteur decisions are publicly named and tracked by processual scholars. The community is small enough for individual-level tracking to be feasible. The costs are not concentrated at the institutional level but are not fully diffuse at the individual-justice level either.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the adversarial paper's diagnosis of the current equilibrium.** The adversarial paper explicitly does not contest the (β, β) Nash equilibrium characterization. I accepted this and didn't restate it as a supportive claim — that would be restatement, not support.

**Defending the paper by identifying a specific institutional actor with oversight over reasoning quality.** The adversarial paper's surrender condition 3 requires identifying an actor with the authority to impose costs on the STF for non-engagement. No such actor obviously exists. Trying to identify one would risk manufacturing a claim I cannot support. I declined to meet that surrender condition.

**Arguing that the plenário virtual mechanism itself creates visibility.** There is a version of this argument: the plenário virtual record is public, and patterns of brief dismissals in high-quality cases are observable. But this is essentially the same argument as the diffuse academic monitoring argument, which has the collective action problem the adversarial paper correctly identifies. I didn't use this.

**Defending the access-to-justice section.** The adversarial paper accepts §5.3. No defense needed.

## What the paper accepts

- The endogenous threshold problem is structurally real. The STF has and exercises discretion to adjust its engagement threshold through the plenário virtual mechanism.
- Academic criticism of the systemic practice has not changed STF behavior; the adversarial paper is correct about this.
- The differential cost-reduction premise is the weakest part of the paper's argument; the adversarial paper correctly identifies the analytical/textual distinction.
- The rapporteur-structure critique is a genuine modeling gap; the game model needs to be specified at the individual-justice level.

## Assessment

The paper is an honest partial defense. It identifies the specific structural feature the adversarial paper's core attack overlooks — that threshold adjustment carries its own costs — without overclaiming that the inflection point is guaranteed. The historical-evidence distinction (systemic-practice monitoring vs. individual-argument monitoring) is the cleanest part of the defense; it engages the adversarial argument's strongest factual claim on its own terms without inventing new evidence.

The weakness: the scope section honestly acknowledges that four of the adversarial paper's surrender conditions remain unmet. This is the right call — manufacturing claims to meet surrender conditions I cannot support would degrade the support to restatement. The adversarial paper has a strong case; the defense narrows the strongest attack without defeating it.
