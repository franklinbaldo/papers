## What I argued

**The core reconceptualization:** The adversarial paper attacks Exit 4 as an *authorization claim* — inferior courts claiming substantive authority to effectively supersede STF binding precedents. This reading is the strongest version of Exit 4, and the adversarial paper is correct that art. 489, §1º, VI does not supply it. The defense reconceptualizes Exit 4 as *transparent contestation*: the prescribed form for an inferior court that has identified error in a ratio to articulate that error fully, accept reversal, and contribute the most analytically complete record available for the apex court's eventual engagement. The "legitimacy" of Exit 4 is the legitimacy of the prescribed form — the court has met its art. 489 obligations, accepted the system's correction mechanism, and fed information to the STF's error-correction process — not the legitimacy of prevailing.

This reconceptualization accepts what the adversarial paper establishes: inferior courts do not *win* when exercising Exit 4. But it contests the binary the adversarial paper constructs: "authorized (prevails)" vs. "not legitimate (will be reversed)." The third category — procedurally well-formed, knowingly-reversed, systemically-constructive departure — is the category Paper 1B describes.

**The constitutional monopoly:** Accepted for STF constitutional precedents. The art. 102 CF *guarda da Constituição* is a constitutional interpretive monopoly that art. 489's procedural provision cannot override. This restricts Exit 4 to the infraconstitutional domain — primarily STJ statutory-interpretation precedents under art. 927. The paper argues this restriction defines Exit 4's practical domain, not eliminates it: the STJ's art. 105 CF jurisdictional grant for legal unification does not carry the same constitutional exclusivity as art. 102. Within the infraconstitutional domain, Exit 4's transparent-contestation structure is compatible with art. 927's mandatory binding force.

**The rational binding theory as a two-directional structure:** Marinoni's formula — "razão que não pode ser desconsiderada sem razão mais forte" — binds the STF as well as inferior courts. When an inferior court provides the stronger reason, the STF is obligated (under the formula's own logic) to engage it. Exit 4 activates this discursive obligation by delivering the challenge in the most articulate available form. The inferior court does not determine that binding force has lapsed; it argues that it should, and the STF determines by deciding whether to affirm or reconsider.

**Exit 5 — ratio-independence criterion:** The adversarial paper correctly identified the absence of a principled criterion. The criterion: Exit 5 is legitimate when the *ratio decidendi of the resolution* is genuinely independent of any question the invoked precedent addresses — when the grounds actually determinative of the court's disposition do not engage the question the precedent's ratio speaks to. The test: would a court properly applying the precedent have been required, in resolving this specific case, to engage the precedent's ratio? If yes, non-invocation is strategic. If no, Exit 5 is legitimate. This criterion tracks the structure of the resolution's grounds rather than the court's mental state, making it more precise than sincerity/integrity alone, and maps to an administrable appellate-review inquiry.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the inferential gap directly:** The adversarial paper is correct that art. 489, §1º, VI establishes a negative sufficiency condition, not a substantive authorization. Contesting this would require showing a statutory provision that affirmatively authorizes inferior courts to supersede STF precedents. No such provision exists. The honest response is the reconceptualization, not a contest of the inferential gap as stated.

**Arguing that the constitutional monopoly does not apply to STJ precedents:** The adversarial paper says the "same structural argument applies" to the STJ. I argued the structure differs — art. 102 CF's *guarda da Constituição* is a constitutional monopoly; art. 105 CF's unification function is a jurisdictional grant. This argument is made in the paper as the domain-restriction defense. I was not fully confident in it (the STJ may contest), but it is the most defensible position available and is genuinely grounded in the distinction between constitutional interpretive monopoly and statutory-jurisdiction grant.

**Full anticipatory-overruling defense:** The adversarial paper is correct that Exit 4 as stated is a stronger claim than anticipatory overruling. Defending it as anticipatory overruling would require the paper to concede Exit 4's distinctive innovation — the inferior court's own rational assessment, not prediction of the STF's direction. The transparent-contestation frame preserves more of Exit 4's content while conceding the authorization claim, which is more honest.

**Deferring for Paper 1A §3.2 response instead:** The adversarial counter-reply to the approach-freedom operationalization landed on 2026-05-30 with three new objections (functional doctrinal purpose, outcome-indeterminacy concession, no doctrinal grounding). This is a live obligation. ## Assessment

The transparent-contestation frame is the best honest support available for Exit 4. It accepts what the adversarial paper establishes (no substantive authorization) while recovering what the adversarial paper's binary obscures (the procedural legitimacy of the prescribed form for transparent departure). Whether this is enough to sustain Paper 1B's claim that Exit 4 is a genuine "saída legítima" — rather than merely a formal compliance mode — is the question the adversarial routine will contest.

The Exit 5 ratio-independence criterion is the substantive contribution of the session: a specific, administrable, structure-based test that does not reduce to sincerity alone. The adversarial paper's surrender condition 3 asked for this criterion; it is now on the table.

The constitutional monopoly concession is honest and was the correct move. Defending Exit 4 across the full art. 927 spectrum — including STF constitutional holdings — would require contesting art. 102 CF's allocative force, which is not defensible.
