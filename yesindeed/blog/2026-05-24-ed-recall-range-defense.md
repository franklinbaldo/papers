## What the attack says

The adversarial paper (`otherwise/ed-cognition-constraint.md`) argues that addressing a
previously unaddressed §489, §1º, IV argument requires three generative steps: (1) determining
whether the argument is capable of undermining the conclusion — a new substantive judgment; (2)
engaging the argument on its merits — new deliberation; (3) determining whether engagement
changes the result — a new merit judgment. All three are generative, not recall. The "no new
autonomous cognition" claim in §5.3 is therefore false for the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case.

The attack reaches Thesis 2 through inheritance: if modification depends on the outcome of new
generative cognition, it cannot be an automatic consequence. The adversarial paper also reads
art. 1.024, §3º's "caso... implique" as a real conditional confirming contingency, and invokes
Paper 1A's own §3.4 acknowledgment of non-modifying grants as confirmation.

Secondary attack: implicit rejection doctrine. The STJ's *rejeição implícita* doctrine defines
the boundary between genuine omissions and implicitly decided arguments — Paper 1A ignores this,
leaving its scope claim without a limiting principle.

The adversarial paper anticipated the strongest reply (Type A/Type B distinction) and argued
it does not suffice: (1) the paper doesn't make this limitation, and (2) the classification of
Type A vs. Type B itself requires generative cognition.

## What the paper argues

**Main move: The adversarial paradigm is unrepresentative.** The adversarial paper used Paper
1A's own illustrative hypothetical — a contested constitutional classification across a
legislative reenquadramento — to characterize what the "paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case" looks
like. That hypothetical was chosen to display doctrinal complexity. The empirical center of mass
of §489, §1º, IV violations in Brazilian appellate practice consists of three categories where
the court's existing framework determines the answer to the omitted argument:

- Binding-precedent omissions (court transcribes a *súmula* or binding ratio, fails to apply it
  to a party's argument that directly engages it)
- Statutory-provision omissions (unambiguous provision the court's own jurisprudence treats as
  governing, failed to be applied to one aspect)
- Internal-consistency omissions (court's own findings are in tension; argument identifies the
  tension and its implication for the dispositif)

For these cases, the court's framework commits it to a determinate answer. Addressing the
argument completes an analytical circuit already opened — recall cognition, not generative.

**On the recall/generative distinction:** The distinction is not about effort or complexity; it
is about whether the court is recovering a position its framework commits it to (recall) or
forming a position its framework leaves open (generative). Multiple analytical steps can be
required for recall cognition if those steps trace out what the framework implies. Generative
cognition requires that the framework be genuinely open on the question — no combination of the
court's prior commitments determines the answer. The adversarial paper's hypothetical is
genuinely generative (constitutional classification open under the court's framework); the
three typical categories are not.

**On the implicit rejection doctrine:** The adversarial paper reads the doctrine as exposing a
gap — Paper 1A needs a limiting principle and the doctrine provides one it ignored. The
defense inverts this: the doctrine is Paper 1A's ally. Cases subject to implicit rejection are
recall cognition in its clearest form (the position exists; embargos surface it). Cases of
genuine omission that are also Type A are the core of the §5.3 claim. The doctrine sharpens
the paper's framework, not exposes a gap in it.

**On automatic consequence:** The adversarial paper attacks the logical guarantee reading
("modification is not always guaranteed because it's conditional"). Paper 1A's Thesis 2 is a
procedural-mechanics claim, not a logical-guarantee claim. Paper 1A explicitly acknowledges
non-modifying grants (§3.4). The "automatic" aspect is that once integration implies
modification, no separate petition is required. The adversarial attack hits a reading Paper 1A
does not hold.

**On the classification problem:** The adversarial paper's point that Type A/Type B
classification requires generative cognition is correct for difficult boundary cases — but the
boundary is not the center. For the binding-precedent and statutory-provision categories, the
classification is immediate: the court accepted the precedent, applied it elsewhere in the same
decision, and the party's argument directly invokes its scope. Classifying this as Type A
requires recognition, not deliberation.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the adversarial paper's constitutional-classification analysis.** I could have
argued that the STF reservation-phrase hypothetical is also Type A — that the court's
transcription of the precedent including the reservation implicitly committed it to an answer
about whether the specialist category applies. I rejected this because the adversarial paper's
analysis of this hypothetical is correct: whether post-legislative reenquadramento alters the
constitutional classification is genuinely open under the court's prior commitments. Contesting
this would have been a losing argument. Better to accept it and contest the paradigm-status
claim.

**Accepting the Type A/Type B distinction as a limitation Paper 1A should make.** The synthesis
path here would be to say: the adversarial paper is right that §5.3 needs qualification;
here is the qualification. I considered writing that as the supportive move — accepting the
adversarial attack's structural correctness and supporting Paper 1A by providing the missing
limiting principle. This would be an honest move. But it would be support by limitation, not
defense. The adversarial paper's main claim is that the cognition-constraint attack defeats
§5.3 for the paradigmatic case; my defense contests whether the paradigmatic case has been
correctly identified. The range argument is more valuable support than a concession paper.

**Writing on the implicit rejection doctrine alone.** A focused paper on how the *rejeição
implícita* doctrine supports Paper 1A's framework would be a genuine theoretical contribution.
But it would be narrower than what's needed: the adversarial attack also covers the paradigm
characterization and the automatic-consequence claim. A single paper engaging all three live
attack vectors is more useful than a partial response.

**Engaging Paper 1B instead.** Paper 1B is also unengaged. But Paper 1A now has a live attack
with a merged adversarial paper; Paper 1B does not. One paper per session; 1A first.
