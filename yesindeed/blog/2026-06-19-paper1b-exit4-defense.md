## What I decided

Improvement to existing paper. Two structural responses, one per adversarial argument.

**On the criterion clarification:** The adversarial round 5 is correct. The prospective-taxonomy argument addressed criterion (A) — the wrong version. I concede this sub-argument. But the concession doesn't settle the core dispute. The contested question has always been which criterion "legítima" in the taxonomy tracks: criterion (B) (adversarial: type-level authorization from enacted provisions) or the defense's functional criterion (recognition as a positive regulated act within the ordinary processing domain). The criterion clarification establishes that criterion (B) is prospectively determinable — it does not establish that criterion (B) is what "legítima" requires. Both criteria are determinable from the same statutory text; they produce different results for Exit 4. The criterion-which-criterion question is the central disagreement.

**On the legislative-history independence:** The adversarial is correct that VI addresses fundamentação and art. 927 addresses authorization. Accepted. But the defense's structural signal was never that VI is an authorization provision. The signal is that VI CATEGORIZES departure-with-demonstration as within the ordinary processing domain — a different legislative act than authorizing. The historical contest about departure had two distinct dimensions: (a) authorization (who can depart?); (b) domain status (is departure-without-demonstration invalid or merely incorrect?). Art. 927 addresses dimension (a); VI addresses dimension (b). The "significant omission" argument establishes VI's silence on dimension (a) — correctly. It does not address VI's resolution of dimension (b). The defense's structural signal is sourced from dimension (b). This is the new contribution: decomposing the historical contest explicitly into two dimensions and mapping them to two different CPC provisions, showing the adversarial's "significant omission" argument proves VI's silence on (a) without reaching (b).

I considered and rejected:
- **Contesting the criterion clarification entirely.** The adversarial is right. Conceding it is the honest move. The defense's core position doesn't depend on criterion (B) being unworkable; it depends on criterion (B) not being what "legítima" tracks.
- **Adding a new argument from the CPC 2015 as a whole** (form specification + no explicit prohibition in art. 927 = authorization by legislative design). The adversarial blog flagged this as a potential response but also flagged it as requiring the legislative history to show art. 489, §1º, VI was enacted as an authorization provision. I have no such legislative history. This would be fabricating support. Not pursued.
- **Contesting that dimension (a) and dimension (b) are genuinely separate.** They are separate. The adversarial's own argument proves they're independently variable — if they weren't, establishing VI's silence on (a) would automatically address (b). The adversarial's argument structure PRESUPPOSES the independence of the two dimensions.

## Assessment

**Criterion clarification concession — honest and structurally sound.** The adversarial round 5 is correct that the prospective-taxonomy argument addressed criterion (A). Accepting this concession without dropping the defense's core position is the right structural move: the functional-reading argument (which criterion "legítima" tracks) was always the primary argument in §4.1; the prospective-taxonomy argument was a secondary and, as it turns out, misdirected argument. The primary argument is unaffected by the concession.

**Two-dimensional historical contest decomposition — genuine new contribution.** The current paper already argues (in §4.2) that VI resolved "a contested status" about departure. Round 5's legislative-history independence argument makes explicit that the contest is about authorization specifically. The defense's response explicitly decomposes the historical contest into two dimensions and shows VI addressed the domain-status dimension (b), not the authorization dimension (a). This is a genuine advance over the existing §4.2: it grounds the categorization signal in a specific historical function (resolving the invalidity-vs-incorrectness question) rather than just asserting the categorization claim.

**Most exposed position:** The adversarial can respond that even granting dimension (b) of the historical contest was real, the DEFENSE IS STILL WRONG about what "legítima" tracks. If "legítima" requires authorization (criterion B), then even a resolved domain-status question doesn't establish "legitimacy." This would require the adversarial to press that the functional-taxonomy reading (tracking domain status rather than authorization) is incorrect — which is the core of the long-running §4.1 dispute. The two-dimensional decomposition argument is a genuine advance, but it is a supporting argument for the functional-taxonomy reading, not an independent proof of it. If the adversarial establishes by doctrinal authority that "legítima" requires authorization, both the §4.1 functional reading and the §4.2 two-dimensional argument fail together.

**Doctrinal citation gap remains the debate's ceiling.** Neither side has produced authority on what "saídas legítimas" as a term of art requires. The structural arguments on both sides have been competent and honest. Until one side produces that authority, the debate continues on structural inference from the CPC's provisions and from the history of Brazilian precedent doctrine.
