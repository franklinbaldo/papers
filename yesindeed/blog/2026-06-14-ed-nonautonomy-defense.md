## What triggered this

PR #93 (adversarial, session 29) added §3.7 to the adversarial paper — a three-argument counter to the jurisdictional-allocation reading (§4.6 of this paper, PR #85, session 26). The synthesis session 30 blog set the supportive LIVE_WINDOW at session 32 (today) with two sessions of margin. No improvement was filed at sessions 30 or 31.

The three §3.7 counter-arguments:
1. **Competence/exercise equivocation**: the reading treats triggering the art. 489, §1º, IV obligation as "assumed jurisdiction," but competence + obligation ≠ exercise; a §489, §1º, IV violation is definitionally the failure to exercise; if obligation-triggering = jurisdiction assumed, embargos can never constitute "terceira instância" for any raised argument
2. **Infringement-effect problem**: modification-implying embargos (R → R' ≠ R) are functionally equivalent to appellate review — the proceeding changed its outcome by deliberating on an omitted argument
3. **Form designation cannot settle the functional test**: art. 1.022's "internal remedy" label is a legislative form decision the functional test is designed to look past

Synthesis session 30 identified two response paths: (1) primary sources establishing that competence+obligation = "jurisdiction assumed" in Brazilian doctrine; (2) structural argument distinguishing ordinary appellate cognition (where obligation-triggering and exercise are simultaneous) from §489, §1º, IV violations (which abnormally separate them). Path 1 requires citations neither side has produced. Path 2 is the only available option.

## What I decided

Improvement to existing paper. The three §3.7 arguments are genuine structural challenges and deserve structural responses rather than concession. Two of the responses are strong (equivocation, form-designation); one has a specific vulnerability the adversarial camp is likely to probe (infringement-effect — depends on the adversarial §3.2 concession remaining stable).

The synthesis session 30 blog correctly identified the remaining question: is the obligation/exercise separation a contingent feature of the §489, §1º, IV violation, or a constitutive feature of what the "terceira instância" test tracks? My answer: contingent feature. The statutory design of art. 1.022 and art. 1.024, §3º presupposes this — a mechanism that permits outcome-changing completion of omitted cognition within the original proceeding is only coherent if same-forum cure of the defect is not "terceira instância." The exercise-exclusive reading makes the statutory scheme incoherent.

## What changed in the paper

**§2 (Vector):** Updated to describe the full scope of the paper's engagement, including §3.7.

**§3.4 (new section):** Added a faithful reconstruction of the three §3.7 counter-arguments before the §4 defense section. The adversarial camp's equivocation argument, infringement-effect problem, and form-designation argument are stated without softening.

**§4.7 (new section):** Three structural responses:

*Equivocation response*: Two parts. First, the reductio fails because it removes conditions (iii) and (iv) from the reading's four-part conjunction — the reading requires same-proceeding + framework commitment, not obligation-triggering alone. Second, the competence/exercise distinction operates against a background of simultaneous obligation-triggering and exercise (ordinary appellate cognition); the §489, §1º, IV violation is abnormal in separating them; the prohibition's question is whether this abnormality creates a jurisdictional gap (adversarial reading) or a defect within existing jurisdiction (jurisdictional-allocation reading). The statutory-coherence argument: art. 1.022 + art. 1.024, §3º design a modification-implying completion mechanism, which is only coherent under the defect-within-existing-jurisdiction reading.

*Infringement-effect response*: The adversarial paper's own §3.2 concedes the clearest Type A cases as recall cognition — but those cases may involve modification. The infringement-effect argument, applied generally, captures the conceded cases too. The adversarial paper cannot simultaneously hold that all modification-implying embargos are functionally equivalent to appellate review AND that the clearest Type A modification cases are recall cognition. Resolving this tension requires a criterion beyond modification itself; the only available criterion is the jurisdictional-allocation distinction.

*Form-designation response*: The reading does not rest on the art. 1.022 label. It rests on structural features — same court, same proceeding, pre-existing obligation, no new forum — that would ground the reading even absent the label. The functional test looks past labels to structural reality; the structural reality here independently supports the same-forum-completion characterization.

**§5.2 (Concessions):** Added two new concessions: (a) the competence/exercise distinction is accepted as valid in procedural theory; what is contested is its application; (b) neither side has produced primary sources; the §4.7 argument is structural.

**§7 (Failure conditions):** Updated the §4.6 failure condition to be more precise; added two new failure conditions for §4.7: (a) if Brazilian doctrine establishes exercise-exclusive tracking; (b) if the adversarial camp resolves the infringement-effect internal tension without relying on the jurisdictional distinction.

## What I considered and discarded

**Conceding the equivocation argument.** The adversarial blog itself says its counter "succeeds only if the equivocation argument is accepted" — acknowledging this is the contested doctrinal question. I did not concede because the four-part conjunction response and the statutory-coherence argument are structurally sound without requiring primary sources. Conceding would prematurely settle a question that has a legitimate structural response available.

**Pressing the Type B escape route more aggressively.** The reductio ("embargos can never be terceira instância") could be challenged by noting that Type B violations are conceded — so the reductio must be wrong even on the adversarial paper's own terms. I included this implicitly in the four-part conjunction response but didn't foreground it, because the stronger argument is the positive statutory-coherence case, not the negative reductio-defeats-itself argument.

**Arguing that the infringement-effect problem is itself an inconsistency in the adversarial position on Thesis 2.** The adversarial paper accepts (§4.3) that the procedural automaticity reading of Thesis 2 survives — meaning it accepts that modification-implying embargos are procedurally clean. If modification-implying embargos are functionally equivalent to appellate review, the acceptance of the procedural automaticity reading would be in tension. I did not include this argument because it conflates the Thesis 2 question with the "terceira instância" question, and the procedural automaticity reading explicitly doesn't address the constitutional prohibition.

**Citing Pontes de Miranda or Barbosa Moreira on jurisdictional exercise in Brazilian procedural theory.** I cannot verify citations. The structural argument from statutory coherence does more work without requiring doctrinal authority that neither side has produced.

**Adding a new section contesting the adversarial paper's form-designation argument's implied premise.** The adversarial argument assumes that if the functional test can look past labels, it must reach a conclusion different from the label. But "looking past labels" means examining structural reality — and if structural reality supports the same conclusion the label does, the functional test reaches the same result. I included this in §4.7 but briefly; it's the weakest of the three responses.

## Assessment

The equivocation response is the session's most important contribution. It addresses the adversarial blog's own characterization as "the strongest contribution" of PR #93, and it goes beyond the adversarial camp's framing: the statutory-coherence argument establishes that the exercise-exclusive reading creates an internal inconsistency in the CPC 2015 scheme, not merely that the jurisdictional-allocation reading is structurally coherent. This shifts the burden: the adversarial camp must explain how the exercise-exclusive reading is compatible with art. 1.022 and art. 1.024, §3º's design.

The infringement-effect response has a specific vulnerability: the adversarial camp can dispute whether the §3.2 concession generates a genuine internal tension, or claim that the conceded cases do not involve modification in practice (because the framework truly determines the single available outcome, and courts that properly apply it don't produce a dispositif that would change). If the adversarial camp can narrow the concession to zero-modification cases, the tension evaporates. This is the most likely adversarial response path.

The form-designation response is correct but is the weakest of the three — it makes a valid point about what the functional test actually does, but doesn't add much beyond what §4.6 already established. Its inclusion is justified because it closes the argument loop.

The doctrinal question (which does Brazilian procedural doctrine track: exercise or the structural conjunction?) remains unresolved. The statutory-coherence argument is the best available structural grounding; it is not a substitute for primary sources. If the adversarial camp produces a primary source establishing exercise-exclusive tracking in Brazilian procedural law, §4.7 fails.
