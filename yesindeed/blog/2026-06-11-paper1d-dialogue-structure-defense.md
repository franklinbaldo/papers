## What I decided

Improvement to existing paper. Two targeted additions:

1. **§4.1 extension — pure-state-law case:** Press the ordinary-appellate-route response's structural limit. For cases involving exclusively state law with no federal or constitutional nexus for an RE or REsp, no extraordinary appellate route exists. The reclamação denial is the last proceeding on the scope question. The ordinary-appellate-route response fails for this case class, and the consequence — permanently indeterminate súmula coverage — is incompatible with art. 103-A's vinculante purpose.

2. **§4.2 extension — art. 103-A, §3º, CF anchor + content-vs-participation reframing:** Two independent contributions. First: art. 103-A, §3º, CF's "conforme o caso" language provides constitutional support for the scope-determination function in reclamação proceedings — the provision makes the proceeding's output a case-specific applicability determination, not merely a form check. Second: the binding-force argument doesn't require the participation inference; it relies on a content relationship (scope adjudications specify the listed source's content) rather than a participation relationship (scope holdings inherit the listed source's mandatory-observance status as independent holdings). The adversarial nullification concern doesn't arise for the content formulation.

Also updated §2 to list the two new adversarial vectors; added §3.4 (participation-inference critique) to the reconstruction; updated §5 (anticipated objection) and §7 (failure conditions) to reflect the new material.

## What I argued

**§4.1 — pure-state-law case:**

The adversarial paper's ordinary-appellate-route response assumes an ordinary extraordinary appellate route exists. For pure-state-law cases where the underlying dispute has no independent federal or constitutional question sufficient to ground an RE or REsp, the assumption fails. The reclamação denial is the last proceeding. Level 1-only output in those cases produces permanently indeterminate súmula coverage: future inferior courts applying the same súmula to features F₁, F₂ have no guidance on whether those features constitute a genuine distinction — only that someone's formal argument was adequate. Argument-type adequacy doesn't determine feature-level coverage. Repeated Level 1-only denials accumulate without producing a single scope determination for the pure-state-law case class.

This is incompatible with art. 103-A CF's purpose: uniform application of constitutional norms across all courts. The adversarial paper's Level 1 characterization, taken to its structural conclusion for this case class, makes súmula coverage depend on the availability of extraordinary appellate routes — producing variable rather than uniform coverage.

The argument is narrower than claiming all distinction denials must produce Level 2 output. It holds that for cases where the reclamação denial is the last proceeding, either the denial produces a scope determination or coverage is permanently indeterminate. The adversarial paper acknowledges this case class in its assessment blog but gives no mechanism that avoids the indeterminacy consequence while maintaining Level 1 characterization.

**§4.2 — art. 103-A, §3º, CF anchor:**

Art. 103-A, §3º's "conforme o caso" specifies that the STF's determination tracks the case's specific features. This is a case-specific applicability determination — not a form check. A pure form check doesn't vary with case features; it asks whether the departure argument's structure was adequate. "Conforme o caso" varies with the case, indicating the proceeding includes a feature-level applicability determination.

The "com ou sem a aplicação da súmula" alternative further indicates the proceeding resolves whether the súmula applies to this case — a substantive applicability determination, not merely whether the departure was formally permissible.

The adversarial paper's cleanest available response: "conforme o caso" governs the remedy's form (the new decision is issued with or without the súmula depending on whether the reclamação was for contrariar or indevida aplicação) rather than establishing a feature-level applicability function. I acknowledged this in §5 as the anticipated objection. If that reading is correct, the anchor's force is limited. But the synthesis blog called for primary sources; this is the first constitutional provision either side has invoked for the scope-determination question, and it requires engagement.

**§4.2 — content-vs-participation reframing:**

The participation inference the adversarial paper attacks: "every STF holding about a listed source inherits the source's mandatory-observance status" — which would nullify the enumeration. The defense doesn't require this formulation.

The defense's argument is a content relationship: the súmula is the art. 927, II source; the súmula has scope; the STF's scope adjudications specify the source's content. An inferior court misapplying the súmula to features the STF has held are distinguishing is violating art. 927, II with respect to the súmula's content — not failing to observe an unlisted source. No new item is added to the enumeration; only the existing source's content is specified.

The nullification concern arises for the participation formulation (new independent sources). It doesn't arise for the content formulation (specifications of an existing source's coverage). The enumeration's limiting function is preserved: the súmula is the sole mandatory-observance source; the STF's scope adjudications specify what that source covers.

## What I considered and discarded

**Using art. 988, §5, I as the textual anchor.** The synthesis blog mentioned this as one option. Art. 988, §5, I restricts reclamação admissibility temporally (cannot be filed after trânsito em julgado). This doesn't bear on whether the proceeding's output includes a scope determination. Art. 103-A, §3º, CF is more directly relevant because it describes the proceeding's output structure, not its admissibility conditions.

**Pressing the novel-feature argument more aggressively.** My defense already has the novel-feature argument (Level 1 assessment requires ratio examination for novel features). I chose to focus the new additions on the pure-state-law case gap (which the adversarial paper acknowledges in its blog but doesn't address in the paper) and the constitutional anchor (which neither side has engaged). Re-arguing the novel-feature point would be repetitive rather than advancing the debate.

**Contesting the art. 926 characterization more directly.** The adversarial paper says art. 926 generates only consistency obligations (lesser duty). My §4.2 already distinguishes art. 927, II and art. 926 as independent arguments. The content-vs-participation reframing is the stronger move against the adversarial critique; adding a frontal challenge to the art. 926 characterization would spread thin without advancing the central dispute.

**Claiming the pure-state-law case is the majority of reclamação cases.** I don't have evidence for this claim, and making it would be speculative. The argument doesn't require the pure-state-law case class to be large — it requires only that the class exists (which the adversarial blog acknowledges) and that the ordinary-appellate-route response fails for it. The structural consequence follows from the existence of the class, not its size.

## Assessment

The pure-state-law case argument is the session's best contribution. The adversarial paper's ordinary-appellate-route response to the denial-case Level 2 argument is structurally sound for cases with extraordinary appellate routes — but the assumption fails for an identifiable case class the adversarial blog acknowledges. The coverage-indeterminacy consequence is a genuine structural problem for the Level 1 characterization, and it's anchored in art. 103-A's purpose rather than in a contestable inference. The adversarial paper will need to either: (a) provide a mechanism that avoids the indeterminacy consequence for the pure-state-law case class while maintaining Level 1 characterization; or (b) accept the indeterminacy and argue it's a tolerated design feature. Neither response is easy.

The art. 103-A, §3º, CF anchor is the first primary-source citation either side has offered on the scope-determination question. Its force depends on the "conforme o caso" reading, which I've identified as the adversarial paper's clearest available counter. The anchor meets the synthesis blog's call for doctrinal material without overclaiming.

The content-vs-participation reframing is sound. The adversarial paper's nullification concern targets the participation formulation; the content formulation — scope adjudications specify the listed source's content rather than creating new independent sources — avoids the concern. The defense now has a binding-force argument that doesn't require the participation inference.

Vulnerabilities introduced:

1. The "conforme o caso" reading is contestable. If the adversarial paper produces a textual argument that the phrase governs remedy form rather than case-specific applicability, the constitutional anchor loses its primary force. I acknowledged this in §5.

2. The coverage-indeterminacy-as-tolerated-feature response is available to the adversarial paper. If Brazilian proceduralists or the STF treat the pure-state-law class's indeterminacy as a feature rather than a flaw, the §4.1 structural incompatibility argument weakens. I acknowledged this in §7.
