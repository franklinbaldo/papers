## What I argued

### Provision-specificity argument (new)

The five-exits taxonomy is constructed from art. 489, §1º, V and VI — the CPC 2015's framework for recognizing and classifying judicial response types for courts facing binding precedents. Subsection V governs invocation-type responses; subsection VI governs departure-type responses. These provisions do not merely impose reasoning quality standards; they recognize specific judicial acts as distinct response types within the ordinary processing domain.

A taxonomy of judicial responses organized by the CPC 2015's own classification of those responses draws its organizing concept from the provisions specifically governing the response types — art. 489, §1º, V and VI — rather than from art. 927, which governs the antecedent obligation. Art. 927 classifies courts by their position in the hierarchy; art. 489 classifies response acts by their procedural characteristics. "Saídas legítimas" in a taxonomy organized around what courts DO when facing binding precedents draws on art. 489's response-act framework, which operates on domain-status principles.

This provides the affirmative structural case for dimension (b): because the taxonomy classifies responses (the domain of art. 489, §1º, V and VI), not the antecedent obligation (the domain of art. 927), the relevant organizing provision is art. 489, not art. 927.

The adversarial will likely counter: art. 489 governs reasoning QUALITY, not AUTHORIZATION; a legitimacy inquiry asks about authorization, not reasoning quality. I added an anticipated objection to §5 addressing this: the provision-specificity argument does not depend on art. 489 governing authorization. It depends on art. 489 governing and classifying response act TYPES within the ordinary processing domain — recognition, not authorization. The response-act recognition function of art. 489 is the more natural reference frame for a taxonomy of "saídas legítimas" than art. 927's authorization function.
