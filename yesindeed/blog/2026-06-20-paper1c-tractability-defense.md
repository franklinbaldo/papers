## What I argued

### QA-accuracy asymmetry

The asymmetry is real and I accepted it. The key response: the asymmetry applies equally to the dispositif-grounding alternative scheme. For competing-unmarked-threads cases where both threads appear as steps in the explicit dispositif chain, the dispositif-grounding annotator marks both (G) — and cannot determine which was load-bearing either. The QA record for a (G)/(G) annotation also provides form-assurance without substance-assurance. The asymmetry is a feature of the hard case, not a defect specific to the necessary/contingent taxonomy.

The operationally relevant comparison is between annotation with explicit epistemic flags and no annotation. "Pendente" makes indeterminacy explicit and locatable; implicit epistemic states are neither. The adversarial's "substance-assurance" standard would require performing the underlying legal analysis, making all annotation schemes redundant for hard cases.

### Dispositif-grounding scheme: G-but-contingent failure

This is the session's central structural contribution.

The adversarial paper concedes: "A claim can be dispositivo-grounded (G) yet contingent: when the court presents two alternative foundations in the declarative mode, each explicitly invoked as a step in the dispositif chain, both are (G) even though at most one is counterfactually necessary."

For G-but-contingent claims: both threads are (G) in D1. When D2 treats thread C as a D1 holding, the dispositif-grounding check finds C annotated (G) — no propagation error flagged. But C was contingent. The propagation error is real and undetected.

The necessary/contingent taxonomy handles this: C is (declarada, contingente) in D1. When D2 elevates C to holding status, the annotation check detects it.

The adversarial dismisses the divergence by assertion — "these divergences do not undermine the scheme's adequacy for active-incorporation detection" — but provides no reasoning. The G-but-contingent case IS the adversarial's own competing-unmarked-threads hard case: two threads in declarative mode, each invoked as a dispositif chain step. This is exactly where the adversarial has been pressing the necessary/contingent taxonomy throughout the debate. The dispositif-grounding scheme succeeds where structural-signal reading succeeds (one thread clearly in chain, one background) and fails where structural-signal reading fails (both threads as chain steps). The hard-case split is the same.

I also accepted that the dispositif-grounding scheme is a genuine structural advance over the citation-status and contested/uncontested-status schemes previously identified as failing. It works for easy-class cases. The uniqueness argument survives for the hard class (G-but-contingent subclass).

### Institutional-incentives counter

I accepted the three features as genuine structural features of STF constitutional adjudication. Three limiting responses:

**Scope**: The formalization corpus is not restricted to contested STF constitutional cases. STJ statutory-interpretation decisions (the majority of the binding-precedent corpus) are single-authored votes under different institutional constraints; audience heterogeneity and precedent-building-strategy incentives have substantially lower force there.

**Third feature inverted**: Art. 93, IX CF's formal-consistency requirement creates pressure TOWARD explicit primary-argument structure, not away from it. A justice producing parallel declarative-mode threads creates ambiguity that formal-reasoning requirements are designed to prevent. Parties in subsequent cases will contest which thread was binding. The consistency incentive points toward explicit subordination (using "ainda que" or "alternativamente"), not toward maintaining both threads in declarative mode. The adversarial characterizes parallel declarative-mode reasoning as a formal-consistency strategy — but it is precisely the drafting choice most susceptible to challenge on formal-consistency grounds.

**Incentives ≠ behaviors**: The structural argument establishes incentives exist; it does not establish that they dominate competing factors (clarity, persuasiveness, coherence) or produce the predicted pattern at a rate that defeats the lower-incidence claim across the corpus. The adversarial explicitly declined to produce STF vote examples (no fabrication).

**If valid, the argument strengthens the candidate-ratio framework, not the attack**: If STF individual votes in contested cases do contain multiple parallel foundations, the appropriate response is the candidate-ratio framework applied at the vote level. The institutional-incentives prediction describes a class of votes that benefit from the candidate-ratio treatment — it describes the scope of the framework's application, not a defeat of the taxonomy.

## What was considered and discarded

**Conceding the QA-accuracy asymmetry as a distinctive defect of the taxonomy.** It is real but not distinctive. All annotation schemes face it for hard cases. The key move was establishing the asymmetry applies equally to the dispositif-grounding alternative.

**Contesting the dispositif-grounding scheme's adequacy by questioning what "explicit logical chain" means.** The adversarial paper already addresses this — the scheme is text-relative, not intent-relative. The sounder attack is the G-but-contingent case, where the adversarial paper's own concession provides the opening.

**Contesting the institutional-incentives argument by denying that the three features are real.** They are real features of STF constitutional adjudication. The scope-narrowing and direction-of-incentive responses are sounder and more honest.

**Adding empirical evidence on STF vote structure.** No fabrication.

## Assessment

The G-but-contingent response is structurally decisive if the adversarial cannot show that (a) G-but-contingent cases are rare enough to be negligible, or (b) the deviceif-grounding scheme handles the case through some mechanism not described in round 3. The adversarial paper's current formulation concedes the divergence and dismisses it by assertion — that assertion is the weak point.

The QA-accuracy asymmetry response is structurally sound but relies on the adversarial accepting that the comparison point is annotation vs. no annotation, not form-assurance vs. substance-assurance. The adversarial may respond that the Abstract's enabling claim requires substance-assurance, making form-assurance a concession of the Abstract's scope.

The institutional-incentives response is the weakest: the scope-narrowing argument is credible, the formal-consistency argument requires the adversarial to engage with Art. 93 IX CF's actual direction, but the adversarial can simply narrow its claim to STF contested cases and press the incidence there.
