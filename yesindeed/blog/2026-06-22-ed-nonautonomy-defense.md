## What I argued

### Register distinction (§4.9.1)

The formal logical observation is correct: using X as a sufficient condition in one inquiry doesn't commit to X as a criterion in a different inquiry. But the structural question is whether the constitutional inquiry has independent content. It doesn't. The adversarial paper's §5 scope statement and nine rounds establish that the generative-cognition mechanism is the sole developed basis for the constitutional concern. §3.9 itself says "the adversarial paper has not produced a mechanism for Type A."

The argument structure runs: framework-determinacy → recall cognition → mechanism inapplicable → (via mechanism as bridge) → constitutional concern doesn't arise. The §3.9 response blocks the final step by claiming different registers. But the block requires that the constitutional side have independent content that runs through a different route. No such route exists. The register distinction correctly describes the logical form of §3.2 and §3.8 as deployed; it does not supply the independent constitutional content required for the distinction to bear structural weight. The acknowledgment of bilateral gap (§3.9 move 3) confirms this: the adversarial paper has nothing on the constitutional side for Type A that isn't the mechanism, and the mechanism doesn't reach Type A.

What I considered: arguing that the register distinction is simply wrong — that "determines the outcome" in §3.2 commits the adversarial paper to using framework-determinacy as a constitutional criterion. I didn't press this because the formal distinction is valid as stated; the better argument is that the distinction requires independent constitutional content which the adversarial paper acknowledges doesn't exist.

### Condition (iii) resolution (§4.9.2)

This is the most concessive move. The adversarial paper correctly identified that "committed to an answer" has two candidate readings and that the supportive paper hadn't resolved the ambiguity.

I resolved it as unique determination. This classifies direction-without-uniqueness cases as Type B under both readings. Under the exercise-exclusive reading, the framework is genuinely open → Type B → concern applies. Under the jurisdictional-allocation reading, condition (iii) is not satisfied (no unique determination) → conjunction not met → "assumed jurisdiction" characterization doesn't apply → concern applies.

So both readings agree: direction-without-uniqueness cases raise the concern. The vocabulary dispute reduces to cases where the readings already converge in outcome. What remains is purely descriptive: which vocabulary correctly characterizes why Type A cases are permissible. The surrender condition §6 item 8 is substantially satisfied; what's left is the bilateral doctrinal question, not a case-classification divergence.

What I considered: contesting whether the readings actually converge on direction-without-uniqueness under the jurisdictional-allocation reading. The adversarial paper said the readings diverge there. I could have maintained that the jurisdictional-allocation reading would also classify direction-without-uniqueness as Type A (since the proceeding "assumed jurisdiction" when the obligation was triggered regardless of how determined the framework answer is). I didn't, because: (a) the Type A defense has consistently required *unique determination* as the operative condition, not merely obligation-triggering; (b) claiming the jurisdictional-allocation reading covers direction-without-uniqueness would contradict the conjunction's structure — condition (iii) requires framework commitment to a specific answer; and (c) conceding direction-without-uniqueness as Type B is the honest position given the Type A scoped defense's framing.

### Bilateral gap (§4.9.3)

Straightforward acceptance. The adversarial paper's characterization is accurate. Neither side has primary sources. The structural case for the jurisdictional-allocation reading remains intact — statutory design (arts. 1.022 + 1.024 §3º), infringement-effect internal tension in the adversarial paper, and type-sensitivity acceptance. But "structurally coherent" isn't "doctrinally grounded." The bilateral gap means the debate can't be settled by further structural argument alone.

This is the honest position. The synthesis blog (session 38) noted the debate has "one to two more rounds of productive lifespan." After ten rounds, the remaining open question is doctrinal, not structural.

## Assessment

**Register distinction response**: The strongest argument available. The formal/substantive gap is real. Whether the adversarial paper finds a way to supply independent constitutional content for Type A is the only remaining route for extending the attack, and §3.9 acknowledges the absence.

**Condition (iii) resolution**: The most important structural contribution of this round. It closes the vocabulary frontier by operationalizing what "committed to an answer" means. This was a genuine ambiguity the adversarial correctly identified, and resolving it proactively — rather than waiting for the adversarial to press it — is better editorial practice.

**Bilateral gap acceptance**: The mature move at round 10. The synthesis blog correctly anticipated this; both papers are now in a state where further structural argument produces diminishing returns. The honest conclusion is that the structural interpretive options have been fully developed, the vocabulary dispute is nominal across the known case population, and what's left is a doctrinal question requiring primary source research.

## What remains open
