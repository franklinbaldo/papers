# Threshold Adjustment Is Not Free: A Partial Defense of Paper 1E's Equilibrium-Shift Prediction

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "Custos Argumentativos e Equilíbrio Institucional" (`paper1E_custos_argumentativos.md`), predicts that technological reduction in argument-production costs will drive the Brazilian binding-precedent system from its current (β, β) Nash equilibrium toward the rational-dialogue equilibrium (α, α). The mechanism: lower costs increase quality-argument volume, which raises the STF's reputational cost of non-engagement, generating an inflection point at which the STF shifts from (β) to (α).

> "Há um ponto — não definível com precisão, mas estruturalmente necessário — em que o custo de reafirmar autoridade sem enfrentar argumentos supera o benefício de não enfrentar. Nesse ponto, o STF tem incentivo para mudar a estratégia: enfrentar os melhores argumentos, distinguir quando a distinção é genuína, revisar quando o argumento revela erro real." (§4.3)

---

## 2. What This Support Adds

**Vector:** Direct defense. This paper responds to `otherwise/paper1e-equilibrium-shift.md`, which attacks the equilibrium-shift prediction through four vectors: (1) the endogenous threshold problem — the STF can raise its engagement threshold proportionally to volume increase, preventing the inflection point; (2) historical refutation of the reputational mechanism — academic criticism of plenário virtual practice has not changed STF behavior; (3) the differential cost-reduction premise lacks support — tools reduce textual costs more than analytical costs; (4) the game model misspecifies the STF as a unitary actor rather than accounting for the rapporteur structure.

This defense accepts the endogenous threshold problem as structurally real and the rapporteur-structure critique as a genuine modeling gap. It contests the adversarial paper's treatment of threshold adjustment as cost-free (vectors 1–2) and the inference from rapporteur structure to fully diffuse reputational costs (vector 4). On vector 3, it accepts the adversarial paper's strongest point with a narrowing qualification.

---

## 3. The Defense

### 3.1 Threshold Adjustment Carries Procedural Costs That Scale with Argument Quality

The adversarial paper frames the STF's response to rising argument volume as a binary choice between option (a) — engage proportionally — and option (b) — raise the engagement threshold proportionally. Under option (b), each increment of quality-argument volume is absorbed by threshold adjustment, preventing the inflection point. The paper treats option (b) as available without specific cost to the STF beyond the abstract "monitoring" problem.

This framing omits the procedural costs that option (b) generates under the CPC 2015's procedural architecture.

A reclamação is a formal judicial proceeding under arts. 988–993 CPC, not a petition the STF may elect to ignore. The rapporteur must issue a decision — monocratic or through plenário virtual. For that decision to be legally adequate, it must satisfy art. 93, IX CF: "fundamentadas todas as decisões, sob pena de nulidade." A decision that dismisses a reclamação is itself subject to embargos de declaração if it omits a "point on which there should have been a ruling" (art. 1022, II, CPC). The adequacy of dismissal reasoning scales with the argument's structural precision.

This creates an asymmetry that the adversarial paper's binary does not capture. A reclamação arguing only "the STF's súmula X applies and the court below didn't follow it" can be dismissed with minimal reasoning: the dismissal identifies the súmula, notes that the court below applied it (or that the departure was compliant), and denies the reclamação. A reclamação that explicitly identifies the súmula, traces the inferior court's departure, specifies the ratio components the inferior court challenged, and provides a legally structured argument for why the challenge succeeds — this requires proportionally more substantive treatment in any dismissal that is to survive an embargos de declaração challenge on omissão grounds.

The adversarial paper correctly observes that the STF exercises discretion through the plenário virtual mechanism. But this discretion operates within the constraint that the resulting decision must be reasoned. "Raising the threshold" means deciding that a class of arguments does not warrant engagement — but for a formally structured, ratio-identifying quality argument, any dismissal that does not address the argument's structure is vulnerable to embargos de declaração for omissão or contradição. The STF's discretion is over *how deeply* to engage, not over *whether* to acknowledge the argument's structure at all.

The practical consequence: option (b) is not free. As argument quality rises, the minimum adequate dismissal reasoning increases. The STF can maintain a rising threshold, but the cost of maintaining it rises proportionally to the quality of arguments it is excluding. This does not establish that the inflection point is reached — but it does establish that the endogenous threshold adjustment is not the free escape valve the adversarial paper describes. The threshold is adjustable at increasing cost per quality argument excluded, not at zero cost.

A further specification is needed on the adversarial counter-reply's empirical grounding for the self-policing argument. The documented scholarly record — Mitidiero's and Marinoni's documentation of brief engagements in reclamação proceedings — concerns primarily the main-proceeding context: whether the STF engages the challenger's departure reasoning in the judgment granting or denying the reclamação. This is a different proceeding from embargos de declaração filed after the reclamação dismissal specifically alleging omissão of the ratio-identification component. The adversarial counter-reply's claim that self-assessed constraints produce the expected outcome conflates two distinct empirical claims: (a) the STF rarely receives embargos de declaração specifically challenging reclamação dismissal adequacy for omissão; and (b) the STF dismisses such embargos, when filed, with brief conclusory responses. The documented pattern is evidence for the main-proceeding behavior that informs both characterizations but does not independently establish (b).

The procedural distinction matters structurally. Embargos de declaração for omissão specifically allege that a named point was present in the argument and absent in the judgment — the responding court must affirmatively determine whether that specific point was omitted. Even a brief "não há omissão" response constitutes an affirmative determination about the completeness of the prior judgment, not merely a reaffirmation of outcome. Whether this structural difference prevents the self-policing dynamic from operating as the adversarial paper predicts is uncertain; what the documented scholarly evidence does not establish is that the STF's practice in responding to adequacy-specific omissão challenges follows the same pattern as its main-proceeding engagement behavior.

### 3.2 The Historical Evidence Does Not Test the Mechanism the Paper Invokes

The adversarial paper argues that the visibility mechanism has an empirical track record: Brazilian academic criticism of plenário virtual non-engagement practice has been sustained and public for years without changing STF behavior. If the mechanism were operative, the criticism would have produced change. It has not. The paper has not explained why a higher volume of individual quality arguments would succeed where sustained academic criticism of the practice has not.

This argument is structurally correct about academic criticism of the systemic practice. It is not a valid test of the mechanism Paper 1E actually invokes.

Academic criticism of the systemic practice — documented, for example, by Mitidiero's critique of cortes superiores versus cortes supremas — operates as diffuse institutional pressure. No individual critic has strong incentive to monitor each reclamação judgment continuously. The criticism is directed at the practice as a policy, not at specific decisions on specific arguments. The STF can accommodate this criticism in its public pronouncements while maintaining the practice in individual case processing. This is the precise collective action problem that Paper 1E identifies in §2.2: systematic non-engagement is individually rational for the STF even when each individual instance generates some diffuse reputational cost.

The mechanism Paper 1E invokes is different: it requires that quality arguments reaching the STF generate concentrated monitoring by actors with both the incentive and capacity to document non-engagement in specific cases. The party that produced the quality argument is exactly this actor. The party has a direct stake in the reclamação outcome; their counsel has professional standing to document and publicize non-engagement; their academic supporters have specific material (the argument and the STF's response) to analyze.

The adversarial paper's objection that this still requires "case-specific monitoring by actors who lack incentive and analytical capacity" does not apply to the argument's producer. The party whose quality argument was ignored, and whose counsel articulates the argument, has concentrated incentive and the specific analytical capacity the argument itself required. They are not diffuse academic observers; they are the party who paid to produce the argument and has litigation incentives aligned with documenting why the STF's dismissal was inadequate.

This argument does not establish that party-level monitoring will succeed in producing the costs that force threshold change. It establishes that the historical evidence cited by the adversarial paper — academic criticism of the systemic practice — does not test the mechanism Paper 1E invokes, because the mechanism operates at the individual-argument level, not at the systemic-practice-criticism level.

The adversarial round 2 accepts the party-monitoring distinction and presses a pattern-formation gap: individual documentation is tractable by parties, but aggregating individual cases into visible cross-case patterns requires diffuse actors who, in the adversarial paper's framing, lack per-case incentive and specific analytical access. This framing assumes the relevant aggregation actors are academic scholars whose analysis cycles are measured in months or years. Professional legal publications — JOTA, Consultor Jurídico, and analogous outlets — perform the aggregation function at a different timescale. These publications employ legally trained journalists who cover individual STF decisions within hours of their release, track doctrinal patterns across cases, and publish observations about STF reclamação engagement adequacy contemporaneously with the decisions being assessed. Their analysis reaches the professional legal community, including STF justices' offices, at the same frequency as the decisions themselves.

The adversarial surrender condition 3 requires an actor who aggregates individual documentation into visible cross-case patterns with both the capacity and concentrated incentive to do so systematically. Professional legal publications satisfy both conditions: analytical capacity (legal journalists with doctrinal familiarity adequate to assess engagement quality in the reclamação context) and systematic professional incentive to cover STF decisions continuously — not contingent on the outcome of any specific case, not subject to the collective action problem affecting parties whose interest in documentation ends when their litigation does. A sustained pattern of brief dismissals of formally structured quality arguments across a short period is exactly the type of cross-case observation these outlets document and publish as pattern-level analysis.

Practitioner and legal-academic commentary on social media platforms extends the real-time aggregation function: doctrinal scholars post immediate reactions to STF decisions and accumulate cross-case observations within professional networks that include STF justices' offices. This does not fully resolve the adversarial §3.2 counter's collective action framing — diffuse commentary is not equivalent to systematic empirical documentation, and the historical evidence that sustained pattern-level scholarly criticism has not produced behavioral change remains relevant. The contribution is narrower: it contests the adversarial paper's implicit identification of aggregation actors exclusively with publication-cycle-constrained scholars, establishing that near-real-time aggregation actors with systematic incentive and analytical capacity exist in the Brazilian legal information environment.

### 3.3 Differential Cost Reduction: Accept the Adversarial Paper's Strongest Point with a Narrowing Qualification

The adversarial paper argues that cost reduction reduces textual production costs substantially and analytical costs less certainly — and that tools producing formally structured but analytically incorrect arguments are cheap to produce, increasing noise without improving signal. This is the adversarial paper's structurally strongest point, and it is not resolved by Paper 1E's acknowledgment of the differential-reduction premise as "reasonable for tools incorporating explicit argument structure, but not guaranteed."

The defense here is narrow: the adversarial paper correctly identifies that tools reduce text-production costs more reliably than they reduce analytical costs. But the mechanism Paper 1E requires does not demand that tools reduce all analytical costs equally. The specific cost component the mechanism requires to fall differentially is the *articulation cost* for arguments where the ratio has already been correctly identified by a legally trained practitioner. When ratio identification is correct, the cost of producing a formally structured and internally consistent argument from that identification is the component tools reduce most reliably. The analytical work — identifying the ratio, mapping the facts — remains a practitioner contribution; the structure that makes the argument auditable is what tools assist.

This narrowing has a consequence the defense must acknowledge directly: the equilibrium-shift mechanism operates primarily for a specific class of practitioners — those who have the analytical capacity to identify the ratio correctly but previously lacked the resources to produce the structural elaboration that makes the argument auditable. For practitioners already producing quality arguments at pre-technological costs, cost reduction may not differentially change behavior. The mechanism is most operative for the practitioners at the margin — skilled enough to identify the ratio, previously resource-constrained in elaboration. Whether this marginal group is large enough to generate the volume effect Paper 1E requires is an empirical question that neither paper has resolved.

The adversarial paper's concern that formally structured but analytically incorrect arguments increase noise is correct and not fully answered by this narrowing. The most honest position: the differential cost-reduction premise holds for the specific narrow class of structurally elaborated arguments where the analytical work is practitioner-supplied and the structural elaboration is tool-assisted. It does not hold for arguments where tools substitute for analytical capacity. Whether quality-argument volume from the first class exceeds noise from the second class in the reclamação docket depends on empirical conditions not established by either paper.

### 3.4 Rapporteur Structure: Accept the Modeling Gap, Contest the Inference to Fully Diffuse Costs

The adversarial paper correctly identifies that the 2×2 game model misspecifies the STF's decision-making structure. Engagement decisions are made by the rapporteur, not the full court. Reputational costs from non-engagement are distributed across eleven justices; engagement decisions are concentrated at one. The inference gap between institutional reputational cost and individual rapporteur incentive is structurally real.

The inference from this gap to "reputational costs are too diffuse to operate at the relevant decision node" overstates the diffusion problem in the Brazilian legal context.

Brazilian STF justices are individually attributed in the academic literature, in bar-community assessment, and in their own academic careers. Each justice's reclamação jurisprudence is publicly attributed to them as rapporteur. Brazilian processual scholars cite rapporteur-specific decisions in their doctrinal analyses; processual monographs track and criticize individual justices' handling of specific doctrinal questions. The professional reputations these justices hold within the academic processual-law community — which is small enough for individual-level tracking to be feasible — are affected by their individual rapporteur decisions on quality arguments.

This does not eliminate the gap the adversarial paper identifies. There is a genuine difference between reputational costs concentrated at the institutional level (affecting "the STF's reputation") and costs concentrated at the individual-justice level (affecting Ministro X's reputation as a constitutional scholar). The adversarial paper is correct that the game model predicts institutional-level strategy change, which is a different thing from individual-justice behavior change.

The narrower claim: individual-level reputational costs are operative at the rapporteur level for the class of justices who hold concurrent academic and institutional identities, which describes most STF justices. Whether these individual-level costs aggregate into the institutional-strategy change Paper 1E predicts requires a reformulated model of the type the adversarial paper's §6(5) surrender condition specifies. The adversarial paper is right that this model has not been provided. The defense's contribution is to contest the move from "rapporteur structure" to "fully diffuse reputational costs" — the costs are not fully diffuse in a small, individually-attributed professional community.

The adversarial round 2 presses an attribution timing problem: academic processual scholarship operates on publication timescales substantially postdating the engagement decisions it assesses. For anticipated reputational costs to function at the plenário virtual decision node, a rapporteur would need to prospectively anticipate scholarly attention, adverse attribution, and career costs — attenuated by multi-year publication lag, selective scholarly attention to individual cases, and temporal discounting. This timing critique is accurate as applied to academic monographs and journal articles as the primary attribution mechanism.

The timing problem does not extend to the professional legal press described in §3.2. A Brazilian STF rapporteur who dismisses a formally structured reclamação with brief reasoning may see contemporaneous coverage in professional publications whose analysis appears the same day or within days of the decision. Attribution through these channels does not require prospective anticipation of multi-year academic publication cycles; it operates within the same professional environment and at a timescale commensurate with the plenário virtual decision cycle. The adversarial §3.4 extension identifies a publication-lag problem specifically for academic attribution; it does not address attribution through professional-press channels at the decision-relevant timescale. Whether professional-press attribution generates sufficient incentive effects to change individual rapporteur engagement decisions is the empirical question the updated failure conditions below address — but the temporal mismatch the adversarial paper identifies is a problem for academic attribution specifically, not for professional-press attribution.

## 4. Anticipated Objection

The §3.1 argument (threshold adjustment carries procedural costs) faces a specific objection: the STF's embargos de declaração practice regarding reclamação dismissals. If the STF routinely disposes of embargos de declaração challenging the adequacy of reclamação dismissals with similarly brief reasoning — citing "no omissão, contradição, or obscuridade" without engagement — then the procedural cost of threshold adjustment is not genuinely constraining. The embargos route would add a processing step without changing the ultimate engagement calculus.

This objection is not resolved by the current defense. If Brazilian procedural practice shows that the STF can maintain brief reclamação dismissals through equally brief embargos responses without meaningful judicial accountability, then the procedural constraint identified in §3.1 does not bind. The constraint operates only if embargos de declaração for omissão in reclamação dismissals have a non-trivial chance of generating substantive review. The adversarial paper's §6(2) surrender condition — identifying "a mechanism constraining threshold adjustment" — remains partially unmet by the §3.1 argument: the procedural mechanism is identified, but its force depends on embargos practice that requires empirical verification.

The adversarial counter-reply's empirical grounding for the self-policing claim draws on the documented scholarly record of brief main-proceeding engagements. As discussed in §3.1, this evidence establishes the main-proceeding pattern and permits the inference that adequacy-specific embargos responses follow the same pattern — but this inference has not been independently documented for the adequacy-challenge omissão subset. The force of the embargos-practice objection is contingent on this empirical distinction: if embargos specifically targeting reclamação dismissal completeness for omissão are not documented as following the main-proceeding pattern, the procedural constraint may bind at a higher level than the adversarial paper's self-policing argument assumes.

## 5. Scope of This Defense

This defense strengthens:

- The endogenous threshold claim, by showing that threshold adjustment carries procedural costs scaling with argument quality, qualifying the adversarial paper's characterization of option (b) as cost-free.
- The reputational mechanism's applicability to individual quality arguments, by distinguishing monitoring of the systemic practice from monitoring by argument producers.
- The rapporteur-structure inference, by contesting the move from rapporteur-level decisions to fully diffuse reputational costs.

This defense does not establish:

- That the inflection point described in §4.3 will be reached: the defense shows threshold adjustment is constrained, not that it is blocked. A constrained adjustment mechanism and a volume increase that outpaces the adjustment rate can still reach the inflection point; but so can a constrained mechanism absorbing the volume if the constraint is loose enough.
- An empirical record of engagement fraction stability (adversarial §6(1)).
- A formal constraint on threshold adjustment (adversarial §6(2) is only partially met).
- An institutional actor with oversight authority over reasoning quality (adversarial §6(3)).

The access-to-justice observation (Paper 1E §5.3) and the cost-reduction trend are accepted by the adversarial paper and require no defense here.

## 6. Conditions Under Which This Defense Would Fail

- If embargos de declaração challenging reclamação dismissal adequacy are routinely disposed with brief conclusory responses by the STF without generating meaningful review, the procedural constraint in §3.1 is not binding and the adversarial paper's endogenous threshold problem operates as described.
- If professional legal publications (JOTA, Consultor Jurídico, and analogous outlets) cover STF reclamação practice only at the systemic-criticism level — pattern-level critiques of non-engagement practice generally, analogous to the sustained academic criticism the adversarial paper cites as failing to produce behavioral change — rather than aggregating individual-case engagement quality observations near-real-time, the near-real-time aggregation argument in §3.2 does not distinguish the legal-press channel from the diffuse academic channel the adversarial paper correctly characterizes as inadequate. Similarly, if professional-press coverage does not reach STF rapporteurs in a form that creates engagement-decision incentives at the plenário virtual timescale, the §3.4 attribution-timing argument fails.
- If the STF's responses to embargos de declaração specifically challenging reclamação dismissal adequacy for omissão are independently documented as systematically conclusory — rather than inferred from the main-proceeding brief-engagement pattern — the §3.1 (a)/(b) distinction collapses and the self-policing problem operates as the adversarial paper describes.
- If the Brazilian processual-law academic community's individual-level attribution of rapporteur decisions is not sufficient to create incentives that change individual rapporteur engagement decisions — for example, because the community's assessment does not translate into costs that outweigh the engagement cost of additional work — then the §3.4 argument does not prevent the rapporteur-structure problem from being fatal to the institutional-strategy-change prediction.
- If the adversarial paper's §3.3 argument is correct about the majority class: if formally structured but analytically incorrect arguments constitute the majority of the additional volume generated by cost reduction — rather than structurally elaborated arguments from analytical-capacity-constrained practitioners — then the noise problem dominates and the signal-volume increase is insufficient to reach the inflection point.
