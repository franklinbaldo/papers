# Document Structure and Candidate Ratios: A Defense of Paper 1C's Claim Provenance Module

---

## 1. Thesis Supported

Baldo (2025), "Categorias Processuais Civis para Formalização Computacional" (`paper1C_categorias_processuais_formalizacao.md`), makes two structural commitments in its claim provenance module (§5) that this paper defends.

**Commitment 1 (preprocessing tractability):** The necessary/contingent determination, applied to claims in Brazilian procedural documents before axiomatization, constitutes a tractable preprocessing step. The (provenance, status) annotation scheme enables formalizers to "correctly implement the categories in formal languages without distorting the underlying legal system" (Abstract).

**Commitment 2 (ratio extraction rule):** For Brazilian colegiado decisions, the ratio is "the minimum common denominator of the grounds of the majority votes — what all majority votes share as necessary foundation for the result. The grounds that each justice added individually but that were not shared by the others are, in relation to the acórdão as precedent, obiter." (§5.3)

---

## 2. What This Support Adds

**Vector:** Direct defense. This paper responds to `otherwise/paper1c-formalization-tractability.md` ("Vocabulary Without Procedure"), which attacks both commitments.

*Commitment 1 attack (preprocessing circularity):* The necessary/contingent determination requires knowing whether the dispositivo could be reached on other available grounds — which is a product of the legal analysis the formalization system is trying to perform. The annotation cannot precede the analysis; it is the analysis. The "pendente" category acknowledges failure to determine necessity rather than constituting a tractable preprocessing step. The adversarial paper further argues that easy-case tractability for explicitly-marked documents does not demonstrate preprocessing value: practitioners who can identify "ainda que" constructions already handle those cases without formal annotation infrastructure, and the scheme was supposed to add value for hard cases, which it concedes to "pendente." The adversarial paper also argues that the three operational contributions (quality-assurance records, error-detection protocols, coordination infrastructure) are properties of structured annotation generally, not of the (provenance × status) taxonomy's specific categories.

Round 3 of the adversarial paper extends these arguments with three new responses: (1) the *QA-accuracy asymmetry* — the recording function's QA value is conditional on annotation accuracy; for the hard class (competing-unmarked-threads), annotation records a potentially incorrect merits judgment, providing form-assurance but not substance-assurance, precisely where substance-assurance is most needed; (2) the *dispositif-grounding scheme* — a concrete alternative annotation enabling active-incorporation detection without the counterfactual necessity analysis, offered as an existence proof against the uniqueness claim; (3) the *institutional-incentives counter* — three structural features of STF constitutional adjudication (audience heterogeneity, precedent-building strategy, formal-consistency requirements) generate competing-unmarked-threads patterns within individual votes independent of single-authorship, contesting the lower-incidence claim for the vote-level circularity.

*Commitment 2 attack (fragmented-majority failure):* The minimum-denominator rule produces empty or trivially thin ratios for STF plenary decisions where majority justices reach the same result through independent foundations. The adversarial paper cites Mitidiero's convergent/parallel-reasoning distinction and Macêdo's reconstruction methods as the established doctrinal framework the paper does not engage. On the candidate-ratio extension proposed in response, the adversarial paper argues the extension requires a vote-level necessity determination that faces the same competing-unmarked-threads problem as the document-level determination, and that ementa cross-referencing is a consistency check rather than a necessity-identification procedure within individual votes.

Round 4 of the adversarial paper provides the reasoning round 3 lacked for the G-but-contingent dismissal and concedes the Art. 93, IX CF direction error. The round 4 Sub-case A/B analysis divides G-but-contingent cases by structural tractability: Sub-case A (structural contingency signal applied to an in-chain thread, such as "ainda que" or equivalent) is addressed by both the taxonomy and, with an "ainda que"-class modification to the dispositif-grounding scheme, by the modified scheme equivalently. Sub-case B (both threads in declarative mode, no structural contingency signal) produces only indeterminacy flags in both schemes — the taxonomy's (declarada, pendente) and the modified scheme's (G)/(G) — without positive propagation-error detection. SC7 names the residual class whose non-trivial existence would establish the taxonomy's unique detection advantage over the modified scheme: G-but-contingent cases where (i) both threads are in the explicit dispositif chain, (ii) the necessity determination is tractable for the taxonomy, and (iii) no "ainda que"-class marker is available that would equally permit the modified dispositif-grounding annotation.

Round 5 of the adversarial paper responds to SC7 in §3.7 with two arguments: (1) the doubly-modified dispositif-grounding scheme — extending the ementa-absence modification to mark as (B) any individual justice's vote thread absent from the ementa regardless of chain position, implementing the failure condition this defense's §6 named — achieves SC7 detection equivalence for the class where ementa cross-referencing is structural; and (2) an ementa-specificity probe contesting the structural tractability condition for contested STF plenary constitutional adjudication, where ementas generalize to principle-level language while individual vote threads reason at doctrinal specificity, making ementa cross-referencing dependent on legal judgment about abstraction coverage rather than surface textual comparison.

This defense accepts: that the preprocessing circularity holds for fully automated formalizers; that the minimum-denominator rule is incomplete for parallel-reasoning decisions; that Mitidiero's convergent/parallel distinction is the appropriate framework for extending it; that the modified dispositif-grounding scheme achieves Sub-case A equivalence for G-but-contingent claims with explicit contingency markers on in-chain threads; that Sub-case B produces parity (both schemes provide only indeterminacy flags); and that the Art. 93, IX CF formal-consistency requirement operates as a counterincentive to parallel declarative-mode reasoning, with the direction accepted and the competing-pressures question deferred to SC6. This defense contests: that the preprocessing circularity applies with equal force to human formalizers relying on structural document signals; that easy-case tractability fails to demonstrate preprocessing value because the taxonomy's contribution is formal recording, not enabling initial identification; that the operational contributions are interchangeable with any structured annotation scheme; that the doubly-modified scheme's (B) annotation for ementa-absent threads is epistemically equivalent to the taxonomy's (pendente) annotation for threads whose contingency cannot be established from structural reading in the contested constitutional class where ementa abstraction prevents determination; that the ementa-specificity probe eliminates the taxonomy's SC7 detection advantage across the full binding precedent corpus including STJ precedential decisions and concrete STF decisions where the ementa-specificity condition does not apply; that the vote-level circularity has the same incidence and severity as the document-level circularity; and that the institutional-incentives argument establishes the lower-incidence claim's defeat for the broader corpus; and that the ementa-specificity probe's internal premise — that thread-absence determination for SC7 requires conceptual-coverage analysis (does the ementa's stated principle extend to cover this thread?) rather than textual-designative analysis (is this specific thread stated in the ementa text as a fundamento determinante?) — applies to the standard SC7 case; the ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function under CPC art. 489, §1º, V and art. 927 makes the textual-designative determination tractable through ementa reading for the standard SC7 case without the legal judgment the probe identifies; and that the adversarial's ESHTR C2 concession of the ementa-as-ratio framing is consistent with the probe's position that ementa text reading for absence determination requires conceptual legal judgment in the contested constitutional class. The SC7 residual class is identified: individual justice votes in STF colegiado decisions where ementa cross-referencing — a structural cross-document operation — reveals a dispositif-grounded thread as G-but-contingent without any in-document "ainda que"-class marker.

## 3. The Defense

### 3.1 The Preprocessing Circularity Holds for Automated Systems; It Is Limited for Human Formalizers Using Structural Signals; and Tractability in the Easy Class Is Not Vacuous

The adversarial paper's hardest example for the preprocessing circularity is the competing-argumentative-threads case: a reclamação acórdão containing two independent argumentative threads each sufficient for the result, where the formalization system's task is identifying which is the ratio. Document structure alone cannot resolve this case without knowing which thread the STF treated as load-bearing — which is a legal-analytical judgment.

The adversarial paper's argument is strongest as an argument against automated systems. For human formalizers, the circularity is substantially narrower. Brazilian procedural writing employs a small set of systematic linguistic markers for contingency that a trained practitioner applies through structural reading before engaging the merits.

In **sentenças and acórdãos**, a distinct class of locutions signals alternative foundations: "Ainda que assim não fosse...", "Mesmo que se admitisse...", "Para além desse fundamento...", "Ad argumentandum tantum...", "Ainda que superado o argumento anterior...". These constructions are the near-universal signal for fundamento alternativo in Brazilian judicial writing. A trained practitioner identifies the "ainda que" construction as a contingency signal through structural reading before determining whether the alternative foundation is legally correct.

In **petições iniciais**, subsidiary causes of pedir are routinely prefaced with hedging locutions: "Subsidiariamente, caso se entenda que...", "Alternativamente, e sem prejuízo do acima exposto...". The distinction between the primary causa de pedir and its alternatives is structural and does not require merits analysis to identify.

In **acórdãos with concurring votes**, the standard Brazilian colegiado format separates votes by justice. A vote beginning "Acompanho o relator, acrescentando que..." signals additional-but-not-shared reasoning at the structural level. The acórdão's ementa consolidates the majority reasoning; cross-referencing ementa with full votes is a structural operation.

These structural signals are not exhaustive. The "pendente" category exists precisely for cases where signals are absent or contradictory — including the adversarial paper's competing-threads hard case. But the adversarial paper treats the hard case as representative of the annotation scheme's operational scope. The representative case for Brazilian procedural documents is not the competing-threads reclamação acórdão; it is the sentença with a dispositive structure and standard alternative-foundation locutions, the petição inicial with explicit subsidiary causes, and the acórdão with individually authored votes whose relationship to the ementa is structurally explicit.

For human formalizers, the preprocessing claim survives for the tractable majority of claims: structural signals permit necessity/contingency annotation before full merits engagement. The "pendente" category correctly marks the non-tractable class without claiming false tractability for it.

The adversarial round 2 argument presses that easy-case tractability does not demonstrate preprocessing value: practitioners who can identify "ainda que" constructions can already handle those cases without formal annotation infrastructure, so the scheme adds nothing for the easy class while failing for the hard class where it would have been useful.

This argument conflates two distinct functions: *enabling identification* and *enabling systematic recording*. The structural-signal tractability claim is not that the taxonomy enables practitioners to recognize what they could not otherwise recognize. It is that the taxonomy provides a formal framework for converting recognized determinations into standardized, cross-document-checkable records. A practitioner who reads "ainda que" and recognizes the construction as marking a contingent foundation does not thereby produce an annotated artifact. The annotation scheme converts an implicit recognition into an explicit, standardized record that downstream processes can operate on.

Without standardized annotation, even for the easy class: (a) different formalizers working across different documents in the same chain may record the same structural determination inconsistently — one annotating the "ainda que" thread as (inferida, contingente), another as (declarada, contingente) — creating coordination failures invisible to downstream processes; (b) propagation-error checks across document chains cannot run without a formal record of the original claim's contingent status to compare against the subsequent document's treatment; (c) quality-assurance audits cannot verify which claims in source documents were treated as necessary and which as contingent without a shared annotation artifact.

The preprocessing contribution for the easy class is not that the scheme enables novel identification. It is that the scheme produces formal records on which the quality-assurance, error-detection, and coordination functions depend. The annotation step converts an implicit competence into an explicit, verifiable artifact — and this conversion is tractable for the easy class before full merits engagement. The adversarial argument would succeed if the taxonomy's claimed value were entirely in enabling identification that practitioners could not otherwise perform. The taxonomy's value for the easy class lies in the formal record, not in enabling the initial recognition.

The scope qualification: this preprocessing tractability is for human formalizers using structural reading; for automated systems, the circularity holds and the taxonomy's contribution is vocabulary for post-analysis recording.

The adversarial round 3 presses the recording-not-identification response further with a QA-accuracy asymmetry: recording value is conditional on annotation accuracy. For the easy class — where structural markers guarantee reliable identification — QA function has genuine bite: practitioners converge on the same annotation for the same signal; propagation-error checks catch subsequent errors; coordination propagates correct records. For the hard class — where annotation records a merits judgment — the taxonomy records the judgment without validating it. A subsequent document that annotates the same claim consistently passes the propagation-error check, even if both annotations are incorrect. QA provides form-assurance — the annotation is formally correct — without substance-assurance — the underlying determination may be wrong.

The asymmetry is real and correctly identifies the structural limit of annotation schemes. The limit is not specific to the necessary/contingent taxonomy: any annotation scheme that records expert judgment for hard cases faces the same asymmetry. The dispositif-grounding scheme offered in round 3 as an alternative faces it equally. For competing-unmarked-threads cases where both threads appear as steps in the explicit dispositif chain — both annotated (G) — the dispositif-grounding annotator cannot determine which thread was load-bearing either. The QA record for a (G)/(G) annotation also provides form-assurance without substance-assurance: it records that both threads appeared in the explicit chain without validating which was counterfactually necessary. The QA-accuracy asymmetry is a feature of the hard case, not a distinguishing defect of the necessary/contingent taxonomy relative to alternatives.

The operationally relevant comparison is between annotation with explicit epistemic flags and no annotation. "Pendente" explicitly marks claims whose status is indeterminate, enabling downstream processes to treat them with appropriate caution rather than as established. The choice for hard-class claims is not form-assurance versus substance-assurance; it is between explicit epistemic flagging (with "pendente" for genuinely indeterminate cases) and implicit, invisible epistemic states. A system with no annotation treats indeterminate claims the same way it treats determinate ones — with no signal that the underlying determination was contested. The taxonomy's contribution for the hard class is making indeterminacy explicit and locating it precisely, which is a genuine operational function distinct from validating the underlying judgment.

### 3.2 The Vocabulary Recharacterization Is Available but Understates the Taxonomy's Operational Contribution; the Operational Contributions Are Specific to the Taxonomy's Categories; and the Dispositif-Grounding Alternative Scheme Fails for the G-but-Contingent Subclass

The adversarial paper offers SC2 as a clean resolution: if the taxonomy is recharacterized as vocabulary for recording the outputs of legal analysis rather than a preprocessing step, the tractability attack is misdirected. The adversarial paper concedes this recharacterization is "available and not dishonest."

The recharacterization correctly captures the taxonomy's epistemic function: it provides categories for what analysis has determined. But the adversarial paper's framing of "vocabulary" understates three contributions.

**Quality-assurance record.** A formalization using (provenance × status) notation produces an artifact that makes the epistemic status of every axiom explicit and auditable. A formalization without this notation has the same underlying epistemic structure but makes it invisible. Vocabulary that makes structure visible enables peer review, iterative correction, and expert contestation of specific claims — consequences that do not follow from vocabulary in the usual sense of terminology for naming.

**Error-detection protocol.** The propagation-by-active-incorporation mechanism (§5.5) is detectable by checking whether a later document treats as "necessária" a claim that was "contingente" in an earlier document. This check requires the annotation scheme to have been applied to both documents; it does not require a fresh merits analysis to detect the propagation error once the annotation is in place. The scheme enables a systematic retrospective check across document chains. This is an operational error-detection protocol, not a vocabulary for naming what analysis has already found.

**Coordination protocol across formalizers.** The (provenance, status) notation enables multiple formalizers to contribute to the same formalization at different stages, with explicit records of which claims were established by which formalizer at which epistemic level. Without it, formalization across teams produces implicit and unresolvable disagreements about epistemic status that are not surfaced in the artifact. The notation is infrastructure for collaborative quality assurance, not a private labeling system.

The adversarial round 2 argument presses that these three contributions are properties of structured annotation generally, not of the (provenance × status) taxonomy's specific categories. Any standardized annotation scheme — one marking claims as contested or uncontested, as backed by cited authority or not — would equally produce auditable records, enable cross-document checks, and support multi-formalizer coordination.

At sufficient abstraction, any annotation scheme provides some structure. The adversarial argument proves too much if taken to the conclusion that annotation schemes are interchangeable at the operational level. The question is whether the scheme's specific categories track the distinctions that are legally and computationally significant for the target use case.

The (provenance × status) taxonomy's categories — necessary/contingent × declared/inferred/pending — track the distinction that determines formalization accuracy: whether a claim was load-bearing for the legal effect of the originating document. This determines whether a claim can be safely elevated to axiomatic status in a downstream formalization. Alternative annotation schemes tracking different distinctions would not enable the taxonomy's specific error-detection use case.

A scheme marking claims as "cited by the court/not cited" would fail to detect propagation-by-active-incorporation when a subsequent decision treats as established a claim that was present but contingent in the prior decision, even if both decisions cited the claim. Citation status does not determine load-bearing status. A scheme marking claims as "contested/uncontested" would fail similarly: a claim can be uncontested in context and contingent in the originating decision. Detecting the specific propagation error — a contingent claim elevated to necessary axiomatic status — requires specifically tracking the necessary/contingent distinction, not a proxy for it.

The adversarial argument correctly observes that the three operational contributions follow from structured annotation generally. It does not establish that alternative annotation schemes would provide these contributions for the specific propagation error the taxonomy targets. An annotation scheme tracking proxy distinctions achieves QA, error-detection, and coordination for different error types, not for this one. Whether the taxonomy's categories are *sufficient* for propagation-error detection (they are) and whether any structured scheme would be equally sufficient (they would not, for this error type) are separate questions.

The adversarial round 3 introduces the dispositif-grounding scheme as a concrete existence proof against this uniqueness claim. The scheme annotates each claim as (G) dispositivo-grounded (appears in the court's explicit logical chain to the dispositivo) or (B) background (present in the opinion but not in the explicit chain). For claims marked (B), the scheme enables active-incorporation detection: if C is (B) in D1 and D2 invokes C as a D1 holding, the annotation check identifies the error. This detection capability is genuine for the subclass where one argumentative thread is explicitly in the dispositif chain and the other is demonstrably background — the explicit alternative-foundation case.

The adversarial paper concedes that the schemes diverge: "A claim can be dispositivo-grounded (G) yet contingent: when the court presents two alternative foundations in the declarative mode, each explicitly invoked as a step in the dispositif chain, both are (G) even though at most one is counterfactually necessary." The adversarial then asserts this divergence "does not undermine the dispositif-grounding scheme's adequacy for active-incorporation detection."

The assertion fails for G-but-contingent claims. For this subclass — claims present in the court's explicit dispositif chain but not counterfactually necessary — the dispositif-grounding scheme marks both competing threads as (G). When a subsequent document D2 treats thread C as a D1 holding, the scheme's check finds C annotated (G) in D1 and identifies no propagation error. The annotation correctly records that C appeared in the explicit chain — but does not detect that C was contingent. The actual propagation error — an unnecessary ground elevated to axiomatic status — goes undetected by the (G)/(B) check.

The necessary/contingent taxonomy handles this subclass. A formalizer who determines that C was contingent despite appearing in the explicit chain annotates C as (declarada, contingente). When D2 elevates C to holding status, the annotation check detects the error: C was (contingente) in D1, yet D2 treats it as established. For genuinely indeterminate cases — where the formalizer cannot resolve which thread was load-bearing — the taxonomy marks the relevant claim (pendente), flagging it as epistemically uncertain rather than silently assigning it to an established-in-chain category.

The G-but-contingent case is not an edge case. It is the competing-unmarked-threads hard case that the adversarial paper has pressed throughout this debate: two argumentative threads in declarative mode, each invoked as a step in the dispositif reasoning, with no explicit subordination signal. The adversarial's own central hard case generates exactly the G-but-contingent pattern. The dispositif-grounding annotation check fails for precisely this pattern: the adversarial's scheme succeeds where structural-signal reading succeeds (when one thread is clearly in the chain and the other is clearly background), and fails where structural-signal reading fails (when both threads appear as chain steps).

The adversarial implication holds for the easy class: for claims where one thread is (B) in D1, the dispositif-grounding scheme enables equivalent active-incorporation detection without the counterfactual necessity analysis.

Round 4 provides the reasoning round 3 lacked for the G-but-contingent dismissal. The Sub-case A/B tractability analysis divides the G-but-contingent class by the availability of structural contingency signals.

**Sub-case A — tractable G-but-contingent with "ainda que"-class markers.** The necessity determination can be completed because a structural contingency signal ("ainda que," "para além do exposto," "alternativamente," or equivalent) is applied to a thread that nevertheless appears as an explicit step in the stated dispositif chain. In Sub-case A, the taxonomy annotation proceeds from the structural marker. Critically, the same signal that enables the taxonomy's Sub-case A annotation also permits a modification to the dispositif-grounding scheme: treating "ainda que"-class markers as (B) regardless of chain position provides Sub-case A detection capability equivalent to the taxonomy's. The adversarial accepts the unmodified scheme's Sub-case A failure, as the round 3 response demonstrated. With the "ainda que"-class modification, the two schemes converge on Sub-case A. This acceptance is incorporated.

**Sub-case B — non-tractable G-but-contingent.** Both threads appear in the explicit chain in declarative mode without any structural contingency signal. Neither scheme provides positive propagation-error detection: the taxonomy marks the claim (declarada, pendente); the modified scheme marks both threads (G). Both produce only indeterminacy flags. Sub-case B parity is accepted.

**SC7 — the residual class.** SC7 asks whether a non-trivial class of G-but-contingent cases exists where (i) both threads are in the explicit chain, (ii) the necessity determination is tractable for the taxonomy, and (iii) no "ainda que"-class marker is available to equally permit the modified dispositif-grounding annotation. Such a class exists within STF colegiado decisions, through the ementa cross-referencing mechanism.

In an STF plenary decision, each justice writes an individual vote. The acórdão's ementa consolidates the majority's shared necessary grounds. When a justice's individual vote contains a thread that (a) appears in the vote's explicit dispositif chain, (b) lacks any "ainda que"-class structural marker, but (c) is not reflected in the ementa's summary of the majority's grounds — the ementa cross-referencing operation establishes that thread as G-but-contingent. The thread appeared in the individual justice's reasoning chain; the majority did not adopt it as a shared ground; it is therefore contingent within the individual vote as a precedent source even though structurally it is (G) under any single-document classification.

The taxonomy annotates this thread as (declarada, contingente) through ementa cross-referencing — a structural operation (comparing individual vote text against ementa text) that does not require performing the underlying constitutional analysis. The modified dispositif-grounding scheme cannot: it classifies threads by their position in the individual vote's explicit chain and by "ainda que"-class markers within that same document. Ementa non-inclusion is a cross-document signal, not an in-document marker. The modified scheme marks the thread (G) — correctly by its own criteria — but fails to detect the contingency established by the ementa cross-reference. When a subsequent decision D2 invokes this thread as a holding of the prior decision, the taxonomy check detects the propagation error (C was annotated (declarada, contingente) via ementa cross-referencing; D2 treats it as established). The modified scheme check finds C annotated (G) and reports no propagation error.

This class is non-trivially populated. In STF parallel-reasoning decisions — precisely the class where Mitidiero's convergent/parallel distinction is operative — individual justices frequently include argumentative threads in their votes that the majority does not adopt as shared grounds. The candidate-ratio extension's clustering step depends on exactly this observation: comparing individual votes against the ementa to identify which grounds are shared (candidate ratio material) versus individual additions (not ratio). The ementa cross-referencing used for the candidate-ratio clustering step provides the same information the taxonomy uses for SC7 annotation. The structural operation is the same; the applications are complementary. This connection establishes that SC7 ementa-based detection is tractable to the same degree as the candidate-ratio clustering step — and the adversarial's own §4.3 concession that inferior courts informally reconstruct from individual grounds confirms that vote-level ground identification is practically available through structural reading.

The institutional-incentives argument (audience heterogeneity, precedent-building strategy) is connected to SC7 as well: if these pressures do produce argumentative threads in individual votes that the majority does not adopt as shared grounds — the institutional-incentives prediction for contested STF constitutional cases — those threads will appear in individual votes but not in the ementa, populating the SC7 class. Whether these pressures dominate the formal-consistency counterincentive is SC6's empirical question; SC7's population is correlated with the answer to SC6. Whether or not SC6's empirical question is resolved, the ementa cross-referencing detection mechanism is structurally available wherever individual votes contain grounds not reflected in the ementa, and the candidate-ratio extension's own operation confirms that this is a regular occurrence in the STF's parallel-reasoning decisions.

The taxonomy's detection advantage over the unmodified dispositif-grounding scheme is bounded to the SC7 class: individual STF justice votes where ementa cross-referencing establishes a dispositif-grounded thread as G-but-contingent without any in-document "ainda que"-class marker. For Sub-case A (modified scheme achieves parity), Sub-case B (both schemes at parity producing indeterminacy flags), and the easy class (explicit background threads), the modified scheme achieves equivalent detection capability. The taxonomy's specific advantage is cross-document structural reasoning, which the modified scheme's "ainda que"-class modification alone does not capture.

Round 5 of the adversarial paper engages SC7 with two responses (§3.7). The doubly-modified dispositif-grounding scheme extends the ementa-absence modification: threads in an individual justice's vote chain that are absent from the ementa are marked (B) regardless of chain position. For the class where ementa cross-referencing is structural — where the ementa and individual vote operate at comparable specificity levels — the doubly-modified scheme detects D2's SC7 propagation error through the same cross-document comparison as the taxonomy. The schemes converge on detection capability for this class; this convergence is accepted.

The ementa-specificity probe contests the structural tractability condition for contested STF plenary constitutional adjudication: ementas in that class generalize to principle-level constitutional language while individual vote threads reason at doctrinal specificity. Bridging the abstraction gap — for example, determining whether a specific doctrinal argument about the prestational dimension of art. 5°, caput, CF falls within or outside an ementa characterizing the majority's position as "observância do princípio da isonomia" — requires legal judgment about what the principle covers at that specificity level, not surface textual comparison. The probe applies to both schemes: the doubly-modified scheme's ementa-absence (B) marking depends on the same judgment when the ementa abstracts above thread specificity. Both schemes face this tractability ceiling for the contested constitutional class; the probe is accepted as applying to that class.

Two qualifications constrain the probe's scope. First, corpus distribution. Brazilian binding precedent encompasses STJ repetitive resource decisions and binding legal theses under arts. 927, II and IV, where ementas address specific statutory provisions and operative doctrinal constructions rather than abstract constitutional principles. In this significant portion of the corpus by volume, the ementa-specificity challenge operates with substantially less force. The SC7 mechanism retains structural tractability for STJ precedential decisions and for STF concentrated control decisions where ementas are tied to specific constitutional provisions; the doubly-modified scheme achieves detection equivalence for these classes as well.

Second, epistemic divergence in the ambiguous class. For contested constitutional adjudication where both schemes face the ementa-specificity ceiling, the handling of indeterminacy differs. The doubly-modified scheme marks ementa-absent threads (B) by the ementa-absence criterion, treating textual absence from the ementa as sufficient grounds for background classification even when the ementa's principle-level formulation may cover the specific thread under a broader characterization. The taxonomy marks indeterminate threads (pendente): the contingency determination cannot be established from structural reading alone in this class. For downstream formalizers, the distinction carries operational implications: a thread marked (B) is classified as definitively background in subsequent processing; a thread marked (pendente) is flagged as requiring further determination before consequential use. When ementa abstraction makes the determination structurally unavailable, (pendente) accurately captures the epistemic state; (B) treats textual absence as sufficient grounds for background classification even when the ementa's generalization may cover the specific thread. The two annotations are not informationally equivalent: (B) asserts, (pendente) suspends judgment.

Third, the probe's internal premise. The two qualifications above address the probe's scope (corpus distribution) and the schemes' relative accuracy under the probe's conditions (epistemic divergence). A third qualification addresses the probe's premise: that determining thread absence from the ementa requires conceptual-coverage analysis.

The probe conflates two distinct determinations. (A) *Textual-designative absence*: is this specific thread stated in the ementa text as a fundamento determinante? (B) *Conceptual-coverage absence*: does the ementa's stated principle extend to cover this thread as an application? SC7 requires (A), not (B).

The ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function grounds this distinction. Under CPC art. 489, §1º, V and art. 927's precedential framework, the ementa authoritatively states the fundamentos determinantes for Brazilian precedential purposes. This is the same reconceptualization operative in the ESHTR C2 debate (`yesindeed/phase3-coherence-defense.md`, §2, C1 ementa reconceptualization): "the cited precedent's fundamentos determinantes are specified by its ementa — the authoritative ratio characterization for Brazilian precedential purposes under CPC art. 489, §1º, V and art. 927 — converting the collegial-fragmentation task from multi-voto-synthesis to ementa-reading." Under this function, a thread is a fundamento determinante if and only if the ementa states it as such. Textual absence from the ementa establishes that the thread is not among the decision's stated fundamentos determinantes.

For the standard SC7 case — an individual justice's vote containing a thread specifying a particular provision, doctrinal construction, or party-specific argument that the ementa does not mention — the textual-designative determination is available without conceptual-coverage analysis. The specific content is not in the ementa text; therefore the thread is not a stated fundamento determinante; SC7 applies. This determination does not require asking whether the ementa's principle-level formulation conceptually covers the thread. It requires asking whether the ementa text states the specific thread. When it does not, absence is textually established.

The probe's internal premise holds for (B). For the probe to apply to the standard SC7 determination, it must show that the relevant question is (B) — that establishing textual absence requires prior conceptual judgment even when the specific thread is not in the ementa text. The circumstance where (A) and (B) genuinely converge is when the ementa's stated formulation IS the thread at its level of specificity — in which case the thread is present in the ementa, not absent, and SC7 does not apply. In the SC7 case by definition the thread is textually absent from the ementa; that determination is (A), available through ementa text reading.

A cross-front tension follows. The adversarial's ementa-specificity probe implicitly contests the ementa-as-authoritative-ratio framing: if determining what a principle-level ementa covers requires legal judgment, then annotators reading the ementa to identify fundamentos determinantes — the ESHTR C2 C1 annotation task — face that same judgment. The ESHTR C2 debate accepted the ementa-as-ratio reconceptualization as a structural simplification, treating ementa reading as a tractable single-document task for ratio identification (the adversarial's C1 objection was narrowed to residual ementa-complexity questions, not the basic ementa-as-ratio framing). That acceptance implies textual-designative determination is tractable: reading the ementa identifies its stated ratio without independently determining what the stated principle covers in extension. If this is correct for ESHTR C2 C1 annotation, it is correct for SC7 absence determination through the same operation. The adversarial cannot consistently maintain: (ESHTR C2) ementa text reading is tractable for ratio identification; and (Paper 1C probe) ementa text reading for thread-absence determination requires conceptual legal judgment about coverage. If both hold, the ESHTR C2 C1 ementa reconceptualization does not resolve the collegial-fragmentation problem — since ementa reading would require the same legal judgment it was intended to avoid; if only the former holds, the probe's internal premise fails for the standard SC7 case.

The probe's effective scope narrows accordingly: it applies to the edge where even textual-designative absence requires prior conceptual judgment — specifically, where the ementa's formulation is so abstractly stated that determining whether specific thread content is mentioned requires interpreting what the stated principle covers. This is a real tractability problem at extreme levels of constitutional principle abstraction. It does not characterize the standard SC7 case, where specific provisions, parties, or doctrinal constructions are textually absent from the ementa without any need to interpret the scope of the ementa's stated principle.

### 3.3 The Candidate-Ratio Extension Addresses the Fragmented-Majority Failure Case; the Vote-Level Circularity Has Lower Incidence Than the Document-Level Circularity; and the Institutional-Incentives Argument Narrows but Does Not Defeat the Lower-Incidence Claim

The adversarial paper is correct that the minimum-denominator rule produces empty ratios for parallel-reasoning STF decisions. This is a genuine limitation. Mitidiero's convergent/parallel distinction, cited by the adversarial paper as the established framework the paper should engage, provides the appropriate basis for extension.

For **convergent-reasoning decisions** — where majority justices share a set of common necessary grounds for the result — the minimum-denominator rule applies as stated. The ratio is the intersection of the grounds all majority votes treated as necessary.

For **parallel-reasoning decisions** — where majority justices reach the same result through independent or incompatible foundations, and the intersection of their necessary grounds is empty or trivially thin — the minimum-denominator rule requires extension rather than abandonment.

The extension: where the intersection of majority votes' necessary grounds is empty, the formalizer constructs candidate ratios by identifying clusters of majority votes sharing common necessary grounds. Each cluster constitutes a candidate ratio, marked with:

1. **Support**: the subset of majority votes that include this ground as necessary.
2. **Epistemic status**: "candidate ratio — parallel-reasoning decision — applicable in case constellations where [ground description] is the operative reasoning framework."
3. **Scope**: fact patterns where this candidate ratio applies, derived from the constellation of features the votes in this cluster treat as legally significant.

The candidate-ratio framework produces a well-defined output — a set of marked candidate ratios — rather than the null output the minimum-denominator rule generates for parallel-reasoning decisions. It preserves the minimum-denominator rule for convergent decisions, where it produces a single unambiguous ratio. For inferior courts applying a fragmented-majority precedent, the candidate-ratio framework provides actionable guidance: apply the candidate ratio whose reasoning framework most closely matches the case before it.

The adversarial round 2 argument presses a further difficulty: the candidate-ratio extension requires a vote-level necessity determination — identifying which grounds each individual justice treated as necessary in their individual vote before the clustering step runs. The extension decomposes the document-level problem into vote-level subproblems; those subproblems inherit the competing-unmarked-threads structure from §3.1. Ementa cross-referencing checks consistency between an individual vote and the consolidated majority position; it does not resolve, within a vote consistent with the ementa, which of that vote's multiple argumentative threads were necessary for that justice's conclusion.

The structural claim is correct: vote-level necessity determination is required, and ementa cross-referencing does not substitute for it. The question is whether the vote-level competing-unmarked-threads problem has the same incidence and severity as the document-level problem.

The document-level competing-unmarked-threads problem arises partly from the multi-authored structure of colegiado decisions. The collective acórdão merges reasoning streams from multiple justices, each arriving at the same dispositivo through different argumentative paths, without explicit subordination signals across those paths. The problem is structurally generated by the act of merging multiple independent argumentative contributions into one document. Individual justice votes are single-authored. A justice arguing their position typically advances a coherent argumentative line; the parallel-tracks-without-subordination pattern that produces the hard case at the document level has lower incidence within a single justice's reasoning, because a justice is arguing from a single perspective rather than producing a resolution among multiple independently arrived-at conclusions.

This does not eliminate the vote-level problem. A justice's individual vote can contain multiple supporting arguments for the same conclusion, sometimes without explicit subordination signals. But the incidence is substantially lower than at the document level, and the structure of the relevant question differs. For the clustering step, the question at the vote level is not "which thread did the court as a whole treat as the ratio?" — a question requiring determination of the court's collective interpretive stance — but "is this ground necessary for this justice's stated conclusion?" The latter question has a narrower scope, answerable in part from the vote's internal argumentative structure: when a ground would eliminate the justice's conclusion if removed, it is necessary; when the justice's reasoning would survive its removal, it is contingent. For the case where two grounds are both present without explicit subordination, both may be genuinely necessary for the justice's conclusion — in which case both are included in the vote's necessary-ground set, and the clustering step correctly receives a larger ground set for that justice's vote.

When determination is genuinely unavailable — a justice's vote contains competing unmarked threads that the formalizer cannot resolve from the vote's internal structure — the "pendente" marker applies at the vote level, and the clustering step proceeds on the grounds that can be determined. The candidate-ratio output for that justice's vote is correspondingly uncertain, flagged as such.

The adversarial paper's own §4.3 concession — that "inferior courts observe which way the STF decided and apply that decision as a directional signal, supplemented by the individual grounds they find most persuasive" — confirms that vote-level ground identification is practically available through structural reading. Courts performing this reconstruction operate without performing a fresh constitutional analysis from first principles. The candidate-ratio extension formalizes what courts already do. The adversarial argument that the formalization requires an intractable judgment sits in tension with the adversarial paper's acknowledgment that courts routinely perform the underlying reconstruction.

The adversarial round 3 presses the lower-incidence claim by introducing three institutional features of STF constitutional adjudication that it argues generate competing-unmarked-threads patterns within individual votes independent of single-authorship: (1) *audience heterogeneity* — votes address multiple simultaneous audiences through multiple parallel argumentative lines without explicit subordination; (2) *precedent-building strategy* — multiple doctrinal foundations for the same result create multiple future-precedent anchors, with explicit subordination narrowing precedential reach; (3) *formal-consistency requirements* (Art. 93, IX CF) — identifying a primary line creates implicit hierarchy contestable as evidence of obiter, so parallel declarative-mode lines are maintained to preserve multiple footholds.

The three features are genuine structural features of STF constitutional adjudication. Three responses limit their force.

First, scope. The formalization system's target corpus is not restricted to contested STF constitutional adjudication. Brazilian binding precedents subject to formalization include STJ statutory-interpretation decisions under arts. 927, II and IV (repetitive resources and binding STJ legal theses), where individual justices resolve statutory-construction questions under defined record constraints. Audience heterogeneity and precedent-building-strategy incentives operate with substantially lower force in STJ repetitive-resource adjudication, where the institutional setting is more constrained and the audience is primarily defined by the specific legal question under review. The lower-incidence claim holds for this significant portion of the corpus.

Second, the formal-consistency requirement (Art. 93, IX CF). The adversarial round 4 accepts that Art. 93, IX CF's formal-consistency requirement operates as a counterincentive to parallel declarative-mode reasoning, not as a generator of it — withdrawing the round 3 characterization. The round 4 reframing positions the question as competing pressures: audience heterogeneity and precedent-building strategy generate tendencies toward parallel declarative-mode threads in individual votes; the formal-consistency counterincentive operates against them; whether the former dominate the latter in contested STF constitutional adjudication is SC6's empirical question. This acceptance and reframing are incorporated. The direction of the counterincentive (toward explicit subordination, not toward parallel declarative-mode reasoning) was correctly identified in the prior defense and is no longer contested.

The connection to SC7: if the competing pressures do generate argumentative threads in individual votes that the majority does not adopt as shared grounds — the institutional-incentives prediction for contested STF constitutional cases — those threads will appear in individual votes but not in the ementa. This is precisely the pattern the ementa cross-referencing mechanism detects as G-but-contingent (§3.2). SC7's population is thus correlated with the degree to which the institutional pressures operate: the adversarial's institutional-incentives argument and the SC7 residual class are structurally linked. Whether SC6's empirical question is ultimately resolved in the adversarial's favor or the supportive's, the ementa cross-referencing detection mechanism is structurally available wherever individual votes contain grounds not reflected in the ementa.

Third, structural incentives predict behavioral tendencies, not universal outcomes. The adversarial explicitly declines to produce examples of STF individual votes with competing-unmarked-threads patterns (no fabrication). SC6 names the empirical question: whether the competing pressures dominate the formal-consistency counterincentive in contested STF constitutional adjudication sufficiently to produce a competing-unmarked-threads rate that defeats the lower-incidence claim for this decision class. The structural argument establishes that incentives exist; it does not establish that they dominate competing factors (clarity, persuasiveness to colleagues, reasoning coherence) or produce the predicted pattern at a rate sufficient to undermine the claim across the formalization system's corpus.

If the institutional-incentives prediction is correct for contested STF constitutional cases, the appropriate formalization response is the candidate-ratio framework applied to those votes (individual votes with multiple parallel foundations produce multiple candidate grounds from which clustering proceeds) combined with ementa cross-referencing for SC7 detection. The institutional-incentives argument, if valid, describes a class of votes that benefit from both the candidate-ratio treatment and the ementa-based G-but-contingent annotation — it strengthens the case for the taxonomy's SC7 detection capability, not the case against it.

## 4. Anticipated Objection

The adversarial paper's §4.1 anticipates the human-formalizer response and argues it does not suffice. Two reasons: (a) the paper's scope includes automated formalizers ("human or automated"), and for them the circularity holds; (b) for human formalizers, "pendente" cannot serve as a resting state for load-bearing claims — a formalization with "pendente" on its key axioms has documented its own incompleteness rather than demonstrating tractability.

On (a): this defense accepts it. The preprocessing tractability claim is qualified to human formalizers using structural signals. For automated systems, the vocabulary recharacterization applies, and the taxonomy's value lies in the quality-assurance and error-detection contributions identified in §3.2 — specifically, those contributions depend on the taxonomy's categories tracking the necessary/contingent distinction, not on any structured annotation scheme generally.

On (b): "pendente" is correctly characterized as documenting incompleteness — but the objection misstates its role. "Pendente" does not claim to be a completed preprocessing step; it marks claims requiring further analysis before they can serve as high-confidence axioms. A formalization that marks load-bearing claims as "pendente" tells its users which parts of the model require resolution before the formalization is used for consequential purposes. This is a quality-assurance function: it makes the model's vulnerabilities explicit rather than hiding them behind apparently resolved annotations.

The adversarial paper's further objection — that the easy class's tractability does not demonstrate value because practitioners can handle those cases without formal infrastructure — is addressed in §3.1. The taxonomy's preprocessing contribution for the easy class is not enabling identification but enabling formal recording; the formal record is what the error-detection and coordination functions require, and it is not produced by practitioners' implicit recognition without annotation.

The adversarial round 3's QA-accuracy asymmetry does not require separate anticipatory response beyond what §3.1 establishes: the asymmetry is real but applies to all annotation schemes for hard cases, and the operationally relevant comparison is between explicit epistemic flagging and no annotation — not between form-assurance and substance-assurance. "Substance-assurance" at the annotation level requires performing the underlying legal analysis, which is what the taxonomy supports rather than replaces.

## 5. Scope of This Defense

This defense establishes:

- The preprocessing tractability claim survives for **human formalizers** using structural reading, for the majority of claims in standard Brazilian procedural documents, where linguistic markers permit necessity/contingency annotation before full merits engagement.
- The taxonomy's preprocessing value for the easy class lies in producing formal records — not in enabling initial identification that practitioners could not perform without it. Systematic recording is the preprocessing contribution.
- The (provenance, status) taxonomy contributes quality-assurance records, error-detection protocols, and coordination infrastructure beyond notation — and these contributions are specific to the taxonomy's necessary/contingent categories, not properties of structured annotation generally, because the propagation error the taxonomy detects requires specifically tracking load-bearing status.
- The dispositif-grounding scheme, modified to treat "ainda que"-class markers as (B) regardless of chain position, achieves detection capability equivalent to the taxonomy's for Sub-case A of the G-but-contingent class (tractable G-but-contingent with explicit structural contingency signals on in-chain threads). For Sub-case B (both threads in declarative mode, no structural contingency signal of any kind), both the taxonomy (marking (declarada, pendente)) and the modified dispositif-grounding scheme (marking (G)/(G)) generate only indeterminacy flags without positive propagation-error detection: Sub-case B parity is accepted. The taxonomy's unique detection advantage over the unmodified dispositif-grounding scheme is bounded to the SC7 residual class: individual STF justice votes where ementa cross-referencing establishes a dispositif-grounded thread as G-but-contingent without any in-document "ainda que"-class marker. The doubly-modified dispositif-grounding scheme (with ementa-absence modification, adversarial round 5) achieves SC7 detection equivalence for the tractable subclass — where ementa and individual vote operate at comparable specificity levels. The taxonomy's remaining SC7 position is: (a) corpus-scope advantage for STJ precedential decisions and concrete STF decisions where the tractability condition holds; (b) epistemic-accuracy advantage in the contested constitutional class where both schemes face the ementa-specificity ceiling — the taxonomy's (pendente) accurately captures indeterminacy while the doubly-modified scheme's (B) default treats textual ementa absence as sufficient grounds for background classification regardless of potential ementa abstraction above the thread's specificity level; and (c) a narrower effective scope of the ementa-specificity ceiling than the probe's initial application assumes — for the standard SC7 case where specific thread content is textually absent from the ementa, the absence determination is textual-designative and tractable under the ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function (CPC art. 489, §1º, V and art. 927); the probe's tractability challenge is limited to the edge case where the ementa's formulation is too abstractly stated to permit textual-designative determination without prior conceptual-coverage judgment.
- The QA-accuracy asymmetry is real and applies to all annotation schemes for hard cases; it does not distinguish the necessary/contingent taxonomy from the modified dispositif-grounding alternative. Both provide form-assurance but not substance-assurance for Sub-case B.
- The minimum-denominator rule is correctly formulated for convergent-reasoning decisions and extendable via a candidate-ratio framework for parallel-reasoning decisions, without abandoning the rule for its primary case.
- The vote-level necessity determination required by the candidate-ratio extension faces a structurally parallel but lower-incidence version of the competing-unmarked-threads problem: individual justice votes are single-authored and have lower parallel-tracks incidence; the vote-level question ("is this ground necessary for this justice's conclusion?") is narrower in scope than the document-level question.
- The formal-consistency incentive (Art. 93, IX CF) operates as a counterincentive to parallel declarative-mode reasoning; whether audience heterogeneity and precedent-building strategy dominate it in contested STF constitutional adjudication is SC6's empirical question. The direction of the counterincentive is no longer contested. The institutional-incentives argument narrows the lower-incidence claim for contested STF constitutional cases but does not defeat it for the broader corpus; if the competing pressures do generate vote-level threads not reflected in the ementa, those threads constitute the SC7 class (§3.2) and are addressed by ementa cross-referencing.

This defense accepts:

- The preprocessing circularity holds for automated systems.
- "Pendente" annotations on load-bearing claims indicate formalization incompleteness — this is an honest outcome for the hard class of claims, not a tractability claim.
- The minimum-denominator rule, unextended, fails for parallel-reasoning decisions; the adversarial paper's §3.3 citations to Mitidiero and Macêdo identify a real and significant gap.
- The candidate-ratio extension may produce complex outputs for highly fragmented decisions with many independent parallel votes.
- Vote-level necessity determination is required for the candidate-ratio clustering step, and ementa cross-referencing alone does not resolve necessity within each individual vote beyond identifying which grounds that vote shares with the majority.
- The QA-accuracy asymmetry correctly identifies that annotation accuracy is conditional on the quality of the underlying determination.
- The modified dispositif-grounding scheme (with "ainda que"-class modification) achieves Sub-case A equivalence and is a genuine structural advance over the unmodified scheme and over the citation-status and contested/uncontested-status schemes already identified as inadequate.
- Sub-case B parity: both the taxonomy and the modified dispositif-grounding scheme produce only indeterminacy flags for competing-unmarked-threads cases in declarative mode.
- The doubly-modified dispositif-grounding scheme (with ementa-absence modification) achieves SC7 detection equivalence for the tractable class where ementa cross-referencing is structural — where the ementa and individual vote operate at comparable specificity levels.
- The ementa-specificity probe correctly identifies a tractability ceiling for the subclass of contested STF plenary constitutional adjudication where the ementa's formulation is abstractly stated at a level that makes determining whether specific thread content is mentioned require interpreting what the stated principle covers; both schemes face this ceiling for that edge.
- The Art. 93, IX CF direction correction: the formal-consistency requirement operates as a counterincentive to parallel declarative-mode reasoning, not as a generator of it.

This defense does not address the paper's claim for automated systems beyond conceding the adversarial paper's preprocessing circularity for that class.

## 6. Conditions Under Which This Defense Would Fail

- If systematic study of Brazilian procedural documents shows that the competing-argumentative-threads pattern — multiple independent threads without standard contingency markers — occurs in a substantial proportion of contested cases across document types, not only in complex reclamação acórdãos, the structural-signal argument loses its force for the standard-case class. High rates of ambiguous structural marking across sentenças, petições, and routine appellate acórdãos would confirm the preprocessing circularity for human formalizers.
- If trained Brazilian proceduralists without prior merits analysis show high interrater disagreement on necessity/contingency classifications for representative documents, this would be direct evidence against the structural-signal argument's tractability claim.
- If the claim that easy-class formal recording constitutes a preprocessing contribution is contested on the ground that the formal record adds nothing beyond what informal annotations already capture in practice — i.e., that formalizers already produce consistent annotation records without the specific taxonomy's categories — the recording-not-identification argument would require empirical support regarding actual formalizer practice.
- If the candidate-ratio extension produces unstable or practically unwieldy outputs for the majority of STF plenary decisions — because most consequential decisions produce highly fragmented parallel reasoning without stable vote clusters — the extension provides theoretical coverage without practical utility.
- If Brazilian courts and doctrine treat parallel-reasoning decisions as producing no binding ratio (rather than multiple candidate ratios from which inferior courts select), the candidate-ratio framework formalizes a practice that does not exist rather than one that does.
- If the adversarial camp shows that the SC7 residual class is trivially small — specifically, that in STF colegiado decisions, individual justice votes rarely contain argumentative threads that appear in the vote's explicit dispositif chain, lack "ainda que"-class markers, and are also absent from the ementa — the ementa cross-referencing detection advantage does not extend to a practically significant class. The connection to the candidate-ratio extension's clustering step suggests this class is non-trivially populated; if systematic study shows otherwise, the SC7 response weakens.
- The ementa-specificity probe (adversarial round 5, §3.7) establishes a tractability ceiling for the subclass of contested STF plenary constitutional adjudication where the ementa's formulation is abstractly stated at a level that makes determining whether specific thread content is mentioned require interpreting what the stated principle covers; this edge condition is accepted. This defense's §3.2 (third qualification) contests the probe's application to the standard SC7 case, where specific thread content is textually absent from the ementa and the textual-designative determination is tractable under the ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function (CPC art. 489, §1º, V and art. 927). This argument would fail if: (a) the ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function is incorrectly attributed — ementas are not the authoritative statement of fundamentos determinantes for Brazilian precedential purposes, or the ementa's principle-level formulation is understood to include specific applications as fundamentos determinantes even when not textually stated; (b) typical contested constitutional ementas are so abstractly formulated that the textual-designative determination always requires prior conceptual judgment, eliminating the standard-case/extreme-abstraction-edge distinction; or (c) the adversarial successfully resolves the cross-front tension noted in §3.2 — reconciling the ementa-as-ratio ESHTR C2 concession with the Paper 1C ementa-abstraction argument — without conceding the tractability of textual-designative absence for the standard SC7 case. Separately: if systematic evidence shows that ementas in STJ repetitive resource decisions and concrete STF decisions also regularly generalize above the specificity of individual vote threads, the corpus-scope qualification (first qualification, §3.2) narrows further and the SC7 tractability advantage diminishes in the tractable-class as well.
- The doubly-modified dispositif-grounding scheme (adversarial round 5, §3.7) implements the ementa-absence modification, achieving SC7 detection equivalence for the tractable class where ementa cross-referencing is structural. The taxonomy's remaining advantage in the tractable class is not detection capability (the schemes converge) but annotation accuracy under ambiguity: in the contested constitutional class where both schemes face the ementa-specificity ceiling, the taxonomy marks indeterminate threads (pendente) while the doubly-modified scheme marks them (B). If downstream formalizers treat (pendente) and (B) as equivalent indeterminacy signals — taking both as grounds for further review rather than treating (B) as a settled background classification — the epistemic-accuracy difference is eliminated and the schemes become functionally equivalent across both the tractable and ambiguous classes. In that case, the doubly-modified scheme would be a full substitute for the taxonomy's SC7 mechanism, and the taxonomy's unique advantage over the doubly-modified scheme in active-incorporation detection would require identification of a class beyond Sub-case A, Sub-case B, SC7-tractable, and SC7-ambiguous where the taxonomy's necessity determination succeeds through a mechanism neither the modified nor the doubly-modified scheme can replicate.
- If the adversarial paper's SC5 (as updated through round 4 to Sub-case B scope) is satisfied: evidence that detecting active-incorporation propagation errors in Sub-case B requires positive confirmation of contingency (not merely an indeterminacy flag) and that the taxonomy provides this positive confirmation where the modified dispositif-grounding scheme does not. No such mechanism has been identified; both schemes produce only indeterminacy flags for Sub-case B.
- If systematic empirical study of individual STF votes in contested constitutional adjudication confirms that competing-unmarked-threads patterns occur with substantial frequency — directly establishing the institutional-incentives prediction rather than arguing it structurally — the lower-incidence claim would fail for the STF contested case. This defense's response (scope narrowing to the broader corpus; formal-consistency counterincentive) would need to be sufficient to maintain the claim for the non-STF-contested portions of the corpus.
- If the scope qualification in §3.1 (human formalizers only) is accepted as the defense's maximum concession, the Abstract's "human or automated" claim remains partially exposed. The defense does not rescue the automated-formalizer preprocessing claim.
