# Departing Without Authority: The Inference Gap in Paper 1B's Rational Supersession

---

## 1. Thesis Attacked

Paper 1B ("As Cinco Saídas Legítimas do Tribunal diante de Precedente Vinculante,"
`paper1B_cinco_saidas_precedentes.md`) advances three connected claims. The first
two — that the CPC 2015 demands substantive reasoning when invoking or departing from
a binding precedent, and that silent partial application of a ratio constitutes an
autonomous reasoning defect — are well-founded in the text of arts. 489, §1º, V and
VI. The third, which is this paper's target, is the thesis of Exit 4:

> "A superação racional é a saída mais exigente e, ao mesmo tempo, a menos explorada.
> Ela consiste na superação do precedente pelo próprio tribunal — **inclusive o tribunal
> inferior ao tribunal-fonte** — mediante apontamento expresso de erro na ratio do
> precedente." (§3.4, emphasis added)

The thesis: inferior courts are *authorized* to supersede STF binding precedents
through sufficiently qualified argument identifying error in the ratio. The paper
presents this as one of five *legitimate exits* — the most demanding but genuine —
available to any court facing an invoked binding precedent.

This attack is not directed at Exits 1, 2, 3, or 5, nor at the silent partial
application concept (§5). It targets the claim that Exit 4, as described, constitutes
a *legally authorized* response rather than a procedurally articulated departure
pending reversal.

---

## 2. Faithful Reconstruction

The strongest case for Exit 4 integrates three distinct supports.

**The normative support from arts. 926 and 489.** Art. 926 CPC requires all courts
to maintain coherence, integrity, and stability of jurisprudence. A court aware of a
demonstrable logical defect in an STF ratio but applying it regardless is being
incoherent: it is treating as binding a reason it has identified as failing. Art. 489,
§1º, VI requires that departures from precedents be accompanied by "demonstration of
the existence of distinction or supersession." The provision uses "superação" without
limiting it to supersession by the source court. Read expansively, the provision
contemplates court-generated supersession as a recognized departure mode — otherwise
the word is redundant with the recognition of supervening source-court supersession
already covered by Exit 3.

**The rational binding theory.** Under Marinoni's account, a precedent is "a reason
that cannot be disregarded without a stronger reason." The binding force derives from
the quality of the reasoning, not from the authority of the source. A ratio with a
demonstrable logical defect — vício lógico na estrutura do argumento, inconsistency
with supervening norms, or conflict with principles the precedent failed to consider —
is a weakened ratio. Its binding force is attenuated at the source. An inferior court
that identifies and articulates this attenuation is not creating new power to override;
it is revealing an attenuation that exists in the precedent's normative fabric. The
inferior court that exercises Exit 4 and exposes itself to reversal is participating
in the system's error-correction mechanism, not circumventing it.

**The systemic efficiency argument.** A system without Exit 4 cannot self-correct
except through the STF's own initiative. The STF operates as a filter court addressing
thematic issues of constitutional importance. Erroneous rationes that inferior courts
apply mechanically — because no inferior court can articulate a challenge — never
generate the record that might trigger STF reconsideration. Exit 4 creates an
information channel: when inferior courts identify and articulate qualified error, the
STF has material for reconsideration that mechanical compliance would suppress.

## 3. The Attack

### 3.1 The Inferential Gap: Procedural Form Does Not Establish Substantive Authority

The paper's legal derivation of Exit 4 rests primarily on art. 489, §1º, VI. The
provision's operative logic is negative: it defines what makes a departure *unfounded*.
A decision is "not considered reasoned" if it "fails to demonstrate the existence of
distinction or supersession." By contraposition: a departure that provides demonstration
of reasoning is not unfounded.

The paper reads this contraposition as establishing Exit 4's legitimacy: a court that
demonstrably identifies error and articulates qualified argument has provided
"demonstration of supersession," and its departure therefore satisfies the formal
requirement of art. 489, §1º, VI.

The inference fails because it conflates two distinct conditions: *formal validity*
(being a reasoned decision, satisfying art. 489) and *substantive authority* (acting
within the court's jurisdictional power). Art. 489, §1º, VI establishes the first.
The authorization for inferior courts to supersede STF binding precedents requires the
second. The two are not equivalent.

This is not a formal distinction without practical content. Consider: a driver who
exceeds the speed limit and provides an elaborate, well-documented justification for
the excess has met any notional formal requirement for reasoned rule-departure; this
does not authorize the departure. The driver remains in violation, and the
documentation may be evidence of deliberateness. Similarly, an inferior court that
provides qualified argument identifying error in an STF ratio has met art. 489's
formal reasoning requirement for a departure — the decision is a "reasoned" decision.
But the formal requirement for reasoning does not transmute an act outside the court's
authority into an act within it.

The binding obligation imposed by art. 927 CPC is not suspended by the quality of the
inferior court's reasoning. Art. 927, *caput*, states that "os juízes e os tribunais
observarão" the decisions enumerated — a mandatory formulation that applies
independently of whether the observing court has identified defects in the observed
ratio. Art. 927's force does not attenuate when the inferior court articulates a
qualified objection; it persists until the STF itself modifies the precedent. Art.
489, §1º, VI tells us that departures must be *explained*; it does not tell us that
*sufficiently explained* departures become authorized.

### 3.2 The Rational Binding Theory Does Not Supply the Missing Authorization

The paper draws on Marinoni's formula — precedent as "razão que não pode ser
desconsiderada sem razão mais forte" — to argue that rational binding implies the
possibility of overcoming binding force with stronger argument. The formula is precise
in both parts: a precedent is a *reason*, and a *stronger reason* is required to
disregard it.

But the formula does not specify who is authorized to determine when the stronger
reason threshold has been crossed. In Marinoni's own account, this determination is
made by the precedent-creating court in the exercise of its revision function —
precisely because the authority to assess whether the precedential reason has been
overcome by a stronger reason is a question of interpretive jurisdiction, not merely
one of logical quality. A reason that is sufficient in logical quality to overcome a
prior ratio is still only legally effective when assessed by the court with jurisdiction
to modify the precedent.

Put differently: the rational binding theory is a theory about what makes precedential
force *legitimate*, not a decision procedure for who determines when it lapses. Even
if we accept that binding force derives from reasoning quality — that a logical defect
attenuates a ratio's normative force at the source — it does not follow that individual
inferior courts are positioned to assess, case by case, when the attenuation threshold
has been crossed. The theory requires an authoritative external arbiter of when the
threshold is reached. Under the CPC 2015 and the CF/88, that arbiter is the STF, not
the inferior courts.

The paper's §4.2 cites Raz's normal justification thesis to ground Exit 4: authority
is legitimate when subjects better achieve their guiding reasons by deferring than by
acting directly; this sometimes fails when the inferior court is better positioned to
detect a specific error. But Raz's account is about when authority is *generally*
legitimate (across a population of decisions), not about individual exceptions within
an established authority structure. And crucially, Raz's service conception supports
deferring to authority even in cases where the subject thinks the authority is wrong,
precisely because the systematic benefits of stable authority exceed the costs of
individual errors that the authority structure is designed to correct through its own
mechanisms. Exit 4 is exactly the kind of individual escape from authority that Raz's
account counsels against absent the authority structure's own authorization.

### 3.3 The Constitutional Monopoly Constraint

The paper operates at the CPC level throughout. The §7.1 response to the hierarchical
conception argues that art. 489, §1º, VI requires only "fundamentação expressa" (not
"autorização do tribunal superior") for departures — a CPC-level argument.

But the most important binding precedents under art. 927 are constitutional: STF
decisions in concentrated control, binding súmulas on constitutional matters, and
full-bench orientations on constitutional questions. Art. 102, *caput*, of the CF/88
assigns the STF the custody of the Constitution ("guarda da Constituição"), which
encompasses the definitive interpretation of constitutional questions. This assignment
is not a procedural preference for efficiency; it is a constitutionally specified
allocation of interpretive authority. An inferior court that exercises "rational
supersession" of an STF constitutional holding — however qualified the argument — is
exercising a function the CF/88 reserves to the STF.

No CPC provision can modify this constitutional allocation. Art. 489, §1º, VI is a
procedural provision regulating the form of judicial decisions; it cannot operate as a
grant of substantive interpretive authority over constitutional questions that art. 102
CF assigns exclusively to the STF. Even if "superação" in art. 489, §1º, VI were read
expansively to encompass court-generated supersession — the paper's preferred reading —
the provision cannot be read to authorize that which the Constitution prohibits.

The paper does not engage with the art. 102 dimension. The objections in §7 address
the CPC-level hierarchical conception (§7.1), the Marinoni/Mitidiero orthodoxy (§7.2),
the Streck critique (§7.3), redundancy (§7.4), the Goodhart ratio conception (§7.5),
and the stability concern (§7.6). None addresses the constitutional allocation of
interpretive authority over the class of precedents that constitutes the paradigm
application of art. 927.

For non-constitutional precedents (STJ repetitive resources on federal statutory
questions), the constitutional constraint is less direct — but art. 105, *caput*, CF
assigns to the STJ the function of "unifying the interpretation of federal law,"
subject to which the same structural argument applies: the allocation of authority to
assess when a federal statutory precedent should be superseded belongs to the STJ, not
to inferior courts.

### 3.4 The Anticipatory Overruling Concept Does Not Support Exit 4

The paper invokes "anticipatory overruling" from comparative law (§7.2) as a parallel
to Exit 4. In the Anglo-American tradition, anticipatory overruling is a practice by
which an intermediate appellate court, observing signals that the supreme court is
likely to abandon a prior position — through dissents, narrowing decisions, changes in
court composition, or stated doctrinal direction — rules in accordance with what the
supreme court is predicted to hold, rather than with the current binding precedent.

The critical feature: anticipatory overruling is *predictive and instrumental*. The
intermediate court is not asserting its own authority to declare the prior precedent
wrong; it is making a strategic judgment about the supreme court's likely direction and
positioning its decision accordingly, typically with an express notation that signals
the case for the supreme court's attention. The intermediate court's "departure" is
justified not by the quality of its own reasoning against the precedent but by its
assessment of what the supreme court itself will shortly hold.

Paper 1B's Exit 4 is categorically different. The paper does not present Exit 4 as a
prediction about what the STF will hold; it presents it as an exercise of the inferior
court's own rational authority — the inferior court departing because *it has identified
the error* and *the error warrants departure*. The qualification of the argument (ônus
argumentativo qualificado) is understood as the internal threshold that the inferior
court applies to determine when departure is warranted. This is not predictive
deference; it is a claim of direct rational authority.

This distinction matters because the authorization structures differ. Anticipatory
overruling can be defended without a claim to substantive authority: the intermediate
court is trying to get the answer right given the supreme court's own trajectory, not
asserting independent interpretive power. Exit 4 requires the substantive authority
claim — inferior courts may supersede STF precedents on the strength of their own
assessment of the ratio's quality — for which the paper lacks a legal source.

### 3.5 The Exit 5 Coherence Problem

The paper simultaneously defends Exit 5 (legitimate abstention from invocation) and
condemns afastamento silencioso as one of two fundamental pathologies of the CPC 2015
regime (§1). These positions stand in unresolved tension.

If Exit 5 is a genuine legitimate response — as the paper asserts — then the precedent-
following obligation under art. 927 is triggered only by the court's choice to invoke
the precedent. A court that is aware of an inconvenient binding precedent may
legitimately route its analysis around it, citing alternative grounds, and never
incur the obligations that invocation would generate. The paper states this explicitly:
"quem invoca vincula-se às quatro saídas substantivas" (§3.5). The implication is that
who does not invoke does not incur this obligation.

But the paper's condemnation of afastamento silencioso (§§1, 5.1) targets courts that
"simply do not mention the precedent or mention it without engaging it." If Exit 5 is
legitimate — if non-invocation is a genuine option — then what distinguishes a court
that legitimately exercises Exit 5 from one that is committing afastamento silencioso?
The paper offers no principled criterion.

The obvious candidate is sincerity: a court exercises Exit 5 legitimately when the
alternative grounds are genuinely sufficient to resolve the case without the precedent,
not as a pretext for avoiding an inconvenient result. But the paper does not articulate
this criterion, which means Exit 5 is formally open to any court that can construct
alternative grounds — a permanent structural escape from binding precedent in any case
complex enough to admit multiple analytical paths.

Under a demanding reading of the binding precedent system, a precedent that *is*
applicable to the contested question should be invoked and engaged; a court that knows
the precedent is applicable and constructs alternative grounds to avoid it is
committing afastamento silencioso in substance, regardless of whether it nominally
invoked the precedent. If this reading is correct, Exit 5 is available only when the
precedent is genuinely inapplicable — which reduces Exit 5 to a clarification that
courts should not invoke inapplicable precedents, a trivially true proposition that adds
nothing to the taxonomy. If the more permissive reading governs — courts may elect
alternative grounds even when the precedent applies — the boundary between legitimate
Exit 5 and strategic afastamento silencioso requires a principled criterion the paper
does not provide.

## 4. Anticipated Reply and Why It Does Not Suffice

**On the inferential gap (§3.1):** The most direct reply is that the paper does not
claim Exit 4 produces a *final* legally effective supersession; it claims Exit 4 is a
legitimate *procedural* response that exposes the inferior court to reversal. The
superior court's reversal does not invalidate Exit 4 as a legitimate response; it
demonstrates the binding force in operation.

This reply concedes too much. If Exit 4 produces a decision that will be reversed —
that is, a decision that the legal system treats as *wrong* — then calling it a
"legitimate exit" misdescribes its legal status. A decision that correctly meets the
formal requirement of art. 489 (it is "reasoned") while being substantively outside
the court's authority (it supersedes a binding STF precedent the court has no power to
supersede) is procedurally valid but substantively erroneous. Calling this a "legitimate
exit" from the binding obligation conflates the decision's procedural form (meets
reasoning standards) with its substantive legal status (is contrary to binding law).
The paper cannot have it both ways: if Exit 4 is a legitimate exit, the decision is
legally authorized; if it will be reversed, the authorization is absent and the
"legitimacy" is merely formal.

**On the rational binding theory (§3.2):** The reply would argue that Marinoni's theory
implies the binding force lapses when the ratio is demonstrably defective, so the
inferior court applying the defective ratio is not violating a binding obligation but
a lapsed one.

The difficulty with this reply is that the determination that the ratio is "demonstrably
defective" — that the threshold for lapsed binding force has been crossed — is itself
contested. Every superior court that affirms the precedent on review has determined
that the inferior court was wrong about the defect. The inferior court's assessment of
the defect, and consequently of the lapse of binding force, is presumptively incorrect
from the perspective of the authority that holds the defect-assessment function. The
inferior court cannot simultaneously hold that it correctly identified the defect and
that the system confirms its authority to make this determination — the reversal on
review is precisely the system's determination that the defect identification was
incorrect.

**On the constitutional dimension (§3.3):** The reply might argue that the constitutional
concern applies only to concentrated constitutional control (controle concentrado) and
binding súmulas with constitutional content, and that Exit 4's practical application
is to infraconstitutional STJ precedents and similar non-constitutional binding norms
where art. 102 CF does not apply.

This concession would substantially narrow Exit 4's scope. The paper presents Exit 4
as a general feature of the five-exit taxonomy applicable to "precedente vinculante"
under art. 927 without distinction as to constitutional content. If the constitutional
monopoly constraint is accepted, Exit 4 would be unavailable for the class of precedents
that constitutes the core of art. 927 binding force — STF decisions in concentrated
control and binding súmulas. The modified thesis would hold only for infraconstitutional
statutory interpretation precedents from the STJ, a substantially reduced claim that
the paper does not present.

**On the Exit 5 coherence problem (§3.5):** The reply would argue that the
sincere/pretextual distinction is implicit in the paper's analysis and that art. 926
CPC's integrity requirement provides the criterion: a court exercises Exit 5 legitimately
when the alternative grounds are an honest assessment of the case's best resolution;
strategic avoidance violates art. 926's integrity obligation.

This reply shows the problem rather than solving it. If art. 926 integrity supplies the
criterion, then the sincere/pretextual distinction is a substantive requirement the
paper has not articulated, and its enforcement is left to the reviewer to assess on a
case-by-case basis — a standard so fact-intensive that Exit 5 provides courts with a
permanent formal defense against charges of afastamento silencioso: "I genuinely
believed the alternative grounds were sufficient." The paper's taxonomy requires a
more precise criterion for Exit 5's scope than the integrity obligation alone provides.

## 5. Scope of the Attack

This attack targets Exit 4 as a claim of substantive authorization for inferior courts
to supersede STF binding precedents. It also targets the internal coherence of the
taxonomy, specifically the tension between Exit 5 and the paper's condemnation of
afastamento silencioso.

This attack does not challenge:

- The paper's central claim that the CPC 2015 mandates substantive rather than formal
  engagement with binding precedents, and that the two-exit binary (follow or not follow)
  understates the options the art. 489, §1º, V and VI framework creates.
- Exit 1 (correct application), Exit 2 (distinguishing), or Exit 3 (recognition of
  supervening supersession by the source court), which are well-grounded.
- The silent partial application concept (§§5.1–5.3), which correctly identifies a
  distinct failure mode that art. 489's formal requirement does not fully capture.
- The quality of the paper's engagement with Marinoni and Mitidiero's theory of
  precedent, which is accurate and substantively engaged.
- The existence of qualified argumentative burdens as a structural feature of
  precedent-departure justification — the paper is correct that departures require
  more than assertion.
- The functional desirability of some error-correction mechanism within the precedential
  system. The attack contests the legal grounding of Exit 4 as that mechanism, not the
  desirability of having one.

## 6. Surrender Conditions

The attack would not hold, or would hold only partially, under the following conditions.

1. **A specific legal source for Exit 4's authorization is identified.** If a
   provision of the CPC 2015, of the CF/88, or of constitutional doctrine specifically
   authorizes inferior courts to depart from binding STF precedents on the basis of
   qualified argument identifying error in the ratio — independently of the source
   court's own subsequent decisions — the inference gap in §3.1 is closed. The paper
   does not currently identify such a provision; the search for one is the clearest
   path to rescuing Exit 4.

2. **A constitutional doctrine supporting Exit 4 for infraconstitutional precedents
   is established.** If the art. 102 CF constraint is accepted for constitutional
   binding precedents (as the §3.3 analysis implies it should be), the attack could
   be partially defeated if the STJ-level precedents under art. 927 have a different
   authorization structure. A legal argument showing that the STJ's unification function
   under art. 105 CF is compatible with Exit 4 for statutory-interpretation precedents
   would narrow the attack's scope to constitutional precedents only.

3. **The Exit 5 criterion is articulated.** If the paper or a response to this attack
   develops a principled criterion distinguishing legitimate abstention (Exit 5) from
   strategic afastamento silencioso — specifying the conditions under which a court
   may resolve a case on alternative grounds without engaging an applicable binding
   precedent — the §3.5 coherence problem is resolved.

4. **The rational supersession mechanism is recharacterized as predictive
   (anticipatory overruling), not authoritative.** If the paper concedes that Exit 4 is
   not a claim of substantive authority but rather a strategic maneuver predicting (and
   soliciting) STF reconsideration — analogous to the Anglo-American concept — the §3.4
   objection is addressed. This concession would require reframing Exit 4 as the
   inferior court's signal to the STF rather than the inferior court's exercise of its
   own rational authority, and would resolve the authorization gap at the cost of
   substantially narrowing Exit 4's original formulation.

5. **Empirical evidence of systemic function.** If it can be shown that, in practice,
   inferior court decisions exercising rational supersession (as Paper 1B defines it)
   demonstrably increase the rate of STF reconsideration of erroneous precedents
   compared to regimes of mechanical compliance — and that this increase justifies the
   due process costs to parties whose cases produce known-to-be-reversible decisions —
   the functional argument for Exit 4 is substantiated. This would not close the
   authorization gap but would shift the debate toward whether the authorization should
   be recognized as a matter of legal development, making the current paper's descriptive
   claim (Exit 4 exists as a legitimate option under the CPC 2015) a normative proposal
   (Exit 4 should be recognized) whose legal foundation requires explicit statutory or
   constitutional grounding.
