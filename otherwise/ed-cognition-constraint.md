# Embargos de Declaração Cannot Generate What They Can Only Recall: The Cognition Constraint on Integrative Relief

---

## 1. Thesis Attacked

The paper "Embargos de Declaração no CPC 2015: Escopo Amplo dos Vícios e Infringência como Consequência" (Baldo, 2025; `paper1A_embargos_declaracao.md`) defends two related theses about the scope of embargos de declaração under the CPC 2015.

**Thesis 1 (scope):** The mode of defect — obscurity, contradiction, omission — determines what is curable by embargos, not the subject matter on which the defect falls. A merit-based omission falling within art. 489, §1º, IV (failure to address an argument capable of undermining the conclusion) is a curable defect under art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II, of the CPC 2015.

**Thesis 2 (infringement effects):** Modification of the embargoed decision is an automatic consequence of granting embargos when cure implies modification — not an autonomous claim requiring a subsidiary request. The formula "subsidiariamente, requer-se a atribuição de efeitos infringentes," routinely used in practice, is dogmatically imprecise.

The paper grounds both theses in a key structural claim (§5.3): when embargos address a §489, §1º, IV violation, "não há nova cognição autônoma" — there is no new autonomous cognition, only completion of the existing decision's reasoning path.

---

## 2. Faithful Reconstruction

The paper's strongest form rests on a distinction between two types of judicial activity: *correcting the representation* of a decision (clarifying what was meant, completing what was omitted) versus *replacing the substance* of a decision (reaching a different judgment on the merits). Both theses, the paper argues, concern only the former.

**On Thesis 1:** Art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II, explicitly equates the art. 489, §1º conducts with omission for purposes of embargos de declaração: "Considera-se omissa a decisão que: ... II — incorra em qualquer das condutas descritas no art. 489, §1º." This is not a doctrinal inference but a direct textual command. The legislature chose to denominate art. 489, §1º, IV violations — failure to engage decisive arguments raised by the parties — as omissão for purposes of the clarification remedy. Under this reading, the question of whether the unaddressed argument concerned a merit issue is irrelevant: the mode (omission) is what triggers the remedy, not the matter.

The §5.3 response to the "third instance" objection is the heart of the defense: embargos address the *defect of the reasoning path* (the absence of engagement with an argument that was already before the court), not the merit question *de novo*. The decisive argument was raised by the parties in the original proceeding; it was before the court; the court was obligated to engage it; its failure to do so left the decision incomplete. Embargos require the court to complete what it was obligated to do — not to reach a new judgment as though the merits had not already been addressed.

**On Thesis 2:** Art. 1.024, §3º treats modification as the presupposition of the embargado's procedural rights ("caso o acolhimento dos embargos de declaração implique modificação"). By treating modification as the trigger for those rights — rather than as the object of a separate claim — the legislature signaled that modification flows automatically from granting embargos when the integration implies it. The §3º's protective mechanism for the embargado is positioned post-hoc precisely because modification is treated as an outcome of the court's integrative activity, not as an object of the embargante's petition. The traditional formula that requests infringement effects "subsidiarily" mistakenly treats the consequence as a separate claim and thereby subjects the substantive remedy to a formal requirement the statute does not impose.

**Supplementary defense 1 — Autonomy versus generativity:** The §5.3 claim uses "autônoma," not "nova." New deliberation that is bounded by the decision's fixed controlling precedents, fixed factual findings, closed evidentiary record, decided issues out of scope, and no new legal theories not raised by the parties is non-autonomous even if it produces judgments not previously stated. Compared to de novo appellate review or rescisória — where none of these constraints apply — embargos deliberation is categorically more constrained. The constraints prevent the court from reconstructing the case; they permit only completion within the established framework. Under this reading, new deliberation in embargos is non-autonomous even when it generates judgments for the first time.

**Supplementary defense 2 — Empirical range:** The original attack's paradigm case — contested constitutional classification of a servant's cargo across a legislative reenquadramento — was chosen to display doctrinal complexity. It is not representative of the distribution of §489, §1º, IV violations in Brazilian appellate practice. The empirical center consists of three categories where the court's existing framework commits it to a determinate answer: binding-precedent omissions (court transcribes and accepts a controlling súmula, fails to apply it to a party's argument that directly engages it), statutory-provision omissions (provision whose coverage is settled, failed to be addressed), and internal-consistency omissions (tension in the court's own findings, with the argument identifying the implication for the dispositif). In each category, addressing the omission completes an analytical circuit already established — recall cognition, not generative.

## 3. The Attack

### 3.1 The Cognition Constraint

Embargos de declaração perform one of two cognitive functions, and both theses depend on conflating them.

**Recall cognition:** The court retrieves and makes explicit what its decision already, implicitly, decided. A dispositif that is obscure in its reach over a specific matter already decided that matter — the embargos only require the court to state explicitly what the implicit judgment was. A decision that identified a controlling ratio but failed to articulate it clearly already applied the ratio — the embargos require explicit articulation. In these cases, no new substantive judgment is formed; only the expression of an existing judgment is completed.

**Generative cognition:** The court reaches a substantive judgment it had not previously reached. An argument raised by a party that was never addressed — not implicitly, not structurally, not through the logical entailments of what was explicitly decided — has not been disposed of. When embargos require the court to address it, the court must form a substantive judgment about the argument for the first time.

The paper's defense of Thesis 1 relies on classifying art. 489, §1º, IV violations as triggering only recall cognition: the decisive argument was before the court, the court was obligated to address it, therefore addressing it in embargos merely completes the circuit of cognition already opened. But this classification is false for the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case. Consider what the court must do to address a previously unaddressed argument on the merits:

First, the court must determine whether the argument is indeed "capable of undermining the conclusion in principle" (em tese). This is not a factual question with a determinate answer; it is a legal judgment that requires evaluating the argument's quality, its relationship to the applicable precedents, and whether those precedents, if engaged, would require a different dispositif. This determination did not exist before the embargos — it is formed for the first time in response to them.

Second, the court must engage the argument substantively: weigh it against the considerations that supported the original conclusion, determine how it fits or fails to fit the relevant legal framework, and reach a position on its persuasiveness. This is new deliberation on the merits of the question, not retrieval of a judgment already made.

Third, the court must determine whether this new engagement changes the result. If the court concludes the argument is decisive, it reverses; if not, it affirms. This conclusion is not a consequence flowing automatically from the original decision — it depends on the outcome of the new deliberation just performed.

None of these three steps is "recall cognition." All three involve forming a substantive judgment not previously formed.

### 3.2 Constraints Bound; They Do Not Determine

The autonomy defense identifies four structural constraints on embargos deliberation: fixed controlling precedents, fixed factual findings, closed evidentiary record, decided issues outside scope, and no new legal theories not raised by the parties. These constraints are genuine and distinguish embargos from de novo appellate review and rescisória. They do not, however, establish that the deliberation within those constraints is non-autonomous in the sense §5.3 requires.

A constraint bounds the permissible space of deliberation. Cognition is non-autonomous — in the operative sense Paper 1A needs — only if the constraints *determine* the outcome of deliberation: if the only position consistent with all the constraints is the position the court will reach. If the constraints are compatible with more than one outcome and the court must choose among them, the court's choice is a new substantive judgment not logically entailed by the prior decision. Such a judgment is autonomous in the relevant sense: it was not predetermined by what the court had already decided.

For the clearest cases within the binding-precedent category, the constraints do determine the outcome. A court that has accepted Súmula X, applied it to facts α through δ in the same decision, and faces a party's argument invoking Súmula X for fact ε that is structurally identical to α — here the constraint leaves the court with one answer. The deliberation required is recognition, not formation of a new position. This is recall cognition, and the attack does not reach it.

But art. 489, §1º, IV defines the relevant omissions as arguments "capable of undermining the conclusion in principle." An argument capable of undermining the conclusion is one whose engagement might change the dispositif. If the court's fixed precedents and factual findings already determined the argument's resolution — if there were only one consistent answer — the argument would not be capable of undermining the conclusion; the court's framework would already have answered it, either explicitly or through implicit rejection. The statutory definition selects for cases where the framework has not determined the answer.

This is the operational bite of the cognition constraint: within the category of arguments art. 489, §1º, IV reaches — decisive, unaddressed, not implicitly rejected — the autonomy defense's constraints narrow but do not collapse the deliberative space. The court forms a new judgment among the remaining options. That judgment is autonomous in the operative sense: the prior decision did not determine it.

The comparison to de novo review confirms rather than contests this analysis. The comparison establishes that embargos deliberation has fewer degrees of freedom than de novo review. Fewer degrees of freedom is not zero degrees of freedom. A court with n options makes new substantive judgments within those n options; a court with n−k options (under embargos constraints) still makes new substantive judgments within the remaining n−k options. The quantitative reduction in deliberative space is not a qualitative change in cognitive type. The relevant question is not whether embargos constrain more than de novo review (they do) but whether the constraints determine the outcome for the arguments that §489, §1º, IV reaches (they do not, by the statute's own definitional requirements).

The paper's §5.3 needs "autônoma" to mean "pre-determined by the prior decision's logic," not merely "bounded by the same legal framework." If it means only the latter, every second-degree appellate court's cognition qualifies as non-autonomous — they too work within precedent and factual findings. The claim that embargos cognition is distinctively non-autonomous requires the stronger reading. The stronger reading fails for the arguments art. 489, §1º, IV selects.

The most developed response to this analysis proposes a different operationalization: "autonomous" means not "pre-determined in outcome" but "approach-free" — the embargos court lacks the freedom to select its own analytical framework, invoke authorities not previously identified in the proceeding, or approach the omitted argument from an independent analytical standpoint. Ordinary second-degree appellate courts retain full approach freedom within the legal order: they may invoke precedents the lower court omitted, apply different legal theories to the same facts, decide issues the lower court failed to reach under art. 1.013, §3º CPC, and supplement the lower court's reasoning with independent doctrinal analysis. Embargos courts have none of these freedoms — they are bound to the specific prior decision's reasoning structure. Outcome indeterminacy within the constrained space is therefore compatible with non-autonomy: the embargos court may face genuine options within a fixed framework, but the framework itself is not of the court's choosing.

Three objections block this operationalization.

*Approach freedom is not the operative criterion for the doctrinal distinction §5.3 requires.* The paper's §5.3 claim is the answer to the "terceira instância" objection — that allowing embargos to address §489, §1º, IV violations constitutes a prohibited third instance of merit review. The functional test for "terceira instância" in Brazilian civil procedure is: does the court re-examine merit questions and produce new substantive determinations capable of altering the case outcome? This test does not track approach freedom. A court executing a limited remand — instructed by a superior court to determine damages within an already-established liability framework — lacks approach freedom: it cannot revisit liability, invoke precedents outside the damages domain, apply a different theory, or expand its scope. By the approach-freedom criterion, the cognition is "non-autonomous." Yet the damages determination is unambiguously a first-time substantive judgment on a previously undecided question, and no Brazilian proceduralist would characterize it as involving "no new autonomous cognition." The "terceira instância" functional test correctly classifies it as new merit cognition; the approach-freedom criterion does not. Because §5.3's purpose is precisely to answer the "terceira instância" objection, the correct criterion is the functional one. Under the functional criterion, embargos courts addressing previously omitted decisive arguments produce a first-time substantive determination on a previously undecided merit question — exactly what §5.3 denied.

*The outcome-indeterminacy concession establishes what §5.3 needed to deny.* The approach-freedom defense accepts that "constraints narrow without determining outcomes" and that outcome indeterminacy within the constrained space is present for genuine §489, §1º, IV violations. If the embargos court may affirm or reverse depending on how it engages the omitted argument, the court is forming a new outcome-determining judgment not entailed by the original decision. §5.3's "no autonomous new cognition" was designed to show that embargos produce no such judgment — that the outcome was already fixed by the decision's structure and embargos merely surface it. Once outcome indeterminacy is conceded, this claim is surrendered. The approach-freedom label characterizes the deliberative *process* (approach-constrained deliberation within a fixed framework) without changing the deliberative *product* (a new contingent merit determination whose outcome depends on the embargos court's deliberation). §5.3's claim is about the product, not the process: the paper denies that new determinations are formed, not merely that they are formed within approach constraints.

*No Brazilian procedural authority operationalizes "cognição autônoma" as "approach freedom."* The concept operates within the specific doctrinal context of CPC 2015 embargos practice, where the relevant contrast class is the prohibited "terceira instância" as understood in Brazilian civil procedure. No statutory text, established doctrine, or jurisprudential authority establishes that the embargos/terceira-instância boundary tracks approach freedom rather than the production of new substantive merit determinations. The approach-freedom operationalization, however philosophically coherent as a distinction, requires a doctrinal foundation in Brazilian procedural law that has not been supplied. Until a textual or doctrinal basis for this reading is established in Brazilian procedural scholarship, the operationalization functions as an ad hoc move that rescues §5.3 from the adversarial critique without grounding the rescue in the legal system the paper describes.

### 3.3 The False Premise in the Scope Argument

The paper's textual argument for Thesis 1 — that art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II, equates art. 489, §1º violations with omissão — is correct as a matter of statutory construction. The legislature did classify art. 489, §1º conduct as omissão for embargos purposes. This establishes that embargos *can* be filed in response to such violations; it does not establish that embargos can *cure* them in the cognitively simple way the paper assumes.

A statute can denominate a set of facts as "omissão" for purposes of triggering a remedy without determining whether the remedy is curative in the integrative sense. The art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II classification extends embargos standing to art. 489, §1º, IV violations; it does not guarantee that the embargos remedy, when granted, will achieve its integrative function without generating new cognition. These are separate questions. The first (standing) the paper resolves correctly by textual analysis. The second (functional scope) the paper resolves incorrectly by assuming that "omissão" in the statutory sense tracks "omission" in the cognitively thin sense that recall cognition can supply.

The paper's illustration (§4) exposes this ambiguity. In the hypothetical, the court transcribed the STF precedent including its express reservation ("excluídos os especialistas em educação") and then reached a conclusion that included the category the reservation excludes. The paper characterizes this as an omissão (failure to engage the decisive argument about whether the party's original appointment as especialista triggers the reservation). But what does curing this "omission" require?

The court must now determine: (a) whether the post-legislative reenquadramento under state law altered the constitutional classification of the cargo; (b) if so, whether the constitutional classification for the merger-effect purpose in art. 37, XVI, "b," should track the original appointment or the current official designation; and (c) whether the STF's reservation phrase, read contextually, covers servants who were specialists at appointment but reclassified to teachers by state legislative act. These are substantive constitutional questions on which no prior court judgment exists in the case. Curing the "omission" requires generating the answers to these questions — genuine new cognition on the merits of a disputed constitutional classification.

### 3.4 The Selection Effect

The empirical range defense argues that binding-precedent omissions, statutory-provision omissions, and internal-consistency omissions constitute the predominant category of §489, §1º, IV violations and that these are Type A — determinate under the court's framework. The attack, on this view, reaches only the minority.

The argument faces a selection effect operating through both the statute's definitional requirements and the implicit rejection doctrine.

Art. 489, §1º, IV requires that the omitted argument be "capable of undermining the conclusion in principle." An argument that a court's existing framework definitively resolves in one direction — one that produces a determinate answer regardless of how the court reasons within its constraints — is not typically the type of argument that goes unaddressed while remaining capable of undermining the conclusion. Courts that face arguments with obvious determinate outcomes under their accepted framework tend to address them explicitly or dispose of them through implicit rejection. The combination of decisiveness and genuine omission selects against the most determinate cases.

The implicit rejection doctrine operates as a second filter. Courts whose reasoning structure logically disposes of an argument — even without explicit discussion — have, under the doctrine, implicitly rejected it. Such arguments are not genuine §489, §1º, IV violations: the court had a position, and the position was expressed in the decision's structure. What remains past the implicit rejection filter are arguments for which the court formed no position, explicit or implicit. This is the category of genuine omissions: arguments the court's framework neither determined nor implicitly disposed of.

The combination of these two filters — decisiveness threshold and implicit rejection — creates a structural selection effect within the §489, §1º, IV category. The violations that survive both filters are disproportionately those where: (a) the argument is capable of changing the outcome; (b) the argument was not obviously determined by the framework (otherwise the court would have engaged it or implicitly rejected it); and (c) the court genuinely failed to form any position on it. This is the Type B profile.

The three categories cited in the empirical range defense — binding-precedent, statutory-provision, internal-consistency omissions — are structural patterns, not guaranteed Type A cases. Within each pattern, there is a spectrum from determinate to contested. The selection effect implies that the §489, §1º, IV violations within each pattern that actually survive past the decisiveness threshold and the implicit rejection filter tend toward the contested end of the spectrum. The determinate applications would have been addressed or implicitly rejected. What reaches the remedy is what the framework left open.

A structural-deployment limit has been offered to narrow this selection effect: the implicit rejection doctrine requires not merely that the court's framework provide a determinate answer but that the court's structural reasoning have *actually reached and logically disposed of* the argument. A court that possesses a framework capable of answering argument E but whose reasoning path stopped before E may generate a genuine §489, §1º, IV omission that survives past the implicit rejection filter while remaining Type A — framework-determined but structurally undeployed. The structural-deployment limit is a genuine and accepted refinement: the Type A fraction within genuine omissions is larger than unmodified selection-effect analysis predicts. The structural prediction nonetheless remains directional. The deployment gap does not reverse the selection pressure; it modifies its magnitude. The statutory combination of the decisiveness threshold and the implicit rejection doctrine — even under its deployment-limit construction — still creates systematic bias toward framework-undetermined cases among the violations that reach the embargos remedy. A court that possesses a framework answering argument E and whose structural reasoning stopped before E has suffered a reasoning failure of the kind art. 489, §1º, IV addresses; that the framework would provide a determinate answer does not mean that curing the omission involves no new cognition — the court must now deploy reasoning it previously failed to run to its conclusion, and within the deployment, the framework's answer may be more or less determinate. At the contested end of the deployment gap — where the framework's answer required further specification before application — the resulting cognition is not purely Type A. The frontier between the deployment gap and genuine framework openness is itself contested and does not yield an automatic Type A majority.

### 3.5 The Implicit Rejection Doctrine Confirms the Attack

The paper does not engage with the implicit rejection doctrine (rejeição implícita). This absence is significant not as a gap in coverage but as a structural problem for the thesis. The doctrine defines the boundary between two categories of court silence: arguments the court's reasoning structure has implicitly disposed of (not genuine omissions) and arguments the court genuinely omitted (genuine §489, §1º, IV violations). This boundary is exactly where the recall/generative and autonomous/non-autonomous distinctions matter most.

The supportive position holds that the doctrine's presence supports Paper 1A's §5.3: implicit rejection cases are recall cognition at its clearest (the position exists; embargos surface it), and genuine omissions that are also Type A are §5.3's core application domain. But this reading does not help the paper where the attack bites.

Consider the logic. Cases subject to implicit rejection are, by the doctrine's definition, already decided — the court has a position. Embargos for these cases require surfacing the existing position. This is recall cognition, and §5.3 holds unqualifiedly for these cases. But these are not the cases the attack concerns. The attack is directed at the genuine omissions — the arguments past the implicit rejection filter, where the court had no position at all.

Among genuine omissions, the distinction between Type A and Type B is operationally significant. Type A requires the court's framework to determine the answer. The selection effect (§3.4) establishes that genuine omissions past the implicit rejection filter are disproportionately those where the framework did not determine the answer. The implicit rejection doctrine is itself part of the selection mechanism: it removes from the §489, §1º, IV category the arguments the framework has already answered, leaving the arguments the framework has not answered.

The doctrine's filtering function therefore confirms rather than qualifies the attack. The implicit rejection filter removes the clearest Type A cases from the §489, §1º, IV category, leaving the genuine omissions where generative cognition is most evidently required. Paper 1A's failure to engage the doctrine is not incidental; it is the omission that prevents the paper from establishing the limiting principle its §5.3 claim requires. The supportive papers invoke the doctrine as Paper 1A's ally, but its filtering function places it on the adversarial side: it removes from the §489, §1º, IV category precisely the cases where §5.3 would be correct, leaving the cases where §5.3 is false.

### 3.6 The "Automatic Consequence" Claim Cannot Be Sustained

Thesis 2's claim that modification is an automatic consequence of granting embargos when cure "implies" it inherits the structural problem from Thesis 1. The claim fails in two forms — both the broader theoretical characterization and the narrower procedural reading.

**The generative cognition problem:** If addressing the previously omitted argument requires generative cognition — as §§3.1–3.2 establish for the arguments art. 489, §1º, IV selects — then whether modification follows depends on the outcome of that new cognition, not on a logical implication from the decision's existing structure. The embargos court forms a new judgment on the omitted argument; from that new judgment, the court determines whether its dispositif must change. The modification is contingent on both the new judgment and its implication — neither of which flows automatically from anything in the original decision.

**The procedural automaticity problem:** A narrower reading of Thesis 2 is available: once integration implies modification, no separate petition is required from the embargante. "Automatic" on this reading means procedural — the modification materializes without a subsidiary request. This reading is compatible with the paper's practical proposal (substituting the traditional formula), and the attack accepts the practical proposal's defensibility.

But even on this procedural reading, the "automatic consequence" characterization misrepresents the deliberative sequence. The procedural mechanism — no separate petition required — is triggered after the court has determined that its integration "implies" modification. This determination is not procedural; it is substantive. The court integrates the decision by addressing the omitted argument through new cognition; it then judges whether the outcome of that cognition requires a different dispositif. If the new cognition is generative (§§3.1–3.2), the determination that integration implies modification is itself a new substantive judgment. The procedural automaticity begins after this judgment, not before. "Automatic consequence" correctly describes the final procedural step (no separate petition needed); it mischaracterizes the deliberative process that precedes that step as non-generative.

Art. 1.024, §3º's grammar confirms this. "Caso o acolhimento dos embargos de declaração implique modificação" — the conditional "caso" indicates that whether integration implies modification is a contingent determination. The provision addresses what happens if modification follows, not that it will. The provision's design anticipates that embargos are sometimes granted without modification (the §3.4 cases the paper acknowledges) because the court's new cognition sometimes leads to affirmation, not reversal. The conditional's reality undermines the "automatic consequence" characterization at both the logical level (modification is not guaranteed) and the procedural level (the mechanism is triggered only after new deliberation produces a result that implies modification).

## 4. Anticipated Reply and Why It Does Not Suffice

Three distinct counter-arguments are available to the paper and its defenses. Each is addressed in turn.

### 4.1 The Generative/Autonomous Distinction

The most developed counter-argument is the approach-freedom operationalization: §5.3's "autônoma" means not "pre-determined in outcome" but "approach-free." Embargos cognition is non-autonomous because the court lacks the freedom to select its own framework, invoke new authorities, or approach the question from an independent standpoint — not because the outcome within the constrained space is certain. Ordinary appellate courts retain full approach freedom within the legal order; embargos courts are bound to the specific prior decision's reasoning structure. On this reading, outcome indeterminacy within the fixed framework does not establish autonomy: the court may face genuine options without being free to choose its analytical approach.

This reply is philosophically coherent as a distinction between types of constraint but does not suffice for three reasons.

First, approach freedom is not the criterion §5.3's doctrinal purpose requires. §5.3's claim is designed to answer the "terceira instância" objection — that embargos addressing §489, §1º, IV violations constitute a prohibited third instance of merit review. The Brazilian procedural test for "terceira instância" is functional: does the court produce a new first-time substantive determination on a previously undecided merit question capable of altering the case outcome? This test does not track approach freedom, as §3.2 demonstrates through the limited-remand comparison. Because §5.3 exists to answer the "terceira instância" concern, the operative criterion is the functional one. Under the functional criterion, an embargos court addressing a previously omitted decisive argument produces exactly what §5.3 denied — a new substantive determination on a previously undecided merit question.

Second, the outcome-indeterminacy concession surrenders the core claim. The approach-freedom defense accepts that outcome indeterminacy within the constrained space is present for genuine §489, §1º, IV omissions: the embargos court may affirm or reverse depending on how it engages the argument. This is a concession that the embargos court is forming new outcome-determining merit judgments not entailed by the original decision. §5.3's "no autonomous new cognition" is a claim about the *product* of embargos activity — no new substantive determinations — not merely about the *process*. Once outcome indeterminacy is conceded, the claim about the product is abandoned regardless of how the process is characterized.

Third, the approach-freedom operationalization lacks doctrinal grounding in Brazilian procedural law. "Cognição autônoma" in §5.3 calls for interpretation within the CPC 2015 embargos context, where the operative contrast is the embargos/terceira-instância boundary as Brazilian courts and procedural scholars understand it. No statutory text or doctrinal authority establishes that this boundary tracks approach freedom rather than the production of new substantive determinations. The operationalization is a philosophical import with no procedural law support.

### 4.2 The Empirical Range Argument

The second counter-argument claims that the cognition-constraint attack uses an unrepresentative paradigm. The adversarial hypothetical — contested constitutional classification across a legislative reenquadramento — is an atypical Type B case. The empirical center of §489, §1º, IV violations is three categories (binding-precedent, statutory-provision, internal-consistency omissions) where the framework determines the answer. §5.3's claim applies to the predominant category; the cognition-constraint attack reaches only the minority.

This reply does not suffice for two reasons.

First, the selection effect (§3.4) undermines the empirical characterization. The §489, §1º, IV category is defined by arguments that are decisive and genuinely unaddressed. Both features select against cases the framework has already determined. Courts that face arguments with determinate outcomes under their accepted frameworks address them or implicitly reject them; courts that genuinely omit decisive arguments tend to do so for non-trivial reasons. Within each of the three structural patterns cited in the defense, the §489, §1º, IV violations that survive both the decisiveness threshold and the implicit rejection filter are disproportionately the contested cases — the ones the framework left open. The determinate applications would not survive as genuine §489, §1º, IV violations.

Second, even granting that a substantial category of binding-precedent and statutory-provision omissions are Type A, the paper's §5.3 claim is stated without this limitation. Paper 1A presents its hypothetical — the STF reservation case — as a paradigm example of embargos curing a §489, §1º, IV violation. That hypothetical is Type B. A general thesis that invokes a Type B case as its central illustration, stated without explicit limitation to Type A, cannot be defended by empirical evidence that Type A cases also exist. §5.3's "no autonomous new cognition" applies to all §489, §1º, IV violations as the paper frames it; the empirical range defense establishes, at best, that the claim is correct for a subset of them.

### 4.3 The Procedural Automaticity Defense

The narrowest available defense of Thesis 2 reads "automatic" as procedural: once integration implies modification, no separate petition is required. Paper 1A's §3.4 explicitly acknowledges non-modifying grants, so the paper does not claim modification is always guaranteed. The "automatic" aspect is that the modification materializes without a subsidiary request when integration does imply it.

This reading is compatible with the paper's practical proposal and the adversarial attack accepts it as such. The practical proposal survives in any case.

But the procedural reading does not save Thesis 2's characterization as "automatic consequence" of the original decision's structure. §3.6 establishes that the procedural mechanism is triggered after two new substantive judgments: the court's new cognition about the omitted argument, and the court's judgment that this cognition implies a different dispositif. Both of these are new judgments — formed for the first time in embargos, not logically entailed by the prior decision. The modification is the consequence of this new deliberation, not the automatic consequence of the original decision's integration. "Automatic consequence" correctly describes the final procedural step (no additional petition needed once the court determines modification is implied); it mischaracterizes the deliberative process as non-generative when the determination that modification is implied is itself a product of new generative cognition.

The paper's reading of art. 1.024, §3º — that the provision presupposes modification as the trigger of the embargado's rights — requires that "caso o acolhimento implique modificação" be read as a conditional on a logically necessary event. "Caso" is conditional grammar; it marks contingency. The provision addresses what happens if the conditional is satisfied — when modification follows from integration. This is consistent with the procedural reading (no separate petition needed when the conditional is satisfied) but confirms that satisfaction of the conditional is contingent on what the court's new deliberation produces. The procedural mechanism does not bypass the new deliberation; it follows from it.

## 5. Scope of the Attack

This attack is directed at the claim in §5.3 that "não há nova cognição autônoma" when embargos address §489, §1º, IV violations, and at the related claim in Thesis 2 that modification is an automatic consequence of integration. The attack does not contest:

- The paper's textual analysis of art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II: the legislature did equate art. 489, §1º, IV conduct with omissão for embargos purposes. Standing to file embargos in response to such violations has textual support.
- The mode-not-matter rule as applied to cases that are genuinely within the recall cognition category: embargos can and should address formal omissions about merit questions where the court's existing framework determines the answer.
- The paper's identification of the traditional formula's problems: "subsidiariamente, requer-se a atribuição de efeitos infringentes" does create structural ambiguity by treating modification as a separate claim. The paper's practical proposal (substituting the traditional formula) is procedurally defensible, and the adversarial attack does not undermine it.
- The paper's description of STJ jurisprudence: the STJ has long admitted embargos for merit-based omissions and contradictions. The paper's characterization is accurate.
- The paper's overall project of calibrating embargos practice to the CPC 2015's text.

The attack shows that the paper's general thesis, as stated, overclaims by relying on a structural claim about cognition that is false for the arguments art. 489, §1º, IV selects. §5.3's "no autonomous new cognition" is correct for recall-cognition cases where the framework determines the answer; it is incorrect for the genuine §489, §1º, IV violations the statute defines — the decisive, unaddressed arguments the court's framework and the implicit rejection doctrine have not already disposed of. These are the cases the attack targets; these are the cases where embargos require generative cognition that is autonomous in the operative sense.

---

## 6. Surrender Conditions

The attack would not hold, or would hold only partially, under the following conditions.

1. **Explicit limitation to recall-cognition omissions.** If the paper limited Thesis 1 to omissions where the court's existing reasoning determines the answer to the unaddressed argument — making the extension of embargos to §489, §1º, IV violations contingent on the court's ability to resolve the omission by applying its own framework — the cognition-constraint attack would be substantially answered. The limitation would require the paper to acknowledge that Type B violations are not curable by embargos in the straightforward integrative sense and to revise its illustration accordingly.

2. **Engagement with the implicit rejection doctrine as a limiting principle.** If the paper incorporated the implicit rejection doctrine as a principled demarcation between recall-cognition omissions and genuine omissions requiring new deliberation — and then showed that Type A constitutes the predominant category of genuine omissions past the implicit rejection filter — the attack would be substantially narrowed. This requires more than naming the Type A/Type B distinction; it requires showing, with support from Brazilian appellate practice, that the genuine §489, §1º, IV omissions that survive implicit rejection are predominantly Type A despite the selection effect the statute's own requirements create.

3. **Empirical demonstration of Type A predominance against the selection effect.** If the paper demonstrated, with data from Brazilian appellate courts, that the §489, §1º, IV violations that both survive the implicit rejection doctrine and reach embargos are predominantly binding-precedent or statutory-provision cases where the framework commits the court to a determinate answer — and not the contested cases the selection effect predicts — the empirical range argument would be placed on verifiable ground. The structural argument predicts the contrary distribution; the burden of proof falls on the paper.

4. **Reformulation of Thesis 2 as contingent rather than automatic.** If the paper withdrew the "automatic consequence" characterization and substituted a claim that modification follows from integration when the court's new deliberation on the omitted argument implies a different dispositif — connecting this contingency explicitly to the new cognition embargos require — the attack on Thesis 2 would be substantially addressed. The practical proposal (substituting the traditional formula) survives in any case.

5. **Operationalization of "non-autonomous" cognition.** If the paper provided criteria distinguishing non-autonomous embargos cognition from autonomous cognition — criteria administrable in practice, criteria that explain why the court's cognition in embargos falls on the non-autonomous side for the arguments art. 489, §1º, IV selects — the conceptual critique in §§3.1–3.2 would be addressed. The current paper does not provide such criteria; §5.3's "no autonomous new cognition" is asserted rather than operationalized. Any criterion that makes the claim hold for the genuine §489, §1º, IV cases (past the implicit rejection filter) would either reduce to pre-determination by prior commitments (which fails, by §3.2's argument) or would require the paper to accept a limitation the thesis does not currently state.
