# Embargos de Declaração Cannot Generate What They Can Only Recall: The Cognition Constraint on Integrative Relief

---

## 1. Thesis Attacked

The paper "Embargos de Declaração no CPC 2015: Escopo Amplo dos Vícios e Infringência como Consequência" (Baldo, 2025; `paper1A_embargos_declaracao.md`) defends two related theses about the scope of embargos de declaração under the CPC 2015.

**Thesis 1 (scope):** The mode of defect — obscurity, contradiction, omission — determines what is curable by embargos, not the subject matter on which the defect falls. A merit-based omission falling within art. 489, §1º, IV (failure to address an argument capable of undermining the conclusion) is a curable defect under art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II, of the CPC 2015.

**Thesis 2 (infringement effects):** Modification of the embargoed decision is an automatic consequence of granting embargos when cure implies modification — not an autonomous claim requiring a subsidiary request. The formula "subsidiariamente, requer-se a atribuição de efeitos infringentes," routinely used in practice, is dogmatically imprecise.

The paper grounds both theses in a key structural claim (§5.3): when embargos address a §489, §1º, IV violation, "não há nova cognição autônoma" — there is no new autonomous cognition, only completion of the existing decision's reasoning path.

---

## 2. Faithful Reconstruction

The paper's strongest form rests on a distinction between two types of judicial activity: *correcting the representation* of a decision (clarifying what was meant, completing what was omitted) versus *replacing the substance* of a decision (reaching a different judgment on the merits). Both theses, the paper argues, concern only the former.

**On Thesis 1:** Art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II, explicitly equates the art. 489, §1º conducts with omission for purposes of embargos de declaração: "Considera-se omissa a decisão que: ... II — incorra em qualquer das condutas descritas no art. 489, §1º." This is not a doctrinal inference but a direct textual command. The legislature chose to denominate art. 489, §1º, IV violations — failure to engage decisive arguments raised by the parties — as omissão for purposes of the clarification remedy. Under this reading, the question of whether the unaddressed argument concerned a merit issue is irrelevant: the mode (omission) is what triggers the remedy, not the matter.

The §5.3 response to the "third instance" objection is the heart of the defense: embargos address the *defect of the reasoning path* (the absence of engagement with an argument that was already before the court), not the merit question *de novo*. The decisive argument was raised by the parties in the original proceeding; it was before the court; the court was obligated to engage it; its failure to do so left the decision incomplete. Embargos require the court to complete what it was obligated to do — not to reach a new judgment as though the merits had not already been addressed.

**On Thesis 2:** Art. 1.024, §3º treats modification as the presupposition of the embargado's procedural rights ("caso o acolhimento dos embargos de declaração implique modificação"). By treating modification as the trigger for those rights — rather than as the object of a separate claim — the legislature signaled that modification flows automatically from granting embargos when the integration implies it. The §3º's protective mechanism for the embargado is positioned post-hoc precisely because modification is treated as an outcome of the court's integrative activity, not as an object of the embargante's petition. The traditional formula that requests infringement effects "subsidiarily" mistakenly treats the consequence as a separate claim and thereby subjects the substantive remedy to a formal requirement the statute does not impose.

## 3. The Attack

### 3.1 The Cognition Constraint

Embargos de declaração perform one of two cognitive functions, and the paper's both theses depend on conflating them.

**Recall cognition:** The court retrieves and makes explicit what its decision already, implicitly, decided. A dispositif that is obscure in its reach over a specific matter already decided that matter — the embargos only require the court to state explicitly what the implicit judgment was. A decision that identified a controlling ratio but failed to articulate it clearly already applied the ratio — the embargos require explicit articulation. In these cases, no new substantive judgment is formed; only the expression of an existing judgment is completed.

**Generative cognition:** The court reaches a substantive judgment it had not previously reached. An argument raised by a party that was never addressed — not implicitly, not structurally, not through the logical entailments of what was explicitly decided — has not been disposed of. When embargos require the court to address it, the court must form a substantive judgment about the argument for the first time.

The paper's defense of Thesis 1 relies on classifying art. 489, §1º, IV violations as triggering only recall cognition: the decisive argument was before the court, the court was obligated to address it, therefore addressing it in embargos merely completes the circuit of cognition already opened. But this classification is false for the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case. Consider what the court must do to address a previously unaddressed argument on the merits:

First, the court must determine whether the argument is indeed "capable of undermining the conclusion in principle" (em tese). This is not a factual question with a determinate answer; it is a legal judgment that requires evaluating the argument's quality, its relationship to the applicable precedents, and whether those precedents, if engaged, would require a different dispositif. This determination did not exist before the embargos — it is formed for the first time in response to them.

Second, the court must engage the argument substantively: weigh it against the considerations that supported the original conclusion, determine how it fits or fails to fit the relevant legal framework, and reach a position on its persuasiveness. This is new deliberation on the merits of the question, not retrieval of a judgment already made.

Third, the court must determine whether this new engagement changes the result. If the court concludes the argument is decisive, it reverses; if not, it affirms. This conclusion is not a consequence flowing automatically from the original decision — it depends on the outcome of the new deliberation just performed.

None of these three steps is "recall cognition." All three are "generative cognition": the court forms substantive judgments it had not previously formed. The paper's claim that "não há nova cognição autônoma" is false for this paradigmatic case.

### 3.2 The False Premise in the Scope Argument

The paper's textual argument for Thesis 1 — that art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II, equates art. 489, §1º violations with omissão — is correct as a matter of statutory construction. The legislature did classify art. 489, §1º conduct as omissão for embargos purposes. This establishes that embargos *can* be filed in response to such violations; it does not establish that embargos can *cure* them in the cognitively simple way the paper assumes.

A statute can denominate a set of facts as "omissão" for purposes of triggering a remedy without determining whether the remedy is curative in the integrative sense. The art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II classification extends embargos standing to art. 489, §1º, IV violations; it does not guarantee that the embargos remedy, when granted, will achieve its integrative function without generating new cognition. These are separate questions. The first (standing) the paper resolves correctly by textual analysis. The second (functional scope) the paper resolves incorrectly by assuming that "omissão" in the statutory sense tracks "omission" in the cognitively thin sense that recall cognition can supply.

The paper's illustration (§4) exposes this ambiguity. In the hypothetical, the court transcribed the STF precedent including its express reservation ("excluídos os especialistas em educação") and then reached a conclusion that included the category the reservation excludes. The paper characterizes this as an omissão (failure to engage the decisive argument about whether the party's original appointment as especialista triggers the reservation). But what does curing this "omission" require?

The court must now determine: (a) whether the post-legislative reenquadramento under state law altered the constitutional classification of the cargo; (b) if so, whether the constitutional classification for the merger-effect purpose in art. 37, XVI, "b," should track the original appointment or the current official designation; and (c) whether the STF's reservation phrase, read contextually, covers servants who were specialists at appointment but reclassified to teachers by state legislative act. These are substantive constitutional questions on which no prior court judgment exists in the case. Curing the "omission" requires generating the answers to these questions — genuine new cognition on the merits of a disputed constitutional classification.

### 3.3 The Implicit Rejection Problem

The paper's §5.3 response to the "third instance" objection rests on the premise that the decisive argument was "already before the court." This is true in the trivial sense (the argument was filed in the brief). But it elides a critical distinction: an argument can be *before* the court without having been *implicitly decided* by the court's reasoning.

Brazilian courts, including the STJ, have developed the doctrine of *rejeição implícita*: a court that reaches a conclusion that is logically inconsistent with an argument has implicitly engaged with and rejected that argument, even if the argument is not addressed explicitly. Under this doctrine, not every failure to mention a decisive argument is an "omissão" in the art. 1.022 sense. If the court's overall reasoning structure implicitly disposes of the argument — because accepting the argument would require rejecting a premise the court explicitly affirmed — the court has in effect reached a judgment on the argument, albeit without stating it.

The paper does not engage with the implicit rejection doctrine. This is significant because the doctrine defines the boundary between omissions that embargos can cure by recall cognition and omissions that embargos cannot cure without generative cognition. Omissions subject to implicit rejection are, by the doctrine's own logic, already decided — the court has a position, and embargos only need to make it explicit (recall cognition). Omissions that are not subject to implicit rejection — where the court's reasoning is genuinely agnostic or silent on the argument's merits — require the court to form a new position (generative cognition). The paper's thesis, if it is to avoid the cognition constraint, must be limited to the first category. The paper does not make this limitation.

### 3.4 The "Automatic Consequence" Claim Cannot Be Sustained

Thesis 2's claim that modification is automatic when cure "implies" it inherits the false premise from Thesis 1. If addressing the previously omitted argument requires generative cognition, then whether modification follows depends on the outcome of that new cognition — not on a logical implication from the decision's existing structure.

The paper's illustration again exposes the problem. After the embargos court addresses the omission about the STF reservation and the implicit contradiction, does modification follow "automatically"? Only if the court's new engagement with the precedent leads it to conclude that the party's reenquadramento does not alter the constitutional classification. That conclusion is not entailed by anything in the original decision; it depends on how the court reasons through the constitutional questions identified in §3.2 above. If the court concludes that post-legislative reenquadramento effects the constitutional classification, the original result stands. If not, it falls. The modification is not an automatic consequence of curing the defect; it is contingent on the outcome of the new merit reasoning the cure requires.

The paper's reading of art. 1.024, §3º as treating modification as a *pressuposto* (presupposition) is grammatically creative but structurally confused. The §3º says: "caso o acolhimento implique modificação." The word "caso" is a conditional, not a statement of sequence. The §3º's conditional grammar means: IF granting the embargos leads to modification, THEN the embargado has certain rights. The "if" is real. Modification is not presupposed; it is contingent. The legislature anticipated that granting embargos sometimes leads to modification and designed a procedural protection for that case — but did not mandate that granting embargos always, or automatically, leads to modification.

The paper's §3.4 tacitly acknowledges this: "Há casos em que os embargos são acolhidos para integrar a decisão sem que a integração implique modificação do dispositivo." If modification were truly automatic when cure implies it, and if the court's cognitive activity in granting embargos were purely recall rather than generative, there would be no cases of curing without modifying — the court would simply articulate the conclusion its reasoning already contained. The acknowledged existence of non-modifying grants confirms that the court's activity in addressing embargos includes genuine deliberation about what result the integration supports. That deliberation is new cognition; its outcome determines whether modification occurs. Neither is automatic.

## 4. Anticipated Reply and Why It Does Not Suffice

**The strongest available reply** would distinguish between two types of art. 489, §1º, IV violations:

*Type A:* Omissions where the argument, if addressed, would produce a determinate outcome under the court's already-articulated legal framework — cases where the court's existing reasoning, if extended to the unaddressed argument, would resolve it without the court forming a new substantive position. In these cases, the embargos remedy is indeed recall cognition: the court applies its existing framework to a point it failed to reach.

*Type B:* Omissions where the argument raises a question that the court's reasoning does not resolve — cases where addressing the argument requires the court to form a new position on a matter genuinely open under the applicable legal framework.

Under this reply, the paper's thesis would be defended as limited to Type A violations. The cognition constraint attack would be accepted for Type B; the paper would survive for Type A.

This reply does not suffice for two reasons.

First, the paper does not make this limitation. Its thesis is stated in general terms: mode, not matter, determines embargos' scope; art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II, extends the remedy to all art. 489, §1º violations. The paper's illustration is a Type B case — the constitutional classification question is not answered by anything in the original court's framework — and the paper presents it as a paradigm example of correct embargos application. The general thesis, as stated, does not limit itself to Type A.

Second, and more fundamentally, the Type A/Type B distinction is not administrable within the embargos framework. The decision whether a given omission is Type A or Type B is itself a substantive judgment about whether the court's existing framework determines the answer to the unaddressed argument. This judgment requires examining the existing reasoning, identifying its implications, and determining whether those implications resolve the argument. For the cases where this inquiry is non-trivial — precisely the cases where the argument was genuinely capable of undermining the conclusion — the inquiry is not recall cognition; it is generative cognition about the scope and implications of the existing reasoning. The Type A/Type B distinction smuggles generative cognition back in at the classification stage.

**The paper's textual argument** is the hardest to evade: art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II says what it says. The legislature denominated art. 489, §1º conduct as omissão. If the legislature intended to limit this classification to Type A omissions, it would have said so.

This argument establishes that embargos can *be filed* in response to all art. 489, §1º, IV violations. It does not establish that the embargos remedy — when granted for a Type B violation — achieves integration without generative cognition. Standing to file is distinct from the cognitive scope of the remedy when granted. A statute that extends standing to a remedy does not thereby transform the remedy's cognitive function. The legislature may have intended to make art. 489, §1º violations cognizable via embargos (as part of a broader project of enforcing the fundamentação standard through multiple procedural channels) without intending that embargos, when granted for such violations, would operate without any new substantive deliberation.

## 5. Scope of the Attack

This attack is directed at the claim in §5.3 that "não há nova cognição autônoma" when embargos address §489, §1º, IV violations, and at the related claim in the paper's Thesis 2 that modification is an automatic consequence of integration. The attack does not contest:

- The paper's textual analysis of art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II: the legislature did equate art. 489, §1º, IV conduct with omissão for embargos purposes. Standing to file embargos in response to such violations has textual support.
- The mode-not-matter rule as applied to cases that are genuinely within the recall cognition category: embargos can and should address formal omissions about merit questions where the court's existing framework determines the answer.
- The paper's identification of the traditional formula's problems: "subsidiariamente, requer-se a atribuição de efeitos infringentes" does create a structural ambiguity by treating modification as a separate claim. The paper correctly identifies this as a potential source of procedural confusion.
- The paper's description of STJ jurisprudence: the STJ has long admitted embargos for merit-based omissions and contradictions. The paper's characterization of this practice is accurate.
- The paper's overall project of calibrating embargos practice to the CPC 2015's text. The analysis of art. 1.022 and art. 1.024 is careful, and the proposed substitution of the traditional formula is procedurally defensible.

The attack shows that the paper's general thesis, as stated, overclaims by relying on a structural claim about cognition that is false for the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case the paper invokes. The attack is limited to this structural claim and its implications for the scope of Thesis 1 and the "automatic consequence" claim in Thesis 2.

---

## 6. Surrender Conditions

The attack would not hold, or would hold only partially, under the following conditions.

1. **Explicit limitation to recall-cognition omissions.** If the paper limited its Thesis 1 to omissions where the court's existing reasoning determines the answer to the unaddressed argument — making the extension of embargos to §489, §1º, IV violations contingent on the court's ability to resolve the omission by applying its own framework — the cognition constraint attack would be substantially answered. The limitation would require the paper to acknowledge that Type B violations (§3.2 above) are not curable by embargos in the straightforward integrative sense, and to revise its illustration accordingly.

2. **Engagement with the implicit rejection doctrine.** If the paper showed that the scope of §489, §1º, IV violations that trigger embargos coincides with violations where implicit rejection is not available — that is, cases where the argument was genuinely not addressed either explicitly or implicitly — the cognition constraint would be confined to the category of cases the paper already treats as outside the embargos remedy (pure error-of-judgment cases). This would require the paper to incorporate the implicit rejection doctrine as a limiting principle rather than ignoring it.

3. **Reformulation of Thesis 2 as contingent rather than automatic.** If the paper withdrew the claim that modification is "automatic consequence" and substituted a claim that modification *may* follow from integration when the court's new engagement with the omitted argument persuades it to a different result — and if the paper connected this contingency to the generative cognition required by Type B violations — the attack on Thesis 2 would be substantially addressed. The paper's practical proposal (substituting the traditional formula) could survive; only the theoretical characterization of modification as automatic would require revision.

4. **Demonstrated category of determinate-outcome omissions.** If the paper showed, with examples from Brazilian appellate practice, that a substantial and identifiable category of §489, §1º, IV violations produces determinate outcomes when the court applies its own framework — cases where the overlooked argument, engaged through the court's existing reasoning, can be resolved without new substantive deliberation — this would empirically narrow the cognition constraint. The attack would remain for the residual category (Type B violations) but would not affect the paper's central practical application.
