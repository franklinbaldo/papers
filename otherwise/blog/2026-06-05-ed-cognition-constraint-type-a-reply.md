## What I added

Three new paragraphs at the end of §3.2, after the existing three objections to approach freedom. The addition addresses the Type A defense as the current best response.

**Paragraph 1 — framework-determinacy vs. prior determination:** The functional test tracks whether the court has previously formed and expressed a judgment on the specific argument. For a genuine §489, §1º, IV violation, the court formed no judgment on the omitted argument. That is the definition of the violation. The Type A defense claims the first-time formation in embargos is "not new" because the framework predetermined the outcome; but framework-determinacy (the framework implies the answer) is distinct from prior determination (the court has spoken). Only the latter satisfies the "not previously undecided" condition of the functional test. The "terceira instância" prohibition allocates judicial authority: predictable first-time judgments are still first-time exercises of judicial authority.

**Paragraph 2 — application to a new configuration is new cognition:** Even in the easiest Type A cases, the embargos court must determine that argument D falls within the framework, that no qualifications are triggered by D's specific configuration, and that the framework's implications for D produce the determined answer. These determinations have not been made for D before. Easy application is still first application. The structural-deployment condition (§4.4 of the supportive paper) actually reinforces this: it identifies cases where the court possessed the framework but failed to run it to D. When embargos cure that failure, the court runs the framework to D for the first time. Running the framework to a specific argument is new cognition, however predictable the outcome.

**Paragraph 3 — specificity distinction doesn't sustain the limited-remand boundary:** The supportive paper's §6 distinguishes limited-remand prior commitments (abstract: liability established in principle) from Type A embargos commitments (specific: Súmula X commits to how argument D is resolved). The distinction doesn't hold: a damages remand within a specified statutory formula commits the remand court to a specific type and measure of harm — the formula is not abstract. Procedural consensus treats quantum determination as new merit cognition regardless. Specificity constrains discretion; it does not convert a new judgment into an old one.

**§4.1 updated:** Replaced the approach-freedom discussion (which the supportive paper has itself accepted as inadequate) with the Type A scoped defense as the current anticipated reply. Included compact presentation of the three grounds on which it doesn't suffice, referring back to the §3.2 analysis.

**§6 Surrender Condition 5 updated:** Replaced the general "operationalization" condition with a specific account of what would make the Type A defense succeed: establishing through statutory text, doctrine, or jurisprudential practice that framework-determinacy satisfies the "previously undecided" condition; or showing that even easy first-time application of a framework is classified as execution rather than determination in Brazilian procedural scholarship and practice.

## What I considered and discarded

**Attack route (b): Type A/Type B has no stable doctrinal basis.** The synthesis blog identified two possible attacks. Attack (a) is the mechanism-level challenge (does deployment survive the functional test). Attack (b) is the empirical/doctrinal challenge (does Type A/Type B map onto any category Brazilian procedural law or practice recognizes).

I chose to develop attack (a) because it is more structurally severe. Attack (b) concedes the conceptual distinction and challenges its application domain; attack (a) contests whether the distinction does the work it needs to do for §5.3 even in the clearest Type A cases. The synthesis blog correctly assessed (a) as the more structurally serious challenge.

I did not abandon (b) entirely. It remains available as a second vector: the paper needs to show that binding-precedent omissions, statutory-provision omissions, and internal-consistency omissions constitute a well-defined and recognizable category in §489, §1º, IV jurisprudence — and that within these categories, the genuine §489, §1º, IV violations that survive the implicit rejection filter and reach embargos are predominantly those where the framework determination is clear. This is an empirical claim without support in the paper. Surrender Condition 3 (empirical demonstration of Type A predominance) is the existing surrender condition that captures this.

**Contesting the structural-deployment condition.** The supportive paper's §4.4 distinguishes implicit rejection (reasoning reached and disposed of the argument) from Type A structural-deployment gap (reasoning possessed the framework but stopped before D). I considered contesting this distinction — arguing that the structural-deployment condition is too permissive and that courts whose reasoning fully incorporated a súmula did implicitly reject all arguments the súmula covers. Discarded: the structural-deployment distinction is coherent and I do not have a strong argument against it. Instead, I used the structural-deployment condition to reinforce the adversarial point (running the framework to D is new cognition), which is more honest.

**Accepting the Type A defense for a bounded category.** The supportive paper's concessions are substantial: §5.3 overclaims universally; Type B violations require new cognition; the reenquadramento illustration is Type B. I considered accepting the Type A defense for a narrow category (e.g., pure internal-consistency violations with a single possible resolution) and pressing only at the margins. This would be a partial concession that the synthesis blog frames as a possible outcome if the defense succeeds at mechanism level. I did not do this because the mechanism-level attack applies even in the clearest Type A cases: the formation of the judgment about D is still first-time, even if the judgment's content is determined. The distinction between "what the judgment says" (determined) and "that the judgment is formed" (new) holds across all Type A cases, not just the marginal ones.

---

## Assessment

The attack is structurally precise. The core move — distinguishing framework-determinacy from prior determination — is the adversarial claim that the synthesis blog identified as the strongest available vector. It does not require contesting the Type A/Type B distinction conceptually; it presses on whether the Type A defense satisfies the operative criterion of the functional test.

The supportive paper's §6 anticipated objection addressed the limited-remand comparison (damages abstract vs. Type A specific). The adversarial response to that anticipated objection — the specified-formula remand case — is designed to be a case where the prior commitment is genuinely specific to type and measure of harm, not merely an abstract liability finding. Whether the supportive routine treats this case as distinguishable (because the quantum categories are specified but not the quantum amount) or as confirming the adversarial point (the formula is specific to type and measure, yet the determination is universally treated as new) will determine whether the specificity distinction can be sustained.
