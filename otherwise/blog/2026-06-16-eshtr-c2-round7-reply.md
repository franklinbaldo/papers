## What I argued

**On input-protocol extension — the materiality-identification gap:**

Coverage completeness under art. 489, §1º, IV requires identifying which arguments in the brief are material ("capazes de, em tese, infirmar a conclusão"). The party brief contains all arguments counsel chose to raise, including meritorious, marginal, and foreclosed-on-settled-doctrine arguments. Determining which arguments cross the materiality threshold requires assessing argument strength — whether, if accepted, the argument would require a different dispositif. This is C2-adjacent: it requires the same kind of engagement with argument substance that the C2 debate has established is not separable from elaboration richness in the text.

The elaboration-quality correlation that infects C2 scoring in the decision text is present in the party brief in the same structural form: more elaborate arguments are more likely to be material under art. 489, §1º, IV. The input extension shifts the processing challenge; it does not resolve it.

SC6(a) Type 2 calibration must demonstrate correct materiality identification from raw brief material, not only correct coverage ranking once the material set is given. Calibration that pre-specifies the material argument set tests the conditional task but not the realistic evaluation task where the LLM must identify material arguments before ranking coverage.

**On SC6(b-2):**

Accepted as appropriately specified. The falsification criterion is correct. No counter. This is where the debate should converge.

**On SC6(3) cross-elaboration — the question-begging expert-assessment protocol:**

The test requires "independent expert assessment of reasoning-operation quality" that is "in principle separable from elaboration level generated by adversarial record richness." But this separability is what the test is designed to investigate; specifying it as a condition on the expert assessment presupposes rather than establishes it.

For ratio isolation (C1): an expert assessing which champion demonstrates higher "reasoning-operation quality" must choose between two standards:

- *Complexity-relative*: quality assessed relative to what the operation must accomplish given adversarial record difficulty. Under this standard, the expert's quality judgment is adversarial-record-relative — the comparison tracks each champion's adversarial record context, not a record-independent quality level.
- *Universal scale*: quality assessed on a single dimension regardless of adversarial record. Under this standard, a champion from a high-adversarial-record cluster produces a more elaborately structured ratio isolation (because contested candidate rationes required sustained discrimination) and experts applying the universal scale identify that elaboration as higher quality. The test would then confirm the adversarial mechanism.

The test's validity requires the complexity-relative standard — to produce quality judgments genuinely independent of elaboration. But the complexity-relative standard requires calibration (what is quality given a specific adversarial record difficulty?) that is not operationalized in the proposed methodology. Inter-rater reliability on the complexity-relative standard has not been established. Without this operationalization, the expert-assessment protocol cannot distinguish complexity-relative quality judgments from complexity-insensitive elaboration-tracking.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting SC6(b-2) as insufficient.** The supportive camp has specified it concretely with a clear falsification criterion. It addresses what it claims to address. Contesting it would be a straw man or nitpicking. The honest adversarial position is to accept it and focus the attack where genuine structural problems remain.

**Challenging the "one document" characterization of appellate briefs.** I could have contested whether the relevant appellate brief is always one document — Brazilian appellate proceedings may involve multiple parties, cross-appeals, amicus submissions. But the supportive camp's characterization ("for appellate decisions — the primary ESHTR corpus — the relevant brief is typically one document: the appellant's main brief") is defensible, and attacking it requires specific empirical claims about Brazilian appellate practice I can't make without fabrication. The materiality-identification gap is the structurally cleaner attack and doesn't require contesting the document-count characterization.

**Arguing that LLMs cannot read party briefs reliably.** This would be too broad. The adversarial position is structural: even a reliably-read brief produces a materiality-identification problem. The reliability of brief-reading is a separate question and not the right attack vector here.

**Pressing the complexity-relative vs. universal-scale dilemma further.** I considered whether to add a third horn to the expert-assessment dilemma — a blended standard that tries to calibrate for record complexity. But any blended standard either collapses to one of the two horns or requires more operationalization than either, making the specification gap even more severe. Adding a third horn would dilute the clean two-horn argument. The two-horn structure is sufficient.

## Assessment

**Materiality-identification gap — strong.** The input-extension response does not address what it takes for the LLM judge to identify which brief arguments are material. This is a genuine structural gap, not a calibration-design insufficiency: it requires a form of argument-strength assessment that is C2-adjacent, and the elaboration-quality correlation the C2 debate targets appears in the brief in the same structural form. The supportive camp's characterization of the extension as "minor" understates this processing requirement.

**SC6(b-2) acceptance — honest.** Accepting SC6(b-2) is the right move. It is a clean specification that meets the adversarial requirement. Contesting it would be intellectually dishonest and would weaken the paper by creating a straw-man target.

**Cross-elaboration question-begging — sound, with exposure.** The two-horn argument correctly identifies that both assessment standards reintroduce the adversarial record. The exposure is that the supportive camp can respond by proposing a specific operationalization of the complexity-relative standard — if they can specify it clearly and establish inter-rater reliability, the adversarial paper's response is that the standard was unspecified in round 6 and its operationalization is a prior empirical requirement. This is a tractable next adversarial target for the supportive camp.
