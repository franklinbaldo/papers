## Attack chosen

Paper 1B advances five theses of varying strength:
1. Exit 1–3 and 5 are well-grounded textually and doctrinally.
2. Exit 4 (rational supersession by inferior courts) is the paper's most novel and most
   fragile claim.
3. Silent partial application as autonomous defect (§§5.1–5.3) is solid but rests on a
   contested theory of ratio identification.
4. The taxonomy structure has a coherence problem around Exit 5 vs. afastamento silencioso.

I chose to attack Exit 4 as the primary target and Exit 5 coherence as secondary. Both
concern the paper's most distinctive contributions; neither is anticipated in a form
that the paper's §7 objections adequately address.

**Why Exit 4 specifically:** The paper's most innovative claim is that inferior courts
are authorized to supersede STF binding precedents through qualified argument identifying
error in the ratio. This is contested by Marinoni and Mitidiero themselves (acknowledged
in §7.2) and runs against the CPC 2015's hierarchical structure. The paper derives this
authorization from art. 489, §1º, VI (procedural form requirement) and the rational
binding theory — but neither source establishes substantive authorization. This is an
inference error that the paper's own responses (§7.1-7.2) partially recognize but do
not resolve.

**Why not attack §5.3 (silent partial application):** The §5.3 attack would require
contesting the paper's theory of ratio identification — arguing that qualifications may
be obiter rather than ratio under the Goodhart conception. The paper anticipates this
(§7.5) with a defensible response drawing on Cross/Harris and MacCormick. The attack
on §5.3 would be engaging with territory where the paper is stronger and more prepared.
Exit 4 is weaker and less defended.

**Why not attack Exits 1–3:** They're correct. Attacking defensible positions is noise.

## What I argued

**Primary vector — inference error (§3.1):**

Art. 489, §1º, VI establishes a formal sufficiency condition for departures: a decision
is "not considered reasoned" if it fails to demonstrate distinction or supersession. The
paper reads this as authorizing inferior courts to supersede STF precedents when they
demonstrate qualified argument. But the provision's operative logic is negative: it
defines what makes a departure *unfounded*. Contraposition: a departure with
demonstration is not unfounded. But formal validity (the decision is reasoned under
art. 489) ≠ substantive authority (the court is within its jurisdictional power). Art.
489, §1º, VI establishes the first; the authorization to supersede STF precedents would
require the second. The provision tells us *how* to depart; it does not tell us *that*
inferior courts may depart.

Art. 927 uses mandatory language ("observarão") that does not attenuate when the
observing court has articulated a qualified objection to the observed precedent. The
binding force of art. 927 precedents persists until the STF modifies them.

**Secondary vector — rational binding theory doesn't supply authorization (§3.2):**

Marinoni's formula ("razão que não pode ser desconsiderada sem razão mais forte") is a
theory about what makes precedential force *legitimate*; it is not a decision procedure
for who determines when the force lapses. Even granting that defective rationes have
attenuated force, the assessment of when the attenuation threshold is crossed requires
an authoritative arbiter. Under the CPC 2015 + CF/88, that arbiter is the STF. The
Raz service-conception argument (§4.2 of Paper 1B) actually counsels deference to
authority in individual cases precisely because systematic benefits of stable authority
exceed costs of individual errors that the authority's own mechanisms are designed
to correct.

**Third vector — constitutional monopoly (§3.3):**

Art. 102 CF vests the STF with "guarda da Constituição." For constitutional binding
precedents — STF decisions in concentrated control, binding súmulas — an inferior court
exercising "rational supersession" is exercising an interpretive function the CF/88
reserves to the STF. No CPC provision can modify this constitutional allocation.
Art. 489, §1º, VI is a procedural provision; it cannot authorize what the Constitution
prohibits. The paper's §7 objections do not engage with the constitutional dimension.

**Fourth vector — anticipatory overruling is predictive, not authoritative (§3.4):**

The Anglo-American anticipatory overruling is a strategic prediction about what the
supreme court will hold, not an assertion of the intermediate court's own rational
authority. Paper 1B converts it into something different: the inferior court departing
on the strength of its own assessment of the ratio's quality. This requires the
substantive authority claim that anticipatory overruling avoids. The paper invokes the
comparative concept but relies on a stronger version of it.

**Fifth vector — Exit 5 coherence problem (§3.5):**

The paper defends Exit 5 (legitimate abstention from invocation) and condemns
afastamento silencioso. But Exit 5's legitimacy — non-invocation triggers no
precedent-following obligation — provides a permanent formal escape from binding
precedent: any court can find alternative grounds and avoid the inconvenient precedent
without invoking it. The boundary between legitimate Exit 5 and strategic afastamento
silencioso requires a principled criterion the paper doesn't provide.

## What I considered and discarded

**Attacking §5.3 (silent partial application):** The Goodhart objection is anticipated
in §7.5 and the response (Cross/Harris, MacCormick, necessity criterion) is defensible.
The attack would require contesting the theory of ratio, not the inference from ratio to
defect. Less clean and less likely to survive.

**Attacking the "five" count:** Exits 1-3 are genuine; Exit 5 is a legitimate
clarification (don't invoke inapplicable precedents). The count criticism is more
rhetorical than substantive. The real innovation is Exit 4; attacking the count is
peripheral.

**Attacking Marinoni/Mitidiero on their own terms:** Both authors hold views in tension
with Exit 4 (the paper concedes this in §7.2). But their authority for Exit 4 is
already contested by their own works. An attack on their internal consistency would
be a scholarly dispute that doesn't land directly on the paper's thesis.

**Attacking the hypothetical illustration (§6):** The illustration is convincing for
silent partial application (§5), not for Exit 4. Using the illustration to attack §5
would require contesting whether the STF's qualification was ratio or obiter in that
specific case — too fact-specific.

## Surrender conditions set

1. A specific legal provision or constitutional doctrine authorizing inferior courts to
   supersede STF binding precedents through qualified argument is identified.
2. The constitutional monopoly argument is accepted for constitutional precedents but
   the attack is shown not to reach STJ infraconstitutional precedents.
3. A principled criterion distinguishing Exit 5 from strategic afastamento silencioso
   is articulated.
4. Exit 4 is recharacterized as anticipatory (predictive, not authoritative) — which
   would resolve §3.4 at the cost of substantially narrowing the original claim.
5. Empirical evidence that rational supersession increases STF reconsideration rates
   (shifting the debate to whether authorization should be recognized, not whether
   it currently exists).

## Assessment

Exit 4 is the paper's most vulnerable claim. The inference from procedural sufficiency
condition to substantive authorization is a logical gap the paper does not close, and
the constitutional dimension (art. 102 CF) is not engaged at all in the objections
section. The Exit 5 coherence problem is a secondary attack that can survive
independently of the primary.

The anticipated reply in §4 is the hardest case: if Exit 4 is "merely" procedurally
articulated disobedience that the inferior court knows will be reversed, then calling
it a "legitimate exit" is a mislabeling, not a false claim about substantive authority.
The paper might respond that "legitimate" means "procedurally valid" rather than
"substantively authorized." But this reading makes Exit 4 a very small claim: the court
may depart if it reasons expressly — which is just a restatement of art. 489's general
requirement. The paper presents Exit 4 as a genuine contribution to the taxonomy;
reducing it to a formal requirement would eliminate the contribution.

This is a genuine attack, not a straw man.
