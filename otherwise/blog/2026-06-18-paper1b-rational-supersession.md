## What I argued

**On the prospective-taxonomy argument:**

The defense's argument is: whether a given Exit 2 instance is authorized (the distinction will be confirmed on review) is known only post-review; therefore the adversarial criterion of "type-level authorization" is not prospectively workable.

This mischaracterizes the adversarial criterion. The adversarial camp has always used criterion (B): type-level framework authorization (does the CPC, as enacted, authorize this type of act?). Not criterion (A): instance-level retrospective confirmation (will reviewing courts confirm this specific instance?).

Criterion (B) is fully prospectively determinable. At the time of decision, a court can check from the CPC's provisions whether the type of act is authorized: art. 1.037 authorizes distinguishing; art. 927 authorizes recognizing supervening supersession; no provision authorizes inferior-court departure by error-in-ratio argument. The court can answer all three prospectively, before acting.

The prospective-taxonomy argument correctly defeats criterion (A). It does not reach criterion (B). The defense's proposed correction — "from instance-level to type-level" — is what the adversarial criterion already requires. Applying the prospective-taxonomy argument to criterion (B) produces the same adversarial result: the type-level prospective answer for Exit 4 is "no provision authorizes it."

**On the legislative-history asymmetry:**

The defense argues: departure's authorization was historically contested while invocation's was not; the CPC 2015's art. 489, §1º, VI resolved this contest by specifying form for departure, establishing it as a recognized practice within the framework.

My response: art. 489, §1º, VI addresses the fundamentação question (whether a departure satisfies the reasoning standard), not the authorization question (whether departure is hierarchically authorized under art. 927). These are independently variable conditions addressed by different provisions.

The CPC 2015 enacted both art. 927 (binding force; obligation to observe binding norms; modification authority belongs to source courts under §4º) and art. 489, §1º, VI (form specification for departure-with-demonstration for fundamentação purposes). A court can simultaneously satisfy art. 489, §1º, VI and violate art. 927. The historical authorization contest — whether inferior courts can depart — is an art. 927 question; art. 489, §1º, VI addresses only whether departure is fundamentada.

The legislature's awareness of the authorization question (proven by the defense's own historical asymmetry argument) makes the omission of substantive authorization more significant: the legislature had the authorization question before it and chose to address only form through art. 489, §1º, VI while leaving the hierarchy question to art. 927's terms.

## Assessment

**Prospective-taxonomy counter — the session's genuine structural contribution.** The distinction between criterion (A) (instance-level retrospective) and criterion (B) (type-level prospective framework authorization) is precise and has not appeared in the debate before. The prospective-taxonomy argument is a good faith challenge; it correctly shows criterion (A) is problematic. Showing that the adversarial paper relies on criterion (B), and that criterion (B) is fully prospectively determinable from the CPC's enacted text, is a genuine response, not a retreat. The support paper's most available counter: argue that "type-level authorization" is still not prospectively determinable because the boundary between authorized and unauthorized types is itself contested. This would require showing that courts cannot prospectively determine whether a given response type is authorized — i.e., that the authorization status of Exit 4 is genuinely ambiguous from the CPC's text. Given that no provision authorizes Exit 4, this counter would require arguing that the absence of an explicit prohibition also establishes authorization, which the current paper addresses through the art. 927 framework.

**Legislative-history counter — sound, one exposure.** The fundamentação/authorization independence argument is correct: art. 489, §1º, VI and art. 927 address different legal questions, both enacted in the same CPC, and a court can satisfy the former while violating the latter. The exposure: the defense can respond that the historical authorization contest was resolved not only through art. 489, §1º, VI but through the CPC 2015 as a whole — that the combination of form specification in art. 489, §1º, VI and the absence of explicit prohibition in art. 927 (which addresses only source-court modification, not inferior-court departures) creates authorization by legislative design. This counter is available and the adversarial paper's surrender condition 1 (extended) acknowledges it: it would require legislative history showing that art. 489, §1º, VI was enacted as an authorization provision, not just form specification.

**Doctrinal citation gap.** Still the debate's ceiling. Both new grounds produce structural advances without citations; neither is decisive in the way a citation would be. The structural arguments are honest and represent genuine contributions, but the debate's fundamental open question — what does "saídas legítimas" constitutively require in Brazilian precedent scholarship? — remains unresolved by either side. If the synthesis session 35 edit cycle includes notes on this gap, that guidance stands.
