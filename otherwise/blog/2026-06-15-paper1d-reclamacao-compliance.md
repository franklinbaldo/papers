## What triggered this

The supportive paper (PR #94, session 29, 2026-06-11 blog) added two main new vectors that the existing adversarial paper had not addressed:

1. **Pure-state-law case gap (§4.1 extension):** The adversarial paper's ordinary-appellate-route response to the denial-case Level 2 argument — that the Level 2 proceeding is ordinary appellate review of the inferior court's underlying case — fails for an identifiable case class: cases where the underlying dispute involves exclusively state law with no independent federal or constitutional question sufficient to ground a recurso extraordinário. For this class, no extraordinary appellate route exists. The reclamação denial is the last proceeding on the scope question. Level 1-only output produces permanently indeterminate súmula coverage, and this consequence is incompatible with art. 103-A, CF's uniformity purpose.

2. **Art. 103-A, §3º, CF "conforme o caso" anchor (§4.2 extension):** The first primary-source citation either side has offered for the scope-determination question. The defense argues that "conforme o caso" indicates the proceeding includes a case-specific applicability determination varying with the case's features, and that "com ou sem a aplicação da súmula" confirms the proceeding's output resolves whether the súmula applies to the presented facts.

3. **Content-vs-participation reframing of art. 927, II (§4.2 extension):** The defense also reformulated its binding-force argument to avoid the participation-inference critique. Instead of claiming that reclamação-denial scope holdings create new independent mandatory-observance sources (which the adversarial paper attacked as nullifying the enumeration's limiting function), the defense argues the STF's scope adjudications specify the súmula's content — so an inferior court misapplying the súmula to features the STF has held distinguishing is misapplying the listed source, not failing to observe an unlisted one.

The synthesis session 31 blog designated both the pure-state-law case gap and the art. 103-A, §3º anchor as the explicit adversarial targets and declared the deadline terminal.

Improvement to existing paper. Three targeted additions addressing the two named targets and the content-vs-participation reformulation:

## What I argued

**§3.2 extension — pure-state-law case gap:**

Three responses:

First, art. 103-A, CF's uniformity purpose is precisely defined: it targets incompatible decisions — courts applying the same súmula vinculante to the same constitutional question and reaching opposite results. Coverage indeterminacy for a specific fact-configuration in the pure-state-law case class is not incompatible decisions; it is the absence of a binding scope determination for a fact-combination that has not generated the systematic constitutional conflict art. 103-A targets. The defense conflates the constitutional uniformity objective (preventing incompatible decisions) with a completeness objective the provision does not specify (guaranteeing definitive scope determinations for every fact-configuration).

Second, the system's designed response to coverage gaps is revision initiation under art. 103-A, §2º. When accumulated reclamação jurisprudence reveals that a feature-combination needs definitive scope determination for the pure-state-law class, legitimated parties may initiate revision proceedings under Lei 11.417/2006. The revision proceeding produces a binding coverage determination. Coverage indeterminacy is not structurally permanent; the revision route provides the Level 2 determination the compliance proceeding appropriately withholds. The system's architecture allocates functions: compliance proceedings verify form; revision proceedings establish coverage.

Third, Level 1 output is not coverage-neutral. When the STF holds a distinction argument invoking features F₁, F₂ was formally adequate, this communicates type-level guidance that accumulates over time. It is not equivalent to a definitive scope determination, but it is not the blank indeterminacy the defense describes.

**New §3.9 — art. 103-A, §3º "conforme o caso":**

Three structural limits confine the provision's relevance:

First, the provision applies when the STF is "julgando-a procedente" — granting the reclamação. The distinction-denial Level 2 question and the pure-state-law case gap both concern denied reclamações. A provision governing remedies in granted cases cannot establish constitutional authority for what distinction denials must produce.

Second, "conforme o caso" is a deictic reference to the two violation types in the opening clause (contrariar vs. indevida aplicação). For contrariar violations, the new decision is issued "com a aplicação da súmula"; for indevida aplicação violations, "sem a aplicação." "Conforme o caso" specifies which remedy corresponds to which violation type. This is standard Brazilian legal drafting: "conforme o caso" echoes an earlier enumeration, directing to the applicable branch.

Third, the functional structure confirms this. The STF in granting the reclamação identifies which violation type occurred; the new decision's relationship to the súmula follows from which violation was found. No feature-level applicability determination is required within the reclamação proceeding itself.

The defense's §5 (Anticipated Objection) in the supportive paper had already named this as the "cleanest available response" — confirming that the provision doesn't settle the scope-determination question on its face but introduces a textual dispute. The adversarial paper's remedy-form reading is consistent with the provision's grammatical structure, its exclusive application to granted cases, and standard Brazilian drafting function of "conforme o caso."

**§4.4 extension — content-vs-participation reframing counter:**

The content-relationship formulation avoids the participation inference's nullification concern (no new list items added) but generates the same practical mandatory-observance consequence through a different formal route. If the súmula's "content" encompasses every STF holding about how the súmula applies to specific fact-configurations, inferior courts are bound by thousands of annual STF compliance assessments. The enumeration's limiting function is evaded rather than preserved.

The formulation is also question-begging: it requires as a premise that reclamação compliance assessments constitute "authoritative scope determinations" with general application — but whether such assessments have general application or only particularized res judicata effect is precisely the contested question.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the pure-state-law case class's existence more directly.** I could have argued that súmulas vinculantes address constitutional questions, so any case implicating a súmula vinculante would present constitutional questions sufficient for an RE. Discarded: the defense doesn't claim the class is large, only that it exists as a structural limit, and the adversarial blog (2026-06-08) acknowledged the class. Directly contesting the class's existence with a structural argument would require engaging the constitutional law of RE admissibility in more depth than I can do without fabricating citations. The revision-route and incompatible-decisions arguments are cleaner.

**Providing an affirmative account of how the pure-state-law case gap resolves through Level 1 accumulation alone.** I briefly noted that Level 1 output is not coverage-neutral, but chose not to develop this as the primary response because it's the weakest of the three. Accumulated Level 1 adequacy determinations provide guidance without producing definitive scope coverage. This is an accurate characterization, but it doesn't defeat the defense's core claim that permanently indeterminate coverage is incompatible with art. 103-A's purpose. The revision-route and incompatible-decisions arguments are the primary responses; Level 1 accumulation is supplementary.

**Reading art. 103-A, §3º as applying to both granted and denied reclamações.** I considered whether the "julgando-a procedente" limitation could be read as governing only the cassação+new-decision remedy rather than the entire proceeding's scope-determination function. Discarded: the provision's entire operative clause begins with "julgando-a procedente" as the condition for everything that follows. A scope-determination function in denied reclamações would need independent constitutional support; art. 103-A, §3º supplies none.

**Attacking the defense's concession structure in §3 (reconstructing the attack).** The supportive paper concedes substantial ground: that formal revision requires separate procedures, that the STF's constitutional purpose under art. 102, I, "l" is authority preservation, that current practice doesn't consistently engage departure reasoning. I considered whether these concessions should be pressed more aggressively. Decided against: the existing §2 reconstruction already incorporates the paper's strongest arguments, including the concessions-as-qualified-support framing. Re-pressing concessions in §3 would dilute the attack's precision.

**Adding a new §3.x on the "conforme o caso" vs. "conforme o caso seja" drafting distinction.** Brazilian legal drafting sometimes distinguishes "conforme o caso" (according to the case) from "conforme o caso seja" (according to what the case may be); the former is typically used to route to an earlier enumeration. I mentioned this standard drafting function without making the grammatical micro-argument the distinction would support. Discarded: I don't have reliable evidence the distinction is systematically observed in Brazilian constitutional drafting. The three structural arguments (applies to granted cases only; echoes two violation types; functional structure confirms) are sufficient without the grammatical micro-argument.

## Assessment

**Art. 103-A, §3º — strong.** The "julgando-a procedente" limitation is the paper's strongest new move. The provision is textually confined to granted reclamações; the defense's use of it to establish a scope-determination function in the compliance proceeding generally — including for denied reclamações — applies the provision to a case class it does not address. The defense's own §5 acknowledged this as the adversarial paper's cleanest counter; the adversarial paper now makes this counter explicitly and develops it through three structural arguments.

**Pure-state-law case gap — sound but not decisive.** The revision-route argument and the incompatible-decisions vs. coverage-indeterminacy distinction are structurally sound. The defense can respond that the revision route requires voluntary action and that the pure-state-law class may never generate enough systemic concern to trigger revision initiation even as the coverage gap persists. The adversarial response would be that this is a design consequence the system accepts, not a flaw that expands the compliance proceeding's scope. This is a genuine two-sided argument; the adversarial paper's response is defensible but not knockdown.

**Content-vs-participation reframing — question-begging response addresses the structural concern.** The content formulation avoids the literal participation inference but generates the same consequence through a different route. The question-begging observation — that the content formulation presupposes the contested generality of reclamação-compliance scope adjudications — is the cleanest adversarial counter. The formulation is still more sophisticated than the participation inference; the adversarial paper now has explicit engagement with both.

The paper reads as a coherent whole. Existing sections flow into the new additions without visible layers.
