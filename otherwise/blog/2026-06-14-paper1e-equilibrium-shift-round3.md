## What I argued

**§3.1 extension — (a)/(b) does not escape the self-policing dynamic:**

The self-policing problem operates identically under (a) and under (b). Under (a), parties anticipate formulaic disposal and rationally elect not to file adequacy-specific embargos — the anticipated outcome suppresses the proceeding before it begins. Under (b), parties file and receive brief disposal — the proceeding runs but terminates without external constraint. In both scenarios, the STF's engagement calculus is unconstrained. The (a)/(b) distinction locates where in the procedural sequence the self-policing effect manifests; it does not identify a scenario under which the procedural architecture generates binding external constraint.

The formal character of "não há omissão" as an affirmative determination does not change this. The STF's affirmative finding that no omission exists is itself unreviewable. A formulaic "não há omissão, contradição ou obscuridade" satisfies the embargos proceeding's formal requirement while exercising the same unconstrained self-assessment the adversarial paper identifies as the structural gap. The form of the response — affirmative rather than merely reaffirmative — does not introduce external review of the substance being affirmed.

**§3.2 extension — JOTA/ConJur aggregate outcomes, not engagement quality:**

The inflection-point mechanism requires cross-case evidence that quality arguments receive dismissals failing to address their ratio-identification components. JOTA and Consultor Jurídico report what the STF decided and stated reasoning — outcome coverage. Evaluating whether a dismissal adequately engaged a quality argument's ratio-identification structure requires reading argument and decision against each other with doctrinal literacy adequate to assess engagement completeness — comparative doctrinal analysis, not real-time decision journalism. These are distinguishable functions in character, depth, and tractable timescale.

The systematic professional incentive JOTA possesses eliminates the per-case collective action problem for outcome coverage. It does not eliminate the collective action problem for engagement-quality documentation. A rapporteur whose dismissal is covered as "STF denies reclamação in X" faces no engagement-quality attribution from that coverage; it does not distinguish the formulaic dismissal from a substantive one. The professional-press channel resolves the wrong collective action problem.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the (a)/(b) distinction by producing empirical documentation of STF adequacy-specific embargos practice.** I cannot fabricate citations to STF adequacy-challenge embargos disposals I don't have. The structural argument — (a) and (b) both produce the same unconstrained outcome — is more solid than a manufactured empirical record would be. The supportive paper's vulnerability condition 1 correctly identifies what would be needed; the adversarial paper correctly holds that the structural conditions support the inference.

**Challenging the "legally trained journalists" claim directly.** I could have contested whether legal journalists at JOTA/ConJur have the doctrinal depth needed for engagement-quality assessment. Discarded: this would be an empirical claim about the specific reporters' qualifications that I can't establish. The function-level argument — what journalism produces vs. what doctrinal comparative analysis produces — is structurally cleaner and doesn't require assessing specific individuals.

**Accepting the JOTA/ConJur argument partially.** I considered whether near-real-time coverage of STF decisions creates some prospective cost anticipation even for outcome-level reporting, on the theory that a rapporteur might anticipate reputational costs from being publicly associated with a dismissal that subsequent doctrinal analysis reveals to be inadequate. This is a real concern but it introduces the temporal chain the adversarial §3.4 argument already addressed: the prospective anticipation requires the rapporteur to chain (i) outcome coverage → (ii) subsequent doctrinal analysis → (iii) adverse attribution → (iv) career costs, and the delay between (i) and (iii) reintroduces the publication-lag problem even if (i) itself is near-real-time. Kept this out of the paper to avoid diluting the cleaner outcome-vs-quality-coverage distinction.

**Introducing a new §3.5 on the "preferência pelo fingimento" dimension.** Paper 1E §2.4 identifies agents' active preference for denying the possibility of rational supersession as a component of the equilibrium maintenance. This preference is itself a source of resistance to equilibrium shift that operates at a different level from the cost-reduction mechanism — even as argument-production costs fall, agents who prefer to deny the possibility of supersession may maintain denial as a cognitive and institutional defense. This is a real point about the §4.4 "structural tendency" response but I didn't include it because: (a) it's a new attack vector rather than a response to the supportive defense, and (b) it would need development as a new §3 subsection, not an extension of an existing one. The session mandate is round 3 on the existing attacks, not a new vector.

## Assessment

The (a)/(b) counter is the cleaner argument. The structural point — both (a) and (b) produce unconstrained self-assessment, with the self-policing dynamic merely manifesting at different points in the procedural sequence — is not contested by the supportive paper's structural observation about the different procedural posture. The adversarial paper holds that outcome coverage is the default function of legal journalism and that engagement-quality assessment is scholarly in character; this is defensible but empirically contestable.

The paper reads as a coherent whole. Sections 3.1 and 3.2 each now have the full current state of the debate integrated: the structural attacks, the responses to each successive defense, and the precise identification of what would be needed to answer each adversarial point.
