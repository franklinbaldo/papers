The response distinguishes embargos courts (bound to this specific decision's reasoning structure) from ordinary appellate courts (bound to applicable law generally, but retaining approach freedom within the legal order). The response accepts outcome indeterminacy while denying autonomy: the outcome within the constrained space may be uncertain, but the court lacks approach freedom.

---

## What I argued

The approach-freedom operationalization is philosophically coherent as a distinction. The difference between "constrained to applicable law" (ordinary appellate) and "constrained to this specific decision's reasoning structure" (embargos) is real and accurately described. I did not dispute this.

My counter-reply operates on three fronts, all absorbed into §3.2 with a new extended subsection:

**1. Functional doctrinal purpose (new argument):**

The most important point, and the one the supportive defense doesn't address: §5.3's "não há nova cognição autônoma" exists to answer the "terceira instância" objection. The relevant test in Brazilian civil procedure for "terceira instância" is *functional* — does the court produce a new first-time substantive determination on a previously undecided merit question? This test does not track approach freedom. I used the limited-remand example: a court instructed to determine only damages within an established liability framework lacks approach freedom by any measure, yet no Brazilian proceduralist would characterize the damages determination as "no new autonomous cognition." The approach-freedom criterion misidentifies what the "terceira instância" concern is about. This argument was not available before the approach-freedom operationalization appeared — it is the correct response to that specific move.

**2. Outcome-indeterminacy concession (sharpened version of the existing §3.2 argument):**

The supportive defense explicitly accepts "outcome indeterminacy within the constrained space." This is a concession that the embargos court forms new outcome-determining judgments not entailed by the original decision. §5.3's claim is about the *product* of embargos activity (no new substantive merit determinations), not merely the *process* (approach-constrained deliberation). The approach-freedom label characterizes the process without changing the product. Once outcome indeterminacy is accepted, the §5.3 claim about the product is surrendered.

**3. Absence of doctrinal support:**

Approach freedom is a philosophical construct with no established doctrinal meaning in Brazilian procedural law. "Cognição autônoma" in the CPC 2015 context must be interpreted by reference to how courts and scholars delimit the embargos/terceira-instância boundary — not by importing a criterion from analytic philosophy of action. No statutory text or established doctrine grounds the approach-freedom reading.

**Structural-deployment limit (§3.4 update):**

The supportive papers have introduced the structural-deployment limit: implicit rejection requires that the court's structural reasoning have actually reached and logically disposed of the argument, not merely that the court's framework provides a determinate answer. This is accepted as a genuine refinement that increases the estimated Type A fraction among genuine omissions. But it does not reverse the selection pressure — it narrows the magnitude. I added a paragraph to §3.4 acknowledging this narrowing while pressing that the selection pressure remains directional and that the frontier between the deployment gap and genuine framework openness is itself contested.

## What I considered and discarded

**Contesting the ordinary-appellate vs. embargos distinction itself:** The supportive paper's list of approach freedoms ordinary appellate courts have (invoking omitted precedents, applying different theories, art. 1.013, §3º) is accurate. Contesting the distinction would be factually wrong.

**Pressing the Type A/Type B line more aggressively:** The empirical arbiter question (distribution of genuine §489, §1º, IV violations between framework-determined and framework-open) remains unresolved. I didn't press this further because it's already in the paper and both sides have symmetrized their epistemic claims. The more productive attack is the doctrinal-function argument, which is a conceptual objection not resolved by empirical data.

**Attacking the structural-deployment limit substantively:** The limit is epistemically correct — courts can possess frameworks without deploying them structurally. Attacking it would be attacking a true claim. I accepted the narrowing and pressed that it doesn't reverse the direction.

## What remains open

1. **Empirical arbiter:** Neither side has Brazilian appellate practice data on the distribution of genuine §489, §1º, IV violations. Until this data exists, both sides are making structural predictions. §5.6 of the main paper correctly absorbs this as the resolution condition.

2. **Doctrinal grounding of approach freedom:** The key move for the supportive paper would be finding Brazilian civil procedure scholarship or jurisprudence that operationalizes embargos non-autonomy in terms tracking approach constraints rather than new-determination production. If such grounding were found, the third objection in §3.2 would need revision.

## Assessment

The approach-freedom response is the best move available to the supportive side. It accepts the generativity concession (new judgments are formed) while denying that this establishes autonomy (the court lacks approach freedom). This is a genuine and disciplined counter.

The response is defeatable, but not easily. The strongest counter is the functional-doctrinal argument: approach freedom is the wrong criterion because §5.3's purpose is to answer the "terceira instância" concern, and the "terceira instância" test tracks new-determination production, not approach constraints. The outcome-indeterminacy concession seals the argument: once it's accepted that the court might go either way on the omitted argument, the court is forming new outcome-determining judgments — which is exactly what §5.3 was designed to deny.

Whether the paper's §5.3 survives depends on whether the supportive routine can (a) find Brazilian procedural doctrinal support for the approach-freedom operationalization, or (b) show that the "terceira instância" test does track approach constraints rather than new-determination production in Brazilian courts. Neither path looks easy.
