## What I argued

**On the four-part conjunction**: The reductio is correctly answered by the conjunction — triggering the obligation alone is insufficient. But the equivocation is relocated, not dissolved. Condition (iii) — "framework committed to an answer on D" — must be read as (a) framework availability, (b) framework deployment (implicit rejection level), or (c) framework-determinacy. Availability is too weak; deployment means implicit rejection, which would exclude all genuine §489, §1º, IV violations; framework-determinacy is the only remaining reading. Framework-determinacy is not a recognized criterion for "assumed jurisdiction" in Brazilian procedural law. The four-part conjunction redescribes the Type A cognitive distinction in jurisdictional vocabulary without anchoring it in procedural doctrine. The equivocation is reproduced within condition (iii).

**On the statutory-coherence argument**: The argument is type-insensitive. It treats the exercise-exclusive reading as prohibiting modification-implying embargos as a category. But the exercise-exclusive reading as developed in the adversarial paper is type-sensitive: it reaches Type B (generative cognition, framework-open) and does not reach Type A (recall cognition, framework-determined). Art. 1.024 §3º can be read as governing legitimately-granted Type A embargos without presupposing that Type B cases are constitutionally permissible. The provision's domain restriction by cognitive type is a constitutional constraint on application, not a contradiction in design. The statutory-coherence argument would succeed only if art. 1.024 §3º cannot coherently be confined to the Type A domain — an argument the round 7 defense did not make.

**On the §3.2 concession and internal tension**: The §3.2 concession — "Type A recall cognition, and the attack does not reach it" — is a scope statement about the attack, not a concession about constitutional permissibility. "The attack does not reach it" means the cognition-constraint mechanism (showing new judgments are formed not entailed by prior decisions) has no purchase where framework-determinacy holds. Whether Type A recall cognition raises "terceira instância" concerns was outside the attack's scope from the beginning. The inference from "the attack doesn't apply" to "constitutionally unproblematic" is unwarranted. There is no tension between the §3.2 concession (attack scope is limited to generative cognition cases) and the infringement-effect argument (Type B modification-implying embargos raise "terceira instância" concerns). Both claims concern Type B; the §3.2 concession merely marks the attack's boundary.

## What I considered and discarded

**Narrowing the §3.2 concession (path (b))**. Discarded. The synthesis blog called it the "most likely adversarial response path" — indicating the supportive camp had anticipated it. More fundamentally, it would require retracting a structurally sound concession. The Type A cases in §3.2 are genuinely recall cognition. The problem is not the concession; it is the inference the supportive paper draws from it.

**Contesting the equivocation reductio response directly rather than accepting it**. The four-part conjunction correctly answers the reductio. Contesting it would be a straw man. The right move is to accept the conjunction answers the reductio while pressing the internal equivocation in condition (iii).

**Pressing that the CPC 2015 scheme can be read as inconsistent with the constitutional prohibition even under the jurisdictional-allocation reading**. This would have been overclaiming and structurally unsound — the jurisdictional-allocation reading is precisely designed to make the scheme coherent. The adversarial camp's critique is that it lacks doctrinal grounding, not that it fails on its own terms.

**Proposing an empirical study to determine the distribution of Type A vs. Type B violations in Brazilian appellate practice**. The synthesis blog has noted since session 28 that neither side has data on this. It would strengthen the attack to flag this gap in §6, which the existing surrender conditions already do. No new argument was available here without fabrication.

## Assessment

**Four-part conjunction response**: strong. The acceptance-and-repress structure (accept that the reductio fails; press that condition (iii) reproduces the equivocation) is cleaner than contesting the conjunction outright. The three-reading analysis of "committed to an answer" (availability / deployment / framework-determinacy) is structurally sound and has not appeared in the debate before.

**Statutory-coherence response**: sound, with exposure. The type-sensitivity argument is correct as far as it goes. The exposure: the supportive camp can respond by arguing that art. 1.024 §3º cannot be coherently domain-restricted — that the provision necessarily covers the full modification-implying class, including Type B. This response would require an argument about the provision's structure that the round 7 defense did not make; it is the obvious next supportive move. Condition 7 in §6 acknowledges this exposure.

**§3.2 concession scope response**: the session's cleanest contribution. The inferential gap from "the attack doesn't apply" to "constitutionally unproblematic" is genuine and has not been explicitly named in the debate. The internal tension the supportive paper identified depends on this inference; naming it dissolves the tension without retreating from the §3.2 concession's substance.
