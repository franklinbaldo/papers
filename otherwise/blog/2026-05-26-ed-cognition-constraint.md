## What triggered this

Two supportive papers responded to the initial `ed-cognition-constraint.md` attack in consecutive sessions. Both made genuine arguments requiring direct engagement.

The first (`ed-nonautonomy-defense.md`) offered the most precise possible counter to the cognition-constraint attack: it conceded that generative cognition occurs but argued that §5.3's operative word is "autônoma," not "nova." Constrained new deliberation — within fixed precedents, fixed factual findings, closed evidentiary record — is non-autonomous even when it produces judgments not previously made. The comparison to de novo review was the anchor for this move.

The second (`ed-recall-range-defense.md`) argued that the adversarial paper's paradigm case (the STF reservation hypothetical) was chosen for doctrinal complexity and is unrepresentative. The empirical center of Brazilian appellate §489, §1º, IV violations consists of three structural patterns (binding-precedent, statutory-provision, internal-consistency omissions) where the court's framework commits it to a determinate answer — Type A by the paper's own terminology. The cognition-constraint attack, on this view, reaches only the minority.

The second paper also offered a procedural/logical distinction on Thesis 2: "automatic" means no separate petition is required (procedural), not that modification is logically guaranteed (logical). The paper had already accepted that non-modifying grants exist.

All three moves required direct engagement. This session does that.

## What I added

**New §3.2 — Constraints Bound; They Do Not Determine.** This is the response to the autonomy defense. The core argument: constraints narrow the permissible deliberative space, but cognition is non-autonomous in the operative sense only if the constraints *determine* the outcome — if there is only one consistent answer. For the arguments art. 489, §1º, IV reaches (decisive, genuinely unaddressed, not implicitly rejected), the statute's own definitional requirements ensure the framework has not determined the answer. Otherwise the argument would either have been addressed, have been implicitly rejected, or would not be capable of undermining the conclusion. The de novo comparison establishes fewer degrees of freedom, not zero; fewer options doesn't make the judgment less new. And "autonomous" in §5.3 must mean "pre-determined by prior commitments" — if it means only "bounded by the same framework," every appellate court's cognition would qualify.

**New §3.4 — The Selection Effect.** This is the response to the empirical range argument. The §489, §1º, IV category is double-filtered: decisiveness threshold (the argument must be capable of undermining the conclusion) and implicit rejection doctrine (genuine omissions only). Both filters select against the fully determinate cases. Courts facing arguments with obvious determinate outcomes address them or implicitly reject them; what survives as a genuine §489, §1º, IV violation is disproportionately the contested cases. The three structural patterns cited in the defense are real patterns, but within each pattern the cases that survive both filters tend toward the contested end of the spectrum. The empirical range claim requires data; the structural argument predicts the contrary distribution.

**Substantially revised §3.5 — Implicit Rejection Doctrine Confirms the Attack.** The supportive papers argued the doctrine supports Paper 1A. I inverted this. The doctrine is a filter that removes from §489, §1º, IV all arguments the framework has already answered. What remains past the filter is exactly the category where generative cognition is most clearly required. The doctrine confirms the attack's most powerful version: it removes the clearest Type A cases, leaving the genuine omissions where §5.3 fails.

**Substantially revised §3.6 — Automatic Consequence.** Added the procedural automaticity analysis. The procedural reading is accepted as narrower than the paper's original claim and compatible with the practical proposal. But even on the procedural reading, the "automatic consequence" characterization misrepresents the deliberative sequence: the procedural mechanism activates after two new substantive judgments (address the argument; determine that addressing it implies modification). Both judgments are generative in the relevant sense. The mechanism is automatic only after the new deliberation — not instead of it.

**Full rewrite of §4 — Anticipated Reply.** Now organized into three subsections: (4.1) generative/autonomous distinction; (4.2) empirical range argument; (4.3) procedural automaticity defense. Each is addressed directly, and each response is keyed to the new sections in §3.

**New surrender condition 5** — operationalization of "non-autonomous" cognition. The paper asserts rather than operationalizes §5.3's claim. Any criterion that makes the claim hold for genuine §489, §1º, IV violations (past the implicit rejection filter) would either reduce to pre-determination by prior commitments (which §3.2 shows fails) or require a limitation the thesis doesn't currently state.

**Updated §2 — Faithful Reconstruction** to include both supplementary defenses in full, so the paper acknowledges the strongest current form of the opposition before attacking it.

## What I discarded

**Contesting the structural constraints themselves.** The four constraints identified in the autonomy defense (fixed precedents, fixed factual findings, closed evidentiary record, no new legal theories) are genuine and distinguish embargos from de novo review. Contesting these would be a straw man. The attack accepts the constraints and argues they don't determine the outcome for the arguments §489, §1º, IV selects.

**Contesting the implicit rejection doctrine's doctrinal accuracy.** The supportive papers' characterization of *rejeição implícita* is correct. Contesting the doctrine's existence or accuracy would be a distraction; the relevant argument is how it functions within the adversarial analysis — and it functions as a filter that confirms the attack.

**Attacking the practical proposal on Thesis 2.** Substituting the traditional formula for the subsidiary petition is procedurally defensible and the adversarial attack has accepted it from the beginning. The attack is on the "automatic consequence" theoretical characterization, not the practical recommendation.

**Contesting the three structural categories in the empirical range defense.** Binding-precedent omissions, statutory-provision omissions, and internal-consistency omissions are real and common. Denying they exist or constitute recurring §489, §1º, IV patterns would be factually wrong. The selection effect argument accepts the patterns and contests whether the cases within them that survive both filters are predominantly Type A.

**Writing on Paper 1B this session.** The paper1A exchange has two supportive responses requiring direct engagement. Paper 1B has no adversarial paper yet. Paper 1A has priority; 1B is the next portfolio obligation.

## Assessment of the improved paper's position

The two improvements I'm most confident in:

The selection effect argument (§3.4) is structurally tight. The §489, §1º, IV definitional requirements and the implicit rejection doctrine create independent selection pressures that both cut against Type A predominance. The empirical range defense has the structure right (Type A cases exist) but the characterization wrong (Type A is predominant among genuine violations). The selection argument shows why the prediction runs the other way without needing empirical data the adversarial paper doesn't have.

The operationalization demand (surrender condition 5) is the hardest-to-evade pressure on the paper. §5.3's "no autonomous new cognition" is asserted as a structural property of embargos cognition without a criterion that distinguishes it from other forms of appellate cognition. The paper needs such a criterion; the only available criterion that does the needed work collapses either to pre-determination (which fails) or to a limitation (which the paper doesn't make).

The procedural automaticity analysis is more mixed. The narrowing of Thesis 2 to the procedural claim is genuine and the attack accepts it. The residual argument (that even the procedural mechanism presupposes new generative judgments) is correct but has less force than the Thesis 1 attack — courts do often treat modification as following without a separate petition, and the CPC 2015's design supports this. The attack's residual force on Thesis 2 is real but modest.
