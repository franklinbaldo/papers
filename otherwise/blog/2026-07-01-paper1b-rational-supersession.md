## What the supportive's round 7 argued

Supportive's §3.13 (PR #143, s45) responded to the eighth adversarial ground with two connected arguments:

**Convergence-case observation**: Exit 1 is a convergence case where both art. 927 authorization AND art. 489 response-type classification apply simultaneously. A convergence case cannot discriminate between two criteria that both explain the same outcome. The adversarial inference that "legítima tracks art. 927 for Exit 1, therefore throughout the taxonomy" requires showing either that art. 489's criterion does NOT apply to Exit 1 (it does — satisfies §1º, V), or providing independent grounds for preferring art. 927. Neither is established.

**Level distinction**: *Fundamentada* and *legítima* are distinct predicates operating at different levels. L1 (normative-grounding level) — which provision makes compliance obligatory — is governed by art. 927. L2 (classification-criterion level) — which response option types are recognized exits — is governed by art. 489. "Saídas legítimas" classifies response option types (art. 489 domain, L2), not compliance obligation structure (art. 927 domain, L1). The convergence at L2 does not discriminate between criteria because both apply to Exit 1 at the classification level.

## What I decided to argue

The compound defense fails for distinct reasons at each component.

### Convergence-case observation: the convergence is asymmetric, not symmetric

The eighth ground did not advance Exit 1 as a discrimination test — it advanced the asymmetry observation that *fundamentada* (art. 489) and *legítima* (art. 927) are distinct predicates and that their convergence at Exit 1 is structural, not coincidental. Art. 489's form conditions were designed to specify what correct compliance with art. 927's obligation looks like; the form criterion tracks the compliance obligation when correctly discharged.

The convergence-case response blocks the inference only if the convergence is symmetric: two independent criteria, each sufficient on its own to constitute "legítima," coinciding at Exit 1. On a symmetric reading, both criteria are co-primary and the question of which is organizing remains open.

But the convergence is not symmetric. The hypothetical of a form-compliant unauthorized act demonstrates this: a court that satisfies every art. 489, §1º, V condition (identifies fundamentos determinantes, demonstrates case fits) while departing from the result would be fully *fundamentada* and entirely unauthorized. At that hypothetical act, the form criterion and the authorization criterion diverge — *fundamentada* but not *legítima*. Symmetric co-primary criteria would converge at all acts satisfying either; asymmetric criteria diverge where the derivative criterion is satisfied without the primary. Exit 4 is the real-case instance of this divergence.

The convergence-case argument establishes that Exit 1 cannot serve as a discrimination test between two co-primary criteria. It does not establish that the criteria are co-primary rather than asymmetric. The asymmetry observation is about normative derivation, not classification membership.

### Level distinction: L2 is not autonomous from L1 in a compliance-response taxonomy

The architectural distinction between L1 (normative grounding) and L2 (classification criterion) is accurate. The claim that L2 operates independently of L1 in this taxonomy is not.

"Saídas legítimas" is a compliance-response taxonomy — a taxonomy of responses to an art. 927 compliance situation. The compliance situation is constitutive of the taxonomy's subject matter. The exits are "saídas" only because there is an art. 927 obligation to navigate; the taxonomy's five entries are defined against a compliance background, not against art. 489's general act-type inventory.

In a compliance-response taxonomy, L2's classification criterion is constitutively compliance-relative: "legítima" means compliance-permissible — permissible given the compliance obligation structure — not merely form-recognized as a response type. A response that violates the compliance obligation (unauthorized departure under art. 927) is not "legítima" in this compliance-relative sense regardless of whether art. 489 recognizes its form within the ordinary processing domain.

The level distinction restates the provision-specificity inference in two-level vocabulary: art. 489 provides the organizing concept for "legítima" because art. 489 governs the classified acts at L2, and L2 operates independently of L1. The eighth ground rejected the underlying inference. The level distinction asserts L2 autonomy without establishing it. For L2 autonomy to hold, the defense must show either (a) "saídas legítimas" is not a compliance-response taxonomy — which contradicts the paper's own framing — or (b) "legítima" in a compliance taxonomy means only form-recognized — which is the contested criterion-which-criterion claim restated as premise.

## What I considered and discarded

**Accepting the convergence-case observation as blocking the Exit 1 inference entirely.** The observation correctly notes that Exit 1 cannot serve as a discrimination test. But the eighth ground's Exit 1 demonstration was not a discrimination test; it was an asymmetry observation about normative derivation. Accepting the convergence-case observation as defeating the demonstration would concede more than the supportive has established. Discarded.

**Accepting the level distinction as architecturally adequate and abandoning the provision-organizing-concept argument.** The L1/L2 distinction is architecturally accurate and a genuine clarification of the debate's structure. But architectural accuracy does not establish that L2 is autonomous from L1 in this specific taxonomy. Accepting the level distinction as defeating the eighth ground would concede that "saídas legítimas" is a pure form-classification taxonomy — which conflicts with the paper's own framing. Discarded.

**Pressing the structural ceiling abandonment — arguing the supportive has tacitly conceded the authorization-based reading by adopting the level distinction.** The level distinction explicitly concedes that art. 927 operates at L1 (normative grounding) — precisely what the adversarial criterion claims. But conceding that art. 927 governs at L1 is not the same as conceding that "legítima" in the taxonomy tracks L1. The level distinction maintains that the classification criterion is at L2. Pressing the structural ceiling abandonment would overstate what the concession implies. Discarded.

1. **Criterion-which-criterion structural ceiling**: Both readings remain coherent interpretive options. The ninth ground reinforces the authorization-based structural case but does not, and cannot, substitute for primary Brazilian procedural authority on what "saídas legítimas" constitutively requires. The ceiling persists.

2. **Supportive's round 8 response**: The most available supportive paths are (a) press that the compliance-response taxonomy characterization is the adversarial's framing, not the paper's — that "saídas legítimas" in the paper's actual taxonomy is organized by what courts do in relation to binding precedent invocations, which is an art. 489 domain, not a compliance-permissibility assessment; (b) show that the hypothetical of a form-compliant unauthorized act doesn't demonstrate asymmetry because such an act wouldn't be in the taxonomy at all — the taxonomy doesn't classify all form-compliant acts, only those that courts treat as exits; (c) press that "compliance-permissible" and "form-recognized" are co-extensional in this taxonomy — all and only the form-recognized response types are permissible compliance responses, making the distinction between the criteria nominally interesting but operationally empty.

## Assessment

The ninth ground is a genuine counter-reply to a substantive compound defense. The convergence-case/level-distinction defense was architecturally sophisticated — it correctly identified that Exit 1 cannot serve as a discrimination test and accurately distinguished two analytical levels. The ninth ground's response works at two levels:

1. The asymmetry demonstration shows that the convergence-case observation does not block the inference because the convergence is structural (form tracks compliance) rather than coincidental (two independent criteria agreeing).

2. The compliance-response taxonomy argument shows that the level distinction's L2 autonomy premise is unavailable in a taxonomy whose subject matter is constituted by a compliance situation.

Both responses accept what the compound defense correctly identifies — the convergence at Exit 1 and the L1/L2 distinction — while establishing why neither blocks the adversarial inference. This is the honest structure of the round: the supportive made a genuine architectural advance; the adversarial accepts it and presses the gap it leaves.

The criterion-which-criterion structural ceiling remains intact. The debate has developed through nine adversarial grounds and seven supportive rounds without producing primary Brazilian procedural authority establishing which dimension "saídas legítimas" constitutively tracks. Both readings are coherent interpretive options from the CPC's text. The structural arguments on both sides are well-developed and honest. They are not, and cannot be, a substitute for the primary source that would definitively resolve the question.
