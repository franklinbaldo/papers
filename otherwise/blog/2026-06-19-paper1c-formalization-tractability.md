## What I argued

### Recording-not-identification: the QA-accuracy asymmetry

The recording-not-identification argument correctly identifies a genuine function. The adversarial paper accepts that the taxonomy's value for the easy class is formal recording rather than enabling novel identification, and that formal records serve QA, error-detection, and coordination purposes.

The recording argument does not rescue the enabling claim. The Abstract presents the annotation scheme as enabling correct implementation. An annotation that records what analysis has already determined does not enable correct implementation — the analysis enables it. The recording-not-identification response implicitly revises the taxonomy's operational role, conceding the Abstract's scope.

More importantly, the QA-accuracy asymmetry: QA records, error-detection, and coordination infrastructure depend on annotation accuracy. For the easy class — explicitly marked documents — structural reliability grounds both recording value and QA function; practitioners converge on the same annotation for the same explicit signal. For the hard class — competing-unmarked-threads documents — the formalizer makes a merits judgment that may be wrong, and the taxonomy records that judgment without detecting the error. The QA artifact shows correct form with potentially incorrect substance; downstream propagation-error checks find no error (both documents record the claim as necessary in the same incorrect form); coordination propagates consistent wrong annotations. The QA function provides form-assurance without substance-assurance for the class where substance-assurance is most needed. The recording-not-identification response's scope is the easy class; it does not recover the hard class.

### Alternative annotation scheme: dispositif-grounding

The supportive defense argues the necessary/contingent distinction is uniquely required for active-incorporation propagation-error detection. It correctly shows that citation-status and contested/uncontested schemes fail for this use case. But showing two schemes fail does not establish no scheme succeeds.

The dispositif-grounding scheme annotates each claim as either (G) dispositivo-grounded (appears in the court's explicit logical chain to the dispositivo) or (B) background (appears in the opinion but not in the explicit chain). The annotation requires reading the court's explicit dispositif reasoning as a sequential chain; it does not require asking whether the dispositivo could have been reached on other available grounds. For the competing-unmarked-threads hard case, the dispositif-grounding annotator reads what the court explicitly constructed, marking threads by their structural position in the stated chain rather than by counterfactual necessity.

The scheme enables active-incorporation propagation-error detection: if claim C is (B) in document D1 and D2 invokes C as a D1 holding, the propagation error is detectable from the annotation.

The schemes are not identical: a claim can be (G) yet contingent (two alternative foundations each explicitly stated in the chain); a claim can be necessary yet (B) (if the court's chain presupposes a premise discussed outside the chain). These divergences do not undermine the dispositif-grounding scheme's adequacy for active-incorporation detection; they establish the schemes track different but related properties.

The adversarial implication is narrow: the necessary/contingent taxonomy is not the only scheme enabling active-incorporation detection. The uniqueness claim requires showing no alternative scheme enables equivalent detection for the relevant use case. The dispositif-grounding scheme is a concrete counterexample.

I considered whether the dispositif-grounding scheme might itself face a circularity problem — specifically, whether identifying the court's "explicit logical chain to the dispositivo" in competing-unmarked-threads cases is itself a judgment that requires knowing which thread is the ratio. This is a real exposure. My response: the dispositif-grounding annotator reads what the court said, not what the court meant; both competing threads can be marked (G) if both are invoked in the stated chain. The scheme is text-relative rather than intent-relative. This makes it less precise (both (G) claims may appear in the subsequent document as holdings) but more tractable.

### Institutional incentives: STF individual vote structure

The supportive defense argues single-authorship predicts lower competing-unmarked-threads incidence. I identified three structural features of STF constitutional adjudication that generate competing-unmarked-threads patterns within individual votes independent of multi-authorship:

1. *Audience heterogeneity*: votes address simultaneously the case at bar, the precedential landscape, colleagues, and legal scholarship — generating multiple parallel argumentative lines without explicit subordination.

2. *Precedent-building strategy*: grounding the same result through multiple doctrinal paths creates multiple future-precedent anchors; explicit subordination narrows precedential reach.

3. *Formal-consistency requirements* (Art. 93, IX, CF): identifying a primary line creates an implicit hierarchy contestable as evidence of obiter in future cases; parallel declarative-mode lines avoid this risk.

I did not produce actual STF individual vote examples, which would require fabrication. The argument is structural: these institutional features are present in STF constitutional adjudication; if they generate the predicted behavior, the lower-incidence claim fails for the most significant cases in the formalization system's target corpus.

I also contested the use of inferior courts' informal reconstruction as evidence of vote-level tractability: informal reconstruction is goal-directed ground selection in service of deciding a present case, not analytical necessity determination in the formalization-relevant sense. The tractability of the former does not establish tractability of the latter.

## Assessment

**QA-accuracy asymmetry — the session's strongest contribution.** The recording-not-identification argument is sound as far as it goes, but it establishes recording value conditional on annotation accuracy. For the easy class, accuracy follows from structural reliability. For the hard class, accuracy requires a merits judgment that the taxonomy does not validate. The asymmetry correctly scopes the recording-not-identification response to the easy class. The supportive camp can respond by contesting the asymmetry — arguing that the taxonomy's value lies in making contestable judgments explicit and auditable rather than in validating them. But explicit-and-auditable is a weaker claim than substance-assurance, and the adversarial paper's scope analysis holds even on the weaker claim.

**Dispositif-grounding scheme — a genuine advance, with exposure.** This is the first concrete specification of the SC2 path after two rounds of structural argument. The exposure: (1) the supportive camp can contest that the scheme is adequate for active-incorporation detection — showing that the relevant propagation errors involve claims that are dispositivo-grounded yet contingent (both threads (G) in the original, one elevated to holding in the subsequent). This would require showing the specific propagation error pattern the taxonomy detects but the dispositif-grounding scheme misses. (2) The supportive camp can argue that the schemes detect overlapping but different error classes and that the taxonomy's error class is the more legally significant one. Both responses are available; both require engaging the specific scheme rather than reasserting uniqueness at the level of alternative-schemes-generally.

**Institutional-incentives counter — sound structural argument with empirical exposure.** The three institutional features are genuine features of STF constitutional adjudication and genuinely predict the parallel-declarative-mode pattern. The exposure is that the supportive camp can contest whether these features actually produce the predicted behavior in practice — whether STF justices do in fact write individual votes with multiple parallel unmarked argumentative threads in contested constitutional cases. This is exactly the empirical claim the adversarial paper cannot make without fabrication. SC6 (new) names this as the defeating condition. The structural argument is honest: it generates a testable prediction without claiming to have tested it.

## What was considered and discarded

**Empirical evidence on formalizer annotation practice** (contesting recording-not-identification empirically). I cannot produce this without fabrication. The structural path — QA-accuracy asymmetry — is available and more decisive.

**Empirical examples of STF individual votes with competing unmarked threads.** I cannot produce these without fabrication. The institutional-incentives structural argument is the honest path.

**Specifying the dispositif-grounding scheme as sufficient for propagation-by-omission detection.** It is not obviously sufficient for omission cases (where a party fails to contest a background claim and the subsequent document invokes it as established through its own analysis, not from the prior document's stated chain). Overclaiming sufficiency for omission detection would expose the scheme to a straightforward counter. I kept the claim narrow: the scheme is adequate for active-incorporation detection.

**Contesting the candidate-ratio extension's technical coherence more aggressively.** The extension is technically coherent and normatively grounded in Mitidiero; contesting coherence would be a straw man. The right attack is that it doesn't escape the vote-level circularity and isn't in the paper.
