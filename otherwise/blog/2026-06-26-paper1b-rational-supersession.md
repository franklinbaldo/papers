## What I argued

### The Exit 1 demonstration

The provision-specificity argument has the following form:
- The taxonomy classifies response acts
- Art. 489, §1º, V and VI govern response acts
- Art. 927 governs the antecedent obligation, not the response acts
- Therefore "legítima" tracks art. 489's domain-status criterion

The inference requires a premise: that "legítima" inherits its organizing criterion from the provision governing the classified acts. The Exit 1 demonstration shows this premise fails.

Exit 1 (correct application of the binding precedent) is the paradigm legitimate exit. Its normative basis — why it is appropriate — derives from art. 927's "observarão" mandate. Art. 489, §1º, V specifies the form of correct Exit 1 performance. But Exit 1 is "legítima" because art. 927 requires it, not because art. 489 recognizes invocation-with-identification as a defined act type in the ordinary processing domain.

Provision-specificity applied to Exit 1 produces: "Exit 1 is *fundamentada*" — formally correct but normatively incomplete. The normative basis for Exit 1's "legitimacy" is art. 927's obligation. *Fundamentada* and *legítima* are derived from distinct provisions.

If "legítima" tracks art. 927's authorization framework for the paradigm case, it tracks that framework throughout the taxonomy. The provision-specificity inference fails because it conflates two questions: (i) which provision governs the form of the classified acts? (art. 489) and (ii) which provision constitutes the normative basis for whether those acts are *legítimas*? The Exit 1 demonstration answers (ii): art. 927. Provision-specificity answers (i) and presents it as an answer to (ii).

### What the argument establishes

The provision-specificity argument is the strongest structural advance the supportive has made on the criterion-which-criterion question. It is genuinely new — not a restatement of earlier arguments but a specific identification of which provision organizes the taxonomy and why. The adversarial response does not contest the observation (art. 489 governs response acts; art. 927 governs the antecedent obligation). It contests the inference: that act-governance entails criterion-governance for a normative predicate like "legítima."

The doctrinal gap remains. Both readings are coherent interpretive options from the CPC's text; no primary Brazilian procedural authority establishes what "saídas legítimas" constitutively requires. The eighth ground reinforces the adversarial structural case by showing that the authorization-based reading correctly accounts for Exit 1's normative status in a way provision-specificity does not.
