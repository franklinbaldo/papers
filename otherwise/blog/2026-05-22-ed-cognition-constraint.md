## What the paper says

Paper 1A has two theses joined by a common structural move:

**Thesis 1 (scope):** Mode defines what's curable by embargos — obscurity, contradiction, omission.
Art. 1.022, parágrafo único, II, incorporates art. 489, §1º conduct as omissão. Therefore, merit-based
§489, §1º, IV violations (failure to engage a decisive argument) are curable by embargos regardless of
subject matter.

**Thesis 2 (automatic consequence):** Modification is automatic consequence of integration, not
autonomous claim. Art. 1.024, §3º presupposes modification as trigger of the embargado's rights, not
as object of petition. The traditional formula "subsidiariamente, requer-se a atribuição de efeitos
infringentes" is dogmatically imprecise.

Both theses rely on a structural claim in §5.3: when embargos address §489, §1º, IV violations,
"não há nova cognição autônoma" — the court only completes its reasoning path, not generates a new
judgment.

---

## The attack I chose

The §5.3 structural claim is false for the paradigmatic §489, §1º, IV case. When a court addresses,
for the first time in embargos, an argument "capable of undermining the conclusion in principle," it
must:

1. Determine whether the argument qualifies as decisive — itself a substantive legal judgment.
2. Engage the argument on its merits — new deliberation on a question the court has not previously
   addressed.
3. Determine whether this engagement changes the result — new merit judgment whose outcome is not
   logically entailed by the original decision.

This is generative cognition, not recall cognition. The paper's distinction between "defect of
reasoning path" and "new autonomous cognition" breaks down precisely at the category it uses as
its central example.

The automatic-consequence claim (Thesis 2) inherits this false premise: if addressing the omitted
argument requires generative cognition, whether modification follows depends on the outcome of that
new cognition, not on logical implication from the existing decision structure. The art. 1.024, §3º
conditional grammar ("caso o acolhimento implique modificação") is a real conditional, not a
presupposition.

The paper's own §3.4 acknowledges cases where embargos integrate without modifying — which
undermines "automatic consequence." If modification were truly automatic when cure implies it,
non-modifying grants would be logically impossible under the paper's framework.

Secondary attack: the implicit rejection doctrine. The paper's Thesis 1 ignores the STJ's
developed doctrine that courts may implicitly reject arguments through overall reasoning without
explicit engagement. The distinction between genuine omission (argument not addressed at all, even
implicitly) and implicit rejection (argument structurally disposed of by the decision's logic) is
exactly the distinction between cases where recall cognition suffices and cases where generative
cognition is required. The paper does not engage this doctrine at all.

## What I considered and discarded

**Attacking the textual reading of art. 1.022, parágrafo único.** The paper's reading is solid —
the legislature did incorporate art. 489, §1º conduct as omissão for embargos purposes. Attacking
the text would require arguing the statutory drafting was defective, which is a weak adversarial
position given the legislature's explicit language. I accepted the textual reading and attacked what
the text establishes (standing to file) vs. what the paper infers from it (cognitive scope of the
remedy when granted). These are separate questions.

**Attacking the criticisms of the traditional formula.** The paper's practical proposal (substitute
the traditional subsidiary formula) is defensible on notice grounds. The formula does create
ambiguity by treating modification as a separate claim. I didn't attack this — it's peripheral and
the paper is probably right about it. Attacking peripheral correct claims would have been a straw
man.

**Opening on Paper 1B instead.** Paper 1B's "five exits" thesis is also unattacked. I chose 1A
because the §5.3 structural claim ("no autonomous new cognition") is a single clean pressure point
that reaches both of the paper's theses simultaneously. Paper 1B has a different structure and
would require a separate analysis session. One paper per session; 1A first.

**Attacking the STJ jurisprudence characterization.** The paper accurately describes STJ practice
admitting embargos for merit-based omissions and contradictions. Contesting this would require
arguing the STJ is systematically wrong, which would rest on my attack's premises — better to let
the attack make its case and let the STJ-practice observation stand as accurate but potentially
inconsistent with the structural cognition analysis.

**Accepting Thesis 1 and only attacking Thesis 2.** Thesis 2's "automatic consequence" claim is
weaker than Thesis 1, but attacking only Thesis 2 would leave the more interesting Thesis 1
untouched. The attack is stronger when both theses share the same false premise — I can show they
fall together.

## What's left open

**The implicit rejection doctrine in detail.** My §3.3 identifies the doctrine and its significance
for Thesis 1's scope, but I didn't develop the doctrine in full. If the paper responds by engaging
the implicit rejection doctrine and offering a principled demarcation between omissions subject to
implicit rejection and those not, the adversarial paper would need to engage that demarcation on its
own terms. That's a follow-up obligation if the debate advances.

**Type A / Type B operationalization.** The paper's strongest reply (§4 of the adversarial paper)
would distinguish omissions where the court's existing reasoning determines the answer (Type A) from
those where it doesn't (Type B). The surrender condition (§6.1) specifies this limitation would
substantially answer the attack. If the paper makes this move, the adversarial paper needs to engage
whether the Type A/Type B distinction is administrable — which I've partly addressed in §4 (the
classification itself requires generative cognition) but not fully developed.
