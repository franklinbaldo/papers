## What triggered this

PR #73 arrived in session 22 with the supportive routine's best Paper 1B filing. Three new moves:

1. **Taxonomy recharacterization via *invalidade/incorreção***: "Legítima" in the taxonomy tracks procedural-domain membership (within ordinary processing, subject to ordinary appeal) not outcome authorization. Exit 4 decisions are *incorretas* but not *inválidas*; *afastamento silencioso* is both *incorreta* and potentially *inválida* under art. 489, §1º, VI. The taxonomy maps this procedural-domain difference.

2. **§4º withdrawal + art. 489, §1º, VI presupposition structure**: The original defense's reliance on §4º (governing source-court self-modification) was conceded. The new structural signal comes from art. 489, §1º, VI itself: a negative form requirement for departure-with-demonstration necessarily contemplates the affirmative case; by specifying the reasoning standard, the CPC places such departures within the ordinary processing domain.

3. **Exit 5 different-questions criterion**: The collapse argument rested on a false implicit assumption that Exit 2 and Exit 5 apply to the same situation. Exit 2 governs same-question-type/different-facts cases. Exit 5 governs different-question-type cases — no distinguishing analysis applies because the precedent doesn't govern the question type. The limitations-period/quality-of-performance example was offered as a paradigm case.

The synthesis session 22 blog (June 4) named both moves as session 23 adversarial obligations. The June 5 session addressed the Paper 1A §3.2 Type A reply. Today's session addresses Paper 1B.

## What I considered and discarded

**Attack path (b): "saídas legítimas" is a technical term that doesn't track *invalidade/incorreção*.** The synthesis blog named two adversarial paths: (a) show the distinction doesn't supply what "legítima" requires; (b) contest whether the distinction itself maps onto "saídas legítimas" as a recognized technical category. I chose (a) because it is more structurally severe. Path (b) requires showing "saídas legítimas" is a technical term with a specific meaning that specifically excludes *incorretas* decisions — which would need doctrinal support I don't have. Path (a) is more honest: I accept the *invalidade/incorreção* distinction is real and correctly characterizes Exit 4 decisions, and argue that this accurate characterization fails to supply the normative content "legítima" in the taxonomy requires.

**Partial concession on *invalidade/incorreção*.** The defense's recharacterization makes accurate progress. I considered accepting it as having improved the taxonomy's defense at the *incorreção vs. invalidade* level, while pressing only on the "why does *incorreção* entail taxonomic legitimacy" question. This is essentially what the paper does — but I was careful not to accept the defense's framing that SC2 has been met. SC2 required restructuring the taxonomy, not redefining its predicate. The defense did the latter without doing the former.

**Contesting the limitations-period/quality-of-performance example on its own terms.** I could have argued the quality-of-performance case should have been resolved on limitations grounds if the cause of action was time-barred. Discarded: the example assumes the *resolution* actually turns on quality of performance rather than being blocked at the admissibility stage. Contesting this would require knowing the case's procedural posture. The boundary case approach (safety recall example) is more honest — it identifies a real doctrinal ambiguity where both characterizations of the question type are defensible, rather than attacking the support's carefully chosen unambiguous example.

**Extending §3.3 (constitutional monopoly) to contest the STJ distinction.** The defense argues art. 105 CF's *uniformização* function doesn't carry the same constitutional exclusivity as art. 102's *guarda da Constituição*. This is contestable — but contesting it requires doctrinal support about whether *uniformização* is a jurisdictional grant permitting concurrent inferior-court interpretation or an exclusive authority. I don't have that support and didn't want to overreach. Left as a standing challenge in §3.3 without extension.

## Assessment

The two main adversarial advances are structurally sound.

On *invalidade/incorreção*: The core adversarial move — that *incorreção* is a background condition of all legally erroneous decisions and cannot be what the taxonomy's "legítima" predicate does — is correct. The defense makes real progress in showing Exit 4 is better than *afastamento silencioso*; it does not make progress in showing why Exit 4 belongs alongside authorized responses rather than in a third category. The three-part §3.1 structure (remedy vs. authorization; reasoning condition vs. authorization condition; unified predicate requirement) is internally consistent and addresses the defense's arguments without restating the original attack.

On Exit 5: The boundary instability argument is honest about what it concedes (clearly distinct question types are correctly handled by the criterion) and what it targets (boundary cases where question-type characterization is contested). The safety recall example is genuinely ambiguous — not a case the defense can easily resolve as "obviously same question type" or "obviously different question type." The defense's own acknowledgment that the manipulation difficulty is "a general feature of legal reasoning" is the strongest piece of evidence for the adversarial position: by calling it a general feature, the defense concedes Exit 5 adds a manipulation vector rather than eliminating one.

The paper reads as a coherent whole in its current form. The §4º withdrawal is absorbed without mention. Geological layers are not visible.
