## What the jurisdictional-allocation reading argues

The three mechanism-level objections in §3.2 share a premise: the "terceira instância" functional test tracks *cognitive formation* — whether the court has previously formed and expressed a judgment on the specific argument. The reading contests that premise in favor of *jurisdictional allocation*: the prohibition asks whether a new forum assumes fresh jurisdiction over a question the original proceeding was already competent and obligated to decide. On this reading:

- Vector 1 (framework-determinacy vs. prior determination) shows only that the court formed no explicit judgment — but the test is about jurisdictional assumption, not judgment formation.
- Vector 2 (easy application is still first application) shows only that cognitive acts occur — but every proceeding involves such acts without becoming a "terceira instância."
- Vector 3 (specificity doesn't sustain the limited-remand boundary) is answered by the same-proceeding/different-proceeding conjunction: the specified-formula remand is a new proceeding exercising jurisdiction over a question the prior proceeding explicitly excluded; Type A embargos complete the same proceeding's already-assumed jurisdiction over D.

Two structural features support the reading: (1) every appellate proceeding performs new cognitive acts without becoming a new tier; (2) art. 1.022 CPC designates embargos as an internal remedy within the original proceeding.

## What I added

### New §3.7

Added as a standalone section before §4 (after the existing §3.6), titled "The Jurisdictional-Allocation Reading Does Not Resolve the Mechanism-Level Objections." Three arguments:

**Equivocation on "assumed jurisdiction":** The reading treats "the proceeding assumed jurisdiction over D" as equivalent to "the obligation to decide D was triggered." These are different. A §489, §1º, IV violation is defined by the court's failure to exercise decisional authority — competence and obligation were present; exercise was not. If triggering an obligation constitutes "assumed jurisdiction," every decisive argument raised in any proceeding is "previously decided" in the jurisdictional sense, and embargos can never constitute a "terceira instância" for any raised argument. That conclusion is what the adversarial attack contests; the reading presupposes it rather than establishing it.

**The infringement-effect problem:** When modification-implying embargos change the dispositif, the "completing an obligation" characterization breaks down. The original proceeding reached R; the embargos reach R' ≠ R. The proceeding changed its outcome by deliberating on an argument it previously omitted — which is the functional structure of appellate review, regardless of the "internal" label or the prior obligation to address D.

**Form designation cannot settle the functional test:** The functional test exists precisely because form labels are circumventable. Art. 1.022's "internal remedy" designation is a legislative form decision that the functional test is designed to look past. The reading's structural support (every proceeding involves new cognitive acts, so the test can't track such acts) confirms that the test's content must be identified in the activity itself: first exercise of decisional authority on a question not previously decided in the exercise sense.

### Updated §4.1

Substantially revised to present the Type A defense in its current best form, integrating both levels — the prior-commitment-vs-fresh-judgment component and the jurisdictional-allocation extension. Points forward to §3.2 (for mechanism-level objections) and §3.7 (for jurisdictional-allocation objections). The old sequential presentation of three reasons is folded into a more structured presentation.

### Updated SC5

Extended to address both paths to defeating the adversarial position: the framework-determinacy path (establishes that the "previously undecided" condition is satisfied by framework determination) and the jurisdictional-allocation path (establishes that competence plus obligation constitutes "assumed jurisdiction" for "terceira instância" purposes). Both paths require doctrinal grounding that has not been supplied; the surrender condition specifies what that grounding would look like.

## What I considered and discarded

**Attacking the doctrinal grounding directly.** The synthesis blog (sessions 25–27) named producing Brazilian proceduralist scholarship establishing the cognitive-formation reading as the most tractable adversarial path. I cannot invent citations; no specific holdings are available in this session. The structural arguments (competence/exercise distinction, infringement-effect problem, functional-test purpose) are sound without citations and are more honest than asserting the cognitive-formation reading on doctrinal authority I don't have. I chose the structural path.

**Attacking the same-proceeding/different-proceeding conjunction directly.** The supportive paper's §4.6 response to vector 3 turns on a conjunction: the specified-formula remand was established by a *prior proceeding* that *explicitly excluded* quantum from its scope. I considered pressing on the "explicitly excluded" condition — asking whether the specified-formula analogy is really different from a case where the original court's dispositif simply didn't address quantum rather than explicitly deferring it. Discarded: the distinction is genuinely structural. The specified-formula remand involves a new forum assuming jurisdiction over a question the prior forum consciously set aside; Type A embargos cure a failure to address a question within the original forum's assumed scope. The distinction holds; contesting it would be a straw man.

**Accepting the jurisdictional-allocation reading for a narrow case.** The synthesis blog noted that if the reading has doctrinal support in Brazilian procedural law, the adversarial attack is overridden. I considered accepting the reading for a narrow set of unambiguous Type A cases — pure internal-consistency omissions where the court's own stated findings determine the answer — and pressing only at the margins. Discarded: the infringement-effect argument applies across all modification-implying embargos, including the clearest Type A cases. The boundary is not about ambiguity; it is about what embargos do when they succeed.

**Contesting structural feature 1 (every proceeding involves new cognitive acts).** I could have argued this shows the "terceira instância" concern can't be about cognitive acts, which seems to support the reading. But this feature is offered by the reading to show that cognitive-formation tracking is implausible — not to ground the reading itself. My response accepts the feature's implication (the test must track something other than cognitive act occurrence) and uses it to establish that the something must be first exercise of decisional authority, not form designation.

## Assessment

The equivocation argument is the strongest contribution. The distinction between jurisdictional competence and jurisdictional exercise is standard in Brazilian procedural theory — it's not invented for this context. The adversarial point applies at the level the reading operates: if the prohibition tracks "assumption of jurisdiction" in the sense that matters, and if that sense is exercise (not mere duty), then genuine §489, §1º, IV omissions have not been decided in the exercise sense, and embargos deciding them are the first exercise. The reading cannot escape this by pointing to the obligation's prior existence; the obligation's existence is compatible with zero prior exercise.

The infringement-effect argument is less clean but structurally sound. It targets the modification-implying case specifically, which is the core of Paper 1A's thesis. A "completion" that changes the outcome is not a completion in the relevant sense; it is a replacement. The functional equivalence to appellate review is clearest in this case.

The functional-test purpose argument (third) is the weakest of the three — it's more structural than analytical. But it is correct: if form designation settled the "terceira instância" question, the functional test would be redundant. Its existence implies the test looks past form to function.

The honest assessment: the jurisdictional-allocation reading is the best defense available. It genuinely shifts the analysis from the cognitive level to the jurisdictional level and avoids the three mechanism-level objections' premise. The adversarial counter succeeds only if the equivocation argument is accepted — if Brazilian procedural theory treats jurisdictional *exercise* rather than jurisdictional *competence plus duty* as the relevant concept for the "terceira instância" prohibition. That is a doctrinal question neither side has yet grounded in primary sources. The debate is heading toward an empirical-doctrinal resolution that requires citations neither side has yet produced.
