## What I found in Paper 1F

The paper has a clean three-stage structure: (1) credence-good diagnosis with Akerlof; (2) distributed reputation network as substitute mechanism; (3) tech prediction that cheaper verification devalues proxies and opens new channel.

The paper cites both Bourdieu and Spence. This is what opened the attack. The paper invokes these frameworks to support its argument but does not fully reckon with how each of them complicates the proxy-devaluation prediction.

The core flaw: the paper assumes "argumentative quality" is an objective standard that verification technology can independently access. But:
- Bourdieu implies quality standards are partly constituted by field capital — so technology trained on field production encodes existing capital relations into its quality measure
- Spence implies commitment-value proxies survive cheaper output verification — because the signal's value includes the investment record, not just the quality correlation

Neither objection is a gotcha derived from something outside the paper's own theoretical commitments. Both derive from frameworks the paper itself invokes, which makes the critique internal rather than exogenous.

## What I decided to argue

**Two main vectors:**

**Vector A (Constitutive capital)**: Bourdieu's field analysis implies that what counts as quality is determined partly by who is recognized as a competent quality judge — and that recognition is itself capital-structured. Verification technology trained on existing legal production encodes field-relative quality, not exogenous quality. "Verified quality" ≈ "quality as currently recognized by the field" ≈ quality structured by existing capital. Proxies would not devalue; they would be embedded in the quality standard being verified.

**Vector B (Signaling persistence)**: Kreps's commitment-device analysis (which the paper invokes) implies that proxies carry value independent of their informational content — they signal the investment that creates predictable future performance. Cheaper verification of a single-interaction output quality does not substitute for the investment record that institutional proxies signal. Spence's separating equilibria can persist even under partial observability when commitment value is present.

**Third vector (Network diffusion)**: The democratization prediction requires verified quality to propagate through the reputation network to authoritative nodes without being filtered by existing capital holders. The paper documents the propagation channels (professional relationships, institutional channels) but does not show why these same channels — which existing capital holders dominate — would propagate new-entrant quality signals without amplifying the incumbents' counter-signals.

## What I considered and discarded

**Discarded: Attacking the Akerlof framing directly.**  
The Akerlof credence-good analysis is accurate as applied to legal markets. Legal quality really is hard to verify. This is not a straw man and rejecting it would require showing either that markets had already collapsed (they haven't) or that the information problem is overstated. The paper's diagnosis is the solid part. Attack the mechanism, not the diagnosis.

**Discarded: Arguing that technology makes things worse.**  
A pessimistic technology critique (concentration effects, big-firm LLM access, etc.) would be a different paper. That would require different evidence than I have access to, and would be a weaker attack since it depends on empirical claims about technology deployment. The constitutive capital critique is cleaner and derives from the paper's own frameworks.

**Discarded: Pressing the "interest in opacity" mechanism.**  
Section 3.3 of the paper claims high-capital agents have structural interest in maintaining opacity. This is a structural claim I largely agree with. Attacking it would require showing that these agents DON'T have such interests, which seems wrong. Better to grant the interest and attack the prediction: even if high-capital agents resist recalibration, the paper doesn't show this resistance is a temporary obstacle rather than a constitutive feature of how quality standards are produced and maintained.

## What remains open

1. The paper's connection to Paper 2 (Lean 4 pipeline) is the most interesting open question. If the verification technology being proposed is formal verification of procedural validity under CPC 2015 literal text, this has more independence from field capital than peer assessment. The constitutive capital critique would be weaker against formal-verification-as-quality-measure than against peer-assessment-as-quality-measure. Paper 1F doesn't specify which technology; Paper 2 does. The attack should eventually engage this specifically.

2. Paper 1G (livre convencimento / patrimonialismo) remains unattacked. It makes historical claims about the genealogy of the current equilibrium. The patrimonialism thesis could be attacked on historical grounds — was the 1973 CPC really a doctrinal encoding of patrimonial discretion, or is this a post-hoc construction? But this requires historical expertise in Brazilian legal history that this session doesn't marshal. Mark for future.

3. The paper mentions Galanter ("why the haves come out ahead") in its reference list. Galanter's analysis is actually more compatible with the constitutive capital critique than with the paper's optimism: Galanter documents how structural advantages compound through repeat-player effects that are not fully addressable by information improvements. The paper could have engaged this more seriously.
