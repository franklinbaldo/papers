## What I decided to argue

Three counter-replies, one per adversarial vector. All three accept what the supportive establishes and press the surviving gaps.

### Constitutive capital: core-class exogeneity vs. mechanism-scale

The support's exogenous-standard argument succeeds for the structural compliance core (precedent omission, statutory provision omission, internal inconsistency). These cases involve structural textual comparison without field-relative interpretive judgment.

The key observation: the relationship between exogenous-standard clarity and verification-technology value is inverse. The cases where the standard is clearest are cases where competent professional review already identifies the violation without verification technology. Where verification technology would add most value — quality distinctions at the interpretive margin — the constitutive capital critique retains full force, because what counts as adequate ratio engagement at the doctrinal-specific level is shaped by field practice. The core-class defense bounds the attack but doesn't scale the mechanism to where the paper claims systemic recalibration.

### Signaling persistence: new-entrant asymmetry accepted; mimicry-resistance unestablished

The new-entrant asymmetry is accepted. This is the strongest version of the paper's democratization claim — differential competitive position, not total proxy replacement. Accepted without reservation.

The mimicry-resistance argument: Kreps's separating equilibrium requires commitment signals to be costly to mimic. Institutional investment records have this property structurally (multi-dimensional, time-compressed). Whether formal compliance records share it depends on whether optimizing for the compliance standard requires genuine analytical depth or formal structural completeness. If verification checks formal completeness (citations present, precedents mentioned, no explicit contradiction), a low-quality agent optimizing for compliance markers satisfies the standard at lower cost than a genuinely competent practitioner. The compliance record becomes a gameable proxy of the same type the paper's mechanism replaces. The paper does not establish that formal compliance records are harder to game than institutional proxies.

I considered: arguing mimicry-resistance is impossible for any compliance standard. Discarded — too strong. The mimicry problem is real but degree-dependent: it is worst for formal structural checks and best for standards requiring genuine analytical verification that can't be satisfied by marker optimization. The question is which kind the paper's verification mechanism provides. That is an open design question, not a categorical impossibility.

### Network diffusion: attribution gap

The decision-text channel is real. Court decisions through official channels are genuinely independent of practitioner network control. Accepted.

The attribution gap: court decisions attribute compliance findings to case outcomes, not to the practitioner who identified the violation. The compliance record enters the archive as "case X: violation found; remand ordered" — not as "practitioner Y: argued the violation effectively." For the decision-text record to become a practitioner reputation input, intermediaries must attribute the outcome to the practitioner: institutional victory profiles, academic publications, co-counsel networks, professional directories. These intermediaries are capital-holder-structured.

I discarded: claiming the diffusion channel doesn't exist. It exists — official publication is real. The objection is that the channel produces outcome records, not practitioner reputation signals.

## What remains open

1. **Mechanism-scale question**: Does the paper's proxy-devaluation mechanism operate primarily in the structural-compliance core (where verification adds least value) or in the interpretive-quality domain (where constitutive capital retains force)? This requires a characterization of what fraction of the paper's predicted recalibration depends on each.

2. **Mimicry-resistance**: Whether the formal compliance standard is harder to game than institutional proxies — depends on what the Lean 4 pipeline and art. 489 framework verification actually check. If the verification catches interpretive inadequacy (incorrect ratio engagement, failure to address fundamentos determinantes specifically), mimicry-resistance may hold. If it checks structural completeness (statutory citation present, precedent mentioned), it does not.

3. **Attribution pathway**: Whether any diffusion pathway for compliance outcomes includes practitioner attribution without capital-holder intermediaries — e.g., systematic court databases that track counsel-to-outcome correlations. If such databases exist or are being developed as part of the paper's program, the attribution gap narrows.
