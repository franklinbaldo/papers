## Three moves in §3.9

**Move 1 — Asymmetry resolution.** The round 9 defense argued that §3.2 employs framework-determinacy as an operative criterion for delimiting the attack's scope, while §3.8 rejects framework-determinacy as a procedural criterion — producing internal asymmetry. The resolution: §3.2 uses "determines the outcome" as a sufficient condition identifying when the cognition-constraint mechanism is inapplicable (a cognitive description, not a constitutional criterion); §3.8 rejects framework-determinacy as a criterion for "assumed jurisdiction" under an independent constitutional doctrine. Using X as a sufficient condition in one inquiry does not commit the paper to accepting X as a necessary condition or as a criterion in a different inquiry. The asymmetry bridge is invalid.

**Move 2 — Vocabulary frontier.** The round 9 defense treated the vocabulary dispute between exercise-exclusive and jurisdictional-allocation readings as nominal after the type-sensitivity response — a labeling choice without adjacent-case implications. The response: direction-without-uniqueness cases are a definable frontier class where the readings diverge. The divergence depends on whether condition (iii)'s "committed to an answer" means uniquely determined or merely directed. The supportive paper introduced the four-part conjunction in round 7 without resolving this operationalization. Until it is resolved, the vocabulary dispute retains substantive weight for this class. The §6 surrender condition formalizes what resolution would require.

**Move 3 — Mechanism scope acknowledged.** The round 9 defense correctly identified that nine rounds have produced one mechanism (cognition-constraint), §3.2 concedes it lacks purchase in Type A, and §3.8's formal reservation "holds a place without filling it." This is acknowledged accurately. The adversarial paper's position: the attack is complete for Type B; it does not produce a mechanism for Type A; the constitutional question for Type A is neither answered nor conceded — it remains open in the sense that both sides lack primary Brazilian procedural sources establishing whether the "terceira instância" functional test tracks cognitive formation or jurisdictional allocation. Bilateral doctrinal gap, not unilateral concession.
