# The Reclamação Cannot Carry the Symmetry: A Compliance-Verification Constraint on Paper 1D's Dialogue Thesis

---

## 1. Thesis Attacked

"Vinculação Racional e Diálogo Institucional: o Dever de Fundamentação como Limite Simétrico no Sistema Brasileiro de Precedentes" (Baldo, 2025; `paper1D_vinculacao_racional_dialogo_institucional.md`) defends three connected theses about the Brazilian binding-precedent system.

**Thesis 1 (form, not result):** Art. 489, §1º, VI, CPC does not prohibit an inferior court from departing from a binding precedent; it requires that any departure be reasoned. A court that departs with reasons exposed operates within the system; a court that departs without reasons commits a formal defect.

**Thesis 2 (symmetry):** The duty of reasoning under art. 489, §1º, is symmetric. It applies to the inferior court departing from the precedent and equally to the STF judging the reclamação against that departure. The STF must engage with the reasons exposed by the inferior court — it cannot respond with mere reaffirmation of precedent authority. Art. 489, §1º, IV's obligation to engage arguments capable of undermining the conclusion applies to the STF's reclamação judgment as it does to any other decisão judicial.

**Thesis 3 (institutional dialogue):** This symmetric arrangement produces rational institutional dialogue. The reclamação is not a silencing mechanism but a channel through which arguments that withstand confrontation reach the court competent to revise the precedent. The STF either reaffirms with substantive engagement or distinguishes or revises; the súmula is a proposition maintained while it withstands the best available argument, not an immune truth.

Theses 2 and 3 are the paper's novel contribution. Thesis 1 is shared with the broader literature on art. 489, §1º, VI. This attack targets Thesis 2 and the institutional-dialogue claim in Thesis 3 that depends on it.

## 2. Faithful Reconstruction

The strongest case for the paper integrates its original doctrinal arguments with the most careful development of the adequacy claim and the structural feedback-loop argument.

**On form versus result:** The legislature chose to regulate the *form* of departure, not to prohibit departure. If the legislature had intended prohibition, it would have enacted "é vedado ao juiz deixar de seguir precedente vinculante." Instead, art. 489, §1º, VI establishes that a departure without demonstration of distinction or supersession is *unfounded* — a form defect. The contrapositive is clear: a departure with adequate demonstration is not unfounded. The binding-precedent system achieves uniformity by requiring reasons, not by silencing dissent.

**On symmetry via adequacy assessment for reasoned departures:** The symmetry claim is strongest not as a blanket requirement to re-examine the precedent's merits, but as a structural necessity arising from the compliance assessment itself. For *reasoned* departures — where the inferior court identifies specific features and argues they constitute a genuine distinction from the súmula's paradigm — the STF's compliance determination cannot be purely formal. Art. 489, §1º, VI requires that the departure be accompanied by "demonstração de existência de distinção ou superação" — a *demonstration*, not merely a claim. To assess whether a demonstration is adequate (genuine) or inadequate (pretextual), the STF must examine whether the identified features track differences in the súmula's underlying reasoning. For reasoned departures, the compliance question (question A: was the departure formally adequate?) and the merit question (question B: is the precedent correct?) are inseparable at the level of adequacy assessment. An inferior court offering features F₁, F₂ as distinguishing, when those features are entirely outside what the súmula's ratio tracks, is not providing an adequate demonstration; but determining that requires examining what the súmula's ratio tracks — which is engagement with question B.

**On dialogue via the structural feedback loop:** The strongest dialogue argument rests on the reclamação's option (a): when the STF denies a reclamação by genuinely distinguishing the case from the súmula's scope, the denial generates a binding determination about the súmula's limits. This holding — that the súmula does not apply to cases with features F₁, F₂, ..., Fₙ — is generated within the reclamação proceeding itself, without separate procedures, and is directly responsive to the inferior court's departure reasoning. Successive reclamação denials on distinct grounds progressively define the súmula's scope in response to accumulated inferior-court arguments. The feedback loop: inferior court reasoning → reclamação → STF binding scoping determination → evolution of precedent scope. No revision procedures are required for this loop to operate; option (a) is within the reclamação's structural outputs.

**On deliberative accumulation:** Binding scoping determinations from reclamação denials accumulate into a public doctrinal record. Legitimated parties under art. 103-A, §2º, CF — including the Attorney General and the Federal Council of the Bar — can initiate formal revision proceedings informed by this record. The STF's own reclamação jurisprudence is a structural input into the formal deliberative context in which súmula revision occurs. The channel from reclamação dialogue to formal revision is mediated but not purely discretionary: the STF acts on a deliberative record that its own reclamação reasoning has built.

**On art. 927, §4º as independent textual support:** Art. 927, §4º requires that modifications to established jurisprudence be "fundamentadas de maneira adequada e específica, considerando os princípios da segurança jurídica, da proteção da confiança e da isonomia." When the STF's reclamação judgment affirms a súmula's application to a class of cases or delimits the súmula's scope by genuine distinction, it produces a jurisprudential statement about that class. This output is subject to art. 927, §4º's requirements independently of whether art. 489, §1º, IV's compliance/merit question generates an engagement obligation. Departure reasons bearing on the adequacy of the jurisprudential output trigger §4º.

**On dialogue theory:** The hierarchy objection is answered on different planes. Hierarchy determines *who has the last word* (the STF). Symmetry determines *how that last word is reached* — by reasoning, not by positional reaffirmation. The STF can have the final authority and still be required to earn it through genuine engagement with the arguments that reach it.

## 3. The Attack

### 3.1 The Reclamação Is a Compliance Proceeding, Not a Merit Proceeding

The reclamação constitucional has a defined constitutional purpose: "preservar a competência do Tribunal e garantir a autoridade de suas decisões" (art. 102, I, "l", CF). Its statutory framework (art. 988-993, CPC) confirms: the remedy lies when a lower court decision contrariar a binding precedent or usurp the STF's jurisdiction. The proceeding asks whether the lower court's decision is *compatible with* the existing binding norm.

This compliance-verification function is structurally different from the judicial activity art. 489, §1º, IV was designed to regulate. Art. 489, §1º, IV applies when a court is exercising *substantive jurisdiction* — reaching a conclusion on a disputed legal or factual question. The engagement obligation targets arguments "capable of undermining the conclusion" reached in the exercise of that jurisdiction. The conclusion is a substantive outcome on a contested matter: the court reaches position P on question Q, and an argument A is capable of undermining P when A, if engaged, would change the court's position on Q.

In a reclamação, the STF is not reaching a conclusion about the underlying substantive dispute. It is determining whether the lower court's decision deviates from a norm the STF has already established. The reclamação's conclusion is not "the law on question Q is P" — it is "the lower court decision is (or is not) compatible with the binding norm N." Question Q (the substantive merit of the legal issue) was addressed when the binding precedent was created. The reclamação does not reopen question Q; it applies norm N to the lower court's deviation.

The paper assumes that the inferior court's reasons for departing from the precedent are "arguments capable of undermining the conclusion" within the meaning of art. 489, §1º, IV. But the conclusion of the reclamação proceeding is about compliance (question A: did the departure occur? was it formally adequate?), not about the substantive correctness of the precedent (question B: is the precedent right?). The inferior court's departure reasons bear on question B, not question A. Art. 489, §1º, IV requires engagement with arguments bearing on the court's *conclusion*. In a reclamação, the conclusion is question A. Arguments addressed exclusively to question B do not bear on the reclamação's conclusion in the procedurally relevant sense.

The compliance structure is not incidental. The reclamação was constitutionally designed as a mechanism for *authority preservation*, not for authority revision. A proceeding whose constitutional purpose is to preserve authority cannot simultaneously be required, as a matter of art. 489, §1º, IV, to entertain arguments that the authority being preserved is wrong. These constitutional purposes are in structural tension: the authority-preservation function is undermined when the proceeding is converted into a mandatory re-examination of the underlying authority's merit.

### 3.2 Form Adequacy and Substantive Correctness Are Distinct Questions the Compliance Proceeding Need Not Conflate

The paper's strongest move — the adequacy argument for reasoned departures — holds that determining whether a distinction demonstration is genuine (not pretextual) requires examining the súmula's underlying reasoning, making questions A and B inseparable. This argument conflates two distinct levels of assessment that the compliance proceeding can and should keep separate.

**Level 1 (formal adequacy):** Did the inferior court provide a departure demonstration of the type art. 489, §1º, VI requires? A complying demonstration identifies specific features, applies legal analysis to those features, and concludes they constitute a distinction or a supersession argument. Level 1 asks whether this *type* of showing was provided — whether the departure argument is coherently structured as a distinction attempt relative to the súmula's established scope.

**Level 2 (substantive correctness):** Is the distinction substantively correct? Do the identified features actually track differences in the súmula's underlying reasoning — constituting a genuine distinction from the súmula's paradigm cases rather than a pretextual one?

The compliance proceeding addresses Level 1; the reversal proceeding addresses Level 2. This structural separation is standard in judicial review of procedural requirements: a court assessing whether a required showing has been made determines whether the required type of showing was provided, not whether the showing's substantive conclusion is right. The showing's correctness is the merits question, determined separately.

Applied to the reclamação: the STF's compliance assessment determines whether the inferior court's departure was formally adequate — whether a genuine-distinction *type* of argument was provided — not whether the distinction was substantively correct. A departure that identifies features F₁, F₂ and argues that they constitute a genuine distinction has satisfied Level 1 whether or not the STF agrees that F₁, F₂ substantively distinguish the case from the súmula. When the STF grants the reclamação and cassas the decision, it acts on a Level 2 assessment reached through the reversal proceeding, not through the compliance assessment. The compliance proceeding determines form; the reversal proceeding determines substance.

The adequacy argument responds that even determining whether a distinction argument is coherent — not merely present — requires examining the súmula's substance. This correctly identifies that a pure presence test (was any departure argument provided?) can be satisfied by formally structured but completely irrelevant arguments. But the response overspecifies what coherence assessment requires. The STF in the compliance proceeding determines whether the offered distinction argument *coherently positions itself relative to the súmula's established scope* — not whether the distinction's substantive position is correct. An argument that identifies features the súmula expressly addresses and claims they constitute a distinction is not formally adequate — not because the distinction is wrong, but because the argument is structurally incoherent as a compliance attempt under this súmula. This coherence assessment is narrower than engaging departure reasons as arguments for changing the precedent.

For the harder cases — where the inferior court offers features not expressly addressed by the súmula, requiring examination of the súmula's ratio to assess coherence — the compliance question remains bounded differently from the reversal question. The compliance proceeding asks: does the offered distinction argument cohere with this súmula's scope as established? The reversal proceeding asks: is the inferior court's substantive claim about the súmula's scope correct? Coherence with established scope can be assessed without reaching the correctness of an alternative scope claim. The departure reasons are arguments about the precedent's correct scope — question B. They are not arguments that the departure was formally inadequate — question A. Art. 489, §1º, IV requires engagement with question A arguments; question B arguments do not fall within the compliance conclusion's scope.

The denial-case structural argument holds that for genuine-distinction denials, the Level 1/Level 2 deferral mechanism cannot operate: no reversal proceeding follows a reclamação denial within the reclamação framework. Therefore, either the denial produces a genuine scope determination (Level 2 engagement in the denial itself), or it produces no substantive scoping content at all. Since the adversarial paper has conceded that distinction denials produce "scoping clarifications," the argument contends that Level 1 boundedness is unavailable.

This argument misidentifies where the relevant downstream proceeding is located. The Level 1/Level 2 framework does not require a Level 2 proceeding to occur *within* the reclamação. The Level 2 question — whether the inferior court's substantive scope claim was correct — is addressed in ordinary appellate review of the inferior court's underlying decision. The reclamação is a threshold compliance check, not the exclusive vehicle for reviewing the inferior court's decision. When the STF denies the reclamação on genuine-distinction grounds, the inferior court's decision stands provisionally — but remains subject to whatever ordinary appellate routes apply to the underlying case, including extraordinary review where the underlying decision presents constitutional questions. The compliance denial determines only that the form requirement was satisfied; it does not adjudicate the substantive merit of the underlying claims or close the ordinary appellate routes through which the Level 2 question is reached.

The scoping clarification that distinction denials produce is Level 1 output, not Level 2 output. When the STF holds that the inferior court's distinction argument was formally adequate — that the argument was a coherent attempt invoking features the súmula's ratio could plausibly address — this communicates type-level information: what kinds of argument satisfy the form requirement for this súmula. A Level 2 scope determination would hold that the inferior court's substantive scope claim is *correct* — that the identified features genuinely take the case outside the súmula's coverage. Level 1 scoping (what argument-types satisfy the form requirement?) and Level 2 scoping (what facts are substantively outside the súmula's coverage?) are structurally distinct outputs. The first follows from a compliance determination; the second requires a merit determination. The distinction denial communicates only the former.

### 3.3 The Inference from Art. 489's Scope Does Not Reach the Symmetry Conclusion

The paper argues: art. 489 applies to the STF without exception; therefore art. 489, §1º, IV applies to the STF's reclamação judgment; therefore the STF must engage with the inferior court's departure reasons. Both premises are correct. The conclusion does not follow.

Whether art. 489, §1º, IV is triggered for a specific STF judgment depends not on who is deciding but on what type of judicial activity the judgment represents and which arguments bear on its conclusion. The paper establishes that art. 489 applies to the STF; it does not establish that departure reasons are arguments "capable of undermining the conclusion" of a reclamação proceeding. That conclusion requires the additional premise that departure reasons bear on the compliance conclusion — which §§3.1 and 3.2 establish is false.

The paper's §3.3 correctly notes that hierarchy and symmetry operate on different planes: hierarchy determines who decides last; symmetry determines the reasoning standard for reaching that decision. This is accepted. But neither hierarchy nor symmetry determines *which arguments* fall within the engagement obligation for a given proceeding. Determining which arguments are "capable of undermining the conclusion" requires identifying the proceeding's actual conclusion. For a compliance proceeding, the relevant arguments are formal-adequacy arguments (question A). Arguments about the precedent's substantive correctness (question B) do not bear on the compliance conclusion.

### 3.4 The Distinction Mechanism Does Not Generate Binding Precedent Under Art. 927

The paper's structural-feedback-loop argument builds from the adversarial paper's concession that the STF can deny a reclamação on genuine distinction grounds and that the denial "produces the scoping clarification" within the proceeding. The supportive argument draws a binding-precedent consequence: this scoping clarification generates a general rule — the súmula does not apply to cases with features F₁, F₂, ..., Fₙ — binding on future inferior courts.

This binding-precedent claim fails against the mandatory-observance framework of art. 927, CPC. Art. 927 specifies the sources that inferior courts are required to observe: (I) decisions of the STF in concentrated constitutional control; (II) full-court STF súmulas and STJ súmulas; (III) decisions in recursos repetitivos (STF and STJ); (IV) IRDR and IAC decisions; (V) summary opinions of plenary and special full courts. A reclamação denial on distinction grounds appears in none of these categories. It produces res judicata for the parties to that specific proceeding but does not generate the mandatory observance that art. 927 imposes on inferior courts.

The feedback loop therefore produces informal persuasive precedent, not binding precedent in the art. 927 mandatory-observance sense. Future inferior courts are not required under art. 927 to follow a reclamação denial's scoping holding; they may distinguish the reclamação denial itself. The adversarial paper's characterization — informal channel, not structural mechanism — is confirmed by the art. 927 enumeration.

The binding-authority argument might be reformulated: even without formal art. 927 status, STF reclamação denials function as de facto binding authority through the institutional deference that inferior courts extend to STF reasoning. This reformulation concedes the structural claim while asserting practical equivalence. The concession is the point. A channel that operates through variable institutional deference — which inferior courts may extend differently across jurisdictions, judges, and subject matters — is an informal channel, not a structural design feature of the system. Thesis 3's claim is that the reclamação is the *designed* vehicle for institutional dialogue; a designed vehicle operates through procedural necessity, not through the informal deference that the STF's institutional authority produces.

There is also a circularity problem the structural-loop argument cannot resolve. For distinction denials to generate scope-defining precedent — even informal persuasive precedent — the denials must articulate *which* features distinguish the case and *why*. This requires the STF to engage the inferior court's departure reasoning substantively in the denial judgment. But Paper 1D's Thesis 2 asserts that the STF currently fails to provide this engagement in reclamação judgments — the plenário virtual practice produces brief affirmances and denials that do not engage the departure reasoning. A reclamação denial stating "o caso é distinguível" without specifying which features and why generates no scope-defining content, even informally. The structural feedback loop requires the STF to already be doing what Thesis 2 says it is not doing. The structural claim is conditional on the normative prescription being adopted, not on the current system's operation. The defense accepts this characterization: if the compliance/merit distinction in §§3.1–3.3 is correct, Thesis 2 fails and Thesis 3 is conditional on an unestablished legal requirement. The adversarial camp accepts the reframing. The circularity observation is not an independent argument against Thesis 3; it identifies the dependency structure — Thesis 3's designed-capacity claim holds only if Thesis 2's interpretation of art. 489, §1º, IV is correct. Whether the compliance/merit distinction is correct is the central adversarial question, addressed in §§3.1–3.3.

The defense's art. 927, II reframing holds that following a súmula under art. 927, II extends to following it as authoritatively determined — including the STF's reclamação-denial scope holdings. An inferior court applying the súmula to features the STF has held fall outside the súmula's scope misapplies the súmula itself, violating art. 927, II directly. Art. 926 independently requires inferior courts to maintain consistent jurisprudência with the STF's established scope determinations.

Both arguments rest on a participation inference — that the STF's holdings about how a listed source applies inherit the source's mandatory-observance status — that art. 927's enumeration structure does not support. Art. 927, II makes full-court STF súmulas mandatory-observance sources. The provision does not make every STF case-by-case determination of how the súmula applies to specific facts a mandatory-observance source for future inferior courts. The STF issues thousands of reclamação compliance assessments annually through the plenário virtual, each involving a determination of whether specific case features fall within a súmula's scope. If each inherited the súmula's art. 927, II mandatory-observance status through the participation inference, the enumeration's limiting function would be nullified: any holding about any listed source would automatically carry the source's binding status. The CPC 2015's art. 927 enumeration exists precisely to delimit which STF outputs carry mandatory-observance effect; the participation inference dissolves that limit.

The standard distinction in Brazilian procedural law between res judicata and mandatory-observance precedent tracks this boundary directly. A reclamação-denial holding generates res judicata for the parties in that case — the scope determination binds the reclamante and the reclamada as to those case facts. It does not generate mandatory-observance precedent for future inferior courts facing different fact-configurations. Whether STF reclamação jurisprudence functions in practice as persuasive authority for how inferior courts apply a súmula is an empirical observation about informal influence; whether inferior courts are legally required to follow it under art. 927, II is a structural question the participation inference does not answer.

Art. 926's consistency obligation establishes a quality standard without altering the mandatory-observance boundary. The consistency obligation requires courts to maintain stable, coherent, and integral jurisprudência — a requirement that an inferior court following STF reclamação-denial scope holdings satisfies, and that an inferior court departing from them must explain. This is the lesser obligation that persuasive authority generates in any court system. It is structurally different from the mandatory observance art. 927 imposes, which requires following the listed sources regardless of the court's own substantive assessment. Art. 926 generates consistency obligations; it does not elevate persuasive authority to mandatory-observance status.

### 3.5 The Deliberative Accumulation Bridge Is Informal Throughout

The paper adds a path from reclamação dialogue to formal revision through deliberative accumulation: scoping precedents from reclamação denials accumulate into a public doctrinal record; legitimated parties under art. 103-A, §2º, CF initiate formal revision informed by this record; the STF acts on the deliberative context its own reclamação reasoning has built. This bridge, accepted as described, does not transform the channel into a structural mechanism.

The accumulation problem compounds from §3.4. If reclamação denials are not binding under art. 927, accumulated denials produce a record of informal persuasive precedents — not a record of binding scoping determinations. The deliberative weight of the accumulated record is no greater than the persuasive weight of any collection of non-mandatory STF reasoning. Presenting this record to support a revision proposal is presenting persuasive authority, not a binding doctrinal requirement for revision.

The bridge also requires voluntary action at two steps the system does not mandate. First, legitimated parties under art. 103-A, §2º exercise discretion about whether and when to initiate revision proceedings. Nothing in art. 103-A, §2º, in Lei 11.417/2006, or in the CPC mandates that these parties initiate revision when accumulated reclamação jurisprudence reveals a compelling case. The Attorney General and the Federal Council of the Bar act on their institutional priorities, timing assessments, and doctrinal judgments — not on a procedural obligation triggered by accumulated reclamação records. Second, the STF in formal revision proceedings acts on a deliberative context that includes academic doctrine, legislative signals, constitutional developments, and international law — not exclusively or primarily on reclamação jurisprudence. Reclamação jurisprudence is one input among many, with no structural priority in the revision proceeding.

The characterization of this chain as "structurally mediated" conflates two different senses of structure. Structure in the sense Thesis 3 requires means *procedural necessity*: the system is designed such that compelling arguments in the reclamação trigger mandatory consequences in formal revision proceedings. The deliberative accumulation bridge offers: arguments may reach formal revision through the choices of legitimated parties who exercise discretion about initiation, informed by a record that has persuasive weight. This is informal throughout — a path by which arguments can travel if institutional actors choose to carry them, not a designed channel that carries them by procedural necessity.

### 3.6 Art. 927, §4º Governs Modification, Not Application

Art. 927, §4º requires that "a modificação de enunciado de súmula, de jurisprudência pacificada ou de tese jurídica adotada em julgamento de casos repetitivos observará a necessidade de fundamentação adequada e específica, considerando os princípios da segurança jurídica, da proteção da confiança e da isonomia."

The provision's operative term is *modificação* — modification. A reclamação denial on distinction grounds is not a modification of the súmula. It is an *application*: the STF determines, for this case, whether the súmula's established scope covers the presented facts and finds that it does not. The súmula's text and ratio remain unchanged after the denial. The scope clarification the paper attributes to distinction denials is not a change in the norm; it is a determination about how the existing norm applies — application, not modification.

The paper reads §4º as covering "jurisprudential statements about the súmula's application." This reading is untenable. If every reclamação judgment constituted a *modificação* requiring §4º's standards — "adequate and specific reasoning considering principles of legal certainty, protection of reliance, and isonomy" — the STF would be unable to process reclamações at the required scale. The STF resolves thousands of reclamações annually; §4º's standard is reserved for deliberate norm changes, not for the application of existing norms to specific compliance questions. A provision that triggers for every compliance-proceeding application would disable the mechanism.

The provision's enumeration confirms the scope. It specifically names "enunciado de súmula, jurisprudência pacificada, tese jurídica adotada em julgamento de casos repetitivos" — these are all established norms that the court holds and is changing. A reclamação denial does not change any of these; it applies them. The provision is inapplicable to reclamação judgments as such.

### 3.7 The Practice Gap Reflects the Reclamação's Correct Function

The paper frames the STF's practice — authority reaffirmation without engagement, plenário virtual processing — as a pathological deviation from what existing law requires. This framing is available only if the symmetry thesis correctly describes the existing law's requirements.

The alternative reading is more consistent with the institutional design: the STF's compliance-only review reflects a correct understanding of the reclamação's compliance function. Compliance mechanisms are designed for efficiency — to determine quickly whether the lower court deviated from the norm, and if so, to restore compliance. Efficient compliance mechanisms do not re-open the merit of the norm in each proceeding; that would convert every compliance mechanism into a rolling re-examination of the underlying authority. The STF's reclamação judgments — identifying the binding norm, assessing whether the departure was compliant, ordering cassação when non-compliant — constitute substantive reasoning about the compliance question. They are not failures to reason; they are reasoning addressed to the correct question.

The efficiency explanation the paper offers for plenário virtual brevity addresses the procedural form, not the substantive pattern. Efficiency preference explains why the STF uses the plenário virtual procedure; it does not explain why the STF has consistently addressed only the compliance question across different cases, bench compositions, and legal areas over decades. This substantive pattern reflects institutional understanding of the reclamação's function — not merely a preference for procedural speed that coexists with a different constitutional understanding of what the proceeding requires.

The contestation in procedural doctrine identifies a live scholarly debate, not a resolution in the paper's favor. Paper 1D's burden is to show that the established practice incorrectly understands the reclamação's scope. Citing scholarly criticism as evidence the practice is wrong assumes what needs to be proven.

The scale of the claimed nullity is also evidence. If Thesis 2 correctly describes existing law, virtually every plenário virtual reclamação judgment against a reasoned departure would be null under art. 489, §1º, IV — a finding of extraordinary scope that Brazilian procedural doctrine has not endorsed and that the STF's own decisions do not reflect. Established practices can be systematically wrong; but the pervasiveness and consistency of the practice across the institution is evidence against the symmetry reading.

### 3.8 The Load-Bearing Connection to Paper 1B

Paper 1D's Thesis 3 is the institutional framework that renders Paper 1B's Exit 4 (rational supersession by inferior courts) intelligible as a legitimate practice rather than mere jurisdictional defiance. If the reclamação is genuinely a dialogue channel where good arguments generate precedent evolution, Exit 4's rational supersession is a contribution to that dialogue — not disobedience, but input to an iterative process. Paper 1D supplies the systemic justification that makes Exit 4 something other than a claim that inferior courts have authority to override the STF.

The existing adversarial attack on Paper 1B (`otherwise/paper1b-rational-supersession.md`) identifies an inference gap in Exit 4: art. 489, §1º, VI establishes when a departure is unfounded (form condition) without establishing substantive authorization for inferior courts to override binding STF precedents. Paper 1D is, in effect, the response to this inference gap: the justification for Exit 4's departures derives not from a single provision but from the systemic structure of the dialogue — a structure in which the reclamação channels inferior-court arguments to the STF for authoritative resolution.

The adversarial paper for Paper 1B has established that the transparent-contestation reconceptualization of Exit 4 does not rescue Exit 4 as a *saída legítima* in the taxonomy's legal-authorization sense: Exit 4 produces a decision contrary to binding law, however well-reasoned. Paper 1D's Thesis 3 was the systemic justification that could convert this unauthorized departure into a constructive institutional contribution. If the reclamação cannot carry the dialogue function Paper 1D assigns — because the reclamação is a compliance mechanism not generating mandatory engagement with departure reasons, and because distinction denials do not produce binding scope-precedent — then Exit 4's systemic justification remains structurally unsupported. The departure with reasons is a form-compliant act that the STF is not required to engage through the reclamação channel. The channel is informal, the engagement is discretionary, and the dialogue is the STF's choice — not the system's design.

Note on decoupling: The supportive defense of Paper 1B (`yesindeed/paper1b-exit4-defense.md`) does not rely on Paper 1D's reclamação structure for Exit 4's systemic value. It grounds Exit 4's contribution in the "information-richness" the inferior court's record provides for the STF's eventual deliberation — information that reaches the STF when it reviews and reverses the Exit 4 departure in the ordinary appellate proceeding. If the supportive Paper 1B defense is correct that Exit 4's information-richness function operates independently of the reclamação mechanism, the Paper 1D attack does not automatically transfer to Paper 1B. That independent question — whether the STF's reversal proceeding generates a procedural obligation to engage the Exit 4 departure reasons — is addressed in the Paper 1B adversarial paper directly.

## 4. Anticipated Reply and Why It Does Not Suffice

### 4.1 The Art. 93, IX, CF Response

The paper's §3.1 invokes art. 93, IX, CF as independent constitutional support for the engagement obligation. Even if art. 489, §1º, IV applies differently to compliance proceedings, the constitutional requirement of reasoned decisions applies to the STF unconditionally.

This reply does not suffice. Art. 93, IX, CF requires that decisions be "fundamentadas" — a standard satisfied by reasoning about the question the proceeding actually resolves. When the STF's reclamação judgment identifies the binding norm, assesses whether the lower court's departure was formally adequate, and orders cassação when non-compliant, it is reasoning about the compliance question. This satisfies the constitutional reasoned-decision requirement as applied to the compliance proceeding's actual subject. Art. 93, IX, CF does not require the STF to reason about every question a party raises — only about the questions the proceeding is designed to resolve. Departure reasons address question B (the precedent's correctness); the compliance proceeding is designed to resolve question A (formal adequacy of the departure). The constitutional duty to reason does not independently extend the compliance proceeding's scope to question B.

### 4.2 The Hierarchy/Symmetry Distinction

The paper's §3.3 correctly distinguishes hierarchy (who decides last) from symmetry (how they get there): the STF can have the last word and still be required to justify that word through engagement, not positional reaffirmation. This is accepted.

The distinction does not rescue Thesis 2 because the attack does not challenge the general symmetry of reasoning obligations — it challenges which arguments fall within the engagement obligation for the reclamação's specific conclusion. The hierarchy/symmetry distinction establishes that the STF must reason; it does not establish that reasoning about the compliance conclusion requires engagement with arguments about the precedent's substantive correctness. The STF reasons about the compliance conclusion by identifying the norm, assessing the departure's formal adequacy, and explaining the cassação determination. This satisfies the symmetry standard without engaging question B.

### 4.3 On the Coherence/Correctness Distinction

The most sophisticated response to §3.2 presses that the coherence/correctness distinction is unavailable in practice. Determining whether a distinction argument coherently positions itself relative to the súmula's established scope — the Level 1 assessment the adversarial paper attributes to the compliance proceeding — requires examining the súmula's ratio to assess which features it is "indifferent to." This examination is the question-B inquiry that §3.2 claims is reserved for the reversal proceeding.

The reply correctly identifies that coherence assessment in hard cases involves some examination of the súmula's ratio. But the examination is bounded differently from the reversal-proceeding inquiry. The compliance proceeding asks: does the offered distinction argument cohere with the súmula's scope as established by the STF? The reversal proceeding asks: is the inferior court's substantive claim about the súmula's scope correct? The first question can be answered by examining the súmula's established scope — what it already says and has already been held to mean — without adjudicating a new claim about what the scope *should* be. The departure reasons are arguments that the scope *should* be different; this is the question-B claim reserved for the reversal proceeding.

When the STF grants the reclamação against a reasoned departure, it is not required to explain why the departure's substantive argument is wrong — only why the departure was non-compliant with the binding norm. The departure's substantive argument becomes relevant to the reversal proceeding on the underlying case, where the STF determines whether the inferior court's alternative scope claim is correct. The compliance determination and the substantive determination are sequential, not merged.

The denial-case structural argument — the most pointed version of the Level 1/Level 2 challenge — holds that no reversal proceeding follows a distinction denial within the reclamação framework. The adversarial paper cannot simultaneously acknowledge scoping clarifications and maintain Level 1 boundedness; a scoping clarification must be a Level 2 output if it is to have any content.

The argument rests on locating the Level 2 proceeding within the reclamação framework, which is not what the distinction requires. The Level 2 proceeding for the scope question — was the inferior court's substantive scope claim correct? — is ordinary appellate review of the inferior court's underlying decision on the case's merits. When the STF denies the reclamação on genuine-distinction grounds, the inferior court's decision stands, but the ordinary appellate routes for the underlying case remain available. The Level 2 question is addressed in whatever extraordinary review proceeding reaches the merits of the underlying case. The compliance denial determines form adequacy; the ordinary appellate proceeding determines substantive correctness. These are separate proceedings answering separate questions; the Level 1/Level 2 deferral mechanism operates between them.

The scoping clarification from a distinction denial is Level 1 output: it communicates what types of argument are formally adequate distinction attempts for this súmula, not whether the inferior court's substantive scope claim was correct. Future parties can use the Level 1 scoping information — arguments of this type satisfy the form requirement — without being told that the inferior court's scope claim was substantively right. That determination is reserved for the ordinary appellate merit proceeding. Level 1 scoping (what argument-types are adequate?) and Level 2 scoping (what facts are outside the súmula's coverage?) address different questions; the inference that scoping clarifications from compliance denials are Level 2 outputs conflates these distinct operations.

### 4.4 On the De Facto Binding-Authority Argument

The binding-precedent failure identified in §3.4 may be addressed by arguing that de facto deference to STF reclamação reasoning is sufficient for the structural claim. This argument concedes that reclamação denials are not formally binding under art. 927, while asserting practical equivalence through institutional deference.

The concession is the point. Thesis 3 claims the reclamação is a *structural* channel — designed into the system's procedural architecture. A channel that operates through variable institutional deference rather than through procedural necessity is an informal channel by any consistent definition of "structural." The argument for de facto equivalence recharacterizes the informal channel as practically sufficient without rescuing the structural-design claim. The adversarial paper accepts that an informal channel exists; it contests the structural-channel characterization Thesis 3 requires.

The defense reformulates the binding-force argument through art. 927, II and art. 926 rather than through de facto deference. The art. 927, II argument holds that following a súmula under art. 927, II extends to following it as authoritatively determined: a reclamação-denial scope holding participates in the súmula's binding force; an inferior court applying the súmula to features the STF has held distinguishing misapplies the súmula itself, violating art. 927, II. Art. 926 independently requires consistent jurisprudência with the STF's established scope positions.

The participation inference is the argument's load-bearing structure, and it does not follow from art. 927's text or enumeration. Art. 927, II lists the súmula as a binding source; it does not contain a principle that every STF holding about how the listed source applies inherits the source's mandatory-observance status. If the participation inference held, the enumeration's limiting function — which is the provision's central structural role in the CPC 2015 system — would be nullified: any STF determination about any listed source would automatically carry that source's mandatory-observance status. The full analysis is at §3.4; the same considerations apply here.

Art. 926's consistency argument correctly identifies a real institutional obligation without generating the mandatory-observance status that the structural-channel claim requires. A mechanism that operates through consistency obligations — which courts satisfy by following, or may override with adequate explanation — is not the structural procedural-necessity channel Thesis 3 claims. The adversarial camp's account of the reclamação as an informal channel operating through institutional deference and persuasive authority is fully consistent with art. 926's consistency obligations. That art. 926 generates a pattern of informal influence — inferior courts being consistent with STF reclamação-denial scope holdings — is exactly what the adversarial paper describes as an informal channel, not as a structural designed mechanism.

### 4.5 The Normative Prescription Reading

The paper might be read as lex ferenda rather than lex lata — a proposal for how the system should work, not a claim that the existing provisions already require symmetry. On this reading, the inference-error attack is misdirected: the paper proposes that the reclamação should carry the symmetry obligation, not that it currently does.

This recharacterization is available but substantially weakens the thesis. If the symmetry claim is aspirational, then the institutional dialogue described in Thesis 3 is not an existing structural feature — it is a proposed reform. The reform proposal would face the institutional-design objection: the reclamação's constitutional purpose under art. 102, I, "l", CF is authority preservation, and expanding it to mandatory engagement with departure reasons as merit arguments would require either constitutional amendment or legislative reform linking compelling reclamação arguments to mandatory revision initiation. The paper does not argue for this reform; it argues from texts already in force. If the argument is aspirational, the textual basis it invokes does not support it.

## 5. Scope of the Attack

This attack targets:

- **Thesis 2:** The symmetry of the duty of reasoning as applied to the STF in reclamação proceedings when the inferior court has exposed departure reasons. The engagement obligation under art. 489, §1º, IV for the reclamação's compliance conclusion does not extend to departure reasons bearing on the precedent's substantive correctness (question B), because: (a) the compliance proceeding's conclusion is about formal adequacy (question A); (b) form adequacy and substantive correctness are distinct assessments that the compliance proceeding can and should keep separate; (c) departure reasons are question-B arguments, not question-A arguments.

- **Thesis 3:** The institutional dialogue claim, insofar as it requires the reclamação to be a *structural* channel generating mandatory or quasi-mandatory precedent evolution. The reclamação's distinction-denial outputs are not binding precedent sources under art. 927; the deliberative accumulation bridge requires two voluntary steps that no provision mandates; and art. 927, §4º governs modification, not application. The dialogue that Thesis 3 describes operates through informal persuasion and institutional deference — not through procedural necessity designed into the reclamação framework.

This attack does not contest:

- **Thesis 1:** The paper correctly identifies that art. 489, §1º, VI establishes a form obligation, not a result prohibition. The paper's critique of practice that treats departures as categorically prohibited regardless of reasoning quality is well-grounded.
- The characterization of the current argumentative equilibrium and the costs of rational dialogue, which the analysis in Paper 1E also addresses.
- The paper's invocation of institutional dialogue theory as a normative aspiration — the attack is about whether the reclamação is the specific vehicle for structural dialogue, not whether dialogue is a valuable goal.
- The paper's doctrinal claim that art. 489, §1º, IV applies to the STF without exception.

## 6. Surrender Conditions

The attack would not hold, or would hold only partially, under the following conditions.

1. **A constitutional or statutory provision establishes that the reclamação is a proceeding where the STF must address the substantive merit of the inferior court's departure reasons, not merely verify formal compliance.** If the reclamação's constitutional purpose were established to include merit re-examination of departure quality alongside compliance verification, the compliance/merit distinction in §3.1 would be unavailable. This would require either a direct reading of art. 102, I, "l", CF or an established doctrinal framework extending reclamação's scope to merit review.

2. **A STF precedent or established doctrine holds that reclamação against reasoned departures must address the quality of the reasoning, not merely the fact of the departure.** If the STF's own jurisprudence recognized the §1º, IV engagement obligation with respect to departure reasons in reclamação proceedings, the adversarial paper's reliance on practice as evidence of correct constitutional understanding would require revision.

3. **A procedural mechanism connecting compelling reclamação arguments to mandatory revision-initiation is established or identified.** If the reclamação proceeding already has a formal pathway by which a compelling argument in a reclamação denial triggers mandatory initiation of the revision procedure, Thesis 3's structural-channel claim would have procedural support.

4. **Art. 927, CPC is read to include reclamação denials as binding precedent sources.** If reclamação denials on distinction grounds qualify as mandatory-observance precedent under art. 927 — requiring inferior courts to follow the scoping holdings — the §3.4 analysis requires revision and the structural feedback loop gains the formal-binding-authority structure Thesis 3 requires. The art. 927, II / art. 926 reframing offered by the defense does not meet this condition: the participation inference (scope holdings about a listed source inherit the source's mandatory-observance status) is not supported by the enumeration's structure, and the art. 926 consistency obligation generates a lesser duty that does not elevate persuasive authority to mandatory-observance status. Meeting this surrender condition requires either Brazilian procedural scholarship or STF jurisprudence establishing that reclamação-denial scope holdings produce mandatory-observance effect for future inferior courts as an independent doctrinal matter, or a textual argument that art. 927's enumeration encompasses reclamação denial holdings without the participation inference.

5. **The paper's normative prescription is recharacterized as lex ferenda with a supporting reform proposal.** If the paper argues that the reclamação's scope should be expanded through legislative or constitutional reform to incorporate mandatory engagement with departure reasons and to link compelling arguments to revision initiation, the inference-error attack is misdirected. The reform proposal would then be the proper object of analysis; the institutional-design arguments against expanding the reclamação into a merit re-examination mechanism would be the appropriate adversarial response.
