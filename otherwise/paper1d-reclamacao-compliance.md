# The Reclamação Cannot Carry the Symmetry: A Compliance-Verification Constraint on Paper 1D's Dialogue Thesis

---

## 1. Thesis Attacked

"Vinculação Racional e Diálogo Institucional: o Dever de Fundamentação como Limite Simétrico no Sistema Brasileiro de Precedentes" (Baldo, 2025; `paper1D_vinculacao_racional_dialogo_institucional.md`) defends three connected theses about the Brazilian binding-precedent system.

**Thesis 1 (form, not result):** Art. 489, §1º, VI, CPC does not prohibit an inferior court from departing from a binding precedent; it requires that any departure be reasoned. A court that departs with reasons exposed operates within the system; a court that departs without reasons commits a formal defect.

**Thesis 2 (symmetry):** The duty of reasoning under art. 489, §1º, is symmetric. It applies to the inferior court departing from the precedent and equally to the STF judging the reclamação against that departure. The STF must engage with the reasons exposed by the inferior court — it cannot respond with mere reaffirmation of precedent authority. Art. 489, §1º, IV's obligation to engage arguments capable of undermining the conclusion applies to the STF's reclamação judgment as it does to any other decisão judicial.

**Thesis 3 (institutional dialogue):** This symmetric arrangement produces rational institutional dialogue. The reclamação is not a silencing mechanism but a channel through which arguments that withstand confrontation reach the court competent to revise the precedent. The STF either reaffirms with substantive engagement or distinguishes or revises; the súmula is a proposition maintained while it withstands the best available argument, not an immune truth.

Theses 2 and 3 are the paper's novel contribution. Thesis 1 is shared with the broader literature on art. 489, §1º, VI. This attack targets Thesis 2 and the institutional-dialogue claim in Thesis 3 that depends on it.

---

## 2. Faithful Reconstruction

The paper's strongest case builds on three interlocking claims.

**On form versus result:** The legislature chose to regulate the form of departure — not to prohibit departure. If the legislature had intended prohibition, it would have enacted "é vedado ao juiz deixar de seguir precedente vinculante." Instead, art. 489, §1º, VI establishes that a departure without demonstration of distinction or supersession is *unfounded* — a form defect. The contrapositive is clear: a departure with demonstration is not unfounded. The binding-precedent system achieves uniformity by requiring reasons, not by silencing dissent.

**On symmetry:** Art. 489 addresses "decisão judicial" without carving out any exception for the STF. Art. 93, IX, CF requires all judicial decisions to be reasoned under penalty of nullity; the STF is subject to this constitutional mandate. When the STF judges a reclamação against a departure that has exposed reasons, those reasons are arguments capable of undermining the conclusion of the proceeding. The STF that simply reaffirms precedent authority without engaging the reasons violates art. 489, §1º, IV. The paper acknowledges that this prescribes against current practice; it treats current practice as a pathological deviation from what the existing provisions already require.

**On dialogue:** The reclamação's function on this reading is not merely to cassar but to route argument to the competent authority. The STF in judgment faces two options when the inferior court's argument is strong: (a) distinguish — acknowledging the case is outside the súmula's scope and maintaining the súmula with a redefined range; (b) revise — canceling or revising the súmula when the argument reveals a real error or normative descompasso. The feedback loop runs: inferior court raises argument → reclamação filed → STF engages and distinguishes or revises → new precedent produced. The súmula is a presumption strong enough to require qualified argument to overcome, not a truth immune to challenge.

The paper handles the hierarchy objection directly (§3.3): hierarchy and symmetry of duties operate on different planes. Hierarchy determines *who has the last word* (the STF does). Symmetry determines *how that last word is reached* (by reasoning, not by positional reaffirmation). These are compatible. The STF can have the last word and still be required to earn it through engagement.

## 3. The Attack

### 3.1 The Reclamação Is a Compliance Proceeding, Not a Merit Proceeding

The reclamação constitucional has a defined constitutional purpose: "preservar a competência do Tribunal e garantir a autoridade de suas decisões" (art. 102, I, "l", CF). Its statutory framework (art. 988-993, CPC) confirms: the remedy lies when a lower court decision contrariar a binding precedent or usurp the STF's jurisdiction. The proceeding asks whether the lower court's decision is *compatible with* the existing binding norm.

This compliance-verification function is structurally different from the judicial activity art. 489, §1º, IV was designed to regulate. Art. 489, §1º, IV applies when a court is exercising *substantive jurisdiction* — reaching a conclusion on a disputed legal or factual question. The engagement obligation targets arguments "capable of undermining the conclusion" reached in the exercise of that jurisdiction. The conclusion is a substantive outcome on a contested matter: the court reaches position P on question Q, and an argument A is capable of undermining P when A, if engaged, would change the court's position on Q.

In a reclamação, the STF is not reaching a conclusion about the underlying substantive dispute. It is determining whether the lower court's decision deviates from a norm the STF has already established. The reclamação's conclusion is not "the law on question Q is P" — it is "the lower court decision is (or is not) compatible with the binding norm N." These are different questions. Question Q (the substantive merit of the legal issue) was addressed when the binding precedent was created. The reclamação does not reopen question Q; it applies norm N to the lower court's deviation.

The paper assumes that the inferior court's reasons for departing from the precedent are "arguments capable of undermining the conclusion" within the meaning of art. 489, §1º, IV. But the conclusion of the reclamação proceeding is about compliance with norm N (question A: did the departure occur? was it formally permissible?), not about the substantive correctness of norm N (question B: is the precedent right?). The inferior court's reasons bear on question B, not question A. Art. 489, §1º, IV requires engagement with arguments bearing on the court's *conclusion*. In a reclamação, the conclusion is question A. Arguments addressed exclusively to question B — the precedent's correctness — do not bear on the reclamação's conclusion in the procedurally relevant sense.

The compliance structure is not incidental. The reclamação was constitutionally designed as a mechanism for *authority preservation*, not for authority revision. A proceeding whose constitutional purpose is to preserve authority cannot simultaneously be required, as a matter of art. 489, §1º, IV, to entertain arguments that the authority being preserved is wrong. These constitutional purposes are in structural tension: the authority-preservation function of reclamação is undermined when the proceeding is converted into a mandatory re-examination of the underlying authority's merit.

### 3.2 The Inference from Art. 489's Scope Does Not Reach the Symmetry Conclusion

The paper argues: art. 489 has no STF exception; therefore art. 489, §1º, IV applies to the STF's reclamação judgment; therefore the STF must engage with the inferior court's departure reasons. Both premises are correct — the conclusion does not follow.

Whether art. 489, §1º, IV applies to a specific STF judgment depends not on who is deciding but on the type of judicial activity the judgment represents. The paper establishes that art. 489 applies to the STF; it does not establish that reclamação judgments are the type of judicial activity where §1º, IV's engagement obligation is triggered. That conclusion requires an additional premise: that the reclamação judgment is a merit decision where arguments about the precedent's correctness are capable of undermining the conclusion.

This additional premise is false for the reason §3.1 identifies. The reclamação's conclusion is about compliance, not about the merit of the underlying precedent. Arguments about the precedent's correctness do not bear on the compliance conclusion. Art. 489, §1º, IV therefore applies to the STF's reclamação judgment — the STF must engage arguments capable of undermining the compliance conclusion — but that obligation requires engagement with arguments about whether the departure occurred and whether it was formally adequate, not with arguments about whether the precedent is substantively right.

Put precisely: the inference error is the paper's implicit premise that the inferior court's departure reasons are arguments capable of undermining the reclamação's conclusion. The inferior court's reasons address whether the precedent is correct (question B). They are not arguments that the departure did not occur or was formally adequate (question A). The art. 489, §1º, IV engagement obligation, applied to the reclamação's actual conclusion, requires the STF to engage arguments about question A — not question B.

The paper's §3.3 correctly notes that hierarchy and symmetry operate on different planes. This is accepted. But the distinction does not rescue the inference: hierarchy determines who decides last; symmetry determines the reasoning standard for reaching that decision. Neither hierarchy nor symmetry determines *which arguments* fall within the engagement obligation for a given proceeding. Determining which arguments are "capable of undermining the conclusion" requires identifying the proceeding's actual conclusion. For a compliance proceeding, the relevant arguments are compliance arguments.

### 3.3 The Institutional Dialogue Mechanism Is Informal, Not Structural

Thesis 3's institutional-dialogue claim requires that the reclamação be a *structural* channel through which compelling inferior-court arguments generate precedent revision. Paper 1D §4.2 describes the feedback loop: inferior court raises argument → reclamação filed → STF engages → STF distinguishes or revises the súmula → new precedent produced.

The loop has a break between steps three and four. The reclamação proceeding can produce two outputs: granted (cassação of the lower court's decision) or denied (lower court decision survives). Neither output procedurally produces revision of a súmula vinculante. Revision of a súmula vinculante requires proposal and approval under Lei 11.417/2006, art. 2º — two-thirds of the STF's full bench, following the reiteração requirement. For other binding precedents under art. 927, CPC, revision follows the appropriate precedent-establishing proceeding (RE with repercussão geral, IRDR, IAC). These are separate procedures with their own constitutional and statutory requirements. A reclamação judgment cannot formally cancel or revise a súmula vinculante; it can only grant or deny the reclamação.

The paper's option (b) in §4.2 — "revisa — cancela ou revisa a súmula se os argumentos revelam erro ou descompasso que compromete a ratio" — describes what the STF should do after the reclamação proceeding reveals a compelling argument, not what the reclamação proceeding itself produces. The revision is a separate step that requires the STF's voluntary decision to initiate separate procedures. This is informal channel, not structural mechanism: the STF happens to be moved by the argument, happens to initiate revision. Nothing in the reclamação proceeding mandates or produces this outcome.

For the institutional dialogue to be a structural feature of the system — as Thesis 3 claims — the connection between a compelling reclamação argument and a mandatory revision initiation would need to be procedurally built into the reclamação framework. It is not. The STF can grant the reclamação, cassar the lower court's decision, and decline to initiate revision without any procedural consequence within the reclamação proceeding. The dialogue happens at the STF's discretion, not because the reclamação structure requires it.

The distinction matters for the thesis's claim. A structural channel would produce revision outcomes through procedural necessity; an informal channel produces revision outcomes when the STF chooses to act on arguments it finds compelling. The Brazilian binding-precedent system has the informal channel in the trivial sense that the STF sometimes revises precedents after encountering compelling arguments in litigation. Thesis 3 claims more than this: it claims the reclamação is the *designed* vehicle for the dialogue, not merely one of many possible inputs to informal deliberation.

Option (a) in §4.2 — distinction — is more defensible. The STF can, in a reclamação judgment, clarify the súmula's scope by holding that the lower court's case is distinguishable and denying the reclamação on that basis. This is within the reclamação proceeding's capacity; the judgment itself produces the scoping clarification. But distinction does not revise the súmula; it delimits its application to a class of cases. The core of Thesis 3 — that the reclamação dialogue produces revision when the argument reveals error — depends on option (b), which requires the informal path.

### 3.4 The Practice Gap Reflects the Reclamação's Correct Function

The paper frames the STF's practice — authority reaffirmation without engagement, plenário virtual processing with standardized responses — as a pathological deviation from what the existing law requires. This framing is available only if the symmetry thesis correctly identifies the law's requirements.

An alternative reading is more consistent with the institutional design: the STF's processing practice reflects a correct understanding of the reclamação's compliance function. Compliance mechanisms are designed for efficiency: determine quickly whether the lower court deviated from the norm, and if so, restore compliance. Efficient compliance mechanisms do not re-open the merit of the norm in each proceeding; that would convert every compliance mechanism into a rolling re-examination of the underlying authority. The STF's brief reclamação judgments — identifying the binding norm, identifying the deviation, ordering cassação — constitute substantive reasoning about the compliance question (question A). They are not failures to reason; they are reasoning addressed to the correct question.

The gap between the paper's prescription and the STF's practice is large: virtually every plenário virtual reclamação judgment where the lower court has articulated departure reasons would be deficient under the symmetry thesis. If the paper's reading of the existing law were correct, this would be a systematic and pervasive violation of art. 489, §1º, IV by the STF — a finding of remarkable scope that Brazilian procedural doctrine has not endorsed and that the STF's own decisions do not reflect. The paper presents this as evidence that current doctrine is wrong; the adversarial reading is that current doctrine correctly understands the reclamação as a compliance mechanism not governed by the §1º, IV engagement obligation for question B.

### 3.5 The Load-Bearing Connection to Paper 1B's Exit 4

Paper 1D's Thesis 3 is the institutional framework that renders Paper 1B's Exit 4 (rational supersession by inferior courts) intelligible as a legitimate practice rather than mere jurisdictional defiance. If the reclamação is genuinely a dialogue channel where good arguments generate precedent evolution, then Exit 4's rational supersession is a contribution to that dialogue — not disobedience, but input to an iterative process. Paper 1D supplies the systemic justification that makes Exit 4 something other than a claim that inferior courts simply have authority to override the STF.

The existing adversarial attack on Paper 1B (`otherwise/paper1b-rational-supersession.md`) identifies an inference gap in Exit 4: art. 489, §1º, VI establishes when a departure is unfounded (form condition) without establishing substantive authorization for inferior courts to override binding STF precedents. Paper 1D is, in effect, the response to this inference gap: the authorization for Exit 4's departures derives not from a single provision but from the systemic structure of the dialogue — a structure in which the reclamação channels inferior-court arguments to the STF for authoritative resolution.

If the reclamação cannot carry the dialogue function Paper 1D assigns to it — because the reclamação is a compliance mechanism not subject to the symmetry engagement obligation for question B — then Exit 4's systemic justification collapses. The departure with reasons remains a formal necessity under art. 489, §1º, VI. But the reasons do not generate the mandatory STF engagement that would convert the departure from a formal step into a substantive contribution. Exit 4's departures are formally valid steps that the STF is not required to engage and that the reclamação proceeding does not transform into revision triggers. The form obligation exists; the dialogue structure it was supposed to feed does not.

## 4. Anticipated Reply and Why It Does Not Suffice

### 4.1 The Art. 93, IX, CF Response

The paper's §3.1 invokes art. 93, IX, CF as independent constitutional support for the engagement obligation. Even if art. 489, §1º, IV applies differently to compliance proceedings, the constitutional requirement of reasoned decisions applies to the STF unconditionally.

This reply does not suffice. Art. 93, IX, CF requires that decisions be "fundamentadas" — a standard satisfied by reasoning about the question the proceeding actually resolves. When the STF's reclamação judgment identifies the binding norm, identifies the lower court's deviation, and orders cassação, it is reasoning about the compliance question (question A). This is substantive engagement with the reclamação's actual subject matter; it satisfies the constitutional reasoned-decision requirement. Art. 93, IX, CF does not require the STF to reason about every question a party chooses to raise in a proceeding — only about the questions the proceeding is designed to resolve. The inferior court's arguments about the precedent's correctness are not questions the reclamação proceeding is designed to resolve; therefore the constitutional duty to reason does not independently require engagement with those arguments.

The paper itself acknowledges (§3.1) that art. 489, §1º, CPC is an infraconstitutional operationalization of the constitutional mandate in art. 93, IX. If the §1º, IV engagement obligation does not extend to question B in compliance proceedings, the constitutional minimum in art. 93, IX does not extend there either — the constitutional provision is the source, not a higher-reaching standard.

### 4.2 The Hierarchy/Symmetry Distinction

The paper's §3.3 correctly distinguishes hierarchy (who decides last) from symmetry (how they get there): the STF can have the last word and still be required to justify that word through engagement, not positional reaffirmation. This is correct and accepted.

The distinction does not rescue Thesis 2 because the attack does not challenge the general symmetry of reasoning obligations — it challenges which arguments fall within the engagement obligation for the reclamação's specific conclusion. The hierarchy/symmetry distinction establishes that the STF must reason; it does not establish that reasoning about the reclamação's compliance conclusion requires engagement with arguments about the precedent's substantive correctness. Those are different questions. The STF reasons about the compliance conclusion by identifying the norm, identifying the deviation, and explaining why cassação is appropriate. This is reasoning that satisfies the symmetry standard without requiring engagement with question B.

### 4.3 The Normative Prescription Reading

The paper might be read as lex ferenda rather than lex lata — a proposal for how the system should work, not a claim that the existing provisions already require symmetry. On this reading, the attack on the inference from existing provisions is misdirected: the paper is not claiming that art. 489, §1º, IV currently generates the engagement obligation in reclamação, but that the system would function better if it did.

This recharacterization is available but substantially weakens the thesis. If the symmetry claim is aspirational, then the institutional dialogue described in Thesis 3 is not an existing structural feature — it is a proposed reform. The adversarial attack on the inference from existing provisions would be misdirected, but the reform proposal would face its own objection: the reclamação's constitutional purpose is authority preservation, and expanding it to mandatory engagement with departure arguments would require either constitutional amendment (to modify the reclamação's constitutional purpose) or legislative reform (to add procedural mechanisms linking compelling reclamação arguments to mandatory revision initiation). The paper does not argue for this legislative reform; it argues from the text already in force.

## 5. Scope of the Attack

This attack targets:

- **Thesis 2:** The symmetry of the duty of reasoning as applied to the STF in reclamação proceedings when the inferior court has exposed reasons for departing. The engagement obligation under art. 489, §1º, IV for the reclamação's compliance conclusion does not extend to arguments about the precedent's substantive correctness (question B), because those arguments do not bear on the proceeding's actual conclusion (question A: did the departure comply with form requirements?).
- **Thesis 3:** The institutional dialogue claim, insofar as it requires the reclamação to be a structural channel generating mandatory revision engagement. The reclamação's procedural outputs (grant or denial) do not include revision of the binding precedent; revision requires separate procedures with distinct requirements. The dialogue that Thesis 3 describes is informal channel, not structural mechanism.

This attack does not contest:

- **Thesis 1:** The paper correctly identifies that art. 489, §1º, VI establishes a form obligation, not a result prohibition. An inferior court that departs with reasons operates within the system's formal requirements. The paper's critique of practice that treats departures as categorically prohibited regardless of reasoning quality is well-grounded.
- The paper's characterization of the current equilibrium: the STF's practice of authority reaffirmation without engagement in reclamação judgments is accurately described. The paper's account of this as a systemic problem for precedent quality is independently defensible; this attack contests only the specific legal mechanism the paper proposes to address it.
- The observation that the CPC 2015's art. 489, §1º, IV imposes demanding reasoning standards on decisões judiciais generally, including decisions of the STF in its proper jurisdiction.
- The paper's invocation of institutional dialogue theory (Waldron, Conrado Hübner Mendes) as a framework for understanding the precedent system. The attack targets whether the reclamação is the specific vehicle for that dialogue, not whether dialogue is a useful conceptual frame.

---

## 6. Surrender Conditions

The attack would not hold, or would hold only partially, under the following conditions.

1. **A constitutional or statutory provision establishes that the reclamação is a proceeding where the STF must address the merit of the inferior court's reasons for departing, not merely verify compliance.** If the reclamação's constitutional purpose were established to include merit re-examination of the departure's quality alongside compliance verification, the compliance-verification distinction in §3.1 would be unavailable. This would require either a direct reading of art. 102, I, "l", CF or an established doctrinal framework that extends the reclamação's scope to merit review of departures.

2. **A STF precedent or established doctrine holds that reclamação against reasoned departures must address the quality of the reasoning, not merely the fact of the departure.** If the STF's own jurisprudence on reclamação recognized the §1º, IV engagement obligation with respect to departure reasons, the attack's reliance on practice as evidence of the correct constitutional understanding would require revision. Absent such precedent, the STF's consistent practice of compliance-only review is evidence against the symmetry reading.

3. **A procedural mechanism connecting compelling reclamação arguments to mandatory revision-initiation is established or identified.** If the reclamação proceeding already has a formal pathway by which a reclamação judgment identifying a compelling argument triggers mandatory initiation of the revision procedure, Thesis 3's structural-channel claim would have procedural support. The existence of such a mechanism is denied by the current texts of Lei 11.417/2006 and art. 988-993 CPC; identifying it would require showing either that it exists sub silentio or that it should be read in as a necessary implication of the constitutional structure.

4. **The paper's normative prescription is recharacterized as lex ferenda with a supporting reform proposal.** If the paper argues that the reclamação's scope should be expanded — through legislative or constitutional reform — to incorporate mandatory engagement with departure reasons and to link compelling arguments to revision initiation, the inference-error attack is misdirected. The reform proposal would then be the proper object of analysis; the institutional-design arguments against expanding reclamação into a merit re-examination mechanism would be the appropriate adversarial response.
