# Vocabulary Without Procedure: A Tractability Challenge to Paper 1C's Claim Provenance Module

---

## 1. Thesis Attacked

"Categorias Processuais Civis para Formalização Computacional" (Baldo, 2025; `paper1C_categorias_processuais_formalizacao.md`) proposes to provide "sufficient descriptive precision for formalizers — human or automated — to correctly implement the categories in formal languages without distorting the underlying legal system" (Abstract). The paper covers four modules: claims (pedidos, §2), dispositions (provimentos, §3), appellate claims (pretensões recursais, §4), and claim provenance (proveniência de afirmações, §5).

This attack targets two structural commitments in the claim provenance module:

**Commitment 1 — The preprocessing claim (§5.1–5.4):** The necessary/contingent distinction, applied to each claim in each document before axiomatization, constitutes a tractable preprocessing step. Claims annotated with (provenance, status) attributes prior to formalization produce axiom sets that do not inadvertently propagate contingent claims as necessary ones. The paper presents this annotation as enabling formalizers to "correctly implement the categories ... without distorting the underlying legal system."

**Commitment 2 — The ratio extraction rule (§5.3):** For Brazilian colegiado decisions, the ratio decidendi is "the minimum denominator of the grounds of the majority votes — what all majority votes share as necessary foundation for the result. The grounds that each justice added individually but that were not shared by the others are, in relation to the acórdão as precedent, obiter."

Both commitments fail to deliver what the paper promises. Commitment 1 generates a circularity: the necessary/contingent determination requires the legal analysis the formalization system is trying to perform, not a preprocessing step that precedes it. Commitment 2 produces empty or indeterminate ratios for the class of Brazilian decisions most important for the formalization system's core use case.

A supportive defense (`yesindeed/paper1c-tractability-defense.md`) accepts the preprocessing circularity for automated systems, restricts the tractability claim to human formalizers relying on structural document signals, proposes a candidate-ratio extension for parallel-reasoning decisions, and advances three further arguments: that the taxonomy's value for the easy class is formal recording (not enabling identification), that the necessary/contingent categories are specifically required for detecting propagation-by-active-incorporation, and that individual STF votes have lower competing-unmarked-threads incidence than collective acórdãos. This paper argues that each response fails to rescue the paper's operational claims as stated.

---

## 2. Faithful Reconstruction

The strongest form of the paper's claim provenance argument, integrating the original case and the full defense, runs as follows.

Computational formalization of legal arguments requires selecting claims from prior documents for use as axioms. A claim drawn from a precedent and elevated to axiomatic status carries its full logical weight in the formalization. If that claim was actually *contingent* in the precedent — present in the reasoning but not load-bearing for the legal effect — then its subsequent treatment as an axiom builds on an unstable foundation. The formalization produces valid-looking theorems that do not correspond to what the precedent actually established. The paper's response is the (provenance, status) annotation scheme. Before formalizing any claim as an axiom, the formalizer determines where the claim comes from (provenance) and whether it was necessary or contingent in its origin document (status). Claims annotated as (declarada, necessária) carry high epistemic weight; claims annotated as (inferida, pendente) require verification before load-bearing use. The annotation scheme makes the epistemic hierarchy of the formalization explicit and auditable.

The ratio extraction rule is offered as a determinate, algorithmically-formulated alternative to contested theoretical debates about ratio in civilian systems: identify the grounds each majority justice treats as necessary for the result; take their intersection; that intersection is the ratio. The procedure has a clear output in principle and reduces formalizer discretion.

The supportive defense strengthens this case in several directions. For preprocessing tractability: Brazilian procedural writing employs a systematic set of locutions for contingency — "Ainda que assim não fosse...", "Mesmo que se admitisse...", "Ad argumentandum tantum..." in sentenças and acórdãos; "Subsidiariamente, caso se entenda que..." in petições iniciais; vote-to-ementa structural relationships in colegiado decisions. A trained practitioner identifies these signals through structural reading before engaging the merits. The "pendente" category correctly handles the non-tractable class without overclaiming tractability for it. For quality-assurance and coordination: the taxonomy's value for the easy class is not enabling identification (practitioners who read "ainda que" constructions already identify contingency without formal infrastructure) but enabling formal recording — converting practitioners' implicit recognition into standardized, cross-document-checkable artifacts on which quality-assurance records, error-detection protocols, and multi-formalizer coordination depend. These operational contributions are further specific to the (provenance × status) categories because detecting propagation-by-active-incorporation requires specifically tracking whether a claim was load-bearing; alternative annotation schemes tracking citation status or contested/uncontested status detect different error classes and would fail for this one.

For the ratio extraction failure: following Mitidiero's convergent/parallel distinction, fragmented-majority decisions can receive candidate ratios — clusters of majority votes sharing necessary grounds, each marked with support, epistemic status, and scope — rather than null output from the unextended rule. For the vote-level circularity: individual justice votes are single-authored and therefore have lower competing-unmarked-threads incidence than collective acórdãos; the adversarial paper's §4.3 concession that inferior courts informally reconstruct from individual grounds confirms the tractability of vote-level ground identification.

## 3. The Attack

### 3.1 The Necessary/Contingent Determination Requires the Output It Purports to Enable

The paper's criterion for necessity is functional: "a claim is necessary in a document if, without it, the legal effect of the act would not occur or would be substantially different" (§5.2). For sentença foundations: "the foundation without which the dispositivo would not stand is necessary. Alternative foundations — 'even if this were not so, it would also follow that...' — are contingent."

This criterion correctly identifies what necessity means. The problem is that determining whether a specific foundation is necessary for a specific dispositivo requires knowing whether the dispositivo could have been reached on other available grounds. That determination is a product of legal analysis — the analysis that identifies which of the decision's argumentative threads the issuing court treated as load-bearing. It is not a preprocessing step that can be completed before the analysis runs; it is a central output of the analysis itself.

A concrete illustration from reclamação proceedings. The STF grants a reclamação against an inferior-court acórdão that departed from a súmula. The STF's reclamação acórdão contains two independent argumentative threads: (a) the inferior court failed to demonstrate that its cited factual differences track any feature of the súmula's ratio, so the departure demonstration was structurally inadequate; and (b) even if the demonstration were formally adequate, the identified distinctions would not change the outcome because the súmula's underlying rationale applies equally to the inferior court's case. The reclamação is granted under either argument standing alone.

Is thread (a) necessary or contingent in the STF's acórdão? Under the paper's criterion: necessary if the dispositivo would not occur or would be substantially different without it; contingent if thread (b) alone would have produced the same dispositivo.

Determining the answer requires resolving which thread the STF actually treated as the ratio — as opposed to which thread was offered as reinforcement. This is the formalization system's core analytical task for this decision: identifying the ratio of the STF's reclamação judgment. The claim provenance annotation ("is thread (a) necessary?") cannot be completed before the formalization determines the ratio; it is completed only by determining the ratio. The preprocessing claim inverts the operational dependency.

The paper's annotation scheme has a release valve: the "pendente" status, for cases where provenance or necessity cannot be determined. For the reclamação example above, "pendente" may be the honest answer. But "pendente" is an acknowledgment that the determination was not made — not a tractable preprocessing step. For load-bearing claims in contested proceedings — the claims that matter most for a formalization system's downstream accuracy — "pendente" may be the honest but operationally useless answer precisely because the determination requires the analysis the system is supposed to perform.

**The structural-signal response does not resolve the hard case.** The supportive defense argues that Brazilian procedural writing employs systematic locutions for contingency — "Ainda que assim não fosse...", "Subsidiariamente..." — that allow practitioners to identify alternative foundations structurally, without merits engagement. This argument is accurate for the class of cases it describes: when a court explicitly marks a thread with an alternative-foundation locution, a practitioner can identify that thread as contingent through surface reading.

The adversarial hard case is not this class. The hard case is precisely the document in which neither thread is marked as subordinate — where both argumentative threads are stated in the declarative mode, each presented as a primary ground for the result. The reclamação acórdão in the example above says (paraphrased): "[Ground A: the departure demonstration was structurally inadequate.] [Ground B: the cited distinctions would not change the outcome regardless.]" Neither ground is prefaced with "ainda que" or any alternative-foundation signal. The reader who wishes to annotate these claims cannot determine which is necessary from document structure; she must determine which the STF treated as the load-bearing ground of the decision.

The defense concedes this directly: "These signals are not exhaustive — the 'pendente' category handles cases where they are absent." The concession confirms the adversarial claim: for cases without explicit markers, the preprocessing scheme cannot be completed before the legal analysis. The structural-signal argument limits the scope of the circularity to the unmarked-parallel-threads class; it does not eliminate it.

**The recording-not-identification argument recharacterizes the claim without rescuing it.** The supportive defense's round-2 response introduces a further distinction: the taxonomy's value for the easy class is not enabling identification (practitioners can already identify "ainda que" constructions without formal infrastructure) but enabling formal recording — converting practitioners' implicit recognition into standardized, cross-document-checkable artifacts on which quality-assurance, error-detection, and coordination functions depend. Without standardized annotation, even easy cases produce inconsistent cross-document records.

This distinction is conceptually sound. The adversarial paper accepts that practitioners already identify easy-class contingency signals and that the taxonomy's value for this class lies in producing standardized records rather than enabling novel identification.

The recording argument does not rescue the enabling claim. The Abstract presents the annotation scheme as enabling formalizers to "correctly implement the categories ... without distorting the underlying legal system." An annotation scheme that records what analysis has already determined does not enable correct implementation — the analysis enables it; the annotation records it. The recording-not-identification response implicitly revises the taxonomy's operational role from preceding-and-enabling to following-and-recording. This is a genuine qualification of the Abstract's enabling claim, not a defense of it.

The recording argument further introduces an asymmetry in the quality-assurance function. QA records, error-detection protocols, and coordination infrastructure are valuable when the recorded determinations are correct. When annotation is correct — when the "ainda que" construction is reliably identified as marking a contingent foundation — the QA record is genuinely informative, propagation-error checks catch subsequent elevations, and coordination propagates accurate status information. This QA value follows from the structural reliability of identification in the easy class: practitioners converge on the same annotation for the same structural signal.

When annotation is wrong — when a formalizer makes an incorrect necessity determination in the hard class, marking a contingent claim as (declarada, necessária) through a misdirected merits judgment — the taxonomy records the error without detecting it. The QA artifact shows (declarada, necessária) in correct form but with incorrect substance. The propagation-error check finds no error (subsequent documents that also treat the claim as necessary match the incorrect source annotation). Coordination infrastructure propagates consistent wrong annotations downstream. The taxonomy's quality-assurance function is contingent on the accuracy of the underlying merits judgment it records: it assures the quality of annotation form, not annotation substance.

For the easy class — explicitly marked documents — identification is structurally reliable and annotation is correspondingly accurate; the QA function has genuine bite. For the hard class — competing-unmarked-threads documents — the taxonomy records the judgment's output without validating its correctness. A QA record that marks a contested merits judgment as (declarada, necessária) provides the appearance of epistemic security without its substance. The recording-not-identification argument, combined with the QA-accuracy asymmetry, confirms that the taxonomy's genuine operational contribution is: (a) for the easy class, formal recording of reliable structural identifications, with attendant QA value; (b) for the hard class, documentation of contestable merits judgments, with form-assurance but not substance-assurance. The adversarial claim is that the Abstract presents this limited contribution as the larger one.

### 3.2 The Propagation Mechanism Is Identified Before It Can Be Identified

The paper's propagation analysis (§5.5) identifies two failure modes: propagation-by-omission (a party fails to contest a contingent claim in a subsequent document) and propagation-by-active-incorporation (a subsequent decision treats a prior contingent claim as "as established by the court below"). Both are correctly identified as sources of downstream formalization error.

The practical consequence the paper draws is that a recorrente who demonstrates that an acórdão rests on a claim that was contingent in the first-instance sentença can raise an argument about infra petita reasoning or violation of the devolutive effect. The recorrente identifies and challenges the propagation error.

But this practical consequence reveals the sequencing problem. The recorrente can raise the argument only after having done the legal analysis that determines (a) the claim was contingent in the prior document and (b) the subsequent document improperly elevated it to necessary status. That analysis is the substance of the appellate argument; the taxonomy provides a vocabulary for articulating it, not a procedure for performing it.

For a computational formalization system to implement the propagation check, it would need to:
1. Determine which claims were necessary vs. contingent in each prior document in the chain.
2. Identify when a subsequent document treats a prior contingent claim as established.

Step 1 requires the legal analysis that the annotation scheme was supposed to organize. The scheme provides a notation for step 1's outputs; it is not an algorithm for producing those outputs. A system that cannot independently perform step 1 cannot detect propagation errors; it can only record, after a human has determined necessity, which claims were contingent.

The paper conflates having a vocabulary for describing propagation errors with having a procedure for detecting them. These are different contributions with different computational implications.

### 3.3 The Ratio Extraction Rule Fails for Fragmented Majority Decisions; the Candidate-Ratio Extension Does Not Escape the Same Problem

The minimum-denominator rule is precise and implementable in principle. Its structural weakness is the class of decisions where the intersection of majority votes' necessary grounds is empty or trivially thin.

Brazilian superior courts — particularly the STF in plenary sessions on constitutional questions — regularly produce acórdãos where justices file individual votes that reach the same operative result through substantially different foundations. Consider a stylized but representative pattern: the STF reviews the constitutionality of a legislative measure by an eight-to-three vote. Among the eight votes in the majority: three ground their votes on the constitutional principle of legal certainty; two on the democratic legitimacy of the legislative choice; two on the proportionality of the measure under the administrative-regulatory interpretation of the constitution; one on an interpretive presumption of legislative constitutionality that the other seven do not invoke. There is no single ground that all eight majority votes treat as necessary for the result.

Under the minimum-denominator rule, this decision has no substantive ratio decidendi. The intersection of eight distinct sets of necessary grounds — where no single ground appears in all eight — is empty. The rule's output is a binding precedent without any binding legal ground: the measure was upheld, by whom, with no identified ratio. Future inferior courts applying the precedent have nothing to apply; they know the result but not the reasoning that justifies or limits it.

This is not a peripheral failure case. The STF's most contested and consequential decisions on constitutional questions — tax constitutionality, fundamental rights interpretation, federalism — are precisely those where justices bring distinct constitutional frameworks to the same question, producing fragmented majority reasoning. The minimum-denominator rule systematically fails for the class of STF decisions that generates most of the binding precedent the formalization system would need to process.

The Brazilian literature engages this problem directly. Mitidiero distinguishes "convergent reasoning" decisions (where majority justices share substantial foundational grounds) from "parallel reasoning" decisions (where majority justices reach the same result through incompatible or independent foundations), and argues that the two require different ratio-extraction approaches. Macêdo identifies the minimum-denominator approach as producing unusable results for parallel-reasoning decisions and proposes reconstruction methods that identify the best candidate ratio from fragmented votes. The paper's rule, applied mechanically, produces the pathological output Mitidiero and Macêdo describe — the empty-ratio result — without acknowledging that this is the characteristic output for a substantial portion of the STF's constitutional jurisprudence.

**The candidate-ratio extension requires the same vote-level necessity determination and is not in the paper.** The supportive defense proposes extending the minimum-denominator rule for parallel-reasoning decisions: where the intersection of majority grounds is empty, construct candidate ratios by clustering majority votes sharing common necessary grounds, marking each with support, epistemic status, and scope.

The extension is a genuine advance over the unextended rule. Mitidiero's framework is engaged; the null-output pathology is addressed; and the defense correctly observes that inferior courts already apply something like this informal reconstruction. The adversarial paper accepts the extension as technically coherent.

Two difficulties remain.

The first is structural. The candidate-ratio framework presupposes a vote-level necessity determination: the formalizer must identify, within each individual justice's vote, which grounds that justice treated as necessary for the result. The clustering step follows from this determination. But the preprocessing circularity from §3.1 applies at the vote level: a justice's individual vote in a contested constitutional case may itself contain multiple argumentative threads, presented without explicit subordination signals, each independently contributing to that justice's conclusion. Determining which thread was load-bearing for that justice's vote requires reading the vote as a legal argument and asking which ground was doing the work — which is the same legal-analytical judgment the document-level preprocessing circularity describes. The candidate-ratio framework decomposes the document-level problem into vote-level subproblems, but the subproblems inherit the same structure.

The defense responds that cross-referencing individual votes against the ementa is a structural operation that permits this determination. This misidentifies what the cross-referencing accomplishes. Ementa cross-referencing checks whether a justice's stated grounds are structurally consistent with the ementa's consolidated position — it is a consistency check. It does not identify, within a justice's vote that is consistent with the ementa, which of that justice's multiple argumentative threads were necessary for that consistency. A justice may have written three independent lines of argument each supporting the consolidated result; cross-referencing against the ementa confirms all three are consistent with it and does not discriminate among them. The vote-level necessity determination requires the same analytical judgment the document-level determination requires.

**The lower-incidence claim rests on an institutional presupposition that STF constitutional adjudication does not satisfy.** The supportive defense contests not the structural point (ementa cross-referencing alone does not resolve the vote-level necessity determination) but the claim's incidence and severity: individual STF votes are single-authored and therefore have lower competing-unmarked-threads incidence than collective acórdãos. Single-authorship does not predict lower competing-unmarked-threads incidence in the institutional context of STF constitutional adjudication.

Three structural features of this context generate competing-unmarked-threads patterns within individual votes, independent of multi-authorship.

First, *audience heterogeneity*. An STF justice votes for multiple audiences simultaneously: the specific case at bar, the precedential landscape the vote will create for future cases with different fact patterns, colleagues who may write concurrences or dissents, and the legal scholarship that will interpret the vote. A vote designed for this multi-audience function routinely develops multiple argumentative lines in parallel — one grounded in constitutional text, one in historical precedent, one in systemic consequences — without explicit subordination, because each line speaks to a different audience's concerns and the justice lacks a reason to elevate one at the cost of weakening the others.

Second, *precedent-building strategy*. In high-stakes constitutional cases, justices have institutional incentives to build precedential foundations that support the same conclusion through different doctrinal paths. A vote that grounds the same result in both constitutional text and established precedent simultaneously creates two future-precedent anchors: cases where the textual argument applies and cases where the precedential argument applies. The structural result is a vote with two independently sufficient foundations, neither explicitly marked as subordinate to the other, because explicit subordination would narrow the vote's precedential reach.

Third, *competing pressures against the formal-consistency counterincentive*. Brazilian constitutional jurisprudence requires formal internal consistency of judicial opinions (Art. 93, IX CF). This requirement creates prescriptive pressure toward explicit primary-argument structure: a decision presenting two argumentative threads in declarative mode without explicit subordination signals is precisely the kind of formally ambiguous opinion that Art. 93, IX CF's reasoning requirement is designed to prevent. The direction of this counterincentive is accepted — the claim that parallel declarative-mode reasoning is itself a formal-consistency strategy inverted the incentive's direction and is withdrawn. The adversarial claim is that audience heterogeneity and precedent-building strategy operate as competing pressures against the formal-consistency counterincentive's effectiveness in the most consequential class of STF constitutional decisions. Whether these competing pressures dominate the formal-consistency requirement sufficiently to produce the competing-unmarked-threads pattern at a rate that makes preprocessing materially non-tractable for this decision class is an empirical question addressed in SC6.

Two structural features and one accepted counterincentive therefore characterize the STF constitutional adjudication context: audience heterogeneity and precedent-building strategy generate tendency toward competing-unmarked-threads patterns; Art. 93, IX CF creates pressure against them. Whether the tendency or the counterincentive dominates in practice for the target decision class — contested STF plenary constitutional adjudication — is what SC6 names as the empirical ceiling. The lower-incidence claim for this class has not been established; the existence and direction of the counterincentive does not establish its effectiveness against the competing institutional pressures.

The reliance on inferior courts' informal reconstruction (adversarial §4.3 concession) to establish vote-level tractability conflates two distinct tasks: selecting the most persuasive available ground from a prior decision (what inferior courts do in service of deciding a present case) and determining which ground was load-bearing for the authoring justice's conclusion in the prior decision (what formal annotation requires). Informal reconstruction is goal-directed selection; necessity determination is analytical reconstruction. These are different activities, and tractability of the former does not establish tractability of the latter.

The second difficulty is about the paper's current claim. The adversarial attack targets Paper 1C's §5.3 as written: the minimum-denominator rule without qualification, without acknowledgment of fragmented-majority decisions as a challenge class, without specification of what the formalizer should do when the denominator is empty. The extension is a proposal from the defense paper. A defense paper can establish that an extension is available and technically coherent; it cannot retroactively correct the supported paper's text. SC3 requires the paper to incorporate a specified extension that produces non-empty, practically useful ratios for fragmented-majority decisions — not merely that such an extension has been proposed in a separate filing. The extension remains to be incorporated into Paper 1C's §5.3.

### 3.4 The Paper's Disclaimer Does Not Immunize the Tractability Problem

The paper explicitly disclaims dogmatic innovation: "This is not a paper of innovative dogmatic contribution ... it is a paper of systematic mapping" (§1). This disclaimer addresses one class of objection — that the paper's categories diverge from settled doctrine — but not the class raised here.

The tractability problem is not about whether the paper's categories are correct. The claim provenance categories are correctly described: necessary and contingent claims are real distinctions, provenance attributes are a genuine taxonomy, propagation is a real pathological mechanism. The problem is that having correct categories does not make those categories computationally tractable as a preprocessing step. Describing what to determine ("which claims are necessary?") is different from providing a procedure for determining it. The paper provides the former and presents it as the latter.

The disclaimer that the paper targets "sufficient descriptive precision" reinforces this point rather than answering it. Descriptive precision at the conceptual level does not imply tractability at the operational level. The paper's explicit goal — enabling formalizers to "correctly implement the categories in formal languages without distorting the underlying legal system" — is an operational claim. Meeting it requires not only that the categories be correctly described but that applying them to actual Brazilian court documents be tractable. The tractability argument challenges this operational claim, not the descriptive precision of the categories themselves.

### 3.5 An Alternative Annotation Scheme Enables Equivalent Propagation-Error Detection Without the Necessity Determination

The supportive defense's §3.2 argues that the necessary/contingent distinction is specifically required for detecting propagation-by-active-incorporation: citation-status and contested/uncontested-status schemes would fail for this specific error class because they do not track whether a claim was load-bearing in the originating document. The argument correctly shows those two alternative schemes fail. It does not establish that no alternative scheme succeeds.

The adversarial paper presents a concrete existence proof against the uniqueness claim: the *dispositif-grounding scheme*.

The dispositif-grounding scheme annotates each claim in a document with one of two values: **(G) dispositivo-grounded** — the claim appears in the court's explicit logical chain from the applicable legal standard to the dispositivo; **(B) background** — the claim appears in the opinion but not in the explicit dispositif chain. The annotation requires reading the court's explicit dispositif reasoning as a sequential logical chain: identifying the applicable legal standard, the operative factual findings, and the inferential steps the court explicitly stated as leading to the operative result. Claims appearing in this chain are marked (G); claims that appear in prefatory analysis, alternative-foundation sections, or expository discussion are marked (B).

The scheme is tractable where the necessary/contingent determination is not. The annotator reads the court's explicit chain to the dispositivo without asking whether the dispositivo could have been reached on other available grounds. The question "does this claim appear in the court's explicit logical chain to the dispositivo?" is a text-reading task. For the competing-unmarked-threads hard case — where two argumentative threads appear in the declarative mode with no explicit subordination signal — the dispositif-grounding annotator reads the chain the court explicitly constructs and marks each thread according to its structural position in that chain, without asking which thread was counterfactually necessary. Where the court presents both threads as part of its reasoning chain, both are marked (G); where one appears as additional context rather than as a step in the chain, it is marked (B). This determination is harder than reading explicit "ainda que" signals but easier than the counterfactual necessity analysis the (provenance × status) taxonomy requires for the same case.

The scheme enables propagation-by-active-incorporation detection. The propagation error occurs when a subsequent document treats as established a claim that was background in the prior document. The dispositif-grounding annotation tracks this: if claim C is annotated (B) in document D1 — appearing in the opinion but not in the court's explicit chain to the dispositivo — and a subsequent document D2 invokes C as a holding of D1, the annotator checks C's annotation in D1 (it is (B)) and identifies the propagation error. The detection is structurally equivalent to what the necessary/contingent scheme enables for active-incorporation cases.

The schemes are not identical. A claim can be dispositivo-grounded (G) yet contingent: when the court presents two alternative foundations in the declarative mode, each explicitly invoked as a step in the dispositif chain, both are (G) even though at most one is counterfactually necessary. A claim can be necessary for the dispositivo yet marked (B): if the court's explicit chain presupposes a legal premise that the opinion discusses in a prefatory section rather than in the dispositif reasoning itself, that premise is necessary but not dispositivo-grounded as stated. The schemes track different properties and diverge in these cases.

This divergence establishes two things: the schemes are not the same distinction under a different name, and neither is a strict replacement for the other. For propagation-by-active-incorporation detection specifically, the relevant question is: can a subsequent document elevate to established-holding status a claim that did not appear in the prior document's explicit dispositif chain? The dispositif-grounding scheme answers this question directly and without the counterfactual necessity analysis that constitutes the preprocessing circularity. For this use case, the dispositif-grounding scheme is adequate.

The adversarial implication is narrow: the necessary/contingent taxonomy is not the only annotation scheme that enables active-incorporation propagation-error detection. The taxonomy's specific categories are one workable implementation of the load-bearing/non-load-bearing distinction among several. The supportive defense's uniqueness claim requires showing not merely that citation-status and contested/uncontested schemes fail, but that no alternative scheme enables equivalent detection for the relevant use case. The supportive round 3 identifies a genuine failure in the dispositif-grounding scheme for G-but-contingent claims — where both argumentative threads appear as steps in the explicit dispositif chain yet at most one is counterfactually necessary — and argues this failure defeats the existence proof. The full response to the G-but-contingent challenge is developed in §3.6, below.

### 3.6 The G-but-Contingent Detection Capability Is Bounded by the Same Tractability Ceiling

The supportive round 3 identifies a genuine limitation in the dispositif-grounding scheme: for G-but-contingent claims — where both argumentative threads appear as steps in the court's explicit dispositif chain, each contributing a stated link toward the dispositivo, yet at most one is counterfactually necessary for the result — the dispositif-grounding scheme marks both (G). When a subsequent document D2 invokes the contingent thread as an established holding of D1, the dispositif-grounding annotation check finds it annotated (G) and reports no propagation error. The propagation error — an unnecessary ground elevated to axiomatic status — goes undetected. The necessary/contingent taxonomy, by contrast, can annotate the contingent G thread as (declarada, contingente), enabling detection when D2's check compares against that annotation.

The adversarial round 3 conceded the G-but-contingent divergence and dismissed the detection gap by assertion, without reasoning. The supportive round 3 correctly identifies this as an undefended claim. The adversarial now provides the analysis.

**The detection capability requires completing the necessity determination.** For the taxonomy to annotate a G-but-contingent thread as (declarada, contingente), the formalizer must have determined that the thread was contingent despite its presence in the explicit chain. This determination is the contested preprocessing step — the same step the preprocessing circularity in §3.1 attacks. The G-but-contingent class divides by tractability:

**Sub-case A — tractable G-but-contingent.** The necessity determination can be completed because a structural signal applied to the in-chain thread marks its contingent status. The primary mechanism: an "ainda que" construction, "para além do exposto," "alternativamente," or equivalent contingency locution is applied to a thread that nevertheless appears as an explicit step in the stated dispositif reasoning. Brazilian courts frequently employ this pattern — the opinion reasons through an alternative ground as a chain step while explicitly marking its subordinate status. In Sub-case A, the taxonomy annotation proceeds from the structural signal rather than from an unaided legal-analytical judgment about counterfactual necessity, and detection works as the supportive claims.

However, the structural signal that enables the taxonomy's annotation for Sub-case A also enables a modification to the dispositif-grounding scheme. A dispositif-grounding scheme refined to treat explicit contingency-marked in-chain threads as (B) — recognizing that "ainda que" and equivalent constructions applied to in-chain steps identify non-load-bearing grounds regardless of chain position — provides detection capability equivalent to the taxonomy's for Sub-case A. The modification requires structural reading of the same markers the taxonomy uses. Both schemes thereby depend on the same tractability condition. The adversarial accepts that the unmodified dispositif-grounding scheme fails for Sub-case A, as the supportive demonstrates. With the "ainda que"-class modification, the schemes converge on detection capability for this sub-case.

**Sub-case B — non-tractable G-but-contingent.** Both threads appear in the explicit chain in the declarative mode without any structural contingency signal. Determining which was load-bearing requires the legal analysis the formalization system is designed to perform. In Sub-case B, the taxonomy cannot annotate either thread as (declarada, contingente); the honest annotation is (declarada, pendente). When a subsequent document D2 invokes one thread as an established holding of D1, the annotation check returns "pendente" — flagging indeterminacy but not confirming a propagation error. Whether D2's use constitutes an active-incorporation error cannot be determined from the annotation alone; it requires completing the underlying necessity analysis. Detection exists only in the weak sense of surfacing indeterminacy. Positive identification of a confirmed propagation error is unavailable.

Sub-case B is the adversarial's central hard case and the preprocessing circularity's operative domain. For Sub-case B, the taxonomy and the modified dispositif-grounding scheme produce informationally equivalent outputs: both flag the contested point as indeterminate. The taxonomy does so through "pendente"; the dispositif-grounding scheme does so through the (G)/(G) annotation that provides no positive detection signal for the contingent thread. The informational content is the same: the claim's necessity status is unresolved, and downstream processes should treat it as indeterminate.

**The adversarial implication from §3.6.** The supportive's G-but-contingent argument establishes a genuine failure in the *unmodified* dispositif-grounding scheme for Sub-case A — a failure the adversarial accepts and has now incorporated. This is an advance in precision: the §3.5 existence proof holds for Sub-case A only with the "ainda que"-class modification. For Sub-case B, both schemes provide only indeterminacy flags; the taxonomy's detection advantage does not extend to the hard case where the preprocessing circularity operates.

The taxonomy's unique detection advantage over the *modified* dispositif-grounding scheme — if any — requires a non-trivial class of G-but-contingent cases where: (i) both threads appear in the explicit chain, (ii) the necessity determination is tractable for the taxonomy, and (iii) no structural contingency signal is available that would equally permit the modified dispositif-grounding annotation. SC7 names this class as the adversarial's residual request. The supportive has not identified it. The G-but-contingent argument establishes the taxonomy's superiority over the unmodified scheme; it does not establish superiority over the modified scheme for the class where tractability is possible. The debate between the schemes is now more precisely bounded: the operative scope of the adversarial attack in §3.5 is Sub-case B (where neither scheme detects), and the uniqueness argument requires engaging the modified scheme specifically for the residual Sub-case A class.

### 3.7 The SC7 Residual Class Is Addressed by a Doubly-Modified Dispositif-Grounding Scheme; and Ementa Cross-Referencing Is Condition-Dependent for Contested Constitutional Ementas

The supportive round 4 identifies the SC7 residual class: individual STF justice votes in colegiado decisions where a thread (i) appears in the vote's explicit dispositif chain, (ii) lacks any "ainda que"-class in-document structural marker, and (iii) is absent from the ementa's characterization of the majority's shared grounds. The supportive argues this class is non-trivially populated — parallel-reasoning STF decisions regularly feature individual justices' votes containing argumentative grounds the majority does not adopt as shared grounds — and that the detection mechanism is structurally tractable: ementa cross-referencing is "a structural cross-document operation — comparing individual vote text against ementa text" without performing the underlying constitutional analysis.

Two responses address the SC7 claim.

**The doubly-modified dispositif-grounding scheme achieves SC7 equivalence.** If ementa cross-referencing is a structural operation — as the supportive asserts to establish SC7 tractability — it is equally available as a further modification to the modified dispositif-grounding scheme. The doubly-modified scheme extends the "ainda que"-class modification from round 4 with an ementa-absence modification: in STF colegiado decisions, any thread appearing in an individual justice's vote chain but absent from the ementa's characterization of the majority's grounds is marked (B) — regardless of its chain position in the vote document.

Under the doubly-modified scheme, when a subsequent document D2 invokes an ementa-absent thread as an established holding of D1, the scheme finds the thread marked (B) in D1 — ementa cross-referencing established its background status — and identifies the propagation error. The detection mechanism is equivalent to the taxonomy's SC7 detection: both schemes apply the same ementa cross-referencing operation, classify the thread as non-load-bearing by the same criterion (ementa absence), and detect D2's propagation error through the same annotation check.

The supportive's own failure condition states this explicitly: "If the modified dispositif-grounding scheme is further extended to incorporate ementa-based modifications (treating threads absent from the ementa as (B) regardless of chain position), the scheme achieves SC7 equivalence through the same mechanism" (§6). The adversarial implements this extension. The schemes converge.

This convergence further bounds the uniqueness argument. After accounting for Sub-case A (equivalence through "ainda que"-class modification), Sub-case B (parity through indeterminacy flags), and SC7 (equivalence through ementa-absence modification), the taxonomy's unique detection advantage — if any — requires a class where (i) both threads are in the explicit chain, (ii) neither carries an "ainda que"-class marker, (iii) neither is absent from the ementa, and (iv) the necessity determination is nonetheless tractable for the taxonomy through a mechanism the doubly-modified scheme cannot replicate. No such class has been identified. The supportive must describe such a mechanism to establish detection advantage beyond the classes now addressed.

**Ementa cross-referencing is condition-dependent for contested constitutional ementas.** The doubly-modified scheme's SC7 equivalence, and the taxonomy's SC7 tractability claim, depend on the same structural-tractability condition: that determining whether a vote thread is absent from the ementa is a textual comparison operation rather than a legal-analytical judgment. This condition is not uniformly satisfied for the class of decisions where SC7 is most operative.

STF constitutional ementas — particularly from contested plenary decisions on constitutional questions — are written at a higher level of abstraction than individual vote reasoning threads. An ementa may characterize the majority's holding as involving "a observância do princípio da isonomia e da proporcionalidade" where a contributing justice's vote thread developed a specific doctrinal argument about the prestational dimension of art. 5°, caput, CF applied to a regulatory framework. Whether that specific thread is "absent from" the ementa's principle-level characterization requires legal judgment about what "isonomia" and "proporcionalidade" cover at the level of the specific doctrinal argument — not surface textual comparison between strings at compatible abstraction levels.

The structural-reading claim is strongest where the ementa and the vote operate at the same level of specificity: a concrete-case ementa that lists specific constitutional provisions and applicable doctrinal constructions permits textual comparison of whether a vote's thread is addressed or omitted. This specificity is characteristic of routine precedential decisions rather than high-stakes plenary constitutional adjudication. In contested constitutional cases — where justices bring distinct constitutional frameworks to the same question and the ementa must condense the majority's shared reasoning from heterogeneous votes — the ementa generalizes to principle-level language while individual votes reason at doctrinal specificity. Determining whether a specific doctrinal argument falls within or outside an abstract principled formulation requires legal analysis, not textual comparison.

The supportive's own failure condition names this: "If the ementa cross-referencing operation is shown to require legal analysis rather than structural reading — specifically, if determining that a thread is absent from the ementa requires legal judgment about what the ementa's terms cover, rather than textual comparison — the SC7 detection mechanism faces the same preprocessing circularity as the document-level determination" (§6).

The structural limit is asymmetrically distributed: it is most severe in the class where SC7's population is asserted most confidently. The adversarial's institutional-incentives argument (audience heterogeneity, precedent-building strategy) predicts argumentative threads in individual votes at the level of specific doctrinal constructions in contested constitutional adjudication — exactly the class where ementa abstraction is highest and structural tractability is most questionable. SC7's detection advantage is asserted for a class in which the operational condition supporting that advantage is most fragile.

The two responses converge on the same conclusion. The doubly-modified scheme implements SC7 detection equivalence provided ementa cross-referencing is structural. If ementa cross-referencing requires legal analysis for contested constitutional ementas, neither scheme provides tractable SC7 detection for that class — the preprocessing circularity operates for both. In either case, the taxonomy's claimed unique detection advantage over the doubly-modified scheme does not hold for the class the supportive identifies as SC7's primary population.

### 3.8 The (A)/(B) Textual-Designative/Conceptual-Coverage Distinction Does Not Rescue SC7 Tractability for the Contested Constitutional Class

The supportive paper's third qualification (§3.2, third para) introduces a two-determination distinction in response to the ementa-specificity probe. Determination (A) — textual-designative absence: is this specific thread stated in the ementa text as a fundamento determinante? — is contrasted with determination (B) — conceptual-coverage absence: does the ementa's stated principle extend to cover this thread as an application? The supportive argues that SC7 requires (A) rather than (B), grounding the distinction in the ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function under CPC art. 489, §1º, V and art. 927: the ementa authoritatively states the fundamentos determinantes; textual absence from the ementa therefore establishes non-inclusion without requiring conceptual-coverage analysis of what the principle covers.

The (A)/(B) distinction correctly identifies two determinations that are genuinely different and operationally separable in favorable cases. The adversarial claim is that they cannot be separated for the class of cases where SC7 is asserted to be most populated.

**The (A)/(B) separation holds for concrete-specificity ementas.** When an individual justice's vote thread addresses a specific statutory provision, a named administrative measure, or a specific party-identified factual configuration that is simply not present in the ementa's text, textual absence is unambiguous. An ementa discussing "o regime tributário das empresas de pequeno porte" textually excludes a vote thread about "a aplicação da anterioridade especial ao ICMS de grandes contribuintes" — the specific doctrinal content is absent at the surface level without any need to assess what the ementa's subject conceptually encompasses. For STJ repetitive-resource ementas and concrete STF decisions addressing specific statutory questions, this condition holds regularly: the ementas are specific enough that specific thread absence is determinable by textual comparison. For such cases, the supportive's third qualification is correct and the probe's scope is limited.

**The (A)/(B) separation collapses for principle-level abstract ementas in the contested constitutional class.** The case for SC7's non-trivial population rests on the institutional-incentives argument: contested STF plenary constitutional adjudication generates argumentative threads in individual votes at doctrinal specificity that the majority does not adopt as shared grounds. For this class, ementas systematically generalize to abstract constitutional-principle formulations: "observância do princípio da proporcionalidade e razoabilidade," "proteção da confiança legítima nas relações Estado-cidadão," "mínimo existencial como limite ao princípio da reserva do possível." Individual vote threads reason at doctrinal specificity: "a dimensão prestacional do mínimo existencial como limitação à reserva do possível em prestações de saúde continuada," "a confiança legítima como fundamento autônomo para proteção de situações transitadas em julgado anteriores à virada jurisprudencial."

For these cases, establishing that a vote thread is "textually absent" from the ementa requires settling whether the thread's doctrinal content is covered by the ementa's abstract formulation. The ementa says "proporcionalidade"; the vote thread says "proporcionalidade na sua dimensão prestacional." Is the specific dimension "textually stated" in the general formulation or "textually absent" from it? Answering this requires prior judgment about whether the abstract term encompasses the specific application — which is determination (B). The syntactic form of the ementa is "proporcionalidade e razoabilidade"; the syntactic form of the thread involves "proporcionalidade" in a specific doctrinal construction; whether the latter is textually present in or absent from the former cannot be established by string-matching. It requires interpreting what "proporcionalidade" covers at the thread's level of specificity.

This entanglement is not incidental. The threads most plausibly in SC7's population — generated by the institutional pressures the supportive accepts as real — are specific doctrinal elaborations of the constitutional principles the ementa states. A vote thread entirely unrelated to the ementa's stated principles would be a rare contribution to a majority vote in that case; the characteristic SC7 thread is an application or elaboration of the ementa's stated principles that the majority did not adopt at that specificity. It is precisely these threads — semantically in the field of the ementa's formulation but at a different specificity level — for which (A) presupposes (B): determining that the thread is not textually stated in the ementa requires first settling what the ementa's abstract formulation covers.

**The ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function resolves the normative question without resolving the epistemological one.** The supportive grounds (A)'s tractability in the ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function: the ementa IS the authoritative statement of fundamentos determinantes; textual absence from the ementa establishes non-inclusion as a matter of normative authority. This claim is normatively correct under CPC art. 489, §1º, V and art. 927. But the normative rule — the ementa is authoritative — settles what "fundamento determinante" means (what counts as one, namely what the ementa states), not how to determine what the ementa states and does not state at the specificity level of individual vote threads.

The normative rule requires reading the ementa to identify the fundamentos determinantes. For contested constitutional ementas at principle-level abstraction, reading the ementa to determine whether a specific doctrinal thread falls within or outside the stated fundamentos determinantes is the contested operation. The ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function establishes which document is authoritative; it does not establish that applying the document's abstract formulations to doctrinal-specificity threads is a purely textual exercise. The normative authority makes the ementa decisive; the epistemological difficulty arises from the specificity gap between what the ementa states and the threads being compared against it.

**The cross-front tension with ESHTR C2 is resolved by distinguishing source-reduction tractability from specificity-match tractability.** The supportive presses the cross-front tension: the adversarial accepted ementa-as-ratio for ESHTR C2 C1 (reading the ementa is tractable for ratio identification, converting multi-voto-synthesis to ementa-reading); SC7 uses the same ementa-reading operation for thread-absence determination; if ementa reading is tractable for C1, it should be tractable for SC7 through the same operation; if ementa reading for SC7 requires conceptual legal judgment, the ESHTR C2 C1 reconceptualization does not resolve the collegial-fragmentation problem it was intended to address.

The cross-front tension is resolvable. The C1 and SC7 operations differ in what makes them tractable.

The ESHTR C2 C1 concession was about source reduction: one ementa is tractable where N votos are not, for the specific task of identifying what the ratio IS. This is a task about the ementa's content taken as authoritative — what does the ementa say the majority's shared grounds are? Tractability derives from reading one document rather than synthesizing multiple votes with different framings. The advantage is in the number of documents and their coordination problem: ementa as consolidation.

SC7 thread-absence determination is a different task: comparing individual justice vote threads at doctrinal specificity against the ementa's formulation to determine whether those threads are stated or absent. This task requires source reduction (yes — one document rather than N) AND specificity match (the ementa's formulation is specific enough relative to the thread to permit textual comparison). Source reduction is the C1 tractability condition. Specificity match is an additional SC7 tractability condition that source reduction does not supply. The C1 concession accepts source reduction; it does not commit the adversarial to accepting specificity match for the contested constitutional class where ementas systematically operate at principle-level abstraction relative to individual vote doctrinal threads.

Stated formally: the ementa-as-ratio framing provides two things — (i) the ementa is the authoritative source (normative rule); (ii) reading the ementa is tractable for ratio identification rather than synthesizing N votos (source reduction). The cross-front tension presses that if (ii) holds for C1, it should hold for SC7. But SC7 tractability requires not only (i) and (ii) but also (iii): the ementa's formulation is specific enough at the abstraction level of the relevant threads to permit textual determination of presence or absence. The C1 task requires (i) and (ii); SC7 requires (i), (ii), and (iii). Accepting (ii) for C1 does not entail that (iii) is satisfied for the contested constitutional class. The ESHTR C2 concession addresses (ii); the ementa-specificity probe challenges (iii). These are separable. Holding them simultaneously is not inconsistent.

The cross-front tension therefore dissolves when the two tractability conditions are distinguished: source-reduction tractability and specificity-match tractability. The adversarial's positions are: ementa-as-ratio provides source-reduction tractability for C1 ratio identification (ESHTR C2 concession, accepted); ementa cross-referencing for SC7 thread-absence determination requires specificity-match tractability, which is not satisfied for principle-level constitutional ementas in the contested class (Paper 1C probe, maintained). Neither position contradicts the other. The tension the supportive identifies is between an accepted source-reduction claim and a contested specificity-match claim — not between two claims about the same tractability condition.

**Scope of §3.8.** The (A)/(B) distinction correctly establishes that SC7's standard case — where specific doctrinal content is textually absent from the ementa at a comparable or lower specificity level — involves only (A), and (A) is tractable without (B) for that case. The adversarial accepts this: §3.7's ementa-specificity probe was scoped to contested constitutional plenary adjudication, where ementas generalize at principle-level. §3.8 specifies why the (A)/(B) distinction does not resolve the probe's scope: for the contested constitutional class where SC7's population is asserted to be most plausibly non-trivial, (A) and (B) entangle. The ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function is correct as a normative rule; it does not establish that applying the abstract formulations of principle-level ementas to doctrinal-specificity vote threads is a textually determinate, purely (A) operation. The cross-front tension with ESHTR C2 is resolved by the source-reduction/specificity-match distinction rather than by conceding that SC7 thread-absence determination is tractable for principle-level ementas.

## 4. Anticipated Reply and Why It Does Not Suffice

### 4.1 The Human-Formalizer Response

The paper targets both "human" and "automated" formalizers. The defense accepts the circularity for automated systems and restricts the tractability claim to human formalizers relying on structural signals. For automated systems, the defense redirects: the taxonomy's three operational contributions — quality-assurance records, error-detection protocols, and coordination infrastructure — establish a system-level value that exceeds mere vocabulary.

The structural-signal argument does not suffice for the reasons in §3.1: the signals cover explicitly marked alternative foundations, not the competing-unmarked-threads class that constitutes the preprocessing scheme's hard case. The defense's own concession that "pendente" handles the signal-absent class confirms that the hard case is unresolved. The easy-case tractability the defense establishes — cases where practitioners read "ainda que" constructions structurally — is tractability for cases that practitioners can already handle without formal annotation infrastructure.

The recording-not-identification argument does not suffice for the reasons in §3.1. The response correctly identifies a genuine operational function — formal recording of reliable identifications — but recharacterizes the taxonomy's role from enabling to recording. The Abstract's enabling claim overstates what the taxonomy does. The QA-accuracy asymmetry further shows that the recording function's value is conditional on the accuracy of the underlying identification: for the hard class, the taxonomy records contestable merits judgments without validating their correctness. The QA record provides form-assurance without substance-assurance for the cases where substance-assurance is most needed.

The (A)/(B) textual-designative/conceptual-coverage distinction does not suffice for the reasons in §3.8. The distinction correctly establishes that SC7's standard case involves only (A), and (A) is tractable without (B) for concrete-specificity ementas. For the contested constitutional class where SC7's population is asserted to be most plausibly non-trivial, (A) and (B) entangle: typical SC7 threads are doctrinal elaborations of the ementa's stated principles, and determining whether such a thread is "textually stated" in or "textually absent" from an abstract principle formulation requires settling what the principle's abstract formulation covers at the thread's specificity level — which is (B). The ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function establishes normative authority (the ementa is decisive); it does not resolve the epistemological specificity-gap problem. The cross-front tension with ESHTR C2 dissolves when source-reduction tractability (C1: one document rather than N) is distinguished from specificity-match tractability (SC7: the one document's formulation is specific enough relative to the thread for textual comparison): the C1 concession accepts source-reduction tractability; it does not commit the adversarial to specificity-match tractability for principle-level constitutional ementas.

The operational-contributions uniqueness argument does not fully suffice for the reasons developed in §§3.5–3.7. The claim that no alternative scheme enables equivalent propagation-error detection for active-incorporation cases is an incompletely proven universal. The dispositif-grounding scheme — with the "ainda que"-class modification that treats explicit contingency markers as (B) regardless of chain position — provides detection capability equivalent to the taxonomy's for Sub-case A (tractable G-but-contingent cases). The doubly-modified scheme — adding the ementa-absence modification (§3.7) — achieves SC7 detection equivalence for the class the supportive round 4 identified. The taxonomy's unique detection advantage, if any, now requires a class beyond Sub-case A, Sub-case B, and SC7 where the taxonomy's necessity determination succeeds through a mechanism the doubly-modified scheme cannot replicate. Whether the taxonomy's specific categories provide additional detection capabilities for other propagation error types (e.g., propagation-by-omission) is a separate question; it does not establish uniqueness for the active-incorporation use case the supportive specifically invokes across the full case class.

The QA-accuracy asymmetry argument does not distinguish between the taxonomy and the dispositif-grounding scheme for Sub-case B. For competing-unmarked-threads cases where both threads appear in the explicit chain without structural signals, the dispositif-grounding annotator also cannot determine which thread was load-bearing; both receive (G) annotations; the QA record provides form-assurance without substance-assurance for this class, just as the taxonomy's "pendente" annotation does. The supportive round 3's claim that the QA-accuracy asymmetry is a general feature of hard-class annotation rather than a defect specific to the necessary/contingent taxonomy is accepted for Sub-case B. The operationally relevant comparison — between explicit indeterminacy-flagging and no annotation — applies equivalently to both schemes at the hard-class boundary. The G-but-contingent detection advantage (§3.6) does not change this parity for Sub-case B.

### 4.2 The Incremental-Progress Response

The paper might respond that requiring complete, tractable determination of claim provenance for all claims sets an impossibly high bar. Legal formalization proceeds incrementally: high-confidence (declarada, necessária) claims anchor the model; uncertain (inferida, pendente) regions are progressively resolved. The taxonomy supports this incremental process by making explicit what is known and what is not.

This response correctly captures how formalization work actually proceeds in practice. The practical value of marking epistemic status explicitly — knowing which parts of the model are solid and which are uncertain — is real and non-trivial.

The response does not rescue Commitment 1. The paper presents the (provenance, status) annotation as a preprocessing step that *enables* correct implementation. If the hardest cases — which include the most legally significant claims in complex decisions — cannot be determined without performing the legal analysis that is the formalization's main work, the annotation scheme is not a preprocessing step. It is a notation system for the outputs of the analysis once completed. This is a smaller but still useful contribution; the problem is that the paper presents it as a larger one. An honest statement of the contribution would say: the taxonomy provides vocabulary for recording the provenance and necessity status of claims after the relevant legal analysis has been performed, and this recording serves quality-assurance and auditability functions for the explicitly-marked class. That formulation does not claim preprocessing tractability; it correctly characterizes the taxonomy as vocabulary with form-assurance value for a defined scope.

### 4.3 The "Contestation Tracks Necessity" Response and the Candidate-Ratio Extension

On the ratio extraction failure for fragmented decisions (§3.3), the defense proposes the candidate-ratio extension: for parallel-reasoning decisions where the intersection of majority grounds is empty, construct candidate ratios from clusters of votes sharing necessary grounds. The defense acknowledges the adversarial paper's §3.3 directly and accepts that the minimum-denominator rule requires extension for parallel-reasoning decisions.

The adversarial reply is in §3.3. The vote-level necessity determination faces the same circularity structure as the document-level determination. Cross-referencing individual votes against the ementa is a consistency check, not a necessity-identification procedure within each vote. The extension decomposes the problem without resolving it.

The lower-incidence argument does not suffice for the reasons in §3.3 as updated. Single-authorship does not, by itself, predict lower competing-unmarked-threads incidence in the institutional context of STF constitutional adjudication. Two structural features — audience heterogeneity and precedent-building strategy — generate competing-unmarked-threads tendencies within individual votes for precisely the class of decisions most significant for the formalization system. The formal-consistency counterincentive under Art. 93, IX CF is accepted as operating in the direction the supportive claims — against parallel declarative-mode reasoning — but its effectiveness against the first two pressures in the most consequential STF constitutional adjudication remains SC6's empirical question. The lower-incidence claim for this decision class has not been established; the direction of a counterincentive does not establish its dominance. The reliance on inferior courts' informal reconstruction conflates goal-directed ground selection with analytical necessity determination: selecting the most persuasive available ground from a prior decision for present-case use is a different activity from determining which ground was load-bearing for the authoring justice's conclusion.

The defense's correct observations — that inferior courts already informally reconstruct from individual grounds, that the extension follows directly from Mitidiero's framework — confirm that the extension is available and its normative basis is established. These confirm SC3's path: incorporate the extension into §5.3 with the Mitidiero framework engaged and the vote-level necessity determination operationalized. They do not establish that the paper's current claim is sound.

## 5. Scope of the Attack

This attack targets:

Practitioners who can identify propagation-by-omission and propagation-by-active-incorporation in actual cases gain a sharper attack vector. - The paper's goal of providing systematic vocabulary for formalization work. This limited tractability claim survives, qualified by the QA-accuracy asymmetry: for this class, structural reliability grounds both the recording value and the QA function.

- The candidate-ratio extension as technically coherent and normatively grounded in Mitidiero's framework. The adversarial attack is that (a) the extension does not escape the vote-level necessity circularity, (b) the lower-incidence claim for individual STF votes is not established given institutional incentives, and (c) the extension is not in the paper. The attack does not contest the extension's logical coherence or desirability.

The adversarial argument establishes only that the schemes are not coextensive and that the dispositif-grounding scheme is adequate for active-incorporation detection. Whether the necessary/contingent taxonomy is superior across all use cases, or specifically valuable for propagation-by-omission detection, is a separate question not resolved here.

## 6. Surrender Conditions

The attack would not hold, or would hold only partially, under the following conditions.

1. **A procedure for Sub-case B of the competing-unmarked-threads class.** The G-but-contingent debate (§3.6) has refined SC1's operative scope. Sub-case A (tractable G-but-contingent with structural signals) is handled by the taxonomy and, with the "ainda que"-class modification, equivalently by the modified dispositif-grounding scheme. SC1's operative scope is Sub-case B: documents where both argumentative threads appear in the explicit dispositif chain in declarative mode without any structural contingency signal, and necessity determination requires the underlying legal analysis. If there exists an operational procedure — independent of the formalization system's main legal analysis — that determines necessity for Sub-case B, the preprocessing circularity is resolved for the cases where it bites most severely. The "pendente" annotation does not meet this condition; it marks cases where the determination failed rather than providing a tractable preprocessing output.

2. **Restricted scope: vocabulary, not preprocessing, with honest Abstract revision.** If the paper is recharacterized as providing a vocabulary for recording the outputs of legal analysis — with preprocessing tractability qualified to the explicitly-marked class for human formalizers, QA value qualified to the easy class where identification is structurally reliable, and post-analysis recording for the hard class and for automated systems — SC2 is satisfied at the cost of revising the Abstract's scope. The full SC2 path now requires: (a) revision of the Abstract's "human or automated" specification; (b) acknowledgment that the QA function provides form-assurance but not substance-assurance for the hard class; and (c) either distinguishing the dispositif-grounding scheme from the taxonomy by specifying additional detection capabilities the taxonomy uniquely provides, or conceding that the taxonomy's necessary/contingent categories are one workable implementation of the load-bearing/non-load-bearing distinction for active-incorporation detection.

3. **The candidate-ratio extension incorporated into §5.3, with the vote-level circularity and incidence claim addressed.** SC3 now requires three things: (a) incorporation of the extension into Paper 1C's §5.3 as a specified rule for the parallel-reasoning case, with Mitidiero's convergent/parallel distinction engaged; (b) specification of how the vote-level necessity determination — identifying which grounds in each justice's individual vote were load-bearing — can be performed structurally or procedurally without the legal analysis the §3.1 circularity identifies at the document level; and (c) either empirical evidence that competing-unmarked-threads patterns have low incidence in individual STF votes in constitutional adjudication, or an institutional argument explaining why the three structural features identified in §3.3 do not generate such patterns.

4. **Empirical evidence of reliable interrater agreement on necessary/contingent classification.** The defense has restricted the tractability claim to the explicitly-marked class. Interrater agreement data is now most valuable at two points: (a) for documents with structural markers — do trained practitioners reliably agree on necessity/contingency annotation from surface reading, without prior joint legal analysis of the underlying question? High agreement here would confirm the defense's scope-qualified claim. (b) For documents without markers (the "pendente" class) — what rate of agreement do trained practitioners achieve? High disagreement would confirm that necessity determination in this class depends on the practitioner's substantive legal analysis rather than on structural reading. The interrater evidence would allow the adversarial claim's scope to be calibrated against the structural-signal defense's scope claim.

5. **Evidence that the modified dispositif-grounding scheme is insufficient for active-incorporation detection in Sub-case A.** The G-but-contingent debate (§3.6) has refined this condition. The unmodified dispositif-grounding scheme fails for Sub-case A (G-but-contingent with explicit contingency markers on in-chain threads) — this is accepted. The modified scheme, treating "ainda que"-class markers as (B) regardless of chain position, provides detection capability equivalent to the taxonomy's for Sub-case A. SC5's operative scope is now Sub-case B: if it can be shown that detecting active-incorporation propagation errors in Sub-case B requires positive confirmation of contingency (not merely an indeterminacy flag), and that the taxonomy provides this positive confirmation where the modified dispositif-grounding scheme does not, the taxonomy's unique detection advantage extends to Sub-case B. No such mechanism has been identified; both schemes produce only indeterminacy flags for Sub-case B.

6. **Empirical evidence on STF individual vote structure in contested constitutional adjudication.** The institutional-incentives argument in §3.3 predicts that competing-unmarked-threads patterns occur with substantial frequency in individual STF votes on constitutional questions, given audience heterogeneity, precedent-building strategy, and formal-consistency incentives. If systematic study of individual STF votes shows that the competing-parallel-declaration pattern is rare — that justices typically produce clear primary-argument structures even in contested constitutional cases — the lower-incidence claim in the supportive defense would be empirically confirmed and the institutional-incentives argument would fail as applied to the STF's actual practice.

7. **The doubly-modified scheme achieves SC7 equivalence; residual detection advantage requires a class beyond Sub-case A, Sub-case B, and SC7; and the (A)/(B) distinction must be shown to resolve the specificity-gap problem for contested constitutional ementas.** The supportive round 4 identified the SC7 class: individual STF justice votes in colegiado decisions where a thread appears in the vote's explicit dispositif chain, lacks any "ainda que"-class in-document marker, and is absent from the ementa's characterization of the majority's grounds. SC7's identification is accepted. The doubly-modified dispositif-grounding scheme (adding the ementa-absence modification, §3.7) achieves SC7 detection equivalence provided ementa cross-referencing is structural — a condition the adversarial contests for contested constitutional adjudication where the ementa generalizes at a higher abstraction level than individual vote threads (§3.7). The supportive's third qualification (§3.8) introduces the (A)/(B) distinction as a response to the ementa-specificity probe, arguing SC7 requires only (A) textual-designative determination rather than (B) conceptual-coverage determination.

To survive §3.8's argument, the supportive must establish one of: (a) a class beyond Sub-case A, Sub-case B, and SC7 where the taxonomy's necessity determination succeeds through a mechanism the doubly-modified scheme cannot replicate; (b) that the (A)/(B) distinction resolves the specificity-gap problem for the contested constitutional class — specifically, that determining textual absence from a principle-level constitutional ementa does not require prior judgment about what the abstract principle covers at the vote thread's doctrinal specificity level; or (c) that the characteristic SC7 thread in contested constitutional adjudication is sufficiently non-overlapping with the ementa's stated principles that (A) determination does not entangle with (B). The adversarial's current position: no class satisfying (a) has been identified; for (b), the specificity gap between principle-level ementas and doctrinal-specificity threads makes (A) and (B) operationally entangled for the class where SC7 is most plausibly populated — the typical SC7 thread in contested constitutional adjudication is a doctrinal elaboration of the ementa's stated principle, not unrelated doctrinal content, making determining its textual absence from the abstract principle formulation a (B)-dependent judgment; for (c), the characteristic thread is in the semantic field of the ementa's stated principle (which is why it contributes to a majority vote on that constitutional question), making non-overlap insufficiently established. The ementa-as-authoritative-ratio function establishes the normative rule (the ementa is authoritative); it does not establish that the specificity gap between abstract constitutional principle formulations and doctrinal-specificity vote threads can be bridged by textual comparison alone.
